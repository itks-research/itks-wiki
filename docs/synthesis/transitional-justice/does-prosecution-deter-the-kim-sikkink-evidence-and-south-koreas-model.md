## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (4 sources)

Kim and Sikkink's (2010) quantitative analysis of 100 transitional countries represents the most rigorous empirical test of whether prosecution deters future human rights violations. Their finding -- that human rights prosecutions after transition are associated with improvements in human rights protection -- has been cited extensively and remains the foundational reference for what Sikkink terms the "justice cascade." The analysis controls for country-level factors and demonstrates that the effect is robust across regions and regime types.

Subsequent research refined the finding. Sikkink's 2011 work extended the analysis to show that deterrence operates through both socialization (norm change) and material punishment (fear of consequences), with significant international demonstration effects beyond the prosecuting country. Research with Dancy and Payne (2019) introduced a "multiple mechanisms" framework, finding that prosecutions are most effective when combined with truth-telling and reparations rather than operating in isolation. Critically, the research shows deterrence requires domestic institutional capacity -- international tribunals serve as backstops, but sustainable human rights improvements depend on strengthened domestic accountability institutions.

South Korea provides the concrete illustration. Presidents Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo were convicted in 1996 on charges of treason and insurrection. Chun received a life sentence, Roh seventeen years. Yet in December 1997, President Kim Young-sam pardoned both on the advice of President-elect Kim Dae-jung -- who had himself been sentenced to death by Chun's government. Lee and Kim (2024) analyze these cases of unconstitutional emergency measures, framing TJ as a prerequisite for legal closure. The prosecution-pardon sequence matters for Iran: it established criminal liability through due process while preserving a reconciliation pathway.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> ⚫ **Evidence Confidence: Very Low** — No sources cited

The central pattern is that deterrence and reconciliation are not mutually exclusive -- they can operate sequentially. The prosecution-pardon model establishes a crucial precedent: former heads of state are not immune from criminal accountability, while clemency remains available as a political tool for reconciliation. This calibrated approach appears more effective for long-term human rights outcomes than either pure impunity or maximalist punishment.

A second pattern is that domestic institutional capacity is the critical variable. International tribunals can provide justice for individual cases, but the systematic deterrence effect requires local courts, local prosecutors, and local legal frameworks. This has direct operational implications for transition planning.

## Iran Context

> ⚫ **Evidence Confidence: Very Low** — No sources cited

The Kim-Sikkink evidence base provides the strongest empirical argument for some form of accountability mechanism in a future Iranian transition. The South Korean prosecution-pardon sequence offers a specific procedural template: establish criminal liability through prosecution, then exercise clemency strategically for reconciliation.

This model is particularly relevant for mid-ranking officials in the IRGC, judiciary, and intelligence services. These individuals might cooperate with a transition if they perceive a credible path from accountability to reintegration -- but not if they face maximalist punishment with no exit. Any future TJ process that relies primarily on international mechanisms (ICC referral, universal jurisdiction in European courts) without building domestic judicial capacity would likely produce short-term accountability but not long-term deterrence. The operational priority for transition planners should be domestic institutional development alongside, not instead of, international legal backstops.

## Open Research Questions

- Would Iran's complex institutional structure -- where the Supreme Leader, Guardian Council, IRGC, and judiciary each bear distinct forms of responsibility -- require a prosecution strategy that distinguishes between decision-makers, implementers, and enablers?
- How could a prosecution-pardon mechanism be designed to incentivize cooperation from mid-ranking regime officials without appearing to grant impunity to senior decision-makers?
- What domestic judicial capacity would need to be built during or immediately after a transition to sustain the deterrence effect that Kim and Sikkink's research demonstrates?

## Implications for Iran

> ⚫ **Evidence Confidence: Very Low** — No sources cited

**Institutional Mapping:** Prosecution in a future Iranian transition would target specific institutional chains of command. For the 1988 massacres: the Death Commissions (هیئت‌های مرگ) operated in Tehran, Karaj, and provincial prisons under Supreme Leader Khomeini's fatwa, with identified members including former senior officials. For the 2019 November crackdown: the Supreme National Security Council issued the suppression order; the IRGC ground forces, Basij, and FARAJA executed it; the IRGC intelligence organization and MOIS conducted subsequent arrests and interrogations. For systematic judicial violations: Revolutionary Court judges (قضات دادگاه‌های انقلاب) issued sentences without due process over decades. For the 2022 crackdown: FARAJA's morality police, IRGC intelligence, and Basij forces are documented perpetrators. The judiciary (headed by Supreme Leader appointees Sadegh Larijani, Ebrahim Raisi, Gholamhossein Mohseni-Ejei) bears institutional responsibility for legitimizing repression through legal process.

**Structural Parallels:** South Korea's prosecution-pardon sequence for Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo provides the most directly transferable template. The parallel holds strongly: identifiable heads of state and senior commanders who ordered specific, documented violations. The conviction-then-clemency model established criminal liability while preserving a reconciliation pathway — precisely the balance Iran needs. However, the parallel breaks down in scale: South Korea prosecuted two former presidents and approximately 16 co-conspirators for a single discrete event (the Gwangju massacre and the coup). Iran faces multiple waves of violations spanning four decades, implicating hundreds of senior officials across multiple institutions. The Kim-Sikkink 100-country dataset provides the macro-evidence: prosecutions are associated with improved human rights outcomes. The Dancy-Sikkink-Payne "multiple mechanisms" finding — that prosecutions work best when combined with truth-telling and reparations — argues for a comprehensive rather than prosecution-only approach.

**Key Actors and Sequencing:** The key insight for Iran is the distinction between domestic and international prosecution pathways. International mechanisms (ICC referral, universal jurisdiction in European courts, UN-mandated tribunals) provide legal backstops but cannot build domestic institutional capacity. Domestic prosecution requires: (1) reconstituting the judiciary with independent judges — a massive undertaking given that the current judiciary is thoroughly politicized under decades of Supreme Leader appointments; (2) establishing specialized chambers for transition-era prosecutions, following Tunisia's IVD model of referring cases to dedicated courts; (3) developing witness protection programs given the security apparatus's demonstrated capacity for intimidation; (4) creating a prosecution strategy that prioritizes senior decision-makers while offering cooperation pathways for mid-ranking officials. The South Korean sequencing — prosecution first, pardon for reconciliation later — should be adapted: formal legal proceedings establishing criminal liability, followed by a politically negotiated clemency framework that trades reduced sentences for full truth-telling cooperation.

**Risks:** The primary risk is that prosecution threats deter regime insiders from cooperating with transition. If IRGC commanders, intelligence officials, and senior judges perceive that any transition leads inevitably to personal imprisonment, they will resist transition with all available means — and they control formidable means. The South Korean model mitigates this through the pardon pathway, but credibility is essential: insiders must believe that cooperation genuinely leads to reduced consequences. A second risk is reliance on international mechanisms without domestic capacity-building — universal jurisdiction cases in European courts may produce individual convictions but cannot transform Iran's domestic legal institutions. The prerequisite is early investment in judicial reform and prosecutorial capacity, running in parallel with (not after) political transition. Kim and Sikkink's finding that deterrence requires domestic institutional capacity, not just international backstops, must guide sequencing.

## What We Don't Know

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** —  | 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟡 Moderate** (70%)

**Open Research Questions**

1. **Selection bias in prosecution data**: The Kim-Sikkink dataset covers prosecutions that occurred — but we don't know what would have happened in the counterfactual absence of prosecutions. Countries that successfully deterred human rights violations may never appear in the dataset because no violations occurred.

2. **IRGC-specific deterrence calculations**: Standard deterrence theory assumes perpetrators rationally calculate risks. IRGC commanders operate within an institution with documented culture of impunity and access to resources that can sustain exile, asset protection, and political pressure. How deterrence mechanisms apply to institutionally protected perpetrators is undertheorized.

3. **Temporal dynamics**: Most prosecution deterrence studies focus on the immediate transition period. Little evidence exists on whether deterrence effects persist 10-20 years after transition, when international attention has shifted and domestic political coalitions have realigned.

4. **Selective prosecution effects**: When prosecution is clearly selective (only punishing one faction of former perpetrators, not another), does it deter or provoke? South Korea's prosecution of Chun Doo-hwan but not lower-ranking perpetrators may have had perverse effects on institutional accountability culture.

5. **Interaction with amnesty provisions**: How do partial amnesty provisions (common in transition bargaining) interact with deterrence calculations? Does conditional amnesty increase or decrease the credibility of prosecution threats?

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** —  | 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟡 Moderate** (70%)

Can prosecuting former human rights abusers deter future violations? The evidence says yes — but the mechanism is more complex than simple punishment threat. Kim and Sikkink's landmark study shows that countries with prosecution records have better subsequent human rights outcomes, but the causal pathway involves international norms diffusion, civil society pressure, and institutional design as much as direct deterrence of potential perpetrators. For Iran, this matters because any transition will involve decisions about how to handle IRGC commanders, prison officials, and intelligence officers responsible for documented atrocities. The evidence suggests that prosecution of high-level commanders, even when incomplete, produces better long-term outcomes than blanket amnesty — but the design of prosecution mechanisms matters enormously for both justice and political sustainability.
