## Research Question

How should transitional justice (TJ) frameworks be designed for a post-military-intervention Iran — specifically, when the transition does not originate from negotiated elite pacts but from external armed pressure combined with sustained internal protest mobilization, and when accountability processes must reckon simultaneously with atrocities committed by the prior theocratic-security regime and with new violations generated by the military intervention itself?

### Context: Why This Question Is Analytically Novel

Articles 11 and 13 in this database established robust comparative foundations for TJ in Iran, drawing on evidence from Chile, Spain, Tunisia, Indonesia, South Korea, and the Eastern European post-communist transitions. Those articles operated within the dominant TJ paradigm: pacted transitions in which outgoing authoritarian elites negotiate exit terms including, implicitly or explicitly, degrees of immunity. The evidentiary base remained valid, but the political context informing their Iran implications assumed that regime change, if it came, would emerge primarily from internal fracture or negotiated withdrawal.

The military strikes launched by US and Israeli forces beginning February 28, 2026, followed by the assassination of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and the elevation of Mojtaba Khamenei, have structurally altered the transition environment. As of April 2026, Iran exists in a condition scholars call a "dual-trauma" transition: one trauma is the accumulated record of 45 years of Islamic Republic governance — the 1988 prison massacres, chain murders (قتل‌های زنجیره‌ای), systematic torture, extrajudicial executions (اعدام فراقضایی), enforced disappearances (ناپدیدسازی اجباری), and crackdowns on the Woman Life Freedom movement in 2022–2024 — and a second trauma is the violence generated by the military intervention: civilian casualties from strikes, displacement, and potentially indiscriminate damage to civilian infrastructure near nuclear and military sites.

This dual-trauma structure has no exact comparative precedent in the post-Cold War TJ literature, though partial analogies exist in Iraq after 2003, in Libya after 2011, and more distantly in Germany and Japan after 1945. The research question, therefore, must be framed not merely as "what TJ mechanisms are optimal for Iran?" but rather:

**Primary question:** Under what conditions do post-military-intervention transitional justice processes achieve accountability and institutional reform without producing the legitimacy deficit that coerced or externally designed TJ consistently generates?

**Sub-questions:**
1. How do comparative cases inform the sequencing problem — specifically whether TJ institution-building must await final political settlement, or whether preliminary architecture can be established during ceasefire-negotiation phases such as the current Islamabad framework?
2. What accountability mechanisms are available for a security apparatus (IRGC/سپاه پاسداران انقلاب اسلامی) that retains territorial control and armed capacity even while its political role is being renegotiated?
3. How does external pressure — from states involved in the military intervention and from the Islamabad multi-party negotiating framework — affect the durability of amnesty agreements and the legitimacy of accountability mechanisms in the eyes of Iranian domestic publics?
4. What role can diaspora-held documentation and internationally distributed evidence play in TJ processes given that the intervention itself may have generated additional evidence of state atrocities through signals intelligence and targeting records?
5. How should a dual-trauma TJ process address harms caused by both the prior regime and the intervening powers without collapsing into competitive victimhood narratives that undermine future institutional legitimacy (مشروعیت)?

### Scope and Analytical Boundaries

This article does not advocate for any particular political outcome. All claims are conditional: framed as "if condition X obtains, then outcome Y is comparatively more likely." The article does not assume that any specific political grouping will form a future Iranian government, nor does it predict the outcome of the Islamabad talks. It analyzes the structural conditions under which TJ frameworks tend to succeed or fail under post-intervention conditions, drawing on comparative evidence rather than normative prescription.

The article treats transitional justice as encompassing five overlapping domains: (1) criminal accountability — prosecutions, trials, and international legal proceedings; (2) truth-telling — commissions, documentation, and official acknowledgment; (3) reparations — material, symbolic, and institutional compensation for victims; (4) lustration and vetting — removal of perpetrators from public office and security services; and (5) institutional reform — restructuring the judicial, security, and educational systems that enabled or perpetuated violations. All five domains are implicated in the current Iranian transition environment, but they do not all need to be sequenced simultaneously.

### Methodological Note on Conditional Claims

All claims in this article are structured as conditional: "if condition X obtains, then outcome Y is comparatively more likely." This conditional framing is deliberate and reflects the methodological standards appropriate for comparative TJ analysis in a rapidly evolving context. The claim structure is: (a) identify the activating condition, drawn from comparative cases; (b) identify the outcome variable, measured in the comparative literature; (c) specify the mechanism connecting condition to outcome; (d) identify complicating or disconfirming evidence from other cases; and (e) assess the probability that the activating condition obtains in Iran's current context.

This framework avoids both false certainty (claiming that specific TJ mechanisms will produce specific outcomes in Iran, which no comparative study can guarantee) and false agnosticism (refusing to draw any lessons from comparative evidence). The goal is calibrated conditional analysis: tracking which conditions must obtain for which mechanisms to activate, and which conditions are more or less probable given what is currently known about Iran's April 2026 transition environment.

The article does not argue that Iran "should" adopt any particular TJ framework. It analyzes which frameworks are more or less likely to achieve stated TJ goals — accountability, truth-telling, reparations, lustration, and institutional reform — under specified conditions. Readers who seek to assess specific policy proposals can use this conditional framework to evaluate whether the proposed mechanism's activating conditions are likely to obtain in Iran's current context, and whether the proposed outcome variable is the appropriate measure of success given the evidence base.

## Comparative Evidence from Key Cases

The following case analyses focus on the specific features most relevant to a post-military-intervention Iranian context: external pressure on TJ design, armed security actor accountability, sequencing under active conflict or ceasefire, and the legitimacy of TJ institutions in the eyes of domestic populations with multiple victimhood constituencies.

### Case 1: Iraq After 2003 — The De-Baathification Catastrophe
**Evidence confidence: HIGH**

Iraq represents the most direct and cautionary comparison for post-intervention TJ. Pfiffner [9742], Dodge [9740], and Allawi [9741] document how the Coalition Provisional Authority's de-Baathification order (CPA Order No. 1) and disbandment of the Iraqi military (CPA Order No. 2) constituted externally designed TJ implemented without domestic political buy-in, institutional capacity, or sequencing logic.

The de-Baathification process removed approximately 30,000 individuals from the top four tiers of Baath Party membership across all state sectors. Ziren [9776] and Zeren [9797] show that this figure expanded dramatically in application, ultimately affecting over 100,000 civil servants, teachers, and military officers whose primary qualification for exclusion was formal Baath membership required for professional advancement under Saddam Hussein. The process lacked any appeal mechanism for lower-tier members, made no distinction between ideological commitment and coerced membership, and created no rehabilitation pathway.

The outcomes are analytically instructive: the former Iraqi military, disbanding without pensions or reintegration resources, became the primary recruitment pool for the Sunni insurgency. A functionally motivated TJ mechanism — removing perpetrators of Baath violence from state power — produced a security catastrophe because it was designed and implemented by external actors without domestic legitimacy, operated on overbroad categorical criteria, and lacked sequencing logic. It preceded, rather than followed, the establishment of stable security institutions capable of managing the disruptive social effects.

For Iran, the Iraq case generates a clear conditional pattern: if lustration (پاکسازی اداری) of IRGC personnel is designed by external parties to the Islamabad framework without Iranian domestic ownership, applied on broad categorical criteria rather than individual conduct-based vetting (گزینش و بررسی صلاحیت), and implemented before alternative security structures capable of maintaining order are established, then outcomes comparable to post-2003 Iraq — increased violence, delegitimized TJ institutions, and spoiler insurgency — become significantly more probable.

### Case 2: Chile — Constrained Accountability and Its Long Arc
**Evidence confidence: HIGH**

Chile's post-Pinochet TJ trajectory, spanning 1990 to the present, is among the most extensively documented in the ITKS corpus. The Chilean transition is characterized by what Aguilar [425] terms "constrained accountability": the 1978 Amnesty Law protected military perpetrators during the transition, the 1990 Rettig Commission established a truth record without prosecutions, and subsequent decades saw incremental expansion of accountability through reinterpretation of the Amnesty Law, foreign prosecutions under universal jurisdiction (صلاحیت جهانی), and eventually the arrest and prosecution of Pinochet himself — though he died before trial.

Several Chilean patterns are directly relevant to Iran:

**The Amnesty-Accountability Sequential Model:** Aguilar et al. [496] document that Spanish and Chilean populations showed distinct attitudinal patterns: Spanish populations more strongly supported procedural peace over accountability, while Chilean survey data showed persistent popular support for accountability that increased over time. This finding complicates the assumption that amnesty agreements are politically stable. If a post-war Iranian TJ process includes coerced amnesty elements negotiated under ceasefire pressure in Islamabad, the Chilean evidence suggests these agreements are likely to erode — particularly as younger generations who did not experience the acute conflict phase become politically active.

**The Diaspora Documentation Variable:** Chile's transition benefited from extensive international documentation accumulated during the dictatorship, including by the United Nations, the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, and diaspora organizations. This pre-existing evidence base enabled truth commission work that did not depend solely on perpetrator cooperation. For Iran, the Woman Life Freedom documentation ecosystem — satellite imagery, digital forensics, diaspora testimonial networks — fulfills an analogous function. Additionally, the 2026 military intervention may have generated new intelligence documentation of IRGC communications, command structures, and chain-of-command evidence relevant to prior atrocities.

**The Lustration-Prosecution Dilemma:** Chile attempted to hold DINA (secret police) personnel accountable while reintegrating lower-level military officers into a reformed armed forces. The challenges of this bifurcated approach — distinguishing accountability tiers within a unified command structure — are directly analogous to the IRGC problem. As of April 2026, the IRGC remains a hierarchically complex organization whose members range from senior commanders who directed atrocities to conscript-equivalent personnel whose participation in repression was coerced or marginal.

### Case 3: Argentina — Maximum Prosecution Strategy and Its Institutional Prerequisites
**Evidence confidence: HIGH**

Argentina's post-junta trajectory differs fundamentally from Chile's in that it pursued comprehensive prosecutions of military leadership through domestic courts — first under Alfonsin (the CONADEP truth commission and early trials), then partially reversed (Full Stop and Due Obedience laws under Menem), and then fully revived under Kirchner when the Full Stop laws were struck down as unconstitutional in 2003.

The Argentine case is instructive precisely because it demonstrates that accountability mechanisms can be suspended and then revived under changed political conditions. Kim and Sikkink [167] use Argentina as a central case in their 100-country analysis showing that prosecutions improve subsequent human rights outcomes — but their analysis also shows that this effect is conditional on domestic institutional capacity. Argentina had a functioning civilian judicial system, a bar association with significant independence, and a civil society capable of generating victims' testimony and legal advocacy. The junta, when prosecuted, was no longer in control of territory or armed forces.

For Iran, the Argentine comparison raises a critical conditional: if post-war IRGC factions retain territorial control in peripheral regions (particularly in areas with significant IRGC logistical and garrison presence), then maximum prosecution strategies will face the same "spoiler capacity" (ظرفیت اخلالگری) problem that derailed Argentina's first prosecution wave in the late 1980s. Prosecutions are more likely to advance when the security apparatus has been substantially demobilized, disarmed, or divided, reducing its ability to obstruct judicial processes.

### Case 4: South Korea — The Prosecution-Then-Pardon Sequence
**Evidence confidence: HIGH**

South Korea's post-authoritarian TJ trajectory offers a distinct model: former presidents Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo were prosecuted and convicted for crimes including the 1980 Gwangju massacre, then pardoned by their successor. Kim [5284] documents the later Jeju 4.3 investigation, which sought truth about events that had been officially suppressed for 50 years, completing a truth process decades after the crimes occurred.

The South Korean case demonstrates several conditional patterns relevant to Iran:

**Prosecution Preceding Pardon:** The sequence matters. South Korea's TJ achieved greater long-term legitimacy because prosecutions established factual records — criminal verdicts — before pardons were extended. The pardon without prosecution would have been experienced as impunity (مصونیت از مجازات). The prosecution followed by pardon created a political settlement that acknowledged wrongdoing while creating space for elite reconciliation. This sequencing model may be adaptable to Iran if Islamabad framework negotiations include demands for prisoner release or immunity for specific actors: conditional prosecution-acknowledgment before any amnesty extension represents a more durable arrangement than pre-emptive blanket amnesty.

**Delayed Truth for Suppressed Events:** The Jeju 4.3 process suggests that for specific atrocities — like the 1988 Iranian prison massacres — delayed truth commission investigation is feasible and legitimate even decades after the events, provided civil society documentation has been preserved and victim family organizations remain active. The persistence of Iranian victim family networks, particularly those organized around the 1988 massacres and the 2019-2024 protest crackdowns, suggests the organizational infrastructure for this kind of delayed truth process exists.

### Case 5: Spain — The Pact of Silence and Its Unraveling
**Evidence confidence: HIGH**

Spain's transition from Francoism relied on a formal and informal "pact of silence" (پیمان فراموشی): the 1977 Amnesty Law granted immunity to both sides of the Civil War and subsequent Francoist political crimes. Aguilar [310] documents how this pact achieved short-term democratic consolidation but produced long-term legitimacy deficits. The 2007 Law of Historical Memory and 2022 Democratic Memory Law represent the eventual unraveling of the pact under pressure from civil society exhumation movements, victim family organizations, and generational turnover.

The Spanish trajectory is directly relevant to Islamabad framework negotiations if those negotiations include Iranian government (or successor authority) demands for general amnesty as a condition of political settlement. Wiebelhaus-Brahm [1358] and Aguilar [499] show that pacted silences in Spain took 30-45 years to fully unravel, but Iran's digital documentation ecosystem and the pre-existing international accountability mechanisms (ICC referral considerations, universal jurisdiction cases in European courts) compress this timeline significantly. An amnesty negotiated in 2026 under ceasefire pressure would face challenge from existing legal proceedings in European jurisdictions that cannot be extinguished by Iranian government agreement.

### Case 6: Libya After 2011 — TJ Without Institutional Infrastructure
**Evidence confidence: MODERATE**

Libya's post-Gaddafi experience provides the clearest negative case: transitional justice attempted without functioning state institutions, with multiple armed factions each pursuing their own accountability and impunity simultaneously, and with international actors unable to provide the institutional scaffolding the Libyan state could not supply.

Eljarh [9774] and International Crisis Group [9768] document how Libya's political isolation law — targeting former regime members — became a factional weapon rather than an accountability instrument. Militia-run detention facilities held thousands without judicial process. The ICC's Libya cases (Saif al-Islam Gaddafi) became mired in complementarity disputes between ICC jurisdiction and Libyan domestic courts that lacked capacity to conduct fair trials.

The Libya case generates a stark conditional for Iran: if TJ institutions are designed and launched before a functional state authority capable of maintaining order, operating fair courts, and managing the social disruption of accountability processes has been established, then TJ processes are significantly more likely to be captured by armed factions, weaponized for political exclusion, and ultimately delegitimized. This has direct implications for the sequencing question: whether TJ architecture should be developed during the Islamabad ceasefire negotiations or deferred to post-settlement stabilization.

### Case 7: Tunisia's IVD — The Most Relevant Arab-Islamic Model
**Evidence confidence: MODERATE**

Tunisia's Instance Vérité et Dignité (IVD), established December 2013, received 62,354 complaint files, conducted public hearings broadcast on prime-time television, and sought to integrate Islamic jurisprudential concepts — including sulh (صلح) and tawbah (توبه) — into formal TJ mechanisms. Kazemi [747] documents both the strengths and limitations of this integration: Islamic TJ concepts provided legitimacy with conservative constituencies but created procedural tensions with international human rights standards, particularly regarding the unconditional right to prosecution.

For Iran, the IVD experience offers both a model and a warning. The model: public truth-telling in an Islamic-majority society can achieve broadcast legitimacy and victim participation at scale. The warning: the IVD's ultimate failure — it was politically isolated by successive Tunisian governments and effectively suspended before completing its mandate — was driven by elite resistance from actors who feared accountability. Tunisia's democracy itself subsequently collapsed under Saied's 2021 power consolidation, demonstrating that TJ processes are not self-executing but depend on sustained political protection from governing coalitions committed to accountability.

The Iran comparison suggests that any TJ process developed under or after the Islamabad framework will require ongoing political protection — either from domestic governing coalitions or from international legal mechanisms — to avoid the IVD's fate of political strangulation by successor elites with accountability exposure.

## Cross-Case Patterns

Analysis across the seven primary cases generates the following cross-cutting patterns. Each pattern includes the mechanism through which the effect operates, the conditions under which it activates, and the cases that provide supporting and complicating evidence.

### Pattern 1: External Design Deficit — Legitimacy Inversely Correlated with External Authorship
**Confidence: HIGH**

Across Iraq [9741, 9742, 9740], Libya [9774, 9768], and to a lesser extent Germany and Japan, TJ processes primarily designed by external intervening powers demonstrate systematically lower domestic legitimacy than domestically designed processes, even when the external processes are technically more rigorous. The mechanism is straightforward: populations that experienced the prior regime's violence do not automatically experience externally designed accountability as "their" justice; they experience it as occupation justice, which activates nationalist resistance frameworks that delegitimize the process.

Iraq's de-Baathification illustrates the extreme case: a broadly supported goal (removing Baathist perpetrators from state power) became delegitimized because its implementation was externally controlled, overbroad, and lacked domestic ownership. Germany and Japan represent partial exceptions — denazification achieved greater domestic acceptance — but the German and Japanese cases involved total military defeat, occupation, and a much longer time horizon before domestic political processes resumed control.

For Iran, the pattern generates a conditional: if TJ mechanisms are developed primarily through the Islamabad framework by external parties (including intervening powers) rather than through a domestically convened process with Iranian civil society participation, then the resulting institutions are likely to be experienced as externally imposed, reducing victim community participation, perpetrator cooperation, and political protection from future Iranian governing coalitions.

Activation conditions for this pattern: (a) significant external military presence or political authority in Iran during TJ design; (b) absence of functioning Iranian civil society institutions with demonstrated TJ mandate; (c) narrative framing of TJ as part of external occupation rather than Iranian national reckoning.

Complicating evidence: South Africa's TRC was externally supported but domestically designed and led; Tunisia's IVD was domestically designed but externally funded. Both achieved greater legitimacy than Iraq's de-Baathification despite significant external involvement. The critical variable appears to be who formally controls the design and implementation process, not who provides technical or financial support.

### Pattern 2: Spoiler Capacity — Armed Actors With Territorial Control Obstruct Accountability
**Confidence: HIGH**

Argentina's early prosecution reversal [1357], Libya's militia capture of TJ processes [9768, 9774], and Spain's Amnesty Law [425, 310] all demonstrate the same mechanism: when actors with accountability exposure retain military capacity and territorial control, they can reverse, block, or capture TJ processes through armed or political pressure. The mechanism operates through two channels: direct coercive obstruction (assassinations, intimidation of witnesses and judges) and political obstruction (electoral mobilization to block prosecutorial legislation, appointment of sympathetic judges).

Chile's constrained accountability trajectory illustrates the pattern most clearly: Pinochet's self-appointment as senator-for-life and commander of the armed forces during the transition period explicitly preserved military veto power over TJ design. Accountability expanded only as military political leverage diminished — as the officer corps who had participated in repression retired, as civilian institutions consolidated, and as international legal mechanisms (Pinochet's London arrest) demonstrated that domestic political protection had limits.

For Iran, this pattern is directly and immediately relevant. As of April 2026, the IRGC (سپاه پاسداران انقلاب اسلامی) retains significant armed capacity despite military stress from the US-Israel strikes. IRGC factions control economic assets (through Khatam al-Anbiya/قرارگاه سازندگی خاتم‌الانبیاء), maintain regional proxy relationships, and operate internal intelligence functions. The degree to which IRGC leadership retains cohesion and territorial control under continued military pressure will be a primary determinant of accountability feasibility.

Activation conditions: (a) IRGC retains command cohesion above battalion level; (b) IRGC units remain deployed in urban areas with civilian population leverage; (c) no alternative security force capable of ensuring judicial process protection; (d) IRGC leadership assesses prosecution as more threatening to survival than armed resistance.

### Pattern 3: Documentation Quality Determines Accountability Scope
**Evidence confidence: MODERATE-HIGH**

Spain [427, 428], Chile [1478, 1487, 1489], and South Korea [5284] all demonstrate that the scope of accountability achievable through TJ processes is constrained by available documentation. Where documentation is extensive — as in the CONADEP process in Argentina and the Rettig Commission in Chile, which drew on pre-existing human rights organization archives — accountability processes can proceed even where perpetrator cooperation is minimal. Where documentation depends primarily on perpetrator testimony — as in Chile's initial Pinochet-era arrangements and Spain's early transition period — accountability is severely limited.

The mechanism operates through the evidentiary requirements of prosecutorial processes: without documentation establishing chain of command, individual participation, and specific criminal acts, criminal prosecution becomes technically impossible even where political will exists. Truth commission processes are less constrained — they can acknowledge patterns of systematic violation without attributing individual criminal responsibility — but their findings lack the legal force of criminal verdicts.

For Iran, the documentation landscape is unusually favorable by comparative standards. Three independent documentation streams exist: (1) Iranian civil society and diaspora documentation accumulated since 1988, including the extensive Abdorrahman Boroumand Center records on executions, the 1988 massacre documentation, and the Woman Life Freedom documentation networks; (2) international human rights organization records from Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and the UN Special Rapporteur process; (3) potentially, intelligence documentation generated by the 2026 military intervention — targeting records, intercepted communications, and signals intelligence that may include evidence of IRGC command communications regarding suppression operations.

The third stream is analytically significant but requires careful handling: intelligence documentation generated by intervening powers will face legitimacy challenges if it appears to serve those powers' post-intervention political interests, replicating the dynamic that delegitimized some German denazification processes where Allied intelligence archives were selectively used.

### Pattern 4: Sequential Vs. Simultaneous TJ — Sequencing Determines Sustainability
**Evidence confidence: MODERATE**

South Korea [5246], Spain [310, 498], and Chile [5465] demonstrate a consistent pattern: TJ mechanisms introduced simultaneously across all five domains (prosecution, truth, reparations, lustration, reform) tend to generate political backlash and resource exhaustion more rapidly than sequenced approaches. Chile's staged approach — truth commission first, reparations second, prosecutions decades later as political space expanded — proved more durable than Argentina's initial simultaneous prosecution/truth/reparation attempt, which suffered the Full Stop reversal.

However, the sequencing pattern operates differently in post-military-intervention contexts than in pacted transition contexts. In pacted transitions, sequencing reflects bargained trade-offs between accountability and stability. In post-intervention contexts like Iraq and Libya, sequencing is often driven by external actors with different time horizons from domestic populations — external actors want quick, visible accountability; domestic populations need sustainable institutions.

For Iran, the sequencing question is further complicated by the active Islamabad negotiations. TJ demands may be introduced as negotiating elements — prisoner release, immunity guarantees, or accountability commitments — before any stable political framework exists. The comparative evidence suggests this is high-risk: TJ elements negotiated under acute conflict conditions (when leverage distributions are temporarily extreme) tend to produce agreements that neither side has sufficient legitimacy or incentive to implement once the acute crisis resolves.

### Pattern 5: Civil Society Substitution — Non-State Actors as TJ Drivers When States Refuse
**Evidence confidence: HIGH**

Spain [427], Chile [1487], and South Korea [5284] all demonstrate that when state TJ processes are blocked, delayed, or captured, civil society organizations — particularly victim family associations and diaspora human rights networks — can sustain documentation, advocate for accountability, and eventually provide the evidentiary and organizational foundation for later formal TJ processes. Spain's grassroots exhumation movement, operating independently for three decades before receiving state recognition in 2007, is the most developed example. Chile's vicariate of solidarity and the diaspora networks that sustained international pressure on Pinochet are direct antecedents.

For Iran, the diaspora documentation ecosystem (ایرانیان خارج از کشور) constitutes a significant pre-existing civil society TJ infrastructure. The critical question for post-war Iran is whether this diaspora infrastructure — accumulated over 45 years of exile — has the domestic legitimacy to translate into TJ institutions with Iranian-inside buy-in. The Spanish and Chilean cases suggest that diaspora-held documentation is most valuable as evidentiary input into domestically led processes, rather than as the primary driver of accountability. Diaspora-led processes, when not connected to domestic victim communities, may generate the external-design-deficit problem identified in Pattern 1.

## Causal Mechanisms

Building on the cross-case patterns, this section identifies the primary causal mechanisms through which TJ framework design affects outcomes, with specific attention to activation conditions in the Iranian post-war context.

### Mechanism 1: Legitimacy Production — How TJ Processes Generate or Destroy Public Trust
**Confidence: MODERATE-HIGH**

The comparative literature consistently shows that TJ mechanisms generate legitimacy through three channels: process legitimacy (was the process fair and inclusive?), outcome legitimacy (did it produce truth, accountability, or reparations that matched victim expectations?), and institutional legitimacy (were the institutions seen as independent from powerful actors?). Aguilar [496] demonstrates through Spanish survey data that all three matter independently and interact: high process legitimacy can partially compensate for limited outcome legitimacy, but institutional capture — the perception that perpetrators influenced the process — destroys legitimacy even when outcomes are substantial.

For Iran, activation of this mechanism depends on: (a) who is seen as controlling the TJ process — Iranian civil society vs. intervening powers vs. successor regime elites; (b) whether victim communities across the full spectrum of the Islamic Republic's violence are included — not only the most politically prominent cases but Kurdish massacres, Baluch executions, 1988 prison killings, and protest crackdowns; and (c) whether the TJ process is perceived as addressing the intervention's harms as well as the prior regime's, or treating only one set of victimhood as legitimate.

### Mechanism 2: Spoiler Deterrence — How Accountability Threats Change Security Actor Behavior
**Confidence: MODERATE**

Kim and Sikkink [167] demonstrate through their 100-country analysis that human rights prosecutions improve subsequent human rights outcomes — but the mechanism is not simply deterrence. The mechanism operates through two sub-paths: (a) direct deterrence — actors who fear prosecution modify behavior to avoid acts that would create prosecutorial evidence; and (b) norm internalization — repeated prosecution events signal that the international and domestic normative environment has shifted against impunity, changing the expected payoffs for future violations even among actors not directly threatened by prosecution.

For Iran, the spoiler deterrence mechanism is relevant in both directions. If IRGC leadership calculates that TJ processes are sufficiently certain and comprehensive that resistance is futile — a calculation that might emerge if international legal proceedings are advancing, if their names appear in indictments, and if allied powers are unwilling to provide safe haven — they may find cooperation with TJ processes preferable to continued armed resistance. This is the mechanism through which South Korea's prosecutions functioned: the credible threat of conviction, even for former presidents, signaled that the normative environment had changed sufficiently to make resistance politically unviable.

Activation conditions: (a) credible international legal proceedings (ICC referrals, universal jurisdiction cases in European courts) that cannot be blocked by domestic political amnesty; (b) allied powers (Russia, China, remaining regional partners) unwilling or unable to provide effective safe haven or political protection; (c) IRGC calculation that accountability exposure via cooperation is lower than accountability exposure via continued armed resistance.

### Mechanism 3: The Documentation-Prosecution Feedback Loop
**Confidence: MODERATE**

Documentation and prosecution exist in a feedback relationship: documentation enables prosecution, successful prosecution creates evidentiary incentives for further documentation (as perpetrators defect and provide testimony in exchange for reduced charges), and the resulting expanded evidentiary base enables more comprehensive accountability. This mechanism is most clearly visible in Argentina, where the CONADEP process generated documentation that enabled prosecutions, which in turn generated perpetrator testimony that expanded the evidentiary record for subsequent cases.

For Iran, activating this mechanism requires a threshold of credible prosecution threat sufficient to motivate perpetrator defection. If IRGC mid-level commanders calculate that cooperation with documentation processes offers better personal outcomes than solidarity with leadership, the feedback loop can accelerate the evidentiary foundation for accountability. The Chilean case suggests this threshold is never automatic — it requires judicial credibility, protection for cooperating witnesses, and meaningful distinctions in charging strategies that make cooperation materially advantageous.

### Mechanism 4: Martyrology Contestation — How TJ Processes Navigate Competing Victimhood Claims
**Confidence: LOW-MODERATE**

This mechanism is identified more tentatively because comparative evidence is less systematic. In post-conflict contexts where military intervention creates new victims alongside prior regime victims, TJ processes face what can be called "martyrology contestation": different political communities advance competing claims about whose suffering is primary, legitimate, and deserving of institutional recognition.

Iraq after 2003 illustrates the destructive version: Shia communities sought accountability for Baathist crimes against them while Sunni communities simultaneously experienced the de-Baathification process and military occupation as victimization. The inability to create a TJ framework that acknowledged both sets of victimhood contributed to sectarian fragmentation.

For Iran, the martyrology contestation problem is acute: Iranian civilians harmed by US-Israel military strikes will have victimhood claims alongside the claims of families of those killed by IRGC repression of protests. The Islamic Republic government (or successor authorities aligned with it) will seek to deploy intervention victims as a counter-narrative that delegitimizes accountability demands. TJ frameworks that address only prior-regime violence while ignoring intervention harms will generate this counter-narrative legitimacy problem. TJ frameworks that equivalize prior-regime violence with intervention harms will delegitimize accountability for the far more systematic and extensive prior-regime record.

The comparative literature offers limited guidance on resolving this dilemma, but Kosovo's mixed legacy (Krasniqi et al. [10236]) and Colombia's multi-sided truth commission process suggest that explicit acknowledgment of multiple victimhood categories — while maintaining distinctions in culpability, intent, and scale — is more sustainable than either equivalization or silencing.

## Iran-Specific Implications: April 2026 Context

This section applies the comparative patterns and mechanisms specifically to Iran's situation as of April 16, 2026, incorporating the current political environment: ongoing Islamabad multi-party talks, IRGC organizational stress, the two-week ceasefire, continued protest mobilization, nuclear infrastructure degradation, and the humanitarian situation.

### Implication 1: The Islamabad Talks and TJ Sequencing

The Islamabad framework negotiations — currently engaging multiple parties in a ceasefire-and-settlement framework — represent the highest-risk moment for TJ design from a comparative perspective. The Spain, Chile, and Iraq cases all demonstrate that TJ commitments negotiated under acute crisis conditions tend to produce problematic outcomes: either they are too specific (creating accountability commitments that subsequent governments cannot or will not honor) or too general (creating amnesty frameworks that subsequent civil society will reject).

The specific risk for Iran: if Islamabad framework participants — including US and Israeli representatives, Iranian government representatives, and regional actors — negotiate prisoner release conditions or immunity guarantees for specific individuals as part of a ceasefire-extension agreement, these commitments will generate the classic amnesty-accountability dilemma. McEvoy and Mallinder [10190] demonstrate that under international law, there is no absolute duty to prosecute, but amnesties for crimes against humanity are increasingly legally contested, particularly in jurisdictions with active universal jurisdiction frameworks. An amnesty for IRGC leadership negotiated in Islamabad cannot extinguish prosecutorial proceedings already underway in German, French, or Swedish courts under universal jurisdiction.

Conditional implication: if Islamabad framework discussions include amnesty-adjacent commitments (immunity for specific actors, prisoner exchange without accountability conditions, non-prosecution pledges), the Chilean sequential model — acknowledgment of violation before any amnesty extension — offers a more durable arrangement than pre-emptive blanket immunity, because it creates a factual record that serves future TJ processes even if immediate prosecution is deferred.

### Implication 2: The IRGC Accountability Problem

The IRGC (سپاه پاسداران انقلاب اسلامی) presents the central TJ challenge in post-war Iran. It is not a conventional military organization susceptible to the standard post-conflict security sector reform (اصلاح بخش امنیتی) model. It is simultaneously: a military force with significant combat capacity; an intelligence organization with domestic surveillance infrastructure; an economic actor controlling substantial industrial, construction, and financial assets through Khatam al-Anbiya; a political actor with extensive elite network integration; and a social institution whose membership encompasses hundreds of thousands of Iranians from career Revolutionary Guards to voluntary Basij participants.

Accountability for the IRGC cannot be approached as a single-category problem. The Iraq de-Baathification failure demonstrates the danger of categorical exclusion without conduct-based differentiation. The Chilean experience with DINA vs. regular military differentiation suggests that targeted accountability focused on senior leadership and documented perpetrators of specific crimes, combined with reintegration pathways for lower-level participants, produces more sustainable outcomes than either categorical exclusion or comprehensive impunity.

Three accountability layers can be distinguished:

**Layer 1 — Command Accountability:** Senior IRGC commanders with documented responsibility for systematic atrocities — directing the 1988 prison massacres, the 2019 Bloody November crackdown, the 2022-2024 Woman Life Freedom suppression — represent the clearest targets for individual criminal accountability. This group is relatively small (dozens to low hundreds of individuals), has documented command authority, and includes individuals whose names appear in existing ICC submissions and European universal jurisdiction proceedings.

**Layer 2 — Institutional Reform:** Middle and senior IRGC officer corps who implemented but did not design systematic violations present the vetting problem. The South Korean and Chilean models suggest that conduct-based vetting, with meaningful distinction between participants in systematic atrocities and participants in legitimate military functions, produces better institutional outcomes than wholesale officer corps removal. The Iraqi case demonstrates that removing the officer corps entirely creates a security vacuum filled by violent non-state actors.

**Layer 3 — Rank-and-File Participation:** IRGC and Basij members whose participation in repression was marginal, coerced, or ambiguous represent the largest numerical category and the most complex accountability challenge. The Eastern European lustration experience — Czech exclusion vs. Hungarian exposure vs. Polish confession models (Grodsky [4610]) — suggests that confession-based models, which create individual records without automatic exclusion, produce more sustainable social cohesion outcomes at scale than comprehensive exclusion models that cannot process millions of cases fairly.

### Implication 3: Documentation Strategy in the Post-War Context

The 2026 military intervention has created a documentary paradox. On one hand, the intervention may have generated new evidence of IRGC atrocities: targeting decisions required intelligence assessments of IRGC command infrastructure, communications intercepts may have captured operational orders relating to protest suppression, and the physical destruction of nuclear and military sites may have exposed buried evidence. On the other hand, this evidence is controlled by intervening powers with political interests in how it is used, creating the external-design-deficit problem.

Sadowski and Zavarei [2672] establish that documentation of violations serves TJ goals most effectively when it is maintained and controlled by the affected community rather than by external parties. For Iran, this suggests that priority should be given to transferring intelligence-held evidence to Iranian civil society documentation organizations and international human rights bodies — removing it from the exclusive control of US and Israeli intelligence while preserving its evidentiary value.

Additionally, the digital documentation accumulated by the diaspora (ایرانیان خارج از کشور) and domestic civil society during the Woman Life Freedom movement represents a documentation resource of remarkable depth. Alemzadeh [2707] and Khodaei [2722] document the systematic character of the 2022-2024 repression, including state deployment of lethal force against unarmed protesters, that this documentation captures. Rivetti and Saeidi [2708] document the political convergence that made this movement analytically significant as a potential precursor to regime change — the same convergence that may now produce civil society capacity to demand and design TJ institutions.

### Implication 4: The Dual-Trauma Legitimacy Problem

Iran's post-war TJ environment involves two trauma streams: (1) the accumulated record of Islamic Republic violence spanning 1979-2026; and (2) the violence generated by the 2026 military intervention. TJ frameworks that address only the first stream will face legitimacy challenges from communities primarily experiencing the second. TJ frameworks that equivalize the two streams will face legitimacy challenges from victim communities whose relatives were executed, tortured, or disappeared by the Islamic Republic over 45 years.

The Kosovo comparison (Krasniqi et al. [10236]) suggests that explicit structural separation of the two victimhood streams — parallel documentation processes with explicitly different culpability assessments — is more sustainable than either conflation or silencing. This might involve a single truth commission with distinct investigative chambers for prior-regime violations and intervention-related violations, with clear differentiation in the legal standards applied: crimes against humanity standards for systematic prior-regime violence; international humanitarian law standards for intervention-related civilian harm.

Conditional implication: if a future Iranian TJ framework explicitly acknowledges intervention-related civilian harm while maintaining proportionality assessments that distinguish it from the scale and systematic character of the prior regime's violations, the likelihood of domestic legitimacy across multiple victim communities is higher than if the framework either ignores intervention harm or treats it as equivalent to 45 years of systematic state repression.

### Implication 5: The Islamic Jurisprudential Bridge

Article 13 in this database established the potential of Islamic jurisprudential concepts — qisas (قصاص), diya (دیه), sulh (صلح), tawbah (توبه) — as legitimacy resources for TJ processes in Muslim-majority societies. In the post-war context, these concepts take on additional dimensions.

The political landscape of post-war Iran, to the extent it can be assessed from current information, includes competing factions: Mojtaba Khamenei's leadership claim resting on theocratic legitimacy frameworks; IRGC political role consolidation; and protest movements whose demands are fundamentally secular-democratic in character. A TJ framework that relies exclusively on international law and Western-derived accountability mechanisms will face resistance from religious conservative constituencies who retain organizational and armed capacity. A TJ framework that relies exclusively on Islamic jurisprudential concepts will face resistance from secular protest movements and diaspora communities whose TJ demands are framed in international human rights law.

The Tunisian IVD experience suggests that hybrid frameworks — integrating Islamic concepts as procedural legitimacy resources while maintaining international law standards for substantive accountability — achieve broader constituency coverage than either purely Islamic or purely international-law approaches. Kazemi [747] demonstrates that this integration is possible but requires institutional design care: Islamic TJ concepts must be incorporated into the framework structure, not appended as rhetorical legitimation after-the-fact.

For Iran specifically, the concept of mazlumiyyat — the justice of the oppressed, rooted in Shia theology and the Karbala paradigm — provides a potential legitimacy bridge between secular demands for criminal accountability and religious demands for recognition of suffering. This concept was already present in protest movement rhetoric during the Woman Life Freedom movement; its TJ application would require careful institutional design to avoid capture by political actors who would use it to delegitimize accountability for regime-aligned perpetrators.

## Knowledge Gaps and Open Research Questions

The following knowledge gaps represent areas where the existing ITKS corpus and the broader comparative TJ literature do not provide sufficient evidence to generate high-confidence claims for post-war Iran. These gaps identify research priorities rather than simply limitations.

### Gap 1: Accountability Under Active Ceasefire — No Comparable Cases
**Priority: Critical**

The current Islamabad negotiations represent a genuinely novel scenario in the TJ literature. Existing comparative cases of post-intervention TJ — Iraq 2003, Libya 2011, Afghanistan 2001 — all involved situations where the military intervention had created an effectively complete regime change before TJ questions became operationally urgent. None involved active multi-party ceasefire negotiations in which the prior regime retained partial authority and the intervening powers were simultaneously engaged in settlement framework discussions with regional powers.

The Colombia model, while not involving external military intervention, involved TJ discussions embedded in active peace negotiations — the FARC peace process included TJ commitments as part of the settlement framework. Research on Colombia's Special Jurisdiction for Peace process may offer the closest available model for developing TJ institutional architecture during negotiation processes, but the state-non-state conflict structure in Colombia differs substantially from Iran's state-to-state intervention structure.

Research needed: systematic comparative analysis of cases where TJ institutional frameworks were developed during ongoing ceasefire negotiations, with attention to which elements of institutional design can be usefully advanced before political settlement versus which elements require completed settlement to avoid being captured by acute bargaining dynamics.

### Gap 2: IRGC-Specific Vetting at Scale
**Priority: High**

The existing lustration literature addresses post-communist party vetting (Czech Republic, Poland, Hungary, East Germany), post-junta military accountability (Chile, Argentina), and post-Baathist de-institutionalization (Iraq). None of these cases involves an institution with the IRGC's combination of characteristics: simultaneously military, intelligence, economic, political, and social; with membership ranging from senior commanders to effectively conscripted participants; controlling massive economic assets that create accountability-resistant financial incentives; operating in an Islamic theocratic institutional context where the organization's founding legitimacy narrative is religiously framed.

The IRGC's hybrid institutional character means that standard lustration models from any single comparative case are likely to be inadequate. A conduct-based vetting process for an organization of the IRGC's size and complexity would require institutional infrastructure — investigators, files, hearing mechanisms, appeal processes — that far exceeds what any comparable TJ process has developed at speed. Research needed: organizational analysis of the IRGC's internal structure sufficient to identify which units, positions, and command functions present the highest accountability priority, and what institutional design would be required to process the resulting vetting caseload at realistic timescales.

### Gap 3: Digital Evidence Standards in TJ Proceedings
**Priority: High**

The documentation ecosystem for Iranian human rights violations is unprecedented in its digital character. Alemzadeh [2707] and others document a protest repression regime that generated digital evidence — satellite imagery of crowd sizes and security deployments, social media documentation of individual atrocities, mobile phone evidence, OSINT analysis — at a scale and granularity that no prior TJ process has needed to incorporate.

The legal standards for incorporating digital evidence into TJ proceedings — particularly criminal prosecutions — remain underdeveloped. The ICC and most international tribunals have begun to address digital evidence standards, but the practitioner literature on incorporating open-source intelligence (OSINT) evidence into TJ proceedings is in its infancy. For Iran, where digital evidence may constitute a primary evidentiary foundation, this gap is critical.

Research needed: legal analysis of evidentiary standards for digital documentation in criminal proceedings, focusing specifically on chain-of-custody requirements for social media evidence, satellite imagery authentication, and OSINT-derived documentation.

### Gap 4: Diaspora TJ Authority and Domestic Legitimacy
**Priority: Moderate-High**

The Iranian diaspora (ایرانیان خارج از کشور) has been an unusually active documentation and advocacy community, generating substantial TJ-relevant infrastructure over 45 years. But comparative evidence on the domestic legitimacy of diaspora-driven TJ processes is limited. Spain's diaspora engagement in the Republican exhumation movement offers one model; Chilean diaspora human rights advocacy offers another. Neither involved diaspora communities as large, politically diverse, and geographically dispersed as the Iranian case.

The political diversity of the Iranian diaspora — spanning royalist, MEK/MKO-affiliated, secular-democratic, and Islamic reformist communities — means that diaspora TJ advocacy does not present a unified front. Research needed: assessment of how diaspora political divisions affect the credibility of diaspora-generated documentation and advocacy in the eyes of inside-Iran victim communities, and what institutional designs might maximize the contribution of diaspora resources while minimizing the partisan legitimacy deficit.

### Gap 5: TJ Under Nuclear Infrastructure Destruction
**Priority: Moderate**

The 2026 strikes on Iranian nuclear sites add an environmental and public health dimension to TJ that has no close comparative precedent in the post-Cold War TJ literature. If the nuclear strikes have produced radiological contamination affecting civilian populations, TJ processes will need to address long-term health harm as a category of reparations alongside individual violation categories. The reparations literature has addressed health-related violations — notably in Agent Orange litigation and some indigenous community TJ processes — but not large-scale radiological harm from military strike damage to nuclear infrastructure.

Research needed: analysis of environmental reparations frameworks as potential components of comprehensive Iranian TJ design, including how long-term health monitoring programs can be institutionalized within TJ reparations frameworks.

### Gap 6: Successor Leadership Accountability
**Priority: Moderate**

Mojtaba Khamenei's election as Supreme Leader following his father's assassination creates an unusual accountability problem: a successor leader who emerged from within the prior regime's leadership structure, inheriting institutional authority from a regime with extensive accountability exposure. The relevant comparative case would be a situation where a senior official of an authoritarian regime becomes the leader of a transitional government — a pattern more common in elite pacts (South Africa's transition from apartheid-era figures into the ANC-dominated state) than in post-intervention contexts.

Research needed: analysis of how TJ processes have handled successor-regime authority figures who bear varying degrees of responsibility for prior-regime violations, with attention to whether prosecutorial immunity for the successor leader is necessary for institutional stability or whether targeted accountability for the most severe violations is compatible with functional governance.

## Plain-Language Summary

### What Is This Article About?

This article asks a question that existing research on transitional justice in Iran had not yet addressed: What happens when a government falls not through negotiated political change — where outgoing officials negotiate exit terms — but through military strikes from outside, combined with large-scale protests from within? How do you build fair accountability processes in that scenario?

Iran, as of April 2026, is in exactly that situation. US and Israeli military strikes since late February, the death of Supreme Leader Khamenei, ongoing protests, and multi-party peace talks in Islamabad have created a transition environment with no clean historical parallel.

### Why Does It Matter?

Transitional justice — the set of mechanisms societies use to reckon with massive human rights violations from prior regimes — is one of the most critical factors determining whether countries that escape authoritarian rule actually become stable and democratic. Countries that handle it badly (Iraq after 2003) descend into violence and faction conflict. Countries that handle it carefully (South Korea, Chile over time) achieve both accountability and stability. Countries that try to bury the past (Spain for 45 years) eventually see the past erupt in destabilizing ways.

For Iran, the stakes are particularly high because the prior regime's violations were extensive and systematic over 45 years: the 1988 prison massacres, political killings in the 1990s, the brutal suppression of the 2009, 2019, and 2022-2024 protest movements. The families of victims have been organizing for accountability for decades. Any political settlement that ignores these demands will face serious legitimacy problems.

### What Does the Research Show?

**Lessons from Iraq (the warning):** The biggest warning comes from Iraq after 2003. When American officials removed Baathist officials and disbanded the Iraqi army without careful design, without domestic buy-in, and without thinking about sequencing, the result was catastrophic. Hundreds of thousands of people lost jobs. Former military officers joined insurgencies. What was supposed to be justice became a catastrophe. For Iran, this means that any accountability process designed primarily by the United States and Israel, or imposed through the Islamabad peace talks without Iranian civil society involvement, risks the same catastrophic failure.

**Lessons from Chile and South Korea (the model):** Both Chile and South Korea show that it's possible to achieve meaningful accountability even when former regime security forces retain significant power — but it takes patience and careful sequencing. In South Korea, former presidents were convicted of crimes before being pardoned. The conviction mattered: it established a factual record and a norm that former power does not equal impunity. Chile took longer — Pinochet wasn't arrested until his trip to London in 1998, decades after his dictatorship ended — but the documentation work done in the early transition years eventually made prosecution possible.

**Lessons from Spain (the risk of silence):** Spain's politicians decided in 1977 to have a "pact of silence" — nobody would be prosecuted for the Civil War and Franco-era crimes. This worked for a while in terms of democratic stability. But 45 years later, Spain is still fighting over this — exhumations of mass graves, memory laws, political conflict over the Franco legacy. For Iran, a similar "let's just move forward" approach in Islamabad negotiations is likely to unravel even faster than Spain's did, because Iran's documentation ecosystem is far more developed and legal proceedings in European courts cannot be cancelled by Iranian political agreement.

**The IRGC Problem:** The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps is the core accountability challenge. It's not a regular army — it's simultaneously a military force, an intelligence service, a massive business empire, and a political organization. How do you hold it accountable without creating the chaos Iraq experienced when the army was disbanded? The comparative evidence suggests a tiered approach: prosecution for senior commanders with documented responsibility for specific atrocities; careful vetting for the officer corps; reintegration pathways for lower-level members. But designing this in practice, at scale, in an organization of the IRGC's complexity, is something the existing TJ literature doesn't fully address.

**The Double Victimhood Problem:** One new challenge this article identifies is that Iran now has two sets of victims: those harmed by the prior Islamic Republic government, and those harmed by the 2026 military strikes. A fair TJ process needs to acknowledge both. But it cannot treat them as equivalent — 45 years of systematic torture, execution, and disappearance by the state is different in character from civilian casualties in a military operation against military and nuclear targets. Getting this balance right will be critical for any TJ framework's legitimacy across different communities.

### What Don't We Know?

Several important questions remain unanswered by existing research. We don't know exactly how to design TJ institutions during active ceasefire negotiations — this is a genuinely new situation with limited precedent. We don't know how the enormous digital documentation of Iranian violations (satellite imagery, social media evidence, OSINT) will hold up in legal proceedings — the legal rules for this kind of evidence are still being developed. We don't know how to manage the vetting of an organization as large and complex as the IRGC. And we don't have a clear model for how diaspora advocacy communities with significant political divisions among themselves can contribute to TJ without delegitimizing the process through factional association.

### The Bottom Line

Iran's post-war transition environment requires a transitional justice framework that is: (1) primarily designed and led by Iranian civil society, not imposed by the Islamabad framework's external participants; (2) sequenced carefully, with truth-telling and documentation preceding prosecution rather than attempting everything simultaneously; (3) tiered for the IRGC, distinguishing senior command accountability from officer-level vetting from rank-and-file reintegration; (4) capable of acknowledging multiple victimhood streams without equivalizing them; and (5) realistic about timelines — the comparative evidence suggests comprehensive TJ takes decades, not years, and institutional design should build frameworks capable of operating over that time horizon rather than trying to close all accounts in the immediate post-war period.
