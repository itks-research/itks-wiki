## Synthesis Question: What Do Comparative Models of Ethnic Accommodation Tell Us About Iran's Multi-Ethnic Composition and the Federalism Question?

Iran is a deeply multi-ethnic state. Persians constitute approximately 50–55% of the population, with Azeris (16–24%), Kurds (7–10%), Arabs (2–3%), Baluch (2%), and numerous smaller groups comprising the rest. These are not merely cultural distinctions—they map onto significant political grievances, regional economic disparities, active insurgent movements (Kurdistan, Baluchistan), and cross-border ethnic solidarities that complicate Iran's regional politics. The question of ethnic accommodation in Iran—whether through federalism, regional autonomy, enhanced cultural rights, or continued centralism—is one of the most contested in Iranian political discourse, simultaneously central to transition planning and intensely divisive within both the regime and opposition circles.

The central research question is: **What do comparative models of ethnic accommodation—including ethnic federalism, territorial autonomy, power-sharing, and asymmetric decentralization—teach us about how Iran's specific multi-ethnic composition might be addressed in a post-transition political settlement, and what are the risks and preconditions for different approaches?**

This question is urgent in April 2026 for several reasons. First, the wartime context has activated ethnic dimensions of Iran's political crisis: Kurdish forces in the northwest and Baluch groups in the southeast have both become more assertive during the period of central authority disruption. The IRGC's regional commands, which previously suppressed ethnic mobilization, have been significantly weakened. Second, any constitutional redesign that emerges from the post-ceasefire transition will confront the federalism question directly—there is no politically viable constitutional settlement that avoids it. Third, the comparative evidence is rich but also deeply cautionary: ethnic federalism has succeeded in some contexts (Switzerland, India, post-apartheid South Africa) and failed dramatically in others (Yugoslavia, Iraq post-2003, Ethiopia under stress).

**Scope of Inquiry**

This synthesis draws on 122 sources across Afghanistan, Germany, Indonesia, Iran, Iraq, Japan, Kosovo, and Libya, with heavy emphasis on Iran-specific research (95 sources). Key case studies include: Indonesia's "Big Bang" decentralization after Soeharto's fall (Butt & Lindsey 2018); the impact of decentralization on health systems in post-conflict Indo-Pacific contexts (Brennan & Abímbọ́lá 2023); Iran-specific decentralization research covering school governance (Mehralizadeh et al. 2006), rural council participatory governance (Sarvestani & Ingram 2020), ethnic identity demands (Ahmady 2023), local democracy creation (Tajbakhsh 2022), and authoritarian state-building through decentralization (Tajbakhsh 2020).

**The Central Tension**

Ethnic accommodation in multi-ethnic states involves a fundamental tension between two legitimate imperatives: unity (maintaining the territorial integrity and administrative coherence of the state) and recognition (acknowledging and protecting the distinct identities, languages, and political interests of ethnic groups). Centralist approaches to this tension—suppressing ethnic identity expression in favor of a unified national identity—have historically generated ethnic grievances that eventually destabilize central authority. Federalist approaches that over-segment ethnicity into territorial units risk reifying ethnic boundaries, creating secessionist dynamics, and entrenching minority-within-minority exclusions.

Iran's particular challenge is that both imperatives are exceptionally strong. The national memory of Iran as a unified civilizational space is deep; "balkanization" of Iran is an existential fear in Persian nationalist discourse. Simultaneously, the historical record of ethnic suppression under both the Pahlavi monarchy and the Islamic Republic has generated profound alienation among Kurdish, Baluch, Arab, and Azeri communities that cannot simply be wished away.

**Why April 2026 Matters**

The assassination of Khamenei and the subsequent political disruption has altered the ethnic accommodation calculus in important ways. Kurdish and Baluch movements have become more active. The IRGC—historically the primary enforcer of ethnic control in peripheral regions—has been weakened. The Islamabad peace talks involve regional actors (Pakistan, Turkey, Iraq, Saudi Arabia) who have interests in Iran's ethnic composition. Any constitutional redesign will need to address federalism explicitly. The question is no longer whether Iran will address ethnic accommodation but how, and whether the comparative evidence can guide that process toward stability rather than fragmentation.

## Comparative Evidence Base

Research on ethnic accommodation draws on 122 sources across eight country contexts, providing comparative evidence from post-colonial decentralization (Indonesia), failed federal states (Iraq), ongoing ethnic conflicts (Kosovo), and Iran-specific governance research.

**Indonesia's Big Bang Decentralization (Butt & Lindsey 2018)**

Indonesia's decentralization following Soeharto's fall in 1998 is the most directly comparable case to Iran's transition scenario. The "Big Bang"—rapid devolution of authority to provincial and district levels under the 1999, 2004, and 2014 regional autonomy laws—transferred significant powers to over 500 subnational units. The outcomes were mixed: decentralization prevented secessionism in most regions (though not Aceh, which required a negotiated special autonomy settlement, and East Timor, which ultimately succeeded in independence), enabled ethnic political mobilization at the local level (the West Kalimantan Dayak case, Tanasaldy 2012), and created service delivery problems where local capacity was insufficient. The key lesson from Indonesia is that rapid decentralization without adequate institutional capacity building creates governance gaps that are difficult to reverse.

**Decentralization in Post-Conflict Health Systems (Brennan & Abímbọ́lá 2023)**

Brennan and Abímbọ́lá's synthesis of six Indo-Pacific post-conflict cases provides evidence that decentralization in post-conflict environments requires careful sequencing: central coordination must be maintained for emergency functions while local capacity develops, because premature decentralization of essential services creates equity gaps that disproportionately affect peripheral regions. For Iran, this has direct implications: decentralization of governance authority should be sequenced to precede decentralization of service delivery responsibility only when local institutional capacity exists.

**Iran-Specific Decentralization: Local Democracy (Tajbakhsh 2022)**

Tajbakhsh's empirically rich analysis of political decentralization under the Islamic Republic is the most important single source in this synthesis. It demonstrates that Iran has already developed a significant layer of elected local government—city and rural councils—that functions as a form of "electoral authoritarianism" at the subnational level. These councils are real institutions with genuine electoral competition, but their authority is constrained by central government oversight and IRGC regional commands. The existence of these institutions means Iran already has decentralization infrastructure—the post-transition question is whether to extend their authority, not whether to create local governance from scratch.

**Authoritarian State Building Through Decentralization (Tajbakhsh 2020)**

Tajbakhsh's earlier analysis reveals a crucial paradox: Iran's Islamic Republic used decentralization—the creation of elected local councils in 1999—as a mechanism for state-building and regime legitimation rather than democratization. The councils were designed to increase state capacity at the local level while maintaining central control. This history has two implications: (1) Iran's decentralization infrastructure exists but was built for control, not autonomy; (2) reforming it toward genuine autonomy requires not just legislative change but cultural and institutional transformation of the center-periphery relationship.

**Ethnic Identity Demands Across Five Major Groups (Ahmady 2023)**

Ahmady's grounded theory study of ethnic, national and identity demands among Persian, Azeri, Kurdish, Arab, and Baluch communities in Iran is uniquely valuable. It finds that all five major groups share demands for justice and development, but that these demands are expressed through different ethnic identity frameworks. Kurdish and Baluch communities express the strongest demands for political autonomy; Azeri and Arab communities emphasize cultural rights and economic equity; Persian communities show the highest integration into national identity frameworks. This differentiation suggests that a one-size-fits-all federalism model may be inappropriate—asymmetric arrangements that provide different levels of autonomy to different regions may better match actual demands.

**Iraq's Federal Experience (Dawisha 2009; Stansfield 2007; Haddad 2011)**

Iraq's post-2003 federal constitution, which established Kurdish autonomy (Kurdistan Regional Government) alongside Arab Sunni-Shia power-sharing, provides a cautionary case. Despite formal constitutional federalism, sectarian conflict escalated, oil revenue disputes between the KRG and Baghdad have been persistent, and the federal arrangement has faced constant stress. The failure of Iraqi federalism was not inherent to federalism but to the specific design choices: excessive center-periphery ambiguity, inadequate fiscal equalization, and federal design that reified sectarian cleavages rather than creating cross-cutting civic identities.

**Kosovo's Self-Determination Process (Visoka & Richmond 2016)**

Kosovo's independence (2008) and subsequent state-building process demonstrates both the limits of international liberal peace-building and the importance of local institutional development for democratic consolidation. Kosovo's ethnic Albanian majority achieved self-determination, but at the cost of creating a deeply divided society with Serb minority exclusion. For Iran, the Kosovo case is primarily a warning: ethnic accommodation that produces ethnic separation rather than integration tends to generate persistent minority-within-majority problems.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

Analysis of ethnic accommodation across transitions and multi-ethnic states reveals four robust patterns:

**Pattern 1: Fiscal Federalism Is the Core of Sustainable Accommodation**

Across all successful federal and autonomy arrangements, the central variable is not formal constitutional recognition of ethnic rights but fiscal arrangements—the distribution of revenue, taxation authority, and expenditure responsibilities between central and subnational governments. Federal systems with clear, equitable fiscal arrangements (Switzerland, India's Finance Commissions, post-apartheid South Africa's equalization grants) prove more stable than those with ambiguous or contested fiscal relationships (Iraq's oil revenue disputes, Ethiopia's fiscal centralism under formal federalism).

For Iran, this pattern suggests that ethnic accommodation must be designed as primarily a fiscal and economic equity question, not merely a cultural recognition question. Ethnic mobilization in Kurdish, Baluch, and Arab regions is strongly correlated with regional economic underdevelopment and the perception that resource revenues extracted from ethnic regions (Khuzestan's oil, Baluchistan's gas, Kurdistan's minerals) flow to the center without adequate return. Addressing this fiscal dimension is a prerequisite for stable ethnic accommodation.

**Pattern 2: Asymmetric Arrangements Outperform Uniform Federalism**

Comparative evidence from Spain (Catalonia, Basque Country, and other regions with different autonomy levels), India (states with special constitutional status), Indonesia (Aceh's special autonomy vs. general regional autonomy), and the UK (Scottish and Welsh devolution vs. English regions) consistently shows that asymmetric arrangements—different autonomy levels for different regions based on demonstrated demand, institutional capacity, and historical circumstances—generate more stable outcomes than attempts to apply uniform federalism across heterogeneous regions.

For Iran, this pattern is particularly relevant given the heterogeneity of ethnic demands (Ahmady 2023): Kurdish and Baluch communities demand political autonomy; Azeri communities primarily demand cultural rights and economic equity; Arab communities in Khuzestan demand fiscal equity. A constitutional design that provides Kurdistan and Baluchistan with enhanced autonomy while providing other regions with strengthened cultural rights and fiscal equity may be more politically stable than uniform ethnic provinces.

**Pattern 3: Decentralization Without Capacity Building Creates Governance Gaps**

Indonesia's experience and the post-conflict health system evidence both demonstrate that rapid decentralization to regions lacking institutional capacity creates service delivery failures that disproportionately harm peripheral populations—the same populations whose grievances motivated decentralization. The governance gap problem is particularly severe for: health and education services (requiring professional bureaucracies at the local level); revenue collection and fiscal management; and security functions (where IRGC withdrawal from ethnic regions may create security vacuums).

For Iran's transition, this pattern argues for sequenced decentralization: immediate fiscal equalization and cultural rights; medium-term governance authority transfer as local capacity develops; long-term administrative restructuring after institutional capacity is confirmed.

**Pattern 4: Ethnic Elites' Incentives Must Be Aligned With Accommodation**

Comparative evidence consistently shows that ethnic accommodation succeeds or fails largely based on whether ethnic group elites—political leaders, business figures, armed group commanders—have incentive structures that reward accommodation rather than escalation. In South Africa, the ANC-NP pact succeeded because both elite groups had more to gain from accommodation than from continued conflict. In Yugoslavia, Milosevic-era Serbian elite incentives were structured around ethnic escalation. In Iraq, Kurdish, Sunni, and Shia elites all calculated that federal arrangements could advance their specific interests.

For Iran, this pattern points to the importance of the initial bargaining process: Kurdish armed group leaders (KDPI, Komala, PJAK), Baluch tribal leaders, and Ahvazi Arab political figures must be included in any negotiation about ethnic accommodation, and the arrangements offered must provide them with genuine governance stakes rather than merely symbolic recognition.

## Causal Mechanisms

Four causal mechanisms explain ethnic accommodation outcomes in multi-ethnic states undergoing transition:

---

**Mechanism 1: Fiscal Equalization as Grievance Reduction**

*Logic (if X then Y):* If a constitutional transition establishes transparent fiscal equalization mechanisms—formulae that return to resource-producing regions a calculable share of extracted revenues, that equalize public service provision across regions, and that are governed by independent institutions—then the core economic driver of ethnic mobilization is reduced, and political demands shift from secessionism toward institutional engagement.

*Activation conditions:* The mechanism requires: (a) transparent revenue accounting for resource extraction by region; (b) constitutional formula for regional revenue shares that is legally entrenched, not subject to annual political negotiation; (c) independent fiscal oversight body with regional representation; (d) minimum service delivery standards enforced by center but funded through equalization grants.

*Evidence base:* India's Finance Commission mechanism has maintained fiscal unity across extreme ethnic and linguistic diversity since 1950. Post-apartheid South Africa's equalization grant system reduced provincial inequality while maintaining territorial integrity. Iraq's failure to establish an oil revenue sharing formula between Baghdad and the KRG demonstrates the consequences of avoiding this mechanism.

*Iran-specific application:* Khuzestan's oil revenues, Baluchistan's gas reserves, and Kurdistan's mineral resources have historically been extracted with minimal fiscal return to these regions. Constitutional provisions establishing a resource revenue sharing formula (similar to Indonesia's 80/20 central/regional split under the 2004 law) would directly address the fiscal dimension of ethnic grievance. Given Iran's wartime disruption and Islamabad talks context, international backing for fiscal equalization provisions in any new constitutional framework is feasible.

---

**Mechanism 2: Asymmetric Autonomy Bargaining**

*Logic:* If constitutional negotiations offer different ethnic regions differentiated autonomy levels calibrated to demonstrated political demand and institutional capacity—rather than uniform federalism imposed on all regions simultaneously—then the negotiation space expands because regions with lower autonomy demands can be accommodated at lower political cost, and regions with higher demands can be offered more without triggering a cascade of equivalent demands from all regions.

*Activation conditions:* Requires: (a) constitutional drafters willing to design asymmetric arrangements rather than default to uniform federal models; (b) negotiating processes that include ethnic regional representatives with genuine authority to commit their communities; (c) mechanisms for upgrading autonomy levels over time as institutional capacity develops (avoiding the "Aceh trap" where special autonomy was only available after armed conflict).

*Evidence base:* Spain's Estado de las Autonomías provides the strongest evidence: Catalonia and Basque Country achieved higher autonomy than other Spanish regions through negotiated asymmetric arrangements, and this asymmetry was stable for decades (recent Catalan independence movement represents a second-generation strain, not a failure of the original asymmetric design). Indonesia's special autonomy for Aceh, negotiated after conflict, demonstrates that asymmetric arrangements can end armed conflict.

*Iran-specific note:* Iran's existing constitutional Article 15 (language rights for ethnic minorities) provides a starting point for asymmetric accommodation of cultural demands. Constitutional redesign that extends this to administrative language use, local governance authority, and fiscal autonomy—differentiated by region—is the most plausible accommodation pathway given Iran's centralist tradition and the diversity of ethnic demands.

---

**Mechanism 3: Institutional Capacity Pre-Building**

*Logic:* If decentralization is preceded by deliberate capacity building in subnational institutions—through training civil servants, transferring technical expertise, and establishing independent auditing functions—then the governance gap problem is reduced and decentralization produces service delivery improvement rather than deterioration.

*Activation conditions:* Requires a 3–7 year capacity-building period before major decentralization of service delivery. Requires central government willingness to transfer capacity rather than retaining it as a control mechanism. Requires technical assistance from international partners or more administratively developed provinces.

*Evidence base:* India's graduated decentralization model: constitutional amendments in 1992 established the framework for local government, but actual power transfer occurred gradually over 10–15 years as state governments developed capacity. Indonesia's rapid decentralization in 1999 without capacity building produced the governance gaps documented in Brennan & Abímbọ́lá (2023). Iran's existing local council infrastructure (Tajbakhsh 2022) provides a foundation for capacity building that is more advanced than in Indonesia at the time of its transition.

---

**Mechanism 4: Elite Incentive Restructuring Through Governance Stakes**

*Logic:* If ethnic group leaders—including armed group commanders, traditional authority figures, and diaspora political leaders—are offered genuine governance stakes in a new constitutional arrangement (ministerial positions, regional governorships, legislative representation, control over regional police and courts), and if these stakes are more valuable than continued armed conflict or external support for separatism, then elite incentives shift from escalation to accommodation.

*Activation conditions:* Requires negotiating processes that include, not exclude, armed group leaders. Requires offers of genuine governance authority, not merely symbolic recognition. Requires security arrangements that allow armed group commanders to demobilize without facing immediate prosecution. Requires buy-in from regional actors (Turkey, Iraq, Pakistan) who currently support or tolerate armed ethnic movements.

*Evidence base:* South Africa's CODESA negotiations: ANC, NP, and Inkatha Freedom Party leaders all received governance stakes in the transitional and post-apartheid government. Aceh's Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding (2005): GAM commanders were offered participation in Aceh governance in exchange for demobilization. Iran's 1979 revolution briefly offered Kurdish autonomy negotiations (Kurdstān autonomy talks 1979–80 under Sanjabi/Ghotbzadeh), which ultimately failed but demonstrated the principle.

## Iran-Specific Implications: Ethnic Accommodation in Wartime and Post-Ceasefire Context (April 2026)

### The April 2026 Ethnic Landscape

The 2025–2026 war period has significantly altered Iran's ethnic accommodation calculus. Key developments:

**Kurdish movements**: KDPI, Komala, and PJAK have all increased operational activity during the period of IRGC disruption. Kurdish-controlled areas in Kurdistan, Kermanshah, and West Azerbaijan provinces have experienced increased autonomous organization. The Islamabad talks' success or failure will partly determine whether Kurdish movements view post-ceasefire Iran as a negotiating partner or a continued adversary.

**Baluch situation**: Sistan-Baluchestan has seen intensified insurgency and significant civilian-security force confrontations during the wartime period. Baluch organizations (Jaish al-Adl and others) have taken advantage of IRGC weakening. Pakistan's role in the Islamabad talks is partly driven by concern about spillover effects on Pakistani Baluchistan.

**Azeri and Arab developments**: Azeri political mobilization has remained primarily cultural and economic rather than separatist; the Azerbaijan Republic's relationship to Iran's Azeri population remains a complicating factor. Khuzestan's Arab population has focused on environmental and economic grievances (water, pollution from oil extraction) that could translate into political demands if institutional channels are not provided.

### Institutional Mapping

**Guardian Council:** The current Guardian Council has systematically blocked ethnic minority candidates from national political participation through approbatory supervision (نظارت استصوابی). Constitutional redesign must address Guardian Council powers as a precondition for ethnic inclusion in national politics.

**Parliament (Majles):** Provincial representation in the Majles has been structurally distorted by electoral system design. Ethnic minority MPs face constraints on language use and are disproportionately subject to disqualification. Electoral reform that guarantees ethnic minority representation is a minimum reform requirement.

**Provincial Governors (Ostāndārs):** Under the current system, provincial governors are appointed by the central government and serve as agents of central control rather than representatives of provincial populations. Any meaningful decentralization requires either elected governors or governors appointed by elected provincial councils.

**Ethnic Parties (KDPI, Komala, Baloch organizations):** Currently operating outside the legal political system, with leaders in exile (primarily in Iraqi Kurdistan and Europe). Legitimization of these parties as negotiating partners—and eventual integration into the legal political system—is a precondition for peaceful ethnic accommodation.

**IRGC Regional Commands:** The IRGC has maintained order in ethnic minority regions through a combination of direct repression and economic incorporation (employment, contracts). IRGC withdrawal from ethnic regions creates both a security vacuum and a potential economic development vacuum that must be addressed through alternative institutional arrangements.

### Transition Scenarios for Ethnic Accommodation

**Scenario A: Negotiated Asymmetric Autonomy (Inclusive constitutional process; ethnic parties participate; Islamabad framework supports settlement)**

A constitutional assembly that includes Kurdish, Baluch, Arab, and Azeri representatives negotiates an asymmetric autonomy arrangement: Kurdistan and Baluchistan receive special administrative status with enhanced fiscal autonomy and local security forces; Azeri and Arab regions receive strengthened cultural rights and fiscal equalization; a constitutional fiscal formula ensures regional revenue sharing. Ethnic parties are legalized and participate in post-transition elections.

Timeline: 2–4 years for constitutional design; 4–8 years for implementation. This scenario requires: ceasefire with Kurdish and Baluch armed groups; international mediation through Islamabad framework; IRGC withdrawal from ethnic regions under international guarantee.

**Scenario B: Administrative Decentralization Without Federalism (Tajbakhsh 2022 model extended)**

The existing local council infrastructure is extended with greater fiscal authority and administrative powers, but without constitutional ethnic recognition or territorial autonomy. Provincial governors become elected. Ethnic minority languages gain official administrative status in relevant provinces. Fiscal equalization improves through new formula. Armed groups are not legalized.

This scenario may be achievable more quickly (12–24 months) but is unlikely to satisfy Kurdish and Baluch political demands for genuine autonomy.

**Scenario C: Continued Centralism with Repression (IRGC reconsolidation; constitutional status quo)**

The least stable long-term scenario given the weakening of IRGC regional control capacity and the demonstrated escalation capacity of Kurdish and Baluch movements during the wartime period. Post-ceasefire repression of ethnic movements would likely require significant IRGC reconsolidation that may not be achievable given the wartime disruption.

### Critical Sequencing Considerations

The ethnic accommodation question cannot be separated from broader constitutional design. Attempting to resolve ethnic accommodation before the constitutional framework is settled creates the risk of partial settlements that become contested during the broader redesign. However, leaving ethnic accommodation until after constitutional design creates the risk of Persian-majority national processes that exclude ethnic minority interests.

The recommended sequencing based on comparative evidence:
1. **Immediate**: Ceasefire with Kurdish and Baluch armed groups; legalization of ethnic parties as negotiating participants; suspension of ethnic minority prosecutions
2. **Short-term (6–18 months)**: Fiscal equalization formula through executive order; Majles electoral reform guaranteeing ethnic minority representation; administrative language rights
3. **Medium-term (18–36 months)**: Constitutional assembly with guaranteed ethnic representation; asymmetric autonomy provisions in new constitution; provincial governor elections
4. **Long-term (4–8 years)**: Implementation of autonomy arrangements; IRGC withdrawal from ethnic regions; capacity building in autonomous regions

### Overall Assessment for April 2026

The wartime disruption has created a window for ethnic accommodation that did not exist under the consolidated Islamic Republic. Kurdish and Baluch movements are stronger than at any point since the early 1980s; the IRGC is weaker. Whether this creates an opportunity for genuine accommodation or a catastrophic fragmentation depends critically on the quality of the constitutional process and the inclusion of ethnic voices in that process. The comparative evidence strongly favors inclusive constitutional processes over imposed settlements—but Iran's ethnic accommodation challenge is severe enough that failure to reach a settlement in the post-ceasefire window risks escalation to a much more destructive phase.

## Knowledge Gaps

Six critical knowledge gaps for ethnic federalism and accommodation in Iran's post-ceasefire transition:

**Gap 1: Current Status of Kurdish and Baluch Armed Movements**

We lack current assessments of the operational capacity, political demands, and negotiating conditions of the Kurdish armed movements (KDPI, Komala, PJAK) and Baluch armed organizations (Jaish al-Adl and others) as of April 2026. These movements have been more active during the wartime period; their current strength, territorial control, and leadership structures are not publicly documented. This assessment is a prerequisite for designing ethnic accommodation negotiations.

**Gap 2: Fiscal Geography of Iranian Resource Extraction**

We lack precise data on the fiscal flows between resource-extracting ethnic regions (Khuzestan oil revenues, Baluchistan gas revenues, Kurdistan mineral revenues) and the central government. This data is essential for designing credible fiscal equalization formulas. The wartime disruption may have altered these flows significantly.

**Gap 3: Local Institutional Capacity in Ethnic Regions**

Tajbakhsh (2022) provides evidence that Iran's local council infrastructure is more developed than typically assumed, but current assessments of institutional capacity by region—particularly in Kurdish, Baluch, and Arab areas—are lacking. Capacity assessments would determine the feasibility and sequencing of different decentralization scenarios.

**Gap 4: Diaspora Ethnic Community Political Positions**

Significant portions of Iran's Kurdish, Baluch, Arab, and Azeri diaspora communities have developed political positions on ethnic accommodation that may differ substantially from positions of armed groups or inside-Iran communities. Systematic assessment of diaspora ethnic community positions is needed to understand the full political landscape.

**Gap 5: Regional Actors' Ethnic Accommodation Interests**

Turkey (Kurdish question), Iraq (KRG interests), Pakistan (Baluch spillover), and Saudi Arabia (Arab minority interests) all have stakes in Iran's ethnic accommodation outcome. Systematic assessment of regional actors' ethnic accommodation preferences and red lines is needed to understand the international feasibility space for different settlement designs.

**Gap 6: Comparative Assessment of Iran's Existing Decentralization Infrastructure**

Tajbakhsh's work provides strong evidence about Iran's local governance infrastructure, but comparative assessment of how Iran's existing decentralization infrastructure compares to other cases at transition points (Indonesia 1998, South Africa 1994, Iraq 2003) has not been systematically conducted. This comparative assessment would inform realistic timeline estimates for different decentralization pathways.

## General Reader Summary

Iran is home to many different ethnic and linguistic communities—Persians, Azeris, Kurds, Arabs, Baluch, and others. For most of the past century, and especially under the Islamic Republic, the central government in Tehran has maintained tight control over these communities, suppressing ethnic political organization, restricting minority language use, and concentrating economic resources at the center. This has generated deep grievances, active insurgencies in Kurdistan and Baluchistan, and persistent demands for autonomy across multiple regions.

This article asks: what does the global evidence on how diverse societies manage ethnic accommodation tell us about the options and risks for Iran as it faces a political transition?

**What the evidence shows**

The strongest finding from comparative research is that the economic dimension of ethnic grievance—specifically, whether resource revenues from ethnic regions return to those regions or flow overwhelmingly to the capital—is the central driver of ethnic mobilization. Khuzestan produces most of Iran's oil. Baluchistan sits on major gas reserves. Kurdistan has significant mineral wealth. Yet these are among Iran's most economically underdeveloped provinces. Any political settlement that does not address this fiscal dimension will fail to reduce ethnic mobilization even if cultural rights are formally recognized.

A second finding is that symmetric federalism—creating ethnically-defined provinces with equal powers—often makes ethnic conflict worse by entrenching ethnic boundaries and creating "minority-within-minority" problems. More successful are asymmetric arrangements: different regions getting different degrees of autonomy based on their specific circumstances and demands.

The Indonesia case (after Soeharto fell in 1998) is instructive: rapid, poorly-sequenced decentralization created governance gaps in regions without administrative capacity. Iran, unlike Indonesia in 1998, already has a significant local council infrastructure—the question is whether this infrastructure can be extended with genuine authority rather than merely rubber-stamping central government decisions.

**What April 2026 means**

The Kurdish movements in Iran's northwest and Baluch movements in the southeast are stronger than at any time in recent decades, because the IRGC—which enforced ethnic control—has been significantly weakened by the wartime disruption. This is simultaneously an opportunity and a danger.

If the post-ceasefire transition includes Kurdish and Baluch representatives in constitutional negotiations, and offers genuine autonomy with fiscal equity, then the armed movements have an incentive to participate rather than fight. If they are excluded from the constitutional process, or offered only symbolic gestures, then armed conflict in ethnic regions will continue and likely escalate.

The key immediate steps the evidence recommends: ceasefire with armed ethnic movements; legalization of ethnic political parties; immediate improvement in fiscal flows from resource regions; and a constitutional process that guarantees ethnic minority seats at the table.
