## Synthesis Question: How Do Digital Society and Internet Governance Models Across Transitions Apply to Iran's Heavily-Censored, Wartime Internet Context?

Iran operates one of the world's most sophisticated and pervasive internet censorship and surveillance architectures. The National Information Network (NIN/SHOMA), developed over more than a decade, represents a comprehensive attempt to create a domestically controlled internet infrastructure capable of operating independently from the global internet—often described as a "halal internet" project. The Supreme Council of Cyberspace coordinates policy across the Ministry of ICT, the IRGC Cyber Command, and FATA (cyber police), creating a layered governance structure that combines regulatory control, active surveillance, and targeted disruption capabilities. Against this backdrop, Iran's digital society—88 million people with 60–70% internet penetration—has developed sophisticated circumvention practices, while cyber activists have used digital platforms to organize the 2022–2026 wave of protests under conditions of active suppression.

The central research question is: **What do comparative models of digital society development and internet governance reform across political transitions teach us about how Iran's internet architecture—shaped by wartime disruption, ceasefire politics, and legacy censorship infrastructure—might evolve, and what policy choices would determine whether the transition enables or further constrains digital civil society?**

**Why This Question Matters Now**

The April 2026 ceasefire following US-Israel strikes has created an extraordinary disruption to Iran's internet governance architecture. Physical infrastructure damage from strikes, IRGC cyber force decentralization, and the political uncertainty around Mojtaba Khamenei's new government have created unusual conditions of both fragility and opportunity in Iran's digital space. Simultaneously, the global context has shifted: artificial intelligence, large language models, and next-generation surveillance technologies have made both censorship and circumvention more sophisticated than any of the classic case study comparisons.

**Scope of Inquiry**

This synthesis draws on 20 sources covering Chile (1), Iran (16), and South Korea (3), with particular attention to: social media bot networks and their role in political manipulation (Mendoza et al. 2024 on Chile); the intersection of foreign investment and national security in the data age (Yu 2023); digital capitalism and state management of digital society (Popkova et al. 2021 on Russia); digital inclusion and security as drivers of institutional change (Kraus 2025 on Ukraine); the growth of CCTV and video surveillance (Norris, McCahill & Wood 2002); and digital economy metrics and sustainable development (Imran et al. 2022).

**The Central Tension**

Internet governance in authoritarian-to-democratic transitions involves a fundamental tension between two legitimate state interests: security (preventing use of digital platforms for terrorism, foreign interference, and incitement) and freedom (enabling civil society, economic activity, and political participation). The authoritarian solution to this tension—comprehensive censorship plus targeted surveillance—produces a digital environment that stifles innovation, economic development, and civic capacity. The post-transition challenge is not simply "turning off" censorship but redesigning governance architectures that can address legitimate security concerns while enabling the digital civil society that democratic governance requires.

**Iran's Specific Configuration**

Iran's internet governance architecture is distinctive in several ways that complicate direct comparisons with other transition cases. First, Iran's NIN (National Information Network) is a physical infrastructure investment—fiber-optic backbone, data centers, peering points—not merely a set of policies, making it harder to dismantle than policy-based censorship. Second, the IRGC Cyber Command's dual role in both offensive cyber operations and domestic surveillance means that internet reform intersects directly with IRGC institutional reform. Third, Iran's digital society has developed an exceptionally sophisticated circumvention culture—VPN use, Tor networks, encrypted messaging—that represents both a civil society resource and a technical policy challenge for any government attempting to govern the internet space.

## Comparative Evidence Base

Research on digital society governance across transitions draws on 20 sources spanning Chile, Iran, and South Korea, with broader reference to Russia and Ukraine. The evidence illuminates how different political systems construct, control, and eventually reform digital governance architectures.

**Social Bots and Information Environment Manipulation (Mendoza et al. 2024 on Chile)**

Mendoza and colleagues' study of bot networks in Chilean social media provides evidence highly relevant to Iran's context. They find that bot networks systematically amplify low-quality content, spread misinformation, and disrupt civic action coordination. Critically, bot detection and impact estimation methods now exist that can distinguish automated from authentic political communication. For Iran, this is a double-edged finding: the Islamic Republic has used bot networks (documented in multiple threat intelligence reports) to manipulate both domestic and international information environments; post-transition internet governance will need to address both the legacy of state bot use and the ongoing risk of non-state bot networks.

**Foreign Investment, Data Sovereignty, and National Security (Yu 2023)**

Yu's analysis of the US-China dispute over data-exposing investments demonstrates that data sovereignty concerns are a legitimate driver of internet governance policy even in democratic contexts. The US CFIUS regime, the European GDPR, and South Korean data localization requirements all reflect democratic governments' attempts to balance economic openness with data security. For Iran's transition, this finding is important: some form of data governance infrastructure is legitimate and compatible with democratic norms. The question is not whether Iran should have a National Information Network but whether such infrastructure should function as a censorship and surveillance tool or as a data security infrastructure with civil liberties protections.

**Digital Capitalism and State Management of Digital Society (Popkova et al. 2021 on Russia)**

Popkova and colleagues' conceptualization of Russia's digital society management under "Capitalism 4.0" shows how authoritarian states attempt to capture the economic benefits of digital technology while minimizing its political risks. Russia's approach—selectively integrating digital platforms while maintaining censorship and surveillance capacity—has largely failed to achieve its economic objectives while succeeding in political control. The Russian model is a cautionary tale: the economic costs of digital authoritarianism (stifled innovation, talent emigration, reduced FDI) eventually become prohibitive. Iran's digital economy has suffered significantly from its internet restrictions; the post-ceasefire transition presents an opportunity to recalculate this tradeoff.

**Digital Inclusion and Security in Conflict/Post-Conflict Contexts (Kraus 2025 on Ukraine)**

Kraus's analysis of Ukraine's Digital Quality of Life index changes during the 2021–2023 period (covering the Russian invasion) provides directly relevant evidence for Iran's wartime internet context. Ukraine's experience shows that: physical infrastructure damage is partially offset by mobile internet resilience; wartime demand for reliable information increases VPN and circumvention tool adoption; and international connectivity support (Starlink, donated equipment) creates dependencies that outlast the conflict. Iran may face similar dynamics: wartime infrastructure damage combined with increased demand for uncensored information may have permanently altered the circumvention landscape.

**Video Surveillance and Digital Control Infrastructure (Norris, McCahill & Wood 2002)**

The Norris et al. survey of global CCTV diffusion provides historical context for understanding how surveillance infrastructure, once deployed, tends to persist and expand regardless of political transitions. CCTV systems deployed for security rationales become embedded in urban infrastructure, creating path dependence that makes removal politically and economically costly. Iran's surveillance infrastructure—including facial recognition at protest sites, mobile device monitoring, and network surveillance—exhibits similar path dependence. Post-transition internet governance will need to actively dismantle surveillance infrastructure rather than passively relying on changed political norms.

**Digital Economy Metrics and Sustainable Development (Imran et al. 2022)**

The EU DESI (Digital Economy and Society Index) framework provides a measurement tool for understanding digital development trajectories. DESI dimensions—connectivity, human capital, internet services use, digital technology integration, digital public services—correlate with sustainable development outcomes. Iran scores poorly on most DESI dimensions despite relatively high internet penetration because quality, speed, and access to global platforms are severely restricted. Post-transition internet governance should adopt explicit digital development metrics as policy targets rather than treating internet governance as primarily a security question.

**Cross-Case Synthesis**

Across these cases, the key variables determining digital society development in transitions are: (1) whether internet governance institutions are redesigned or merely re-managed; (2) whether data sovereignty concerns are addressed through civil liberties-compatible means; (3) whether surveillance infrastructure is actively dismantled or preserved under new management; and (4) whether economic digital development becomes an explicit policy priority that competes with security rationales for internet restriction.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

Analysis of digital society governance across transitions reveals four robust patterns:

**Pattern 1: Internet Governance Architecture Is Sticky**

Once built, internet censorship and surveillance infrastructure exhibits strong path dependence. Physical infrastructure (fiber networks, data centers, monitoring equipment), institutional capacity (trained censorship analysts, surveillance technicians), and regulatory frameworks all create constituencies and technical dependencies that resist change even when political will for reform exists. South Korea's transition from authoritarian to democratic governance did not involve dismantling its telecommunications regulatory architecture—it involved repurposing it. Russia's post-Soviet governments retained KGB-era surveillance infrastructure and later expanded it significantly.

For Iran, this pattern has important implications: the National Information Network's physical infrastructure will likely outlast the regime that built it. Post-transition internet governance must consciously choose to repurpose or dismantle specific elements of this infrastructure rather than assuming political change will organically transform its function.

**Pattern 2: Circumvention Culture Becomes Permanent Social Infrastructure**

In heavily censored digital environments, circumvention practices—VPNs, proxy servers, Tor networks, encrypted messaging—become embedded in social practice and develop their own institutional support. Iranian internet users have developed among the world's most sophisticated circumvention cultures, with VPN penetration estimated at 40–60% and extensive peer-to-peer knowledge sharing about circumvention tools. This circumvention culture represents a form of civil society digital infrastructure that has developed in opposition to state control.

Post-transition, circumvention culture does not simply disappear when censorship is lifted. Evidence from other transitions suggests that privacy-preserving technologies become normalized elements of digital society even after their censorship-circumventing rationale is reduced. This creates both an opportunity (existing civil society digital infrastructure) and a governance challenge (establishing legitimate norms around data security and privacy).

**Pattern 3: Bot Networks and Information Quality Are Governance Challenges Independent of Censorship**

Chile's experience and global evidence demonstrate that information environment quality—protection against bot networks, disinformation campaigns, and algorithmic manipulation—is a governance challenge in democratic as well as authoritarian digital environments. Iran's post-transition internet will not automatically produce high-quality information environments simply by lifting censorship. New challenges will emerge: state-sponsored disinformation from external actors (Russia, China), factional disinformation from domestic political competitors, and commercial spam/bot operations.

Effective democratic internet governance requires regulatory capacity to address these challenges—a capacity that is different from censorship infrastructure but still requires government capability. Countries that dismantled all internet governance capacity in transitions found themselves vulnerable to new information environment threats.

**Pattern 4: Economic Incentives for Digital Openness Are Powerful But Slow-Acting**

Evidence from South Korea, Taiwan, and even China's SEZ model demonstrates that economic integration into the global digital economy creates powerful incentives for internet openness—but these incentives operate on 5–10 year timelines, not immediately. The economic costs of internet restriction accumulate gradually: reduced FDI in tech sectors, talent emigration, productivity losses from slow/filtered internet, exclusion from global digital supply chains. These costs eventually create elite business constituencies for reform.

Iran's tech sector, despite restrictions, has developed significant capacity (estimated 100,000+ software engineers, multiple successful app ecosystems operating within domestic parameters). Post-ceasefire sanctions relief combined with internet openness could rapidly integrate this capacity into the global digital economy, creating the economic constituency for sustained reform.

## Causal Mechanisms

Four causal mechanisms explain digital society governance outcomes in political transitions:

---

**Mechanism 1: Infrastructure Path Dependence Lock-In**

*Logic (if X then Y):* If internet governance reform does not include explicit policies for repurposing or dismantling censorship/surveillance infrastructure within the first 18–24 months of a transition, then technical path dependence—institutional knowledge, equipment contracts, trained personnel, embedded regulatory frameworks—will reconstitute censorship and surveillance functions even under politically reform-oriented leadership.

*Activation conditions:* The mechanism is activated by: continued operation of National Information Network infrastructure without functional redesign; retention of IRGC Cyber Command personnel in internet governance roles; maintenance of existing filtering regulations even when unenforced; lack of explicit legislative framework for surveillance limitation.

*Evidence base:* Russia's experience post-Soviet Union: despite political opening in the 1990s, KGB-era surveillance infrastructure was retained and gradually re-activated. Eastern European transitions that explicitly dismantled Stasi/Securitate-equivalent structures achieved more durable digital openness. Ukraine's active civilian oversight of digital infrastructure post-2014 provides a partial counter-example.

*Disruption:* The mechanism can be disrupted by explicit legislative action (Data Protection Law with civil liberties teeth, explicit prohibition of NIN use for content filtering, independent oversight of surveillance authorities) within the transition's early window.

---

**Mechanism 2: Circumvention Culture to Civil Society Digital Infrastructure**

*Logic:* If post-transition governance recognizes Iran's existing circumvention culture as a civil society asset rather than a security threat, and adopts regulatory frameworks that normalize privacy-preserving technologies, then the existing circumvention infrastructure transitions into legitimate digital civil society infrastructure—enabling civic organization, independent media, and encrypted political communication in the new democratic environment.

*Activation conditions:* Requires: explicit legal protection for VPN use and privacy-preserving technologies; regulatory framework that prohibits state surveillance of encrypted communications without judicial authorization; civil society organizations that can maintain technical capacity developed during censorship era.

*Evidence base:* Chile's post-Pinochet digital civil society incorporated communications security practices developed under dictatorship into democratic advocacy. South Korea's independent media ecosystem incorporated digital security practices from the authoritarian period. No direct case perfectly matches Iran's circumvention scale, but partial evidence supports the conversion pathway.

*Disruption:* The mechanism fails if post-transition governments treat circumvention tools as security threats and criminalize their use—recreating the adversarial relationship between digital civil society and the state.

---

**Mechanism 3: Economic Integration Creating Digital Openness Constituency**

*Logic:* If post-ceasefire sanctions relief enables Iranian tech companies to access global markets and foreign investment, and if this economic integration begins producing revenue and employment benefits within 2–3 years, then tech sector business interests create a durable political constituency for internet openness that reduces future reversal risk.

*Activation conditions:* Sanctions relief enabling: app store payments, payment processing (Visa/Mastercard), cloud service access, venture capital inflows. Iranian tech companies capable of competing in regional markets with open internet access. Government commitment to protecting tech sector from re-regulation as political winds shift.

*Evidence base:* South Korea's tech sector development (Samsung, LG, Kakao) created powerful constituencies for internet openness after democratization. Taiwan's Hsinchu technology ecosystem similarly. Iran's existing tech sector (Cafe Bazaar, Digikala, Snapp, Tapsi) has demonstrated domestic market viability; the question is whether sanctions relief and open internet access would enable global integration.

*Disruption:* The mechanism is disrupted if sanctions relief is slow, partial, or conditions Iran's tech companies to operate within NIN rather than global internet frameworks. IRGC commercial interests in domestic digital markets may resist global integration that undermines their market position.

---

**Mechanism 4: Bot Network and Disinformation Governance as Democratic Test**

*Logic:* If post-transition internet governance proactively addresses bot networks and coordinated inauthentic behavior—through transparent platform regulation, independent fact-checking infrastructure, and media literacy programs—then the information environment quality supports democratic consolidation. If these are neglected, disinformation campaigns by domestic factional actors and foreign states (Russia, China) degrade information quality and undermine democratic deliberation.

*Activation conditions:* Requires: regulatory authority for platform transparency (bot disclosure, political ad disclosure); independent civil society organizations with technical capacity to monitor information environment; media literacy programs; cooperation with international information environment monitoring bodies.

*Evidence base:* Chile's experience with social bots (Mendoza et al. 2024) demonstrates the scale of the challenge. Eastern European democracies' experience with Russian disinformation post-2014 demonstrates the stakes. No Middle Eastern democracy has yet developed robust information environment governance—Iran could establish a regional model if post-transition governance prioritizes this.

## Iran-Specific Implications: Wartime Digital Infrastructure and Post-Ceasefire Governance (April 2026)

### The April 2026 Wartime Context

US-Israel military strikes from February 28, 2026 have created significant physical damage to Iran's internet infrastructure. Data centers, fiber-optic backbone routes, and telecommunications facilities have been disrupted. The IRGC Cyber Command—which previously exercised unified control over Iran's offensive cyber capabilities and contributed to domestic surveillance infrastructure—has decentralized under wartime operational pressure, creating fragmentation in the previously coherent internet governance architecture.

Simultaneously, wartime information demand has accelerated Iran's already sophisticated circumvention culture. International internet access providers including satellite internet services have expanded their presence in Iran's digital ecosystem. The April 7–8 ceasefire creates an opportunity to address this fragmented infrastructure either through deliberate redesign or through reconsolidation under new political management.

### Institutional Mapping

**Supreme Council of Cyberspace:** The coordinating body for Iran's internet governance, theoretically chaired by the President. Under Mojtaba Khamenei's new government, the composition and mandate of this body is in flux. A post-ceasefire redesign of the Supreme Council—with independent civil society representation and transparent decision-making—would be a significant reform signal.

**Ministry of ICT:** The primary regulatory body for telecommunications. Post-ceasefire, the Ministry faces both physical infrastructure reconstruction and policy redesign mandates. The choice of Minister and the Ministry's mandate regarding content filtering will be a key early signal.

**IRGC Cyber Command:** Significantly decentralized under wartime pressure. Whether Cyber Command is reconsolidated under new IRGC leadership, restructured as a military-only offensive cyber force, or broken up with civilian oversight functions transferred to civilian agencies is the single most important institutional variable for post-ceasefire internet governance.

**FATA (Cyber Police):** Responsible for domestic enforcement of internet regulations, including arresting cyber activists and processing cases under Iran's Computer Crimes Law. FATA's mandate and prosecutorial approach will be a clear indicator of whether internet governance reforms are genuine or cosmetic.

**National Information Network (NIN/SHOMA):** The physical infrastructure of Iran's domestic internet. Post-ceasefire, physical damage repair must be accompanied by policy decisions about NIN's purpose: a data sovereignty infrastructure compatible with civil liberties, or a continued censorship/surveillance platform.

### Transition Scenarios for Digital Governance

**Scenario A: Democratic Redesign (Rapid reform; Islamabad sanctions relief; new internet governance law)**

The NIN is legally redesignated as neutral infrastructure; the Computer Crimes Law is amended to narrow scope to genuine criminal activity; the Supreme Council of Cyberspace is restructured with civil society representation; IRGC Cyber Command is limited to military-only offensive operations. Result: Iran's tech sector integrates rapidly into global digital economy; circumvention culture converts to civil society digital infrastructure; information environment quality improves with new bot network regulation.

Timeline for digital society transformation: 24–36 months to meaningful openness; 4–6 years to regional digital leader status.

**Scenario B: Managed Liberalization (Selective reform; NIN maintained; partial sanctions relief)**

Content filtering relaxed for some categories (news, political speech) while maintained for others (encrypted platforms, foreign social media). NIN retained as national security infrastructure but reduced content filtering. IRGC Cyber Command reconsolidated under military control. Result: Partial digital opening insufficient to enable full tech sector integration; circumvention culture maintained as political hedge; information environment partially improved.

**Scenario C: Reconsolidation (IRGC reconsolidation; NIN expanded; new leadership doubles down)**

NIN expanded and physically repaired under IRGC oversight. Content filtering restored and enhanced with AI-powered surveillance. FATA operations expanded. Result: Continued digital authoritarianism; talent emigration continues; tech sector stagnates; international isolation maintained.

### Critical Observations for April 2026

The wartime disruption of Iran's internet governance architecture is an opportunity that will not recur. The NIN's physical damage and the IRGC Cyber Command's decentralization have temporarily reduced the technical barriers to reform. However, this window is narrow: institutional reconsolidation under new management—even without explicit policy change—will recreate the infrastructure of digital control within 12–18 months if not actively redirected.

The Islamabad talks should explicitly address internet governance: sanctions relief for digital infrastructure and tech sector integration should be conditioned on verifiable commitments to independent civilian oversight of content filtering. International technical assistance for NIN redesign as neutral infrastructure (rather than censorship infrastructure) should be on offer.

## General Reader Summary

Iran has built one of the world's most sophisticated internet censorship systems. Over more than a decade, the government has constructed a National Information Network—dedicated fiber-optic cables, data centers, and monitoring equipment—designed to give the state control over what Iranians can see, say, and do online. The IRGC's cyber forces and the cyber police work alongside this infrastructure to arrest online activists, block platforms like Instagram and Twitter, and surveil political communications.

This article asks: across the world's political transitions, what do we know about how countries manage internet governance reform? And what does that mean for Iran in April 2026?

**What the evidence shows**

The most important finding from comparative research is that internet censorship infrastructure is "sticky"—once built, it tends to persist even when political conditions change, because the equipment, personnel, technical knowledge, and regulatory frameworks have all been invested and create their own institutional momentum. Transitions that assumed internet freedom would naturally follow political change—without actively dismantling censorship infrastructure—were often disappointed.

The good news is that a second pattern is equally strong: under heavy censorship, ordinary people develop sophisticated workarounds. Iranians are world leaders in VPN use and digital circumvention. This underground digital infrastructure is a real civil society asset—the question is whether post-transition governance treats it as a threat or as a resource.

Economically, the evidence is clear: Iran's internet restrictions cost the economy significantly in lost tech sector development, talent emigration, and exclusion from global digital supply chains. Iran has a substantial tech sector despite these restrictions; with open internet and sanctions relief, rapid integration into the global digital economy is plausible.

**What April 2026 means**

The US-Israel strikes have physically damaged Iran's internet infrastructure, and the IRGC's cyber operations have been disrupted. This is an unusual moment: the technical barriers to internet governance reform are lower than they've been in decades because the censorship infrastructure has been partly disrupted.

The ceasefire and Islamabad talks create an opportunity to address internet governance explicitly—making sanctions relief for Iran's tech sector conditional on verifiable commitments to independent civilian oversight of the internet. This linkage would create international accountability for internet reform in a way that purely domestic political commitments cannot.

The key decision point is the National Information Network: will it be redesigned as neutral data infrastructure with civil liberties protections, or rebuilt as a censorship/surveillance tool under new management? That choice will determine Iran's digital trajectory for the next decade.
