## Theoretical Framework

Framework: Realist Synthesis — Context-Mechanism-Outcome Configuration

This synthesis examines the contested role of digital infrastructure as civic space under authoritarian governance. The CMO configuration:

**Context:** Authoritarian states face a dilemma: internet infrastructure is necessary for economic development but creates communication channels that can facilitate opposition mobilization. **Mechanism:** Regimes respond through a dual strategy — deploying censorship and surveillance to control digital space while simultaneously developing domestic digital infrastructure to reduce dependence on foreign platforms. Citizens develop counter-strategies: diaspora activists operate from safe jurisdictions, activists adopt encrypted tools, and civic movements use platforms faster than states can control them. **Outcome:** The result is not a simple liberation-vs-repression binary but an ongoing co-evolutionary process where state capacity and civic innovation escalate in parallel.

The key empirical debate — whether the internet empowers activists or autocrats (Rød & Weidmann 2015) — is tested through Iran's extensive digital control apparatus and Egypt/Tunisia's revolutionary uses of social media.

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (11 sources)

The evidence reveals a three-phase evolution in the relationship between digital infrastructure and civic space, from early optimism through authoritarian adaptation to the current contested equilibrium.

**Phase 1: Liberation Technology Thesis.** The earliest evidence supported digital optimism. Khamis and Vaughn (2011) document how cyberactivism during the Egyptian Revolution used social media — particularly Facebook and Twitter — to organize protests, distribute citizen journalism, and tilt the balance against the Mubarak regime. Howard (2010) theorized this as 'the digital origins of dictatorship and democracy,' arguing that information technologies fundamentally alter the balance between states and citizens in Muslim-majority countries. Morozov (2009), however, provided an early warning from Iran's 2009 Green Movement: the 'Twitter Revolution' narrative obscured how the regime rapidly adapted, using the same platforms for surveillance, identification, and prosecution of activists.

**Phase 2: Authoritarian Adaptation.** Rød and Weidmann (2015) provide the most rigorous challenge to the liberation thesis. Using cross-national data, they demonstrate that while ICT facilitates opposition organization in some contexts, it simultaneously enhances state repressive capacity. The net effect depends on regime type and institutional capacity. Chang and Lin (2020) confirm this through 153-country time-series data (1995-2018), showing that internet censorship is a reactive strategy used by autocracies specifically to suppress civil society after initial internet diffusion threatens the regime. Lindsay (2015) extends the analysis to cybersecurity, showing how states — particularly China — develop sophisticated digital control capabilities that create models exportable to other authoritarian regimes.

**Phase 3: Iran's Digital Authoritarianism.** The Iran-specific evidence constitutes the richest portion of this synthesis. Conduit (2025) provides the most current analysis, documenting how the global technology industry — through commercial spyware sales to at least 43 authoritarian states — enables digital authoritarianism. For Iran, this means state surveillance capacity extends beyond domestic development to include purchased capabilities. Yalcintas and Alizadeh (2020) analyze Iran's 'digital protectionism' — the national planning strategy for controlling international data traffic, including the National Information Network (SHOMA/NIN) project to create a controlled domestic internet. Michaelsen (2016) documents how digital communication technologies are a double-edged sword for Iran's exiled activists: enabling continued involvement in domestic debates while exposing them to transnational repression through surveillance. Karimi et al. (2024) analyze the challenges of digital transformation in Iran's public sector, showing that even the government's own digital modernization efforts face institutional resistance and capacity constraints.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (11 sources)

The central pattern is **co-evolutionary escalation**: state digital control and civic digital resistance develop in response to each other, with neither achieving permanent dominance. Iran's trajectory illustrates this clearly — the regime's internet shutdown during the 2019 Bloody November protests was technically more sophisticated than any previous intervention, but the subsequent development of circumvention tools and satellite-based communication showed civic adaptation.

A secondary pattern involves **the internationalization of digital civic space**. Michaelsen (2016) documents how Iranian diaspora activists operate from jurisdictions beyond regime reach, while Conduit (2025) shows that authoritarian states purchase surveillance tools to extend control transnationally. This means digital civic space is increasingly deterritorialized — neither contained within national borders nor accessible solely through domestic infrastructure. Any post-transition digital governance framework must account for this deterritorialization.

## Iran Context

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (11 sources)

Iran is arguably the world's most important test case for the liberation-vs-control debate. The regime has invested heavily in both censorship infrastructure (the National Information Network) and surveillance technology (including commercially acquired spyware documented by Conduit 2025). Yet Iran simultaneously has one of the highest internet penetration rates in the Middle East and a digitally sophisticated population — particularly among the under-35 cohort that constitutes the majority.

The transferability assessment reveals that Iran's digital civic space challenge is qualitatively different from both the Egyptian model (where relatively open platforms enabled revolutionary mobilization) and the Chinese model (where a comprehensive great firewall creates near-total domestic control). Iran occupies an unstable middle ground: enough control to suppress large-scale digital organization but insufficient to prevent information circulation through diaspora channels, VPNs, and encrypted messaging. Digital protectionism (Yalcintas & Alizadeh 2020) represents the regime's attempt to resolve this instability by creating a controlled domestic internet, but فیلترنت (filternet) — the popular term for Iran's filtered internet — remains technically and politically contested.

A post-transition Iran would face the inverse challenge: rebuilding digital infrastructure for open civic participation after decades of surveillance-oriented architecture. This would require not just technical infrastructure changes but institutional trust-building, data protection legislation, and mechanisms to prevent surveillance tools from being repurposed by successor governments.

## Open Research Questions

- How can post-transition digital infrastructure be designed to prevent the surveillance architecture built under the Islamic Republic from being repurposed by successor governments?
- What role would Iran's diaspora tech community — concentrated in Silicon Valley and European tech hubs — play in rebuilding digital civic space, and what governance structures would ensure accountability?
- Can internet governance models from post-transition states (e.g., Tunisia's post-2011 digital rights framework) be adapted for Iran, given the far greater scale of Iran's surveillance infrastructure?

## Implications for Iran

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (9 sources)

**Institutional Mapping**

Iran's digital infrastructure sits at the nexus of state control and civic resistance. The Supreme Council of Cyberspace (شورای عالی فضای مجازی) — chaired by the president with membership including IRGC commanders, intelligence officials, and judiciary representatives — sets internet policy. The Communications Regulatory Authority (سازمان تنظیم مقررات) manages telecommunications regulation and filtering. The National Information Network (شبکه ملی اطلاعات/NIN) represents the regime's flagship project for creating a controlled domestic internet. The IRGC Cyber Command (قرارگاه سایبری سپاه) conducts both offensive operations (against foreign targets and domestic dissent infrastructure) and defensive surveillance. The MOIS maintains separate digital surveillance capabilities. The FATA Police (پلیس فتا) — Iran's dedicated cyber police force — prosecutes online speech, social media activity, and digital rights violations. The Telecommunications Company of Iran (شرکت مخابرات), privatized to an IRGC-affiliated entity under Article 44, controls physical infrastructure with embedded surveillance capabilities. Commercial spyware purchased from international vendors (documented by Conduit 2025) augments domestic surveillance technology.

**Structural Parallels**

Iran occupies an unstable middle ground between Egypt's relatively open platforms (which enabled revolutionary mobilization) and China's comprehensive Great Firewall (which creates near-total domestic control). The NIN project aspires to the Chinese model but has not achieved it — Iran's internet penetration is high, VPN usage is estimated at 10-15 million regular users, and the digitally sophisticated under-35 cohort consistently circumvents filtering. Tunisia's AMMAR system provides the most relevant transition analogy: systematic filtering declined from 60% to under 5% within four years of revolution, demonstrating that filtering infrastructure can be dismantled. However, Tunisia's experience also confirms that surveillance capabilities persist under democratic governance — intelligence agencies maintain monitoring capacity regardless of political transition. Estonia's privacy-by-design e-government provides the aspirational model: digital infrastructure designed from inception for citizen access, transparency, and data protection — not repurposed surveillance architecture. Iran's transition challenge is qualitatively different from all comparators because فیلترنت (filternet) is not merely a policy but an infrastructure: the NIN creates physical network architecture that constrains connectivity regardless of policy changes.

**Key Actors and Sequencing**

Iran's tech-literate population — demonstrated through three successive digital mobilization cycles (2009 Green Movement, 2017-18 economic protests, 2022 WLF) — is the most powerful enabler. The WLF movement proved that digital authoritarianism cannot eliminate civic participation: encrypted messaging coordination, decentralized video archiving, and sustained international media engagement continued despite internet shutdowns and active surveillance. Diaspora technology communities (Tor bridge operators, Psiphon distributors, VPN funders) provide technical infrastructure and could contribute to post-transition rebuilding. Iran's IT workforce and startup ecosystem possess technical capacity. Blockers: the Supreme Council of Cyberspace's institutional authority; IRGC Cyber Command's surveillance infrastructure and personnel; IRGC-owned telecom infrastructure with embedded monitoring; commercial spyware already deployed; and the security apparatus institutional culture that views digital monitoring as essential state function. Sequencing: (1) immediately end internet shutdowns and restore full international connectivity; (2) dismantle systematic content filtering at ISP level; (3) comprehensive audit of NIN infrastructure for embedded surveillance — engage international cybersecurity experts and domestic IT community; (4) enact data protection and privacy legislation with independent enforcement authority before deploying e-government services; (5) restructure telecom ownership — genuine divestiture of IRGC-controlled infrastructure; (6) establish independent digital rights commission with civil society representation; (7) develop privacy-by-design e-government platforms leveraging domestic technical talent and Estonian/Nordic models.

**Risks**

The primary danger is surveillance persistence: billions invested in digital monitoring infrastructure — IRGC Cyber Command, MOIS capabilities, commercially acquired spyware, embedded telecom monitoring — would not disappear with regime change. These capabilities could be repurposed by successor governments or captured by security apparatus elements during transition. Tunisia confirms this risk. A second danger is the "digital divide" risk: if NIN infrastructure is dismantled without replacement, populations dependent on domestic digital services (rural, lower-income, less technically sophisticated) could lose access while VPN-savvy urban populations gain. A third risk is diaspora dependency: over-reliance on foreign-based technical solutions rather than building domestic institutional capacity for open digital governance. A fourth risk is the commercial spyware market: even post-transition, the international spyware industry documented by Conduit would continue marketing surveillance capabilities to Iranian security agencies. Prerequisites: pre-transition mapping of surveillance infrastructure; data protection legislation before e-government deployment; telecom ownership restructuring; international cooperation on spyware export controls; investment in domestic digital infrastructure; and recognition that digital transition is a 5-10 year institutional transformation, not a switch to be flipped.
