## Evidence

Judicial independence and the rule of law emerge as deeply contested institutional features across transitional and authoritarian regimes. In Eastern Europe, Poland exemplifies how formally democratic systems can experience systematic judicial backsliding. Drinóczi & Bień-Kacała (2019) argue that "illiberal constitutionalism" in Poland involves populist political movements that relativize rule of law protections, demonstrating how judicial independence can erode even within constitutionally committed frameworks. Similarly, Adamska-Gallant (2022) documents how Poland's backsliding since 2015 has involved systematic challenges to court independence, confirmed by both the European Court of Human Rights and the Court of Justice of the European Union. The European Commission's 2022 Rule of Law Report, cited by Blanke & Sander (2023), highlights "serious concerns about the independence and impartiality of the judiciary" in both Poland and Hungary, indicating that institutional pressures on courts operate across multiple post-communist democracies.

In Iran, judicial independence exists within a fundamentally different constitutional architecture in which religious authority structures judicial legitimacy. Saffari (1993) analyzes how the 1979 Iranian Constitution institutionalizes clerical rule by establishing debates about "legitimacy, representation, concepts of democracy, and the relations between temporal and religious power." Rahmani & Mirzadeh Koohshahi (2016) note that Iran's post-revolutionary courts—including the "clerical" and "revolutionary" courts—underwent radical procedural changes designed to "Islamicize" the judicial system, a structural feature distinguishing Iran from secular transitional democracies. Tabari (2003) locates "the main fault line for the instability of the Iranian system in the fissure caused by those who wish to reform the Islamic Republic," finding that reformers have sought protection in rule of law concepts. These different constitutional contexts reveal how judicial independence takes divergent institutional forms depending on whether religious or secular authority legitimizes state power.

Cross-nationally, the relationship between constitutional design and judicial autonomy proves decisive. Piana (2009) examines how the EU and Council of Europe applied "heavy pressure on domestic elite to promote the adoption of institutional guarantees of judicial independence and judicial capacity" in post-communist candidates, suggesting that external institutional models shape judicial reform pathways. Magalhães (1999) poses the foundational question for comparative analysis: "What kind of judicial institutions is likely to emerge in countries undergoing democratization?" His work on Eastern Europe implies that successful judicial reform requires not only formal institutional guarantees but also political consensus about the judiciary's proper role. In Iran, by contrast, Zonis (1985) argues that "Direct rule by Islamic clerics...is an important new phenomenon in Middle Eastern politics," with clerical legitimacy embedded directly in constitutional structure rather than derived from secular legal traditions.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

Two critical patterns emerge across both case studies. First, **judicial independence proves vulnerable to executive concentration when constitutional structures permit dual power centers**. In Poland, the Law and Justice party's judicial reforms reflect what Zamęcki & Glied (2020) call "gradual destruction of democratic institutions...challenges to the rule of law, as well as to the system of checks and balances." The reformist approach in Iran similarly concentrates executive-clerical power: the Supreme Leader appoints the Head of Judiciary, the Guardian Council vets judicial candidates, and revolutionary courts operate parallel to regular civilian courts (Rahmani & Mirzadeh Koohshahi 2016). Both contexts feature systems in which formal institutional safeguards prove inadequate against coordinated political pressure.

Second, **separation of powers remains institutionally foundational but procedurally contested**. Parra Gómez (2021) argues that "division of powers...consists, substantially, in the fact that power is not concentrated," framing separation of powers as the core institutional requirement. However, Stambulski (2022) demonstrates that in Poland, "constitutional populism" enables majority governments to bypass such constraints through formal constitutional change rather than pure violation. In Iran, Tamadonfar (2001) describes how Khomeini's clerical regime "established an extensive institutional mechanism of legal control" that legally subordinates judicial independence to clerical political objectives. Both cases reveal that checks-and-balances frameworks depend critically on whether political actors view institutional constraints as binding or merely formal obstacles to be overcome.

## Implications for Iran

Iran's judicial system presents a unique challenge: it operates within a constitutional framework explicitly designed to subordinate judicial independence to clerical supremacy. The Guardian Council's vetting of judges and candidates for all offices, the Supreme Leader's direct appointment of the Head of Judiciary, and the existence of parallel revolutionary courts operating outside standard civil law procedures create a system where judicial "independence" exists only within parameters set by religious authority (Saffari 1993, Rahmani & Mirzadeh Koohshahi 2016). Tabari (2003) finds that reformist Iranian movements have specifically invoked rule of law concepts to challenge this structure, but without fundamental constitutional revision, such efforts remain constrained by the velayat-e faqih framework.

The most consequential institutional gap involves constitutional court design and the absence of genuine judicial review. Unlike Poland and other transitional democracies where constitutional courts emerged as rule-of-law safeguards (Piana 2009), Iran's Council of Guardians combines legislative and judicial vetting functions under clerical control, eliminating the separation required for independent constitutional review. Osanloo (2020) documents how Iranian criminal courts operate under dual systems—both formal legal procedure and Islamic jurisprudence—creating potential conflicts unresolved by institutional hierarchy. A path toward judicial independence in Iran would require: (1) constitutional separation of the judiciary from clerical appointment mechanisms; (2) establishment of a genuine constitutional court with authority independent of the Guardian Council; (3) training judges in secular constitutional law rather than fiqh-based jurisprudence; and (4) elimination of parallel revolutionary court systems that circumvent civil law procedures. These reforms mirror institutional designs adopted in post-communist Europe, yet Iran's theocratic constitutional structure creates obstacles absent in secular transitional regimes.

## Open Questions

- How might Iran redesign its constitutional court to achieve genuine judicial independence while preserving Islamic constitutional principles, and what comparative lessons exist from Islamic democracies (such as Malaysia or Tunisia) that have created functional constitutional review?
- Under what conditions might reform coalitions within Iran's judiciary challenge Guardian Council vetting, and what historical parallels exist in post-revolutionary systems where judicial actors mobilized to reclaim institutional autonomy (Poland's judicial resistance in 2015-2023)?
- Can rule of law protections function effectively in systems where judicial independence remains subordinated to religious authority, or does the theocratic constitutional structure create inherent contradictions that only full constitutional reform can resolve?
