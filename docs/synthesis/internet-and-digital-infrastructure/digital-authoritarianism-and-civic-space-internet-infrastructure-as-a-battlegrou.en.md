## Theoretical Framework

CMO Framework for Digital Authoritarianism and Civic Space. Context: Authoritarian states across the Middle East and North Africa have invested heavily in digital surveillance and filtering systems. Iran's National Information Network (SHOMA/NIN) represents a strategic attempt to create a parallel internet ecosystem under government control, while Tunisia's pre-2011 Ben Ali regime deployed the AMMAR system for comprehensive censorship [Source 6211]. These systems reflect broader patterns: digital infrastructure is explicitly designed as an apparatus of control, monitoring, and suppression of dissent [Source 2167]. Mechanism: Digital systems designed for surveillance contain structural features that can be repurposed for civic participation. The mechanism operates through three pathways: (1) Technical architecture for state control often embeds affordances for network communication citizens can appropriate; (2) Administrative systems for digital governance, once captured by democratic processes, provide scalable platforms for civic services; (3) Expertise required to build control infrastructure creates cadres of digital professionals who can redirect skills toward open governance [Source 3338]. Outcome: Two divergent outcomes across similar starting conditions. Tunisia's post-2011 transition produced a relatively open internet ecosystem. Iran's dual architecture creates competing digital spaces—controlled NIN constrains participation while global internet access via VPNs enables a vibrant diaspora-connected digital public sphere [Source 7069]. Evidence certainty: Moderate.

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

Tunisia's AMMAR system represented one of the Arab region's most sophisticated filtering infrastructures. Post-2011 transition produced documented changes: formal abolition of systematic filtering, net neutrality principles adoption, and emergence of civil society organizations monitoring internet rights [Source 6211]. Academic sources track Tunisian internet traffic subject to filtering declining from approximately 60% in 2010 to less than 5% by 2015. Iran presents a contrasting case where comprehensive filtering coexists with widespread circumvention tool adoption. The NIN initiative aims to create parallel internet architecture under state control, while Iran's tech-literate population (estimated 10-15 million regular VPN users) maintains access to global digital spaces [Source 2167]. The Women Life Freedom movement demonstrated civic capacity of Iranian digital networks: coordination of protests via encrypted messaging, international media engagement, and digital archiving of police violence despite active surveillance [Source 3338, 7069]. Estonia's digital governance model provides contrasting comparison—rather than repurposing surveillance infrastructure, Estonia designed systems emphasizing citizen access and transparency [Source 2326]. VPN adoption in Iran and Tunisia correlates with filtering intensity; diaspora technology communities provide technical support and funding for circumvention tools including Tor, Psiphon, and commercial VPNs [Source 854].

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

Four cross-case patterns emerge. Pattern 1: Digital infrastructure built for control contains latent capacity for civic participation. Systems for internet monitoring also capture network data useful for optimization; institutions trained in digital governance can serve public interest with regulatory reorientation; digital professionals trained in state control develop capabilities applicable to open architecture. Pattern 2: Transition moments create windows for internet governance reform. Tunisia's 2011 transition enabled civil society to shape telecommunications policy. Iran's periodic protest cycles (2009, 2017-18, 2022-23) generated international attention to internet rights and accelerated diaspora technical engagement. Early post-transition periods (months to 2-3 years) appear most conducive to institutional reform. Pattern 3: Tech-savvy diaspora bridges repressive and open digital ecosystems. Iranian Silicon Valley entrepreneurs, German-based Tunisian digital rights organizations, and diaspora-funded projects maintain technical bridges. Diaspora communities demonstrate higher organizational resilience and resource access than domestic organizations under surveillance. Pattern 4: Economic digitalization incentives can override surveillance impulses. Government interest in e-commerce, digital payments, and smart city development creates pressure to reduce filtering where it hinders economic activity [Source 2167]. However, states can implement surveillance-compatible digital infrastructure, so this pattern is probabilistic rather than determinative.

## Iran Context

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

Iran's National Information Network embodies the infrastructure paradox: designed for sovereignty protection and economic development, its cloud computing, data centers, and administrative systems could serve open governance with institutional reorientation [Source 6211]. Iran's tech-literate population has demonstrated remarkable civic capacity—the Women Life Freedom movement coordinated protests via encrypted messaging, documented police violence through decentralized video archiving, and sustained international media engagement despite filtering [Source 7069]. This proves digital authoritarianism has not eliminated civic participation. However, surveillance infrastructure persistence represents significant risk. Tunisia's experience shows surveillance capabilities designed during authoritarian periods persist under democratic governance, requiring sustained civil society vigilance [Source 854]. Iran faces similar risks: embedded surveillance in telecoms, security agencies' institutional interests in monitoring, and technical switching costs. Smart city and e-government opportunities create leverage for embedding data protection and privacy into digital infrastructure. Comparative evidence from Estonia shows privacy-by-design principles can be embedded in e-government from inception [Source 2326]. For Iran, this requires civil society input into governance standards, international technical cooperation, and legal frameworks establishing data protection as deployment prerequisite. Evidence certainty on tech population capacity is high (documented 2009, 2017, 2022 protests); on NIN dual potential moderate; on surveillance persistence risk moderate to high.

## Implications for Iran

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

**Institutional Mapping**

Iran's digital infrastructure sits at the intersection of control and potential civic transformation. The Supreme Council of Cyberspace (شورای عالی فضای مجازی) sets internet policy. The Communications Regulatory Authority (سازمان تنظیم مقررات و ارتباطات رادیویی) manages telecommunications licensing and filtering. The National Information Network (شبکه ملی اطلاعات/NIN) represents the regime's effort to create a controlled domestic internet — a filternet (فیلترنت) enabling state-mediated digital services while restricting access to the global internet. The IRGC's cyber warfare command (قرارگاه سایبری) conducts offensive and defensive operations. The MOIS and IRGC intelligence organization conduct digital surveillance of activists, journalists, and diaspora. The Telecommunications Company of Iran (شرکت مخابرات ایران), privatized to an IRGC-affiliated entity, controls much of the physical telecommunications infrastructure. The FATA Police (پلیس فتا) — Iran's cyber police — prosecutes online speech deemed criminal.

**Structural Parallels**

Tunisia's AMMAR filtering system and post-2011 transformation provides the most directly relevant parallel. Tunisia demonstrated that filtering infrastructure can be dismantled: systematic filtering declined from approximately 60% of internet traffic (2010) to less than 5% by 2015 after the revolution. However, Tunisia also demonstrated that surveillance capabilities persist under democratic governance — a directly relevant warning for Iran. Iran's digital authoritarianism differs from Tunisia's in scale and sophistication: the NIN project attempts not merely filtering but construction of a parallel domestic internet, more analogous to China's Great Firewall than Tunisia's content filtering. Estonia's privacy-by-design e-government model provides the aspirational counter-case: digital infrastructure designed for citizen access and transparency rather than surveillance and control. Iran's tech-literate population (estimated 10-15 million regular VPN users) has demonstrated that authoritarian digital infrastructure cannot eliminate civic participation — but it can constrain organization at scale.

**Key Actors and Sequencing**

Iran's digitally sophisticated population is a powerful enabler: the Woman Life Freedom movement demonstrated coordination via encrypted messaging, decentralized video archiving of police violence, and sustained international media engagement despite active surveillance and internet shutdowns. Diaspora technology communities provide technical support (Tor bridges, Psiphon distribution, commercial VPN funding) and could contribute to post-transition infrastructure rebuilding. Iran's IT workforce and startup ecosystem (despite constraints) possess technical capacity for digital transformation. Blockers: the Supreme Council of Cyberspace's institutional authority; IRGC cyber command's surveillance infrastructure; telecom companies with IRGC ownership whose infrastructure embeds surveillance by design; and the security apparatus institutional culture of monitoring that would persist post-transition. Sequencing: (1) immediately end internet shutdowns and restore full global connectivity; (2) dismantle systematic content filtering while maintaining standards-based protections (child safety, fraud); (3) audit NIN infrastructure for embedded surveillance capabilities and either remediate or replace; (4) enact comprehensive data protection and privacy legislation before deploying e-government services; (5) restructure telecom ownership — genuine privatization removing IRGC from infrastructure control; (6) establish independent digital rights commission with civil society participation; (7) develop privacy-by-design e-government platforms drawing on Estonian model and domestic IT talent.

**Risks**

The primary danger is surveillance persistence: Iran has invested billions in digital monitoring infrastructure that would not disappear with regime change. Intelligence agencies' institutional culture, technical personnel, and infrastructure could be repurposed by successor governments — Tunisia's experience confirms this risk. A second risk is the "digital divide" deepening during transition: if NIN infrastructure is dismantled without replacement, rural and lower-income populations dependent on domestic services could lose access. A third risk is over-reliance on diaspora technology communities whose solutions may not address domestic infrastructure needs. Prerequisites: comprehensive audit of surveillance infrastructure before democratic transition activates it under new management; data protection legislation with independent enforcement; telecom restructuring separating infrastructure ownership from security entities; sustained investment in domestic digital infrastructure serving all populations; and international technical cooperation embedding open standards and interoperability from inception.

## What We Don't Know

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

**What this synthesis cannot fully answer:**

1. **Internet kill switch reversibility**: Iran's National Information Network (Intranet) infrastructure — built to allow complete disconnection from global internet — represents a technical control capacity not present in the Tunisia comparison. Whether this infrastructure can be effectively dismantled, or would remain available to future authoritarian actors, is an engineering question not addressed in the political science literature.

2. **VPN ecosystem and informal digital society**: Iran's extensive VPN use and Telegram-based information networks have created a parallel digital public sphere that operates outside state control. How this informal infrastructure would evolve in a transitional environment — whether it becomes integrated into a free internet or creates fragmented information markets — is not theorized in the literature.

3. **Digital economy transition**: Iran's e-commerce, fintech, and technology sector have grown substantially under sanctions-imposed isolation. Their relationship to international digital platforms — and whether Iranian digital companies could compete once trade barriers are removed — is understudied.

4. **Surveillance infrastructure dismantling**: The comparative literature on transitioning from digital surveillance states is almost entirely absent. Cybersecurity expertise required to audit, understand, and dismantle surveillance architecture is scarce, and no model transition has addressed this systematically.

5. **Media ecosystem transformation**: How Iran's dominated media landscape — state television, regime-aligned digital news, censored social media — would transform under free conditions, and what institutional support for independent journalism would require, is addressed only partially in the Tunisia case.

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

Iran has built one of the world's most sophisticated internet censorship systems — and then watched its population find workarounds through VPNs and Telegram anyway. Tunisia's 2011 transition is the most applicable case: it dismantled systematic filtering but struggled to prevent both state and commercial surveillance from persisting under new legal frameworks. For Iran, the technical challenge is greater because the National Information Network infrastructure was built specifically to allow complete disconnection from the global internet. This kill switch capability means that infrastructure dismantling — not just policy change — is required for genuine digital freedom.
