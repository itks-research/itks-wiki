## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Transitions between regime types fundamentally reshape the regional and international positioning of nation-states. Historical precedents demonstrate that the institutional architecture surrounding foreign policy-making becomes a contested terrain during such transformations, with competing actors advocating for divergent strategic orientations. 

The case of Turkey illustrates this dynamic with particular clarity. Following the transition from a single-party system toward competitive democracy in the 1950s, Turkey's regional foreign policy shifted from autarky and isolation toward active engagement with NATO and Western institutions. Yet even within this structural reorientation, continuities persisted. The strategic imperative of managing relations with Iran and the Arab world remained constant, even as the institutional mechanisms—parliament, foreign ministry, military—negotiated the terms of regional engagement. Similarly, Spain's integration into NATO and the European Union represented a dramatic reorientation from Franco-era isolationism, yet geographic relationships with North Africa maintained continuity (Aras & Yorulmazlar, 2014). 

Turkey's experience with Iran has oscillated between strategic partnership and cautious competition, shaped by economic interdependence, sectarian geopolitics, and Kurdish non-state actors across borders. The Arab Spring accelerated this dynamic: both Turkey and Iran sought to position themselves as leading powers in regional vacuums created by state fragmentation in Syria and Iraq. Iran's entrenchment of the Quds Force's presence reflected competing visions of regional order (Aras & Yorulmazlar, 2014). This competition occurred between a partially-democratic Turkey and an Islamic Republic—demonstrating that regime type alone does not determine foreign policy orientation. Rather, institutional capacity to project power, control domestic constituencies, and manage alliance commitments operates as a mediating variable.

Iran's foreign policy architecture reveals institutional fragmentation that would characterize any post-revolutionary transition. The Quds Force, under IRGC command, operates semi-autonomously from the Foreign Ministry, managing proxy networks across Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Yemen. This institutional dualism—formal diplomatic structures alongside coercive state organs—emerged from the Islamic Republic's legitimacy crisis and need to maintain revolutionary credentials while engaging in state diplomacy (Bazoobandi et al., 2023). Any democratic transition would necessarily disrupt this arrangement, forcing a reckoning between professional diplomatic institutions and securitized networks.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Democratic transitions generate predictable tensions in foreign policy orientation. The central dynamic is the shift from security-focused national interest to pluralistic conception incorporating democratic constituencies and civil society preferences.

Transitions involve periods of strategic ambiguity during which factions compete to define external orientation. In Spain's case (1976-1983), NATO and European integration became consensus; Turkey demonstrates this period can extend indefinitely if institutional consolidation remains incomplete.

Regional rivals attempt to shape outcomes through engagement or coercion. Russia and China signal to Iranian opposition that a post-Islamic Republic state would face great power competition. This external pressure can paradoxically strengthen transitional regime resistance.

Democratic transitions increase salience of national security institutions in foreign policy. Civil-military relations become contested, with militaries retaining autonomous control. Iran's IRGC would likely demand institutional guarantees protecting operational autonomy, as Turkey's military did.

## Iran Context

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Iran's current foreign policy architecture reflects three decades of institutional evolution within the Islamic Republic's theocratic-oligarchic structure. Understanding post-transition trajectory requires disaggregating institutional actors and competing strategic visions.

The Quds Force represents the most operationally significant component. Established in 1990 and elevated to full IRGC branch status in 2019, it maintains command relationships with Shiite militant organizations across Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Yemen, and Palestine. Its leadership reports directly to the Supreme Leader, bypassing the Foreign Ministry. This structure emerged from the Islamic Republic's founding premise that revolutionary credentials supersede professional diplomatic protocols. The Quds Force's regional network represents alternative power projection that cannot be readily dismantled through elections (Potapov, 2022; Prorochenko, 2025).

The Foreign Ministry operates within traditional Westphalian frameworks. Its recent articulation of strategic partnerships with Russia and China reflects pragmatic reorientation since 2015-2020, when sanctions intensified and U.S. nuclear agreement withdrawal rendered diplomacy fragile. Democratic transition would likely formalize institutional competition through parliament (Bazoobandi et al., 2023).

A democratic Iran would face immediate foreign policy dilemmas: extraterritorial security networks, great power alignment, and state legitimacy. Retaining the Quds Force signals theocratic continuity and invites sanctions; dismantling it eliminates power projection while alienating IRGC interests. Iran's Russia-China orientation reflects necessity given Western sanctions. The precedent of Spain demonstrates regional leadership can be reconceived through democratic frameworks. Whether comparable reframing is possible in Iran's context remains contingent on post-transition political configurations (Falarti, 2025).

## Open Research Questions

Key research gaps shape the analysis of Iran's potential foreign policy reorientation:

1. **Institutional Path Dependency**: To what extent would a democratic Iran be constrained by the IRGC's three-decade institutional accumulation of wealth, personnel, and autonomous decision-making capacity? The comparative cases of Turkey and Spain suggest civil control is achievable, but Iran's securitized economy may present unique structural obstacles.

2. **Great Power Positioning**: How would Russia and China respond to a democratic Iran potentially realigning toward Western or Gulf institutions? Would they attempt to prevent transition, as they have signaled, and if so, through what mechanisms? The Ukraine precedent suggests great powers are willing to accept regime change but Iran's geostrategic position may raise their stakes.

3. **Regional Integration vs. Revolutionary Authenticity**: Can a post-revolutionary Iranian state maintain leadership of regional Shiite constituencies without the ideological apparatus of theocracy? Does democratic governance offer alternative legitimacy pathways, or does it necessarily diminish Iran's regional influence?

## Implications for Iran

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Moderate source triangulation (6 sources)

**Institutional Mapping:** Iran's foreign policy architecture is institutionally fragmented in ways that would profoundly complicate post-transition reorientation. The **Quds Force** (نیروی قدس), a branch of the **IRGC**, operates semi-autonomously from the **Ministry of Foreign Affairs**, managing proxy networks across Iraq (Popular Mobilization Forces), Syria (Shiite militias), Lebanon (**Hezbollah**), and Yemen (Ansar Allah/Houthis). The **Supreme Leader's office** exercises ultimate authority over both tracks — formal diplomacy and extraterritorial operations — creating a dual foreign policy architecture where the Foreign Ministry manages state-to-state relations while the Quds Force manages sub-state networks. The **Supreme National Security Council** (شورای عالی امنیت ملی) nominally coordinates these tracks but in practice the Supreme Leader arbitrates conflicts. The **Majles National Security and Foreign Policy Committee** exercises limited oversight, and the **Artesh** (regular military) maintains conventional defense relationships distinct from IRGC regional operations.

**Structural Parallels:** Turkey's post-authoritarian foreign policy reorientation — from Franco-era-style isolation to NATO integration and EU candidacy — offers the most relevant parallel for how a democratic Iran might reposition regionally. Spain's transition demonstrated that democratic legitimacy enables rapid integration into Western security and economic frameworks. However, Iran faces a unique complication absent from both cases: an extensive extraterritorial military-political network (the "axis of resistance") that has no parallel in Turkish or Spanish experience. Dismantling or transforming this network is not merely a diplomatic decision but would require managing armed non-state actors whose institutional survival depends on Iranian patronage.

**Enablers and Blockers:** A democratic Iran's foreign policy reorientation would find enablers among **pragmatist diplomats** within the Foreign Ministry who have long advocated for normalization of Western relations, **business elites** who seek sanctions relief and trade integration, and **reformist clerics** who reject the Quds Force's ideological foreign policy. The primary blockers are **IRGC hardliners** whose institutional identity is defined by resistance ideology and regional proxy management, **Quds Force commanders** whose careers and networks depend on extraterritorial operations, and **ideological allies** in Iraq and Lebanon whose political positions depend on continued Iranian support.

**Iran-Specific Challenges:** Iran faces a trilemma absent from the comparative cases. First, abandoning the "axis of resistance" would eliminate Iran's primary source of regional strategic leverage before any alternative security framework is established. Second, maintaining it would signal continuity with theocratic foreign policy and invite continued sanctions and regional isolation. Third, transforming it — converting proxy relationships into conventional diplomatic partnerships — requires that Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, and Houthi forces accept legitimacy frameworks they have historically rejected. The Russia-China strategic partnership adds complexity: a democratic Iran would likely seek Western economic integration, but Russia and China would resist losing a strategic partner, potentially offering spoiler incentives to anti-transition actors within the IRGC. The sequencing question is critical — foreign policy reorientation cannot precede domestic institutional reform (the Quds Force must be subordinated to civilian authority first) but international actors may demand early signals of reorientation as a condition for sanctions relief and diplomatic recognition.
