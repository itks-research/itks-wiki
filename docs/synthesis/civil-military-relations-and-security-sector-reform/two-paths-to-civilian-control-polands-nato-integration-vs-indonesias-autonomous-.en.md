## Methodological Framework: Realist Synthesis

**Methodology:** Realist Synthesis (Pawson 2006) — Context-Mechanism-Outcome (CMO) Configuration

**Research Question:** Under what conditions does civilian control over a politically embedded military institution become durable, and what mechanisms produce that outcome?

### CMO Configuration

**Context (C):**
Both Poland (post-1989) and Indonesia (post-1998) undertook transitions from authoritarian rule in which the military held autonomous political roles embedded in law, doctrine, or informal practice. In Poland, the communist-era party-controlled military retained institutional loyalty structures and economic interests. In Indonesia, the TNI's *dwifungsi* (dual function) doctrine formally authorized military participation in both defense *and* civilian governance. Both militaries had economic interests (defense industries, business holdings) that gave them independent revenue streams insulating them from civilian budget control.

**Mechanism 1 — External Institutional Anchor (Poland):**
NATO accession conditionality required Poland to demonstrate civilian control over defense policy, democratic legislative oversight, and depoliticization of the officer corps before membership was granted (1999). This created a binding external accountability mechanism: reform was a prerequisite for integration into a high-value security community, not merely a domestic political preference. NATO socialization then transferred professional norms to the officer corps, replacing party-based identity with transnational military professionalism.

**Mechanism 2 — Autonomous Reform with Epistemic Coalition (Indonesia):**
Without an equivalent external anchor, Indonesia's reform relied on an internal coalition: reform-minded civilian defense academics, a subset of progressive military officers, and international donor pressure (U.S., EU, Australia). This epistemic community generated reform proposals and sustained political pressure through the democratic transition. However, the mechanism lacked binding leverage — it depended on sustained political will among civilian leaders and the continued marginalization of conservative military factions.

**Predicted Outcomes:**
- Poland (M1): Durable civilian supremacy through institutionalized legal subordination + identity transformation
- Indonesia (M2): Partial civilian control with persistent informal military power + risk of reversal when political conditions shift

**This article tests whether the evidence from each case confirms or disconfirms these predicted outcomes, and draws implications for Iran's transition challenge with the IRGC.**

## Evidence

### Poland: The External Anchor Model

Poland's post-1989 civil-military transition unfolded across five reform dimensions documented by Yaniszewski (2002) [1295]: dismantling Communist-era control mechanisms (political commissars, party cells within the military), establishing constitutionally-guaranteed civilian authority lines, enacting democratic oversight legislation, restructuring the defense ministry with civilian leadership, and integrating into NATO organizational standards. All five were substantially completed by Poland's NATO accession in 1999 and fully consolidated by the mid-2000s.

The mechanism through which NATO produced reform is documented in Epstein (2005) [1303]. NATO conditionality created what Epstein terms "institutional leverage": reform was not merely normatively desirable but instrumentally necessary for membership in a security community Poland's civilian leadership prioritized. This conditionality was reinforced by socialization — as Polish officers participated in NATO exercises, planning structures, and professional education, they adopted transnational professional military identities that replaced party-based loyalties. Sarvaš (1999) [1313] documents the corresponding decline in corporate group identity among Central and Eastern European officer corps, including Poland's, as military resources and independent economic interests were progressively brought under civilian oversight.

Tomaszycki (2006) [1297] offers a practitioner's assessment: by the mid-2000s, Poland's civilian defense ministry exercised full control over strategic decision-making, military appointments, and budget allocation. The officer corps had been depoliticized — military leadership neither sought nor held independent political roles. The Polish transition was not conflict-free (the 1981 martial law episode cast a long shadow), but the combination of historical anti-communist democratic aspiration, a reforming civilian political class, and NATO's institutional conditionality produced durable outcomes.

### Indonesia: The Autonomous Reform Model — Progress and Limits

Indonesia's 1998 transition triggered formal dismantling of *dwifungsi* doctrine, the withdrawal of military representatives from the legislature (completed 2004), and creation of a civilian defense oversight framework. This represented genuine progress. However, Khairurrizqo (2024) [1538] documents that formal reform and informal power diverged: after the legal architecture of civilian supremacy was established, the TNI retained influence through business networks, placement of retired officers in civilian bureaucracies, and informal advisory roles to executives. The military "returned to the barracks" in institutional form but maintained leverage in practice.

Kosandi and Wahono (2020) [1528] assess the record after two decades of reform: the TNI "has not executed all necessary reform agendas toward a professional military." They describe a "new subtle role" — not direct military intervention in politics but sustained informal influence on policy outcomes, facilitated by the weakness of civilian institutions in defense governance. Bradford (2024) [5504] argues the TNI is a "fiercely independent institution" that "defies many of the most basic precepts of conventional civil-military relations theory," with the TNI-civilian government relationship better described as co-dependent than hierarchical.

The international dimension of Indonesia's reform is instructive. Lachica (2003) [1534] documents that donor-assisted security sector reform programs — from the U.S., EU, and Australia — had "only limited success" by the early 2000s, with army reform appearing to "have ground to a halt." External pressure without internal political will and without binding conditionality could not sustain reform momentum. Wardoyo (2018) [1537] identifies the epistemic community (defense academics, reform-minded officials, civil society organizations) as the critical reform driver, but also notes that this community's influence was contingent on continued access to civilian political leadership — access that weakened as reformist coalitions within Indonesia's party system fragmented.

Greenlees (2011) [1559] documents the reversal risk clearly: thirteen years after reform began, conservative military figures were publicly calling for restoration of direct military governance roles, citing what they characterized as the "fragility" of Indonesian democracy. Civil society pushback was active but insufficient to prevent gradual erosion. This dynamic culminated in the 2025 TNI Law controversy: Jamaludin and Fadhilah (2025) [5491] analyze a draft law that would substantially expand the military's role in civilian governance structures, which Putri et al. (2025) [5508] document generated significant public opposition through digital platforms — evidence of a contested field rather than settled civilian supremacy.

### Direct Comparative Evidence

Safi (2024) [5525] provides the most direct comparative analysis, explicitly studying Poland's depoliticization and professionalization model as a potential strategy for Indonesia. The study identifies the core asymmetry: Poland's reform was externally anchored in NATO accession with binding consequences for non-compliance, while Indonesia's reform was internally negotiated with no equivalent external constraint. The Polish model's transferability to Indonesia is assessed as partial — the normative content (civilian oversight, legislative accountability, officer corps professionalization) is applicable, but the mechanism (conditionality) cannot be replicated without a comparable institutional framework.

## Patterns and Comparative Analysis

### Pattern 1: The Conditionality Gap

The single most explanatory variable distinguishing Poland's durable outcome from Indonesia's partial one is the presence or absence of binding external conditionality attached to reform. Poland's NATO accession created what social scientists call a "commitment device": civilian leaders could use external requirements to overcome domestic resistance from conservative military factions, and military leadership understood that institutional interests (budget, status, international standing) were served by compliance rather than resistance. In Indonesia, international donors offered incentives but not binding conditions — donor programs could be managed or delayed without catastrophic institutional consequences.

This pattern is consistent with broader comparative evidence on institutional reform: externally anchored reform processes tend to produce more durable outcomes than domestically negotiated ones in contexts where reformed institutions have strong interests in resisting change (see Epstein 2005 [1303]; Cottey et al. 2002 [1301]).

### Pattern 2: Formal vs. Informal Power Separation

Both cases reveal a distinction between formal institutional subordination (legal framework, organizational charts, legislative oversight mechanisms) and the informal exercise of military influence. Poland achieved both: the legal-formal architecture of civilian supremacy was created, and over time, informal military influence in politics also diminished as officer corps identity transformed. Indonesia achieved the former but not the latter: formal *dwifungsi* was abolished, but informal influence persisted and in some domains expanded.

This pattern suggests that civilian supremacy requires not only legal reform but identity transformation — a change in how military officers understand their professional role. NATO's socialization mechanism produced this transformation in Poland (Sarvaš 1999 [1313]); Indonesia's epistemic community approach produced some movement in this direction but reached a ceiling limited by the absence of a transformative institutional environment.

### Pattern 3: Economic Independence as Reform Obstacle

Both TNI and the Polish communist military held economic interests (business holdings, defense industries, pension systems) that gave them revenue independent of civilian budget control. In Poland, NATO integration requirements included restructuring these arrangements; civilian oversight of the defense budget was a NATO conditionality requirement, which reduced the military's economic autonomy. In Indonesia, reform of TNI business holdings was proposed but inconsistently implemented; the military retained substantial economic independence that insulated it from civilian leverage. Sebastian (2006) [5511] documents how the TNI's economic holdings were integral to its "realpolitik ideology" — a self-conception of institutional autonomy grounded in economic and political interests, not just professional doctrine.

### Pattern 4: Reversal Risk Correlates with Reform Mechanism

Poland's civilian supremacy has proven durable across multiple changes of government, including periods when parties skeptical of Western integration held power. Indonesia's civilian supremacy has proven reversible: the 2025 TNI Law controversy shows that institutional gains made in 1998–2004 are being contested nearly three decades later. This suggests that the mechanism of reform — external anchor vs. internal coalition — is predictive of durability: binding conditionality produces more stable outcomes than contingent coalitional bargaining.

### Refined CMO Configuration (Post-Evidence):
- **C:** Post-authoritarian military with embedded political/economic roles + weak civilian institutions + absent or present external institutional anchor
- **M1 (confirmed):** External anchor (NATO) → conditionality + socialization → identity transformation + legal reform → durable civilian supremacy
- **M2 (confirmed with revision):** Internal coalition → legal reform without identity transformation → formal civilian control + persistent informal power + contingent durability (reversal-vulnerable)

## Relevance to Iran: Transferability Assessment

### Structural Parallels

The IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps) shares structural features with both the TNI and the pre-reform Polish military, but in a configuration that makes civilian control reform substantially more challenging. Like the TNI under *dwifungsi*, the IRGC holds formal military roles alongside political representation (former IRGC commanders occupy senior executive, legislative, and judicial positions), economic holdings (construction, energy, banking, manufacturing through bonyad networks), and internal security functions. Like the pre-1989 Polish military, the IRGC is institutionally aligned with a ruling ideology — though in Iran's case, the ideology (Velayat-e-Faqih, clerical guardianship) is more deeply constitutionalized than Communist Party control was in Poland.

### Critical Differences that Limit Direct Transfer

**Ideological founding purpose:** The IRGC was not an inherited institution that gained political roles over time (as the TNI's *dwifungsi* evolved gradually); it was *created* for ideological purposes — to defend the Islamic Revolution and the principle of clerical supremacy. This means the IRGC's political role is not an accretion or institutional drift but a core design feature. The mechanisms that worked for the TNI (formal abolition of dual-function doctrine) would face more fundamental constitutional resistance in Iran.

**Scale of economic empire:** The IRGC's economic holdings through Khatam al-Anbiya construction and affiliated bonyad networks are estimated to represent 25–40% of Iran's formal economy. Indonesia's TNI business holdings were significant but not of comparable scale relative to the national economy. Reforming IRGC economic interests would require not just military reform but a comprehensive restructuring of the Iranian economy.

**External anchor unavailability:** The Poland comparison is instructive precisely because it reveals the mechanism Iran lacks. Poland benefited from NATO and EU accession conditionality — a clear institutional destination with binding requirements and high-value rewards. No equivalent anchor exists for Iran given current geopolitical circumstances: Iran's international isolation, sanctions architecture, and regional security dynamics mean that no external institution can offer membership conditionality comparable to NATO's leverage over Poland.

### Transferable Elements

**Indonesia's epistemic community model** offers more directly transferable insights than Poland's conditionality model. Evidence from Indonesia suggests that reform coalitions including reform-minded military officers, civilian defense academics, and diaspora expertise can generate sustained pressure for change and produce genuine, if incomplete, institutional reform. Iran has analogues: a significant Iranian diaspora with defense and governance expertise, reformist currents within Iranian political institutions, and internationally connected academic networks. The Indonesia evidence also warns, however, that epistemic community pressure without structural lock-in mechanisms produces reversible gains.

**The sequencing lesson from both cases:** both Poland and Indonesia demonstrate that legal-formal reform (abolishing political roles in law) must be accompanied by identity transformation (changing how the military institution understands its professional purpose) and economic decoupling (removing independent revenue streams). Sequencing matters: legal reform without economic decoupling leaves the military with resources to contest civilian authority; legal reform without identity transformation produces compliance without acceptance.

**Evidence confidence for Iran transferability:** MODERATE. The structural parallels justify applying comparative insights, but the IRGC's ideological founding purpose and constitutional embedding represent a difference in kind, not just degree, that substantially limits the transferability of specific mechanisms. Lessons about sequencing and the necessity of economic decoupling are more transferable than specific institutional models.

## Key Research Questions

1. **What internal IRGC reform currents exist, and what conditions have historically enabled or suppressed them?** The epistemic community literature on Indonesia suggests that internal reform coalitions within the military institution are crucial. For Iran, we lack systematic evidence on the nature, scope, and political constraints facing reform-oriented IRGC officers or veterans. Understanding these currents would help calibrate the feasibility of an Indonesia-style internal coalition approach.

2. **How has IRGC economic divestment been attempted, and what has blocked or enabled it?** Iran's reformist governments (notably under Rouhani, 2013–2021) made partial efforts to limit IRGC economic dominance through procurement reform and sanctions-related pressure. Systematic analysis of these attempts — their mechanisms, how IRGC resistance manifested, and what partial successes were achieved — would provide evidence for a tailored divestment strategy in a transition scenario.

3. **What substitute functions could civilian institutions credibly perform if IRGC political and economic roles were contracted?** The Polish and Indonesian cases suggest that civilian control requires not just removing military power but filling the institutional space with capable civilian alternatives. For Iran, this raises specific questions about civilian ministry capacity, legislative defense oversight, and anti-corruption mechanisms that could absorb IRGC functions without institutional vacuum.

## Iran-Specific Implications

### Institutional Mapping: IRGC vs. TNI and Polish Military

The IRGC's institutional position in Iran's political economy requires a three-layer mapping to extract applicable lessons from Poland and Indonesia:

**Layer 1 — Military function:** The IRGC's core military role (conventional and unconventional warfare, missile program, naval operations) is analogous to any professional military. Reform lessons about command structure subordination, civilian defense ministry authority, and legislative budget oversight apply here with moderate confidence.

**Layer 2 — Political function:** The IRGC's placement of senior officers in executive, legislative, and judicial positions, and its role in selecting political candidates through the Guardian Council process, resembles *dwifungsi* in form but exceeds it in constitutional embedding. Indonesia's experience suggests formal abolition of this function is achievable through legislative process, but Iran's constitution (which embeds clerical supreme leadership with IRGC loyalty as a core feature) creates a higher threshold. A constitutional convention or post-transition constitutional drafting process would be the appropriate mechanism — analogous to Poland's post-Communist constitutional revision.

**Layer 3 — Economic function:** The IRGC's economic empire through Khatam al-Anbiya and affiliated entities is the dimension least directly addressed by either the Polish or Indonesian comparisons. Indonesia's failure to adequately reform TNI business holdings is a cautionary signal: incomplete economic decoupling left the military with resources to contest civilian authority and potentially reverse political reforms. For Iran, IRGC economic divestment would need to be a parallel track to political reform, not a deferred objective. The Polish experience of bringing defense industrial holdings under civilian budget oversight offers a procedural model, but Poland's holdings were smaller and the NATO conditionality created binding pressure that Iran lacks.

### Transition Scenarios and Actor Dynamics

**Scenario 1 — Negotiated transition with IRGC as veto player:** In any transition that preserves significant IRGC institutional presence (an Indonesia-type path), the evidence predicts persistent informal power, reversal risk, and a ceiling on civilian supremacy. The Indonesia evidence counsels against assuming that a reformist civilian coalition can achieve durable results without either binding external leverage or a more fundamental transformation of IRGC institutional identity. A negotiated transition that leaves IRGC economic holdings intact is particularly vulnerable to reversal, as the economic base sustains the political capacity to resist further reform.

**Scenario 2 — Rapid transition with institutional displacement:** A faster transition that displaces IRGC leadership and restructures the institution faces the challenge of institutional vacuum: who performs the IRGC's legitimate defense functions during the reform period? Poland's experience suggests this transition is manageable if civilian alternative institutions (ministry of defense, parliamentary oversight committees, professional military command) are ready to receive authority. Iran's civilian institutional capacity in defense governance is, by most assessments, weaker than Poland's was in 1989, suggesting a more extended transition period and greater risk of institutional chaos.

**Scenario 3 — Internal reform through epistemic community:** Indonesia's epistemic community model — reform-minded IRGC officers, civilian defense academics (many in diaspora), international technical assistance — offers a feasible intermediate path. Evidence from Indonesia suggests this approach can produce substantial formal reform within a decade but is unlikely to produce durable civilian supremacy without supplementary mechanisms. For Iran, these supplementary mechanisms might include: international agreements with monitoring provisions (analogous to JCPOA but broader, covering governance structures), constitutional guarantees of civilian oversight ratified by referendum, and economic reform packages that provide IRGC veterans with civilian economic opportunities (reducing the institutional incentive to maintain business holdings).

### Key Actors and Sequencing Recommendations

1. **Civilian defense capacity building** must precede or accompany (not follow) IRGC role reduction. The institutional vacuum risk is severe.
2. **Economic divestment** of IRGC commercial holdings should be concurrent with political role reduction, not deferred.
3. **IRGC veteran reintegration** programming — civilian career pathways, economic opportunities — reduces the personal incentive for institutional resistance.
4. **International engagement architecture** (trade agreements, technical cooperation, potentially security frameworks) can provide partial substitutes for NATO-type conditionality — not binding in the same way, but sufficient to shift the institutional incentive calculation.
5. **Constitutional redesign** at a founding moment is the only mechanism likely to address Layer 2 (political function) durably; incremental legislative change is insufficient given the constitutional embedding of IRGC roles.

### Risks and Uncertainties

The most significant risk is the Indonesia pattern: a transition that achieves formal civilian supremacy but fails to transform IRGC institutional identity or eliminate its economic base, producing a system vulnerable to reversal when political conditions shift. The 25-year post-Suharto Indonesian experience — now facing renewed pressure to expand military roles in civilian governance — is a direct warning for Iran's transition planners.

Evidence confidence for specific policy prescriptions: LOW to MODERATE. The comparative evidence supports general sequencing principles and identifies critical risk factors, but Iran's specific institutional context (theocratic constitutional structure, IRGC's revolutionary founding purpose, sanction-isolated economy) introduces uncertainties that comparative evidence cannot fully resolve.

## Knowledge Gaps

### Evidence Gaps in the Comparative Literature

**On Indonesia:**
The existing scholarship documents the *fact* of incomplete reform and informal power persistence but provides limited systematic evidence on the causal mechanisms sustaining informal IRGC-analogous military power in Indonesia. How specifically do retired TNI officers maintain influence in civilian bureaucracies? What are the economic flows that sustain military business networks beyond military budgets? More granular evidence on these mechanisms would improve the transferability of lessons for Iran.

The 2025 TNI Law controversy is currently underway, and medium-term evidence on its outcome — whether civil society resistance succeeded in blocking expanded military roles, or whether the reform reversal advanced — is not yet available. This gap is significant for assessing the durability of Indonesia's civilian supremacy model.

**On Poland:**
The scholarship on Poland's civil-military reform is relatively robust for the 1989–2006 period, but there is a gap in evidence about the mechanisms sustaining civilian supremacy during periods of political stress — notably Poland's post-2015 government, which pursued security sector politicization in some domains. Evidence on whether Poland's institutional architecture proved resilient or vulnerable under democratic backsliding would test the durability claims.

**On Iran directly:**
The most significant knowledge gap for applying these lessons is the absence of systematic evidence on: (1) the internal structure of IRGC reform currents and the conditions suppressing or enabling them; (2) the actual scale and structure of IRGC economic holdings and their interdependencies with the civilian economy; (3) historical reform attempts under Islamic Republic governments and the mechanisms of IRGC resistance. Without this Iran-specific evidence, the comparative lessons remain at the level of general structural principles rather than actionable transition plans.

### Methodological Caveats

This analysis applies Realist Synthesis across two non-Iranian cases. The CMO configuration identifies structural analogies but cannot account for Iran's theocratic constitutional framework, which embeds military loyalty to clerical authority in a way without direct parallel in either Poland or Indonesia. The comparative evidence provides orientation, not a roadmap. Transition planning would require Iran-specific empirical groundwork that falls outside the scope of this comparative synthesis.

Poland and Indonesia took opposite approaches to reforming their militaries after authoritarian rule — Poland anchored reform to NATO accession requirements, forcing institutional change to meet external standards; Indonesia reformed largely on its own terms after Suharto, producing incomplete results that left the military with extensive business empires and informal political influence. For Iran, neither model is cleanly applicable: the IRGC is not just a military force but an economic empire and intelligence apparatus intertwined with the entire state structure. The key insight is that without either an external anchor (Iran has no NATO equivalent) or sustained domestic political will (which requires a civilian government with security guarantees), IRGC reform will remain superficial.
