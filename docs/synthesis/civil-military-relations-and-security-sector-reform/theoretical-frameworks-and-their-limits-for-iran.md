# Theoretical Frameworks and Their Limits for Iran

**Status:** 🔍 AI Reviewed | **Last Updated:** 2026-03-29 06:42:53

Huntington's The Soldier and the State (1957) and his 1995 essay provide the dominant theoretical vocabulary in civil-military relations. His concept of "objective civilian control" — professional military autonomy in operational matters combined with civilian authority over policy — and the tortoise strategy of gradual reform have been widely applied. Kuehn and Croissant's (2023) six-case comparison broadly validates this approach: in South Korea, Chile, and Spain, incremental reform achieved more durable results than dramatic confrontation. Kuehn (2008) reinforces this through the Taiwan case, showing how elected civilians gradually curbed military political power and widened influence over former exclusively military prerogatives. These frameworks, while powerful, emerged from contexts fundamentally different from Iran's institutional landscape.

Makara's (2013) coup-proofing framework identifies three mechanisms — parallel institutions, material incentives, communal ties — and tests them against the Arab Spring. Croissant (2011) adds the structural-agential distinction: formal institutional design alone does not determine outcomes; the agency of specific political actors within those structures matters equally. Together these frameworks provide analytical tools for understanding transition dynamics, but each assumes a relatively clean separation between military and civilian domains that does not exist in Iran.

The MIT Security Studies Program's reassessment of Huntington identifies a "paradox of professionalism" — greater military autonomy paradoxically decreases civilian oversight. For conventional militaries, this means professionalization can undermine civilian control. But for Iran, the paradox is inverted: the IRGC's lack of professional military autonomy (it is a political body) means that introducing Western-style professionalism would actually increase civilian control — but only if the ideological infrastructure is simultaneously reformed.

The overarching pattern is theoretical insufficiency. All existing frameworks assume one or more conditions that Iran violates. Huntington assumes a clear military-civilian distinction; the IRGC operates across military, economic, intelligence, political, and ideological domains simultaneously. Makara assumes that coup-proofing is primarily a regime survival strategy; for Iran, the parallel structure is also an economic and ideological infrastructure. Professionalization theory assumes apolitical military norms; the IRGC's professionalism is defined by ideological loyalty rooted in velayat-e faqih doctrine and seminary-linked training. The convergence of these theoretical gaps suggests that Iran may require an entirely new analytical vocabulary — one that integrates security sector reform, economic restructuring, and ideological de-institutionalization as interdependent components of a single challenge.

Iran's IRGC defies every existing theoretical category. It is not a "military" in Huntington's sense because its sphere is governance itself. It is not merely a "coup-proofing" institution in Makara's sense because it generates autonomous revenue. It is not an "unprofessional" military in Ouedraogo's sense because its officers are trained to high operational standards — but trained for regime loyalty, not apolitical service. The velayat-e faqih doctrine means that the IRGC's political role is not an aberration to be corrected but a foundational feature designed into the institution from its creation.

This means any transition framework for Iran must simultaneously address what other countries tackle sequentially: removing political control (as South Korea did with the Hanahoe), restructuring economic interests (as Indonesia attempted with TNI divestment), reforming ideological training (as Spain did with democratic socialization), and building external security frameworks (as Spain did with NATO). The seminary system that provides ideological legitimacy for the security apparatus would need to be engaged as a reform partner or counterpart — a challenge without precedent in the comparative civil-military literature.

The practical implication is that transition planners cannot import any single country's model. They need a composite framework drawing on South Korea's prosecutorial approach, Spain's democratic socialization methods, Indonesia's (failed) divestment timeline as a cautionary benchmark, and entirely new mechanisms for the ideological dimension that no existing case addresses.

- Can a new theoretical framework for military-economic-ideological conglomerates be developed that integrates insights from civil-military relations, political economy, and sociology of religion?
- How would "democratic socialization" of the officer corps work when the existing "professionalism" is explicitly ideological, rooted in velayat-e faqih and seminary-linked training?
- What role could the Shi'a marja'iyya system — with its tradition of scholarly independence from state power — play as a counterweight to IRGC ideological claims during a transition?
