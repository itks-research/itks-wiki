## Theoretical Framework

Realist Synthesis CMO Framework for Decentralization. Context: Both Indonesia and Iran are large, ethnically diverse states with histories of centralized authoritarian governance. Indonesia's New Order under Suharto (1966-1998) maintained centralized control over a vast archipelago of 300+ ethnic groups. Iran's Islamic Republic concentrates power in Tehran through the velayat-e faqih system, governing 31 provinces with significant ethnic diversity (Persian, Azeri, Kurdish, Baluch, Arab, Turkmen). Both states face centrifugal pressures from peripheral regions demanding greater autonomy. Mechanism: Rapid ('big bang') decentralization can manage centrifugal pressures through three channels: (1) fiscal decentralization transfers revenue authority to subnational units, creating local stakeholders in system stability; (2) administrative decentralization delegates service delivery, reducing grievances from perceived neglect; (3) political decentralization creates local democratic accountability, channeling separatist energy into electoral competition [Source 1624, 1625]. Outcome: Indonesia's decentralization (Laws 22/1999 and 25/1999) transferred substantial authority to districts, with mixed results—improved local service delivery but also elite capture, corruption, and uneven capacity [Source 1637]. Evidence certainty: Moderate for Indonesian outcomes, Limited for Iran transferability.

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Indonesia's 'big bang' decentralization following Suharto's fall in 1998 constitutes one of the most rapid and comprehensive devolution experiments in modern history. Laws 22/1999 and 25/1999 transferred authority over most public services to over 400 districts and municipalities, accompanied by fiscal transfers constituting approximately 30% of central government revenue [Source 1624]. Economic performance evidence is mixed: Decentralization and Economic Performance in Indonesia finds positive correlations between fiscal decentralization and district-level GDP growth in resource-rich areas, but neutral or negative effects in resource-poor regions [Source 1642]. Administrative capacity varies dramatically: The Politics of Indonesia's Decentralization Law documents how regional competency frameworks remain unevenly implemented, with wealthy districts attracting qualified civil servants while remote areas face severe capacity gaps [Source 1637]. Indonesian Decentralization: Evaluation, Recent Movement and Future Perspectives provides longitudinal assessment showing poverty reduction gains in first decade (2001-2010) but convergence stalling after 2010, suggesting diminishing returns without institutional deepening [Source 1625]. The evaluation of regional autonomy implementation identifies elite capture as persistent challenge—local political dynasties emerged in many districts, converting decentralized authority into personal patronage [Source 1664]. Fiscal autonomy data shows local revenue generation capacity averaging only 10-15% of district budgets, with dependence on central transfers creating perverse incentives [Source 1676]. Moderate evidence suggests decentralization improved service access but did not automatically improve service quality or democratic accountability.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Cross-case patterns from Indonesia and comparable decentralization experiences reveal four mechanisms. First, speed of decentralization matters: Indonesia's rapid transfer ('big bang' within 2 years) created reform momentum but outpaced institutional capacity. Gradualist approaches (Spain's asymmetric autonomy, 1978-1995) allowed iterative learning but risked losing reform momentum. Moderate evidence suggests optimal pacing matches devolution speed to subnational capacity development [Source 1625]. Second, fiscal design determines outcomes: Unconditional block grants (Indonesia's DAU system) provide flexibility but risk fungibility and corruption. Conditional grants ensure sectoral spending but reduce local discretion. Indonesia's experience shows 75% of local budgets consumed by personnel costs, leaving minimal development spending [Source 1676]. Third, ethnic accommodation through territorial autonomy: Indonesia granted special autonomy to Aceh and Papua, recognizing distinct ethnic/religious identities through asymmetric arrangements. This reduced separatist violence in Aceh (post-Helsinki Agreement 2005) but showed mixed results in Papua. The pattern suggests territorial autonomy works best when accompanied by genuine revenue sharing and political inclusion [Source 1679]. Fourth, democratic accountability requires functioning local elections: Indonesia's direct local elections (pilkada, from 2005) improved accountability compared to appointed officials, but also increased campaign costs, encouraging corruption. Political decentralization without anti-corruption institutional parallel creates new patronage networks [Source 1623, 1664].

## Iran Context

> 🔴 **Evidence Confidence: Low** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources); contains explicit low-confidence language

Iran's centralized governance structure concentrates fiscal, administrative, and political authority in Tehran. Provincial governors are appointed by the Interior Ministry, local councils have advisory rather than legislative authority, and fiscal transfers are centrally determined without transparent formulae. Iran's ethnic geography—Azeris (approximately 16-25% of population) concentrated in northwest, Kurds in west, Baluch in southeast, Arabs in southwest—creates structural demand for autonomy that the Islamic Republic has managed through a combination of suppression, co-optation, and symbolic recognition without meaningful devolution. Indonesia's experience offers several transferable insights: First, fiscal decentralization could address provincial grievances over resource extraction without revenue return—Iran's oil-producing Khuzestan (majority Arab) and gas-rich South Pars region generate national wealth but receive disproportionately low development investment. Indonesia's revenue sharing formula (especially post-2004 revisions giving resource-producing districts larger shares) provides a template [Source 1642]. Second, administrative decentralization of education, healthcare, and infrastructure could improve service delivery in neglected peripheral regions, where development indicators lag significantly behind Tehran and Isfahan. Third, political decentralization through meaningful provincial elections could channel ethnic political energy into electoral competition rather than separatism. However, significant challenges distinguish Iran from Indonesia: (1) Iran's theocratic oversight (Guardian Council veto over candidates) would need reform to permit genuine local political competition; (2) IRGC economic interests in provincial economies complicate fiscal decentralization; (3) Kurdish and Baluch regions face security-first governance that precludes civilian administrative authority; (4) Iran's clerical establishment views centralization as religious obligation (velayat-e faqih implies unified Islamic governance). Limited evidence suggests selective asymmetric autonomy—granting enhanced authority to specific provinces—may be more feasible than comprehensive decentralization.

## Open Research Questions

(1) What fiscal decentralization formula would balance resource-producing provinces' demands with national equity? Could Indonesia's DAU/DAK system be adapted to Iran's petroleum economy? (2) How can administrative decentralization proceed in provinces where IRGC maintains parallel governance structures? (3) What asymmetric autonomy arrangements could accommodate Kurdish, Azeri, and Baluch demands without triggering separatist dynamics? (4) How would decentralization interact with Iran's clerical oversight institutions—can provincial autonomy coexist with Guardian Council authority? (5) What lessons from Indonesia's experience with local elite capture are relevant to Iran's provincial power dynamics? (6) How would decentralization affect Iran's ethnic minorities' political participation and representation?

## Implications for Iran

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

**Institutional Mapping**

Iran's centralization concentrates authority in institutions that would resist decentralization. The Ministry of Interior (وزارت کشور) appoints all 31 provincial governors, maintaining Tehran's administrative control. The Plan and Budget Organization (سازمان برنامه و بودجه) determines fiscal allocation to provinces without transparent formulae. The IRGC maintains separate security command structures in every province, with particularly heavy presence in minority regions — the Hamzeh Seyyed al-Shohada Corps in Kurdistan, the Quds Base in Sistan-Baluchestan, and IRGC Ground Forces commands in Khuzestan and West Azerbaijan. Provincial Islamic Councils (شوراهای اسلامی استان) exist constitutionally but exercise advisory rather than legislative authority. The Guardian Council (شورای نگهبان) would need to approve any constitutional amendments enabling genuine devolution. The Friday Prayer Imams (ائمه جمعه), appointed by the Supreme Leader in every provincial capital, function as regime representatives embedding central authority in local religious life.

**Structural Parallels**

Indonesia's big bang decentralization provides the most relevant structural parallel for Iran. Both countries face ethnic diversity (Iran: Azeri, Kurdish, Arab, Baluch, Turkmen; Indonesia: hundreds of ethnic groups), resource inequality between regions, and a history of centralized authoritarian governance that suppressed ethnic expression. Indonesia's experience shows both promise and limitation: fiscal decentralization improved resource-producing regions' development outcomes but created perverse incentives in transfer-dependent districts. Administrative capacity varied dramatically — wealthy districts attracted talent while remote areas faced severe gaps. Iran would face similar disparities between Tehran/Isfahan and peripheral provinces. The elite capture problem documented in Indonesia's decentralization — local political dynasties converting devolved authority into personal patronage — is directly relevant: provincial IRGC commanders, Friday Prayer Imams, and local bazaari elites could transition from central regime representatives to autonomous power brokers. The parallel breaks down on security dimensions: Indonesia's separatist pressures (Aceh, Papua) were geographically contained; Iran's ethnic minorities have cross-border dimensions that amplify centrifugal risks.

**Key Actors and Sequencing**

Stakeholders demanding decentralization: Azeri cultural movements (Turkey-oriented northwest, approximately 16-25% of national population); Kurdish political organizations with decades of autonomy demands; Arab activists in Khuzestan demanding resource revenue return; Baluch communities in Sistan-Baluchestan combining ethnic and Sunni religious grievances; and reform-oriented technocrats who view decentralization as efficiency improvement. Blockers: the IRGC's provincial security presence; the clerical establishment viewing centralization as doctrinal requirement of ولایت فقیه; Persian nationalist constituencies viewing devolution as dismemberment; and Tehran-based economic interests benefiting from centralized fiscal allocation. Indonesia's lesson suggests asymmetric approaches — enhanced autonomy for specifically mobilized regions — are more feasible than comprehensive decentralization. Sequencing: (1) transparent fiscal transfer formula replacing discretionary allocation — the least politically threatening reform with highest immediate impact; (2) elected provincial governors replacing Tehran appointments; (3) legislative authority for provincial councils over education, healthcare, and local governance; (4) resource revenue sharing — oil-producing Khuzestan, gas-rich South Pars, and mineral-rich provinces retaining a defined share of extraction revenues; (5) asymmetric autonomy arrangements for Kurdistan, Azerbaijan, Khuzestan, and Sistan-Baluchestan; (6) constitutional protections against unilateral autonomy revocation.

**Risks**

Indonesia's experience reveals Iran's central risk: decentralization without adequate institutional capacity produces local elite capture rather than democratic governance. Provincial IRGC commanders could become regional strongmen; Friday Prayer Imams could leverage religious authority for political control; and bazaari networks could capture economic governance. A second risk is fiscal dependency: if most provinces cannot generate significant own-source revenue (Indonesia averaged only 10-15% local revenue), dependence on central transfers would perpetuate Tehran's leverage. A third risk is separatism: Kurdish autonomy perceived as insufficient could fuel independence demands linked to broader Kurdish national aspirations across the region. Prerequisites: administrative capacity building in provincial governments before authority transfer; anti-corruption mechanisms preventing elite capture; graduated timeline — fiscal transparency first, then administrative devolution, then political autonomy; security sector reform reducing IRGC provincial dominance before civilian decentralization; and regional diplomacy ensuring neighbors (Turkey, Iraq, Pakistan, Azerbaijan) support Iran's internal restructuring.
