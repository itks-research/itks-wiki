This synthesis examines pathways for productive brain return (diaspora repatriation) in post-transition Iran, using a Realist Synthesis (CMO: Context-Mechanism-Outcome) framework. Iran hosts one of the world's largest educated diasporas (4-5 million), concentrated in STEM, medicine, and academia across North America and Europe. The synthesis integrates comparative evidence from South Korea, China, Afghanistan, and Israel to identify critical mechanisms enabling successful repatriation: political trust and security guarantees, institutional bridges (diaspora ministries, professional networks), dual engagement models (remote contribution before return), and generational difference in motivations. Key findings highlight that economic incentives alone are insufficient without political liberalization and institutional trust. The analysis identifies unique features of the Iranian diaspora including politicization, Women Life Freedom solidarity, and active Persian-language digital spheres, alongside regime-specific risks (surveillance, property disputes, political instrumentalization) and opportunities (human capital, investment capital, democratic culture transfer).
