# Spain's Consenso Model: Inclusive Drafting and the Price of Ambiguity

**Status:** 🤖 Machine Generated | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-06

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

Spain's 1978 Constitution is the longest-surviving product of the "third wave" of democratization, and its drafting process is the most frequently cited model of consensus constitution-making. The seven-member ponencia (drafting commission) included representatives from the governing UCD, the PSOE, the Catalan CiU, the Communist PCE, and the conservative AP, spanning the political spectrum from former Francoists to former underground communists. Their deliberations across more than 100 sessions between July 1977 and March 1978 produced a text that no party fully endorsed but all could accept.

The consenso approach rested on three pillars. First, procedural equality: all major parties had drafting representation regardless of their electoral weight, preventing majoritarian imposition. Second, substantive ambiguity: on issues where consensus was impossible (the unity-autonomy tension, the role of the monarchy, church-state relations), the drafters chose deliberately vague language that could accommodate multiple interpretations. Third, external anchoring: the prospect of European Community membership provided an external reference point and institutional constraint that disciplined all parties toward democratic norms.

Article 2 of the 1978 Constitution contains the most consequential example of creative ambiguity, simultaneously guaranteeing "the indissoluble unity of the Spanish Nation" and "the right to autonomy of the nationalities and regions." This formulation enabled the initial transition by deferring resolution of Spain's fundamental territorial question, but it also planted the seed of the Catalan crisis that would emerge decades later.

The consenso model's emphasis on constitutional ambiguity as a precondition for agreement finds parallels in other post-transition constitutional moments. Boryslavska (2021) examines how judicial reform in post-communist democracies emerged from constitutional agreements that deliberately left structural arrangements ambiguous to facilitate multi-party consensus. Martin (2011) documents the Iranian Constitutional debates of 1906-1907, where drafters similarly embraced constitutional vagueness regarding state powers to accommodate conflicting ideological factions. Randjbar-Daemi (2013) shows how the 1979 Iranian Constitution preserved competing conceptions of religious and secular authority within a single framework, generating decades of interpretive conflict. These comparative cases suggest that consenso's ambiguity-as-feature strategy reflects a broader pattern in constitutional engineering where competing blocs accept incomplete settlements.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

The fault line emerged where ambiguity was greatest. Colino (2008) traces how bottom-up revision of subnational constitutional arrangements, particularly Catalonia's 2006 Statute of Autonomy reform, tested the limits of the 1978 framework. The Constitutional Court's 2010 ruling striking down portions of the Catalan Statute precipitated the secessionist crisis that culminated in the 2017 unilateral declaration of independence. Rico and Lineira (2014) document how secessionism moved from fringe to mainstream in the 2012 Catalan election.

The pattern reveals a fundamental tradeoff in constitutional design: creative ambiguity can enable transitions by deferring unresolvable conflicts, but the deferred conflicts do not disappear. They re-emerge when political actors push the ambiguous language beyond its elastic limits. The critical variable is whether the constitutional framework includes institutions capable of managing these deferred tensions when they surface: empowered courts, formal intergovernmental forums, and clear procedures for resolving center-periphery conflicts.

Two competing patterns emerge from this comparative analysis. First, constitutional ambiguity can facilitate consensus but generates downstream conflicts requiring authoritative resolution. Spain, Iran 1906, and Iran 1979 all illustrate how fuzzy constitutional language defers rather than resolves fundamental disputes (Martin 2011, Randjbar-Daemi 2013). Second, constitutional designers in fragmented polities face a choice: explicit conflict (impossible consensus) versus deferred conflict (ambiguous consensus). Boryslavska (2021) shows that post-1989 Eastern European democracies often chose earlier, more adversarial constitution-making processes that eliminated ambiguity through struggle rather than agreement.

## Iran Context

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

Spain demonstrates both the power and the limits of creative ambiguity for Iran's constitutional design. Iran's ethnic and linguistic diversity (Persian, Azeri, Kurdish, Baloch, Arab, Turkmen, Lor) maps onto territorial claims in ways that parallel Spain's Catalan question. The Spanish model suggests that deliberate ambiguity on the unitary-federal spectrum could enable initial consensus in Iran, allowing different groups to read their preferred outcomes into a shared text.

However, Spain's subsequent crisis counsels that any ambiguity must be accompanied by robust dispute resolution mechanisms. An empowered constitutional court, formal intergovernmental councils, and clear procedures for resolving center-periphery conflicts must be built into the original design. Iran's current constitution uses the Guardian Council (Shora-ye Negahban) as a dispute resolution mechanism, but its approbatory supervision function has made it an instrument of power concentration rather than neutral arbitration. A future Iranian constitutional court must differ fundamentally from the Guardian Council: its members must be selected through a process independent of any single authority, its jurisdiction must extend to disputes between the center and regions, and its decisions must be binding and enforceable.

The Iranian constitutional tradition exemplifies both the promise and peril of consenso ambiguity. Martin (2011) documents how the 1906 Constitution deliberately obscured the boundaries between secular and religious authority, allowing clerical and secular modernizers to ratify the same document while possessing radically different visions. The 1979 Constitution repeated this pattern: Randjbar-Daemi (2013) shows how ambiguous formulations regarding the Supreme Leader's powers and the Islamic Republic's democratic commitments were essential to securing ratification from competing revolutionary factions. Yet both constitutional moments generated ongoing disputes over constitutional meaning. If Iran pursues consenso-style constitution-making, policymakers must recognize that accepting current ambiguities about institutional relationships might replicate rather than resolve the fundamental disputes that have destabilized successive constitutional orders.

## Implications for Iran

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

**Institutional Mapping:** Spain's consenso model maps onto Iran's constitutional challenge through the lens of managed ambiguity across contested institutional domains. The ponencia's seven-member cross-spectrum representation would need Iranian equivalents: representatives of اصلاح‌طلبان (reformists), اصولگرایان (principalists/pragmatic conservatives willing to negotiate), secular democratic forces, ethnic community representatives (Kurdish, Azeri, Baloch, Arab, Turkmen), religious minority representatives, women's movement representatives, and military/security establishment representatives willing to participate. The institutions whose futures require negotiation include: the Guardian Council (abolition vs. replacement), the حوزه system (autonomy framework), the judiciary (independence mechanisms), regional governance (unitary vs. federal or asymmetric arrangements), and the IRGC (dissolution, transformation, or integration).

**Structural Parallels:** Spain's Article 2 ambiguity — simultaneously guaranteeing "indissoluble unity" and "the right to autonomy of nationalities and regions" — maps precisely onto Iran's territorial question. Iran's ethnic and linguistic diversity (Persians approximately 50-65%, Azeris 15-20%, Kurds 7-10%, Lurs 6%, Baloch 2%, Arabs 2%, Turkmen 2%, and others) creates the same tension between national unity and regional autonomy that Spain deferred through creative ambiguity. The Catalan crisis demonstrates the long-term cost: ambiguity enabled initial consensus but generated a constitutional crisis decades later when the deferred question demanded resolution. Iran's constitutional history confirms the pattern: Martin (2011) documents how the 1906 Constitution deliberately obscured boundaries between secular and religious authority; Randjbar-Daemi (2013) shows how the 1979 Constitution preserved competing conceptions within a single framework. Both generated decades of interpretive conflict. A third Iranian constitutional moment that repeats the ambiguity strategy risks reproducing the same pattern.

**Key Actors and Sequencing:** Spain's three pillars of consenso offer operational guidance. (1) Procedural equality: all major constituencies must have drafting representation regardless of electoral weight — this means ensuring that Kurdish, Baloch, and other ethnic minorities are represented proportionally to their political claims, not just their population share. (2) Substantive ambiguity — but paired with dispute resolution: where consensus on the religion-state relationship, the territorial structure, or the military's role is impossible, deliberately vague formulations may be necessary — but unlike Spain, Iran must simultaneously establish robust dispute resolution mechanisms (empowered constitutional court, formal intergovernmental councils, clear center-periphery arbitration procedures). (3) External anchoring: Spain had European Community membership as a disciplining framework; Iran would need equivalent external institutional commitments — whether regional security arrangements, international economic integration frameworks, or human rights treaty obligations — that constrain all parties toward democratic norms.

**Risks:** The consenso model's price is that ambiguity defers rather than resolves fundamental disputes. For Iran, three specific ambiguities carry the highest long-term risk: (1) The religion-state relationship — any formulation vague enough to satisfy both secular and religious constituencies will generate interpretive conflict when specific legislation tests the boundaries. (2) The territorial structure — language that accommodates both centralist and federalist readings will produce a Catalan-style crisis when a region (most likely Kurdistan) tests the boundaries of "autonomy." (3) The military's role — ambiguity about the IRGC's future that satisfies both abolitionists and institutionalists will generate crisis when reform implementation begins. The Iranian constitutional tradition's repeated use of ambiguity (1906, 1979) suggests a structural pattern: Iranian constitutional moments produce consensus through vagueness that subsequently destabilizes governance. If Iran pursues consenso-style constitution-making, the paramount prerequisite is pairing every deliberate ambiguity with a specific dispute resolution mechanism — ensuring that the next generation has institutional tools for resolving what the founding generation could not agree on.

## Knowledge Gaps

**The conditions under which ambiguity is sustainable remain underspecified.** Spain's Catalan crisis demonstrates that creative ambiguity eventually generates conflict. However, the literature does not systematically identify the conditions under which deferred tensions remain manageable versus escalate — whether the critical variable is dispute resolution capacity, economic grievances, or exit options for disaffected groups. Without this specification, the prescription (pair ambiguity with dispute resolution mechanisms) cannot be calibrated for Iran's specific context.

**Iran's 1906 and 1979 constitutional parallels are invoked but not causally analyzed.** The article draws on Martin (2011) and Randjbar-Daemi (2013) to establish a pattern of Iranian constitutional ambiguity generating conflict, but does not assess whether the mechanisms in those cases are analogous to Spain's or reflect specifically Iranian dynamics (clerical mobilization, foreign intervention, revolutionary coalitional fragility) that the consenso model does not capture.

**Ethnic grievance depth and federalism demand are empirically uncharted.** The article draws the Spain-Iran territorial parallel but does not engage survey or ethnographic evidence on the depth of federalism demand among Kurdish, Azeri, Baloch, and Arab communities, or whether territorial claims are negotiable versus existential. This directly affects whether consenso ambiguity could work or whether explicit commitments are required.

**Dispute resolution institution design evidence is thin.** The article recommends empowered constitutional courts and formal intergovernmental councils as necessary complements to ambiguity, but does not engage comparative evidence on which specific design features have made such institutions effective in managing center-periphery tensions in comparable multi-ethnic post-authoritarian states.

**The external anchoring mechanism is underspecified for Iran.** Spain's EU membership prospect provided a disciplining framework; the article notes Iran would need equivalent commitments but does not assess what realistic external anchoring arrangements are available given Iran's geopolitical isolation and sanctions architecture.

Spain's experience writing its constitution after decades of dictatorship offers a powerful and cautionary lesson: to get agreement across deeply divided political camps, the drafters deliberately used vague language on the most contested questions — leaving the role of religion, the unity of the country, and the rights of regions open to different interpretations. This worked well enough to produce a stable democracy for decades, but the unresolved tension over Catalonia's status eventually exploded into a serious constitutional crisis. For Iran, which has its own long history of using constitutional ambiguity to paper over fundamental disagreements, the Spain lesson is that vagueness can buy time but must always be paired with strong mechanisms for resolving disputes when they inevitably resurface.
