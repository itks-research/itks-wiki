# From Autarky to Integration: FDI Policy Lessons from South Korea and Czech Republic for Post-Sanctions Iran

**Status:** 🔍 AI Reviewed | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-06

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

South Korea's remarkable transformation from autarky to a globally integrated economy offers critical lessons for understanding how FDI can drive development under developmental state models. During the 1960s-1980s, South Korea strategically managed FDI inflows to serve state-directed industrialization goals, rather than allowing unfettered market entry. The government leveraged FDI to acquire technology and management practices while maintaining substantial domestic control through joint venture requirements and sectoral restrictions. Importantly, South Korea combined selective FDI openness with massive public investment in human capital and infrastructure—prerequisites that foreign investors could productively engage with. The government identified priority sectors (electronics, automobiles, petrochemicals) and channeled FDI accordingly, creating industrial ecosystems where multinational firms complemented domestic competitors rather than displacing them. By the 1990s, as institutional capacity matured and local firms gained technological sophistication, South Korea progressively liberalized FDI rules, demonstrating that sequencing—not simultaneous comprehensive liberalization—enabled sustainable development.

Czech Republic's post-1989 transition reveals how institutional credibility and legal frameworks attract FDI in middle-income contexts with institutional uncertainty. After communism's collapse, Czech policymakers prioritized establishing transparent property rights, rule of law, and macroeconomic stability to signal FDI safety to multinational firms hesitant about Central European risk. The government offered tax incentives and designated industrial zones, but these proved secondary to credible legal institutions. Czech FDI surged particularly in manufacturing sectors (automobiles, electronics) where long-term capital commitments required confidence in contract enforcement. Critically, FDI flows accelerated precisely when institutional reforms—independent judiciary, corporate law harmonization with EU standards, financial sector regulation—demonstrated irreversibility. Czech experience indicates that FDI responds not only to incentive structures but to institutional signals of policy durability. Sectoral FDI concentration in manufacturing also reflected Czech comparative advantage and existing infrastructure legacies, showing that FDI patterns reflect both institutional openness and structural economic conditions.

Comparing these cases reveals that successful FDI attraction depends on sequencing institutional reforms before comprehensive liberalization. Both South Korea and Czech Republic invested heavily in complementary state capacity—South Korea in industrial planning and human capital, Czech Republic in legal institutions and regulatory credibility—before opening to market forces. Neither country relied on FDI as a substitute for state capability; rather, both deployed FDI as a tool within coherent development strategies. The sustainability of FDI-driven growth appears contingent on ensuring that inflows generate domestic technological spillovers and industrial deepening, not merely serve as extraction enclaves. Both cases also demonstrate that FDI policy must be sensitive to sequential institutional constraints: premature comprehensive liberalization without institutional foundations leads to volatile capital flows and limited developmental benefits, whereas strategic selectivity paired with demonstrable institutional commitment sustains productive FDI partnerships.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

FDI policy sequencing across successful cases reveals three consistent patterns. First, institutional prerequisites precede liberalization: both South Korea and Czech Republic established effective governance, rule of law, and property right protections before expecting sustained FDI flows. This institutional-first sequencing differs sharply from Washington Consensus recommendations for immediate comprehensive liberalization. Second, FDI policy selectivity combines with sectoral targeting to maximize developmental spillovers. South Korea's joint venture requirements and sector prioritization ensured FDI complemented rather than dominated domestic firms. Czech Republic's industrial zone designation concentrated FDI in manufacturing where backward linkages to local suppliers generated broad-based growth. Third, trade openness and democratic transition appear interdependent rather than sequential: South Korea maintained substantial state direction even as it liberalized trade; Czech Republic linked trade openness to EU accession conditionality and democratic institutions. The relationship is not unidirectional—neither country's trade liberalization preceded institutional reform, nor did democracy precede FDI attraction. Instead, FDI flows appear most sustainable when coupled with credible institutional signaling and embedded within coherent industrial policy frameworks that prioritize developmental spillovers.

## Iran Context

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Iran's prospects for attracting productive FDI face distinct structural obstacles rooted in sanctions legacies, institutional fragmentation, and sectoral control patterns that differ markedly from South Korea and Czech Republic precedents. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) control of key upstream sectors—oil and gas, petrochemicals, telecommunications, construction—creates a foundational institutional problem absent in comparative cases. While South Korea's state directed development, state ownership remained subordinate to developmental objectives pursued through meritocratic technocratic institutions; and Czech Republic's state retained limited direct production roles. In contrast, IRGC control combines political authority with direct commercial interest, fragmenting state capacity and deterring foreign investors uncertain about contract enforcement or profit repatriation in politically-controlled sectors. The Iran nuclear sanctions regime (2006-2015 UNSC resolutions, further intensified 2018) created comprehensive capital flight incentives and institutional distrust among both foreign firms and Iranian private capital holders. Even JCPOA agreement (2015) and sanctions relief proved insufficient to restore FDI inflows because investors perceived lingering uncertainty about sanctions reversal—a rational calculation validated by 2018 U.S. withdrawal.

Institutional reform prerequisites for Iranian FDI attraction substantially exceed those faced by South Korea or Czech Republic. Iran requires legal reforms establishing independent contract enforcement, transparent bankruptcy procedures, and currency convertibility guarantees—all areas where political economy constraints operate through IRGC-controlled security apparatus. Sectoral access restrictions prevent foreign investors from engaging Iran's most productive opportunities (upstream oil/gas) while channeling them toward less productive downstream sectors or resource-constrained manufacturing. Sanctions have degraded institutional capacity in central banking and financial regulation, creating technical barriers to capital inflows even if political willingness emerged. Critically, Iran's experience with JCPOA illustrates that sanctions relief alone cannot overcome deep institutional deficits: despite formal sanctions relief, FDI flows remained minimal because investors perceived political risks rooted in institutional fragmentation and sectoral control patterns. Productive FDI attraction would require visible movement toward institutional pluralism, civilian control of key sectors, and demonstrable policy durability extending beyond electoral cycles—reforms whose political costs exceed those faced by South Korea or Czech Republic.

## Open Research Questions

Key research gaps limit understanding of FDI attraction under sanctions legacy conditions. First, how do sectoral control by politically-aligned state enterprises interact with FDI policy frameworks to shape investor behavior? South Korea and Czech Republic experience offer limited guidance because neither faced comparable institutional fragmentation between state development objectives and direct political authority in commercial sectors. Second, what institutional signals would credibly demonstrate FDI policy durability and sectoral access in contexts where past agreements (like JCPOA) have been reversed? The Czech case benefits from EU conditionality as external enforcement mechanism; Iran lacks comparable institutional anchors. Third, do comparative development trajectories suggest that functional FDI attraction in Iran might require sectoral limitation to downstream petrochemicals, manufacturing, and services rather than upstream hydrocarbon sectors? This would invert South Korea's upstream-first manufacturing strategy and demand theoretical reconceptualization of sectoral sequencing in resource-dependent economies.

## Implications for Iran

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

**Institutional Mapping:** FDI attraction in Iran is structurally blocked by the **IRGC's control of upstream sectors** — oil and gas, petrochemicals, telecommunications, and construction — which combines political authority with direct commercial interest, deterring foreign investors uncertain about contract enforcement or profit repatriation. The **Central Bank of Iran** (بانک مرکزی) lacks the independence and institutional credibility required to guarantee currency convertibility. The **Ministry of Economic Affairs and Finance** (وزارت امور اقتصادی و دارایی) and the **Organization for Investment, Economic, and Technical Assistance of Iran** (سازمان سرمایه‌گذاری) nominally manage FDI policy but lack authority over IRGC-controlled sectors. The **Guardian Council** and **Expediency Council** have blocked FATF compliance legislation necessary for financial integration. The **judiciary** lacks the independent contract enforcement and transparent bankruptcy procedures that FDI requires.

**Structural Parallels:** South Korea's developmental state model — strategically managing FDI inflows through joint venture requirements and sectoral targeting while investing massively in human capital and infrastructure — offers an aspirational framework but presupposes meritocratic technocratic institutions subordinating state ownership to developmental objectives. Iran's state economic control serves rent-seeking rather than developmental purposes, as Amineh's China-Iran comparison demonstrates. Czech Republic's experience shows that FDI responds not merely to incentive structures but to institutional signals of policy durability — precisely the credibility Iran destroyed when the JCPOA collapsed. The Czech pattern of FDI surging only when institutional reforms demonstrated irreversibility carries a direct lesson: Iran cannot attract productive FDI through tax incentives alone without credible legal institutions, independent judiciary, and policy durability extending beyond electoral or regime cycles.

**Key Actors and Sequencing:** The **IRGC' economic apparatus** is the fundamental blocker — its control of the most productive sectors (upstream energy, telecoms, infrastructure) prevents foreign investors from engaging Iran's highest-value opportunities while channeling them toward less productive downstream sectors. The **Supreme Leader's office** controls appointments across all institutions that FDI policy engages. Potential enablers include the **private sector** (بخش خصوصی), particularly technology firms and manufacturers who would benefit from integration into global supply chains, and **diaspora entrepreneurs** possessing both capital and international business networks. Sequencing: (1) FATF compliance and financial system reform as prerequisites for capital account access; (2) establishment of independent contract enforcement, commercial courts, and transparent bankruptcy procedures; (3) civilian control of key economic sectors currently under IRGC authority; (4) sectoral FDI strategy targeting priority industries (manufacturing, technology, renewable energy) where Iranian comparative advantage and human capital align — drawing on South Korea's selective approach rather than wholesale liberalization; (5) special economic zones as institutional testing grounds for FDI governance, allowing demonstration effects before nationwide reform.

**Unique Challenges:** Iran's JCPOA experience created a uniquely toxic precedent: international firms that entered the market during sanctions relief faced secondary sanctions after the US withdrawal, generating institutional distrust among precisely the investors Iran most needs. Rebuilding this trust requires not just diplomatic agreements but demonstrable domestic institutional reform. Iran's human capital — high educational attainment, large engineering workforce, diaspora networks — represents a genuine FDI attraction asset, but only if institutional reforms make it accessible. The sanctions-era import substitution economy has created industrial capacity (pharmaceuticals, auto manufacturing) that could attract partnership-oriented FDI if legal and regulatory frameworks evolve, but this capacity is concentrated in IRGC-connected entities, creating a circular dependency between economic reform and security sector transformation.

## What We Don't Know

- **Severe limitation of South Korea and Czech Republic as comparators for Iran's FDI context:** The article explicitly acknowledges that South Korea's developmental state model presupposed meritocratic technocratic institutions and the Czech Republic's FDI surge depended on EU accession conditionality — neither of which is available to Iran. The degree to which these precedents are analytically transferable to a post-sanctions, post-theocratic, IRGC-dominated economy is not fully resolved; the article identifies the gap but cannot close it with existing comparative evidence.

- **Absence of empirical evidence on investor behavior after a credibility-destroying precedent:** The JCPOA collapse created a specific form of institutional distrust — investors who entered the Iranian market faced US secondary sanctions after withdrawal — that has no direct parallel in the FDI literature. The conditions under which this particular form of investment-risk precedent can be overcome (through treaty architecture, institutional reform signals, or simply elapsed time) are not addressed by the evidence reviewed.

- **IRGC sectoral control as a FDI deterrent is theorized but not measured:** The article argues that IRGC control of upstream sectors — energy, telecoms, construction — fragments state capacity and deters foreign investors, but there is no systematic empirical measurement of the deterrence effect, the sectors in which it is most binding, or whether downstream FDI can achieve meaningful development impact in the absence of upstream liberalization.

- **Diaspora entrepreneur capital is asserted without empirical grounding:** The article identifies diaspora entrepreneurs as potential FDI enablers, but there is no rigorous estimate of their investable capital, what proportion would be directed toward Iran under different institutional scenarios, or the governance challenges of distinguishing diaspora investment from politically motivated capital flows.

- **Special economic zone (SEZ) effectiveness in post-authoritarian contexts is mixed:** The article recommends SEZs as institutional testing grounds, but comparative evidence is not uniformly positive — SEZs have in several cases become enclaves insulated from broader institutional reform rather than demonstration effects. The conditions under which the SEZ model generates positive spillovers versus enclave formation are not resolved for Iran's specific context.

This article examines how South Korea and the Czech Republic successfully attracted foreign investment during their economic transformations, and asks what Iran would need to do to achieve similar results after decades of sanctions. The central finding is that successful foreign investment is not primarily about offering tax incentives — it depends on credible legal institutions, independent courts, and a government whose economic commitments investors believe will survive the next political cycle. For Iran, the article argues that the Revolutionary Guards' control of the most productive sectors of the economy is the fundamental obstacle: foreign investors cannot enter Iran's best opportunities, and the institutional distrust generated by the collapse of the 2015 nuclear deal means that overcoming investor skepticism will require far more than diplomatic agreements alone.
