## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

The evidence spans Iran's historical urban development, contemporary regional poverty patterns, and international inclusive growth frameworks.

**Historical Roots: Company Towns and Authoritarian Modernization.** Ehsani (2003) provides the most analytically rich source, examining the oil company towns of Abadan and Masjed-Soleyman in Khuzestan — the first modern industrial towns in Iran and the Middle East, designed and constructed by the Anglo-Persian Oil Company (APOC) in the early twentieth century. The study reveals a foundational contradiction in Iran's urban development: these cities were simultaneously modern and authoritarian, designed for industrial efficiency rather than human flourishing. The APOC imposed strict spatial hierarchies segregating British managers, skilled workers, and Iranian laborers. Yet the heterogeneity and energy of the population, combined with the cities' unexpected growth beyond the company's control, produced what Ehsani calls 'conditional modernity' — urban cultures that were 'energetic and dynamic, but also eclectic and hybrid.' This historical analysis is significant because it demonstrates that Iran's urban inequality is not merely a contemporary phenomenon but a structural legacy of how industrial modernity was introduced: as a tool of extraction rather than development, creating cities designed to serve external economic interests rather than the populations who inhabited them. The Khuzestan case has direct parallels to contemporary resource-extraction urbanism across Iran.

**Contemporary Regional Inequality: Multidimensional Poverty.** Mahoozi (2015) applies Sen's Capabilities Approach to estimate the frequency and breadth of multidimensional poverty across three Iranian regions: Tehran, other urban areas, and rural areas. The study's most significant finding is that the proportion of rural areas experiencing multidimensional poverty increased between 1999 and 2007, despite relatively high GDP growth rates during this period. This decoupling of aggregate growth from poverty reduction directly challenges trickle-down development assumptions and confirms that Iran's economic structure systematically concentrates benefits in Tehran and major urban centers while leaving rural and peripheral urban areas behind. The study distinguishes specific deprivation types by socio-economic group, providing 'invaluable information for effective policy targeting' — suggesting that universal housing or cost-of-living policies would miss the mark; spatially targeted interventions are needed.

**Inclusive Growth Framework.** The World Economic Forum's Inclusive Growth and Development Report (2017) frames the broader challenge: the global consensus that growth models must be more inclusive remains 'more a discussion topic than an action agenda.' The report introduces a framework and performance indicators for inclusive growth that explicitly link stagnation with dispersion (low growth and rising inequality). Its relevance to Iran lies in the argument that stagnation and inequality are not separate problems but a vicious cycle — addressing one requires addressing the other. The report's emphasis on structural approaches over temporary monetary and fiscal measures aligns with the finding that Iran's GDP growth has not translated into poverty reduction (Mahoozi 2015), suggesting the growth model itself must change, not just its redistribution mechanisms.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

One central pattern emerges: **Iran's urban inequality is a product of growth models that concentrate benefits geographically, and this concentration has persisted across radically different political regimes.**

Ehsani's (2003) company town analysis shows that spatial inequality was built into Iran's modernization from its inception — the APOC designed Khuzestan's cities to extract oil, not to develop the region. Mahoozi's (2015) data demonstrate that this pattern persisted through the Islamic Revolution and into the 2000s, with rural poverty increasing despite aggregate growth. The Inclusive Growth Report (2017) provides the theoretical framework: this is not an Iran-specific failure but a structural feature of growth models that prioritize aggregate output over distributional outcomes.

The implication is that post-transition housing and urban policy cannot be designed in isolation from economic development strategy. Housing affordability, cost of living, and urban inequality in Iran are symptoms of a development model that concentrates investment, infrastructure, and opportunity in Tehran and a few major cities while systematically underinvesting in peripheral regions.

## Iran Context

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

Iran's housing and urban inequality challenge is among the most politically salient issues facing any future government. Tehran's housing prices have increased by over 500% in dollar terms between 2018 and 2024, informal settlements (hashiyeneshini) surround every major city, and the Mehr Housing Project — the most ambitious public housing initiative in the Islamic Republic's history — produced widely criticized results. The evidence reviewed here, while limited, illuminates structural dimensions of this crisis.

Ehsani's (2003) historical analysis of Khuzestan's company towns reveals a pattern that continues to shape Iran's urban landscape: cities designed for extraction rather than habitation produce spatial hierarchies that persist across regime changes. Contemporary parallels include the petrochemical cities of Assaluyeh and Bandar Abbas, where economic activity generates national wealth but local populations experience environmental degradation and inadequate infrastructure. A post-transition urban policy would need to break this extraction-development disconnect by ensuring that resource-rich regions benefit from the wealth they produce.

Mahoozi's (2015) finding that rural multidimensional poverty increased during a period of GDP growth directly challenges the Islamic Republic's development narrative and provides diagnostic data for targeted intervention. The study's disaggregation by region (Tehran vs. other urban vs. rural) reveals that Iran's inequality is fundamentally spatial — not just between rich and poor households but between regions with and without access to state investment, infrastructure, and economic opportunity. This spatial dimension means that housing policy alone is insufficient; it must be embedded in a broader regional development strategy.

A credible post-transition housing and urban strategy would need to: (1) address Tehran's housing crisis as an immediate humanitarian and political priority, (2) develop a regional equalization framework that redirects investment from Tehran to underserved provinces — particularly Khuzestan, Sistan-Baluchestan, Kurdistan, and other peripheries, (3) formalize and upgrade informal settlements rather than demolishing them, and (4) integrate housing policy with economic diversification to ensure that peripheral regions have economic foundations beyond resource extraction.

## Implications for Iran

> 🟡 **Evidence Confidence: Moderate** — Limited source triangulation (3 sources)

**Institutional Mapping:** Housing, urban inequality, and regional development in Iran engage institutions operating at multiple governance levels. The **Ministry of Roads and Urban Development** (وزارت راه و شهرسازی) administers national housing policy, including the controversial **Mehr Housing Project** (مسکن مهر) launched under Ahmadinejad and the subsequent **National Housing Movement** (نهضت ملی مسکن). The **Housing Foundation of the Islamic Revolution** (بنیاد مسکن انقلاب اسلامی) focuses on rural housing and disaster reconstruction but operates as a bonyad under Supreme Leader oversight rather than parliamentary accountability. **Municipal governments** (شهرداری‌ها), particularly Tehran Municipality, control urban planning, zoning, and construction permits — with documented corruption in high-density rezoning that enriches politically connected developers. The **IRGC's Khatam al-Anbiya** dominates large-scale construction, infrastructure, and development projects, often receiving no-bid contracts that crowd out private sector competition. The **Planning and Budget Organization** allocates regional development funds but systematically favors Tehran and major urban centers. Provincial **governorates** lack fiscal autonomy for independent development initiatives.

**Structural Parallels:** Ehsani's (2003) analysis of Khuzestan's company towns reveals that Iran's urban inequality is not a contemporary market failure but a structural legacy of how industrial modernity was introduced — as a tool of extraction rather than development. The Anglo-Persian Oil Company designed Abadan and Masjed-Soleyman to serve British petroleum interests, creating spatial hierarchies that persist across regime changes. This pattern continues in contemporary resource-extraction urbanism: **Assaluyeh** and **Bandar Abbas** generate national wealth through petrochemical and port operations while local populations experience environmental degradation and inadequate housing. Mahoozi's (2015) finding that rural multidimensional poverty *increased* during Iran's GDP growth period (1999-2007) directly parallels the Inclusive Growth Report's (2017) framework: growth models that concentrate benefits geographically produce inequality regardless of aggregate output.

**Enablers and Blockers:** Urban reform would find allies among **municipal reformists** (Tehran city council has periodically pushed for transparent urban planning), **professional urban planners and architects** constrained by political interference, and **civil society housing advocates**. The **private construction sector** would benefit from transparent permitting and reduced IRGC competition. Blockers include **IRGC construction entities** (Khatam al-Anbiya) whose market dominance depends on political connections and no-bid contracts, **politically connected real estate developers** profiting from opaque zoning decisions, **bonyad landholders** controlling large urban and rural properties outside market regulation, and **Tehran-centric bureaucratic culture** that resists fiscal decentralization to peripheral provinces.

**Iran-Specific Challenges:** Iran's housing and urban inequality challenge differs from comparative cases in several critical respects. First, **Tehran's housing price hyperinflation** (over 500% increase in dollar terms between 2018-2024) has created an immediate humanitarian crisis where middle-class families cannot afford housing in the capital, driving political resentment that transcends regime-opposition divides. Second, **informal settlements** (حاشیه‌نشینی) surrounding every major city — estimated to house millions — represent both a governance failure and a political tinderbox, as these marginalized populations bore the brunt of the 2019 fuel price protests. Third, Iran's **spatial inequality** is fundamentally ethno-geographic: the most underserved provinces — **Sistan-Baluchestan**, **Kurdistan**, **Khuzestan**, **Ilam**, **Kermanshah** — are predominantly inhabited by ethnic minorities, meaning that urban inequality is inseparable from minority rights and regional autonomy questions. A post-transition urban strategy must: (1) address Tehran's housing crisis as an immediate political priority through rent stabilization and accelerated construction, (2) develop a **regional equalization framework** redirecting investment from Tehran to underserved peripheries, (3) formalize and upgrade informal settlements rather than demolishing them, (4) break the IRGC's construction monopoly through transparent competitive bidding, and (5) integrate housing policy with economic diversification so peripheral regions develop economic foundations beyond resource extraction.
