## Abstract

This article applies path dependence theory within historical institutionalism to explain the divergent democratic trajectories of South Korea and Japan. The differences between the two democracies cannot be adequately captured by the simple dichotomy of “resistance from below” versus “imposition from above.” Rather, governance choices made at critical junctures interacted with distinct modes of social mobilization to produce self-reinforcing lock-ins ― a dynamic conceptualized here as dual path dependence. 
South Korea’s presidential system, catalyzed by the June 1987 democratization movement, institutionalized mass mobilization and resistance politics, resulting in power concentration and frequent regime change. Japan’s parliamentary system, shaped by postwar elite-led reforms, consolidated power dispersal and policy continuity. These divergent institutional choices are reflected in civil society as well: South Korean civil society developed a politicized, mobilization-centered character, while Japanese civil society adopted a compliance-oriented role anchored in cooperation with state administration. The dual path dependence model demonstrates that such differences are not cultural or economic in origin, but are reproduced through self-reinforcing mechanisms of institutional complementarity and rising transition costs.
