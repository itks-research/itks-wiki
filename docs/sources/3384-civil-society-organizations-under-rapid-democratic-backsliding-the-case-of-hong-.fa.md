## چکیده

ABSTRACT Research about civil society in authoritarian contexts has highlighted how civil society organizations (CSOs) can employ various strategies to adapt to a restrictive environment to ensure organizational survival and preserve the capacity to pursue their goals. This study adds to the literature by examining CSO adaptation in a society undergoing rapid autocratization. Conceptually, this article argues that adaptation strategies need to be understood together with the development of risk approaches and orientational discourses. The former refers to the ways actors assess, mitigate, and determine when to take risks, whereas the latter refers to discourses actors articulate for reasserting the meaningfulness of and giving directions to their work. Based on interviews with activists from 20 CSOs – mostly with an advocacy orientation or close linkages with the pro-democracy movement – in post-National Security Law Hong Kong, the analysis demonstrates how adaptation practices, risk approaches, and orientational discourses combine to help sustain continual state-civil-society negotiation.
