## Abstract

In this paper I argue that constitution making is a central arena of transitional politics. By discussing examples from recent transitions such as Egypt and historical transition processes such as South Africa, Poland and Brazil, I show that transitional politics seem to have an important constitutional dimension that so far has not been sufficiently considered.In a second step, I cast doubt on the conventional view in constitution making research that (elite) individual agency and preferences are solely responsible for constitutional outcomes such as constitutional rights. During the entire transition, because of the overall democratization context, elites have to legitimize and institutionalize their constitutional decision making democratically. To do so they engage in other constitutive practices such as constituency formation, the creation of representative relations to citizens, party formation, and electoral campaigning. During democratic constitution making, thus, drafters are at the same time party politicians and (partisan) representatives.Based on these considerations, I introduce an agenda-setting model of constitution making in which drafters, parliamentarians and popular agents are seen as constitutional rights initiators. According to this model a combination of the type of rights initiating agent, and the question whether the rights proposal becomes subject to partisan conflict determines the specific path the conflict over rejection or inclusion takes. I briefly illustrate this model with two examples of rights inclusion conflicts in the constitution making processes of Poland (1989-1997) and South Africa (1990-1996). In the end of the paper I derive four general conclusions on the political dynamics of, and agents involved in contemporary constitution making as part of democratic transitions.
