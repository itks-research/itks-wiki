## چکیده

The following article traces the official political discourse of the People’s Republic of Poland on the Portuguese Revolution and the process of building democracy that followed. Although, diplomatically, Poland – an ally of the USSR – welcomed the revolution in which the Portuguese Communist Party (PCP) played an important role, events in Europe’s most western country were viewed with ambivalence and even fear. While Poland was willing to establish closer economic ties with the West and although it strongly supported the policy of détente, a major sociopolitical shake-up in a NATO member state posed a problem. In this article, we examine the hopes and fears of the Polish regime, the mutual interest in cooperation, and the local Polish context: economic liberalization without democratization in the so-called ‘Gierek decade’. The upheavals in Portugal, with their radical social demands, posed a challenge to the socialist government in Warsaw, particularly as its political allies were at the vanguard of the fight for democracy. Indeed, the Polish United Workers’ Party (PZPR) wished to present the Portuguese Revolution to society as a quest for liberal-democratic stabilization, rather than a genuine revolutionary process aimed at achieving socialism.
