## چکیده

In a thoroughly researched and well-crafted monograph, political scientist Cath Collins explores the reasons why some Latin American countries that experienced state terrorism or at least serious repression in the 1970s and 1980s have begun to bring human rights violators to justice, while others have not. She does so by focusing on two countries that have followed opposite trajectories, Chile and El Salvador. In Chile, even prior to Pinochet’s arrest and with the 1978 amnesty decree-law still in effect (as it is today), human rights organizations, lawyers, and judges began to dent the shield of impunity so carefully constructed during the dictatorship. In El Salvador, by contrast, the legislatively enacted 1993 amnesty law continues to enjoy broad support and thus impunity remains intact.With the exception of Argentina, where repressors were initially brought to trial, the newly installed democratic governments of the 1980s and 1990s, mindful of credible military threats to their survival, either did nothing or limited their actions vis-à-vis past human rights violations to programs of restorative justice, whether a formal amnesty was in place or not: truth commissions in most cases, reparations in some cases, symbolic actions to dignify victims, and initiatives to change the narrative left in place by the authoritarian regimes. Justice via the courts was, or was thought to be, impossible to achieve, as perfectly expressed in Chilean president Patricio Aylwin’s vow to pursue justice “en la medida de lo posible.” In view of the seemingly insurmountable obstacles to accountability, human rights organizations and activism declined markedly in the early transition years and only the most committed held on to hope for trials. Now in several countries, that hope is being at least partially realized, and Collins offers an analytical framework to explain why a period of “post-transitional justice” has come about in some countries and not in others.Collins’s approach is heavily weighted to domestic factors, thus challenging a number of scholars who give more credit for the renewed activism and the judicial about-face seen in some countries to the international human rights lobby. International pressures and new international human rights jurisprudence matter, but ultimately, she argues, cases are settled in domestic courts. On Pinochet’s October 1998 London arrest and its impact, she writes, “Although the unexpectedness of events in Spain and London is beyond question, . . . the undoubted post-1998 transformation of the Chilean accountability scene was in train even before those external events” (p. 77).Accountability actors, particularly human rights organizations and lawyers, the strategies that the actors devise, and judicial receptivity to arguments against amnesty are seen to fundamentally determine the outcomes of the new challenges to impunity. The first two factors are rooted in Chile’s and El Salvador’s authoritarian past. Chile, along with Argentina, developed Latin America’s strongest human rights movement, and a substantial cadre of lawyers working in the legal department of the Vicaría de la Solidaridad gained experience that proved valuable in pursuing post-transitional justice. In El Salvador, the human rights movement was repressed and too weak to leave a substantive legacy for future work. Although the Chilean movement’s legal strategy of filing thousands of writs of habeas corpus produced scant results during the dictatorship, it yielded a huge archive documenting the human rights violations, which eventually formed the basis for effective legal action. In El Salvador, by contrast, relatively few cases were taken to the courts during the 1980s, and both the human rights organizations and the judiciary were in such disarray that much evidence that could have been used today was lost.The third element in Collins’s analytical framework, judicial receptivity to claims against amnesty, brings us to the contemporary period. Both countries underwent thorough judicial reform in the 1990s, as many Latin American countries did, but with different outcomes. In El Salvador, the new judicial structure actually made it more difficult for individuals and organizations to file suits. In Chile, reform of the criminal justice process — particularly of the Supreme Court, where after 1997 only 4 of 21 judges were Pinochet appointees — opened the courts to new jurisprudence, including the widely embraced concept that amnesties for crimes against humanity are invalid and the locally developed notion of the secuestro calificado, which deems kidnapping an ongoing crime that cannot be amnestied unless a body is found. Judicial realignment with democratic culture, moreover, has progressed further in Chile.Drawing on a wide range of literature and 155 interviews conducted in six countries, the book clearly achieves the author’s objective of making sense of post-transitional justice in Chile and El Salvador. But the significance of Collins’s work extends beyond the two countries examined. The research model can be applied to other countries experiencing, or not experiencing, a resurgence of demands for justice. This is an important contribution to the burgeoning field of human rights studies.
