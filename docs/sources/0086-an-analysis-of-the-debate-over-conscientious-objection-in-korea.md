## Abstract

Although freedom of conscience, the legal foundation of conscientious objection, was\n\nincorporated into the South Korean Constitution in 1948, conscientious objection in\n\nKorea remained taboo until the late 1990s. The goal of this article is to illustrate how\n\nand why attitudes towards conscientious objection remained virtually unchanged in such\n\na fast-moving society like that of Korea. Freedom of religion and conscience represent\n\nthe central concepts of Western Constitutions and conscientious objection in the West is\n\ndeeply rooted in the history of pacifist Christian churches there. However, Korean\n\nsociety had no ideological background or historical experience with respect to\n\nconscientious objection. The concept of conscientious objection was so foreign to the\n\nKorean people that there was no proper terminology for indicating such a notion;\n\ninstead, terms such as jingjip geobu (refusal of conscription) or byeongyeok gipi (draft\n\nevasion) were used. Conscientious objection was perceived not as a human rights issue,\n\nbut as a matter concerning a few particular Christian denominations, denominations\n\nlargely deemed socially unacceptable by the Korean populace. In other words, the\n\nKorean debate on conscientious objection was framed as a confrontation between the\n\nKorean state and specific Christian denominations, particularly the Jehovah’s Witnesses\n\nand the Seventh-Day Adventists, rather than as an issue of confrontation between\n\nindividual citizens and the state.\n\nOnly around 2000 were Korean conscientious objectors able to extricate themselves\n\nfrom such a narrow mindset. Since the late 1990s the determination made out of\n\nindividual conscience to never kill another person or participate in war of any sort, has emerged as a meaningful and controversial issue. This change in the nation’s collective\n\nconsciousness may be summarized in terms of the “formation of the individual.” Not\n\nonly have conscientious objectors begun to represent themselves as individuals, but\n\nSouth Korean society has also come to show sympathy with those individuals who have\n\ndifferent ideas from the majority concerning military service and conscientious\n\nobjection.
