## Abstract

Abstract:This paper examines the evolution of President Raúl Alfonsín’s human rights policies during his presidency. Alfonsín’s 1983 election followed Argentina’s most repressive dictatorship and marked democracy’s return. Leading Argentina’s democratic transition, Alfonsín pioneered new methods of addressing state-sponsored terrorism. He established a truth commission and ordered human rights prosecutions of military generals. The trials, which respected legal codes and due process, demonstrated the law’s ability to address wrongdoing. Such proceedings helped reestablish trust in judicial processes. However, the army’s resistance to prosecution would pose a threat to democracy. Responding to military pressure, Alfonsín limited initial momentum toward accountability by authorizing Full Stop and Due Obedience laws. Both measures narrowed the scope of trials to ensure democratic stability. Alfonsín had dedicated himself to overcoming Argentina’s legacy of authoritarianism and emphasized democratization as his administration’s primary goal. No president had completed his or her mandate without military support since 1928. Considering these political realities, Alfonsín made prudent decisions to achieve his legislative goals without undermining democratic processes and institutions. This approach sought to model democratic governance. Ultimately, Alfonsín’s methods demonstrated that democracy, even when producing complicated and uneven policy victories, had the power to address social problems.
