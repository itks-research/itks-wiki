## چکیده

Few doubt that leaders impact personal and organizational outcomes. But leadership (the collective system of how leaders lead within a system) matters even more. An individual leader is an event, but the collective leadership becomes a sustained pattern. Finding leaders and developing leadership requires moving beyond general maxims (e.g. be authentic, have emotional intelligence) into deeper insights into the context of leadership. This volume offers such deep and rich insights about leadership within the context of three Asian economic settings: China, Korea and Japan. These three economic powerhouses shape much of today and tomorrow’s world economics and offer unique insights on leadership. What can I learn from this remarkable scholarship as someone interested in leadership who may not be an expert in Asia? First, Asia is not Asia. Asia, while geographically proximate and built on some fundamental shared Confucian values, has rich diversity. China, Korea and Japan are as different as England, Germany and France (Europe) or US, Mexico and Brazil (Americas). With different language, customs and political systems, we would assume that leadership would also differ. Second, while leadership differs by context, some of the basic leadership competence domains exist as we have captured in our work on Leadership Code. Leaders are strategists who set direction, executors who get things done, talent managers who manage today’s talent, human capital developers who empower and invest in future talent, and personally proficient by their individual attributes. These five dimensions of the leadership ‘code’ remain stable, but vary by context. In an increasingly transparent and global world, there are global and shared leadership expectations and standards that leaders anywhere should demonstrate. In today’s world, we would not likely have a Jamaican bobsled team or an Eddie the Eagle in the Olympics, because there are global standards. Likewise, there are global expectations of effective leadership. Third, it is useful to examine and learn from unique leadership practices in each of the three countries. China is experimenting with private leadership with alternative values to Communism and Confucianism. China has discovered three general types of organization: state-owned enterprises that tend to hierarchy and tradition; multinational organizations that attempt to adapt Western practices into China and private owned enterprises who are growing dramatically (Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu, Huawei, Haier, etc.). These emerging private-owned enterprises are some of the fastest growing and offer unique insights into a new organizational form that we call the Market Oriented Ecosystem (MOE) where they shared platforms of skills, but create autonomous teams to anticipate and penetrate market opportunities.
