## چکیده

ABSTRACT Since it’s revolution, Tunisia’s road to democracy has been turbulent—with apathy, polarization, and politically motivated violence stifling its transition. While much has been made of internet media’s role during Tunisia’s revolution, far fewer studies have examined its continuing effects throughout the country’s ongoing democratic transition. This study draws on original interviews conducted with politicians, journalists, and democracy activists immediately following Tunisia’s 2019 presidential elections to highlight how internet media and media ownership have hindered democratic consolidation. Since its revolution, internet media has stood as a central pillar of Tunisia’s political discourse—but despite the country’s many subsequent gains in informational freedom and media pluralism, I find that concentrated ownership and weak regulation over the internet/media have created space for powerful elites, former regime officials, and other anti-democratic forces to marshal influence over the online marketplace to stifle and push back against democratic consolidation. Overall, concentrated media markets—especially online—have empowered elite owners to impede media regulation and disseminate an unchecked deluge of bias, misinformation, conspiracies, and extremism—thereby propelling the kinds of deepening divisions, polarization, and mistrust towards the democratic process that has given rise to more populist/authoritarian actors seeking to erode democratic institutions and seize political power.
