## Abstract

Lisa Jakelski’s wide-ranging book on the cultural politics of music in Cold War Poland takes as its central focus a very specific case study: the annual Warsaw Autumn music festival. Established in 1956, this event is still running today, but Jakelski is mainly concerned with its first twelve years (she gives an overview of more recent activity in the Epilogue). The festival serves here as an idiosyncratic example of the bureaucracy surrounding music-making and the specificities of Poland’s (and Warsaw’s) ‘socialist ecosystem’ (4), while also providing a compelling account of Cold War cultural negotiations and the broader transnational trends that criss-crossed these years. Part of the particularity of the Polish situation stemmed from the relative freedom musicians enjoyed there in comparison to others in the Soviet Bloc. In the post-Stalin Thaw, a greater degree of national difference could be expressed within the Bloc, and Polish musicians and administrators felt able to promote avant-gardism and modernism – in the process demonstrating their cultural distance from the Soviet Union. This made the festival a unique site of cultural encounter, situated on ‘the Cold War’s cultural fault line’ (3). Musicians travelled from both sides of the line to make, talk about and listen to music; for those residing in the more repressive atmosphere of the Soviet Union, the festival presented a crucial opportunity to experience the avant-garde music of the West. The book uses the first decade or so of the festival as a way of encountering this critical period in Poland’s cultural politics, just as the nation was emerging from Stalinism and the dictates of heavily state-sponsored socialist realism (with all the concerns about cultural isolationism that had unleashed). The ‘bookends’ here were significant years in their own right: 1956 saw the Polish October Revolution, which centred on Warsaw, and 1968 was of course a more global watershed. The first half of the book is primarily focused on the politics of the festival – from administrative decision-making to the pragmatics of musical performance – and the attempts to define what contemporary music meant in this context. The second half takes mobility as its theme, moving beyond the specific locale to consider the various transnational and international networks that intersected and spun off from the festival. The individual chapters each have a focal point but show evidence of impressive archival and ethnographic work, combined with a ready deployment of the latest theoretical trends in the humanities and social sciences (mobility studies, border studies, transnationalism).
