## چکیده

One of the main objectives of Anne Wolf's excellent book is to debunk the myth of Tunisia as a beacon of secularism in the Arab–Islamic world (p. 11). Indeed, foreign governments and academics alike, with a few exceptions, held the far-fetched view that Tunisia was the sole example of an Arab–Islamic country in which Islam's role was somehow less significant. The prevailing perception was that both western-oriented President Habib Bourguiba and his successor President Zine al-Abidine Ben Ali implemented secular modernization. This overlooked the fact that the great majority of Tunisians remained attached to their Arab–Islamic roots, which is demonstrated by the results of the 2011 Constituent Assembly elections where the Islamic Ennahda party obtained 37 per cent of the votes—more than the next eight parties together which only garnered 35 per cent (p. 134). In demonstrating that Islamism is an intrinsic part of Tunisia's historical reality, Wolf outlines the evolution of the Islamist movement, which she traces back to the reforming tradition of Al-Zaytoonah University. She adopts a multidisciplinary approach, drawing from the fields of history, Islamic studies, political science and sociology, as well as using 400 interviews and informal discussions over a period of four years. Throughout the book, Wolf demonstrates Tunisians' unrelenting search for their Arab–Islamic identity, which The Movement of Islamic Tendency (the predecessor of Ennahda) strove to re-establish when Bourguiba marginalized it in the name of modernization and westernization. While Ben Ali did, at times, seek to play the Arab–Islamic identity card, he did so as part of a strategy to undermine Ennahda's endeavour to partake in the political process.
