## چکیده

The low levels of taxation in the Middle East can be the envy of both governments and citizens of industrialized nations. In 1980 direct taxes averaged less than 11 percent of total government revenue in the Middle East, while the world average was 63 percent.' Instead of taxing their own populations, Middle Eastern and North African governments are heavily dependent on nontax revenues generated from sources outside the physical boundaries of territories they rule. They depend on external capital such as oil revenues, loans, grants, and transit fees. However, whereas in most developing countries the low incidences of taxation are a reflection of the economic poverty of the countries in question, in the Middle East wealth has enabled the state to forgo domestic taxation. In oil producing countries, oil is, of course, the main source of government revenues. But even among nonoil exporters such as Jordan and Syria the share of nontax revenue is relatively high. While oil exporters rely on oil rents, nonoil exporters rely on strategic rents in the form of economic aid, loans, and, more often than not, grants to meet their budgetary needs. In a sense, then, most Middle Eastern states are rentier states. As this essay will demonstrate, rentierism enhances state autonomy by eliminating economically motivated pressure groups and by making a segment of the bourgeoisie dependent on the state. But at the same time it leads to the emergence of culturally and ideologically based groups such as Islamist movements, for whom economic issues are of secondary importance. This article compares the state's relations with the domestic business community in Iran and Turkey in the 1970s. In the late 1970s both states were faced with increased social demands. In the mid 1970s, however, the Iranian state seemed to be autonomous, strong, and stable. The Turkish state, on the other hand, was besieged by pressure groups and seemed incapable of dealing with increasing polarization and political violence in Turkish society. Furthermore, with the exception of a short period in the early 1960s, for most of the twenty-five years prior to the revolution of 1978-79 Iran was politically stable. Turkish politics, on the other hand, were anything but stable. Since the birth of multiparty democracy in 1950, the military has openly intervened in politics on at least three occasions--1960, 1971, and 1980. Democracy has been precarious in Turkey. Ultimately, however, Iran, not Turkey, succumbed to revolutionary forces. In Turkey, the economy was the primary concern of pressure groups, including the Islamist National Salvation Party. For Iranian revolutionaries, on the other hand, economic issues were of only secondary importance.
