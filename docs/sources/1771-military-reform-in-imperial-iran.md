## Abstract

Abstract This paper looks at attempts to reform the Iranian military in the nineteenth century, focusing on the various approaches and the debate as to whether military reform might catalyze wider state reform or, as some European advisors argued, that political and economic reform had to underpin and support the modernization of the military. The paper charts the attempts of Abbas Mirza to impose reform, the challenges he faced, both foreign and domestic, the failure of that reform, and the ultimate decision by the end of the nineteenth century to adopt foreign officered military establishments, principally the Russian-backed Cossack Brigade, which became the principle military force in the country through the Great War. The paper argues that the failure to reform the military reflected wider political failures and was not a reflection of instinctive martial failure. On the contrary, European observers repeatedly applauded the aptitude of Iran’s military to adopt new techniques, not least the new science of artillery.
