## Abstract

In this monograph, the process of production delocalization between Germany and Poland was analyzed, with a focus on the impact of integration processes within the European Union on these phenomena. The aim of the study was to examine the role of integration processes in foreign direct investment flows and production delocalization, as well as the consequences of these processes for Germany and Poland. The monograph also discusses the determinants of foreign capital inflows, the impact of globalization and trade liberalization, as well as the positive and negative aspects of delocalization.

The first chapter analyzed the theoretical aspects of delocalization, considering its connection to foreign direct investments. The second chapter focused on the theory of international economic integration and its impact on specialization and capital flows. The third chapter examined German investments in Poland, studying their size and structure. The fourth chapter dealt with the delocalization of production from Germany to Poland, while the final chapter explored the phenomenon of production relocation from Poland to Germany. The work also included the results of survey research conducted among German and Polish companies.

The results of the conducted analysis indicated that integration processes within the European Union had a positive impact on foreign direct investment flows, which facilitated production delocalization. Poland became an attractive destination for investments, especially from Germany, which had a significant impact on the country’s economic development. The monograph concluded with recommendations regarding further Polish-German cooperation and the deepening of economic integration within the European Union.
