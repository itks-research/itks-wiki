## Abstract

The period from 1926 to 1934 saw major government initiatives in all areas of Iranian society. In the military, in administration, in the economy and in law, the newly-installed Pahlavi government sought to exert its control and to direct Iran's emergence into the twentieth century. Since this period immediately followed the resurgence of Iranian nationalism which had brought Reza Shah to the throne, the era was fraught with possibilities for constructive change. However, despite major expenditures of government money, and despite sincere efforts by some exceptionally talented administrators, the most striking feature of this period is the failure of any changes to extend beyond a fairly superficial level. The primary cause of this failure lies in the nature of the Iranian government and its ruler. Reza Shah himself was responsible for the restructuring of Iranian society, and the monarch tended to view the state as merely an extension of his own power and prestige. He could not distinguish between Iran's welfare and his own, and so all of his reforms were geared to enhancing the latter at least as much as the former. After Reza Shah's 1926 coronation, the state was increasingly identified with the ruler. Traditionalism and patrimonialism, and not any ideological blueprint, guided his rule. While rationalizing tendencies were evident in the first Pahlavi monarch's reforms, the Shah avoided routinizing government work to an extent that would impede his own personal exercise of power.
