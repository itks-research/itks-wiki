## Abstract

Perpous : This Paper  examines the extent to which such appointments conform to constitutional and democratic standards, while offering alternative models that are more accountable and participatory. The appointment of acting regional heads by Indonesia’s central government has sparked significant constitutional and democratic concerns, particularly within the framework of decentralization and local governance. Although this practice is formally regulated under national legislation, the lack of public participation and legislative oversight in the selection process has raised serious questions regarding its democratic legitimacy and its alignment with the principles of local autonomy 
  
Study Design/Methodology/Approach:  Employing a normative legal method supported by narrative review and comparative legal analysis, the study evaluates primary and secondary legal materials, including statutory provisions, Constitutional Court decisions, and scholarly literature from both Indonesian and international sources. 
  
Findings: The reveal substantial regulatory gaps in the appointment framework, including the absence of authority limitations, institutional checks, and procedural transparency. Furthermore, comparative analysis with decentralized countries such as Germany, India, and South Africa demonstrates that these jurisdictions employ interim leadership mechanisms grounded in legislative consent and public scrutiny. 
  
Originality/Value:  This article contributes conceptually to institutional design discourse for subnational power transitions and advocates for regulatory reforms that reinforce substantive democracy, safeguard local autonomy, and enhance governmental accountability. The study aims to inform both scholarly inquiry and public policymaking in developing a more just and constitutionally sound model for regional executive succession.
