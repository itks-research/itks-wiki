## Abstract

The hijab has a long and complex history in Iran, particularly as a symbol of political and social control. In recent years, growing numbers of Iranian women have protested the compulsory hijab law as part of their broader struggle for freedom and human rights. These protests have become increasingly common despite facing severe repression from the Iranian government, which has inflicted harm on women and their families. This paper aims to examine the current state of Iranian womens freedom of dress and the key challenges they face. It draws on case studies and relevant literature to analyze the roots of these challenges, which stem from individual, societal, and institutional levels. The study finds that promoting dress freedom requires a combination of individual education, collective feminist advocacy, and institutional reform. Specifically, Iranian feminist organizations should continue to push for gender equality, while the state should establish legal oversight mechanisms to safeguard womens rights. Moreover, the paper also offers practical recommendations for those seeking to support change for Iranian women.
