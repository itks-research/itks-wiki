## چکیده

Abstract The Constitutional Court Decision No. 28/PUU-XI/2013 annulled Act No. 17 of 2012 on Cooperatives for contradicting the 1945 Constitution and reinstated Act No. 25 of 1992. This study compares the cooperative legal frameworks of Indonesia, the United States, Sweden, South Korea, and India to analyze their legal foundations, identify regulatory weaknesses, and propose reforms based on the Dignified Justice framework. Using a normative juridical method with qualitative analysis of laws and court rulings, this research is grounded in Hatta’s cooperative philosophy and Prasetyo’s theory of Dignified Justice as the grand theory, Sukardi’s framework as the middle-range theory, and Indonesia’s cooperative regulations as the applied theory. The findings indicate that cooperative laws across the five countries reflect principles of justice, solidarity, and social responsibility. Applying Dignified Justice highlights how legal definitions, governance structures, and enforcement mechanisms uphold or undermine member dignity and legal certainty. In Indonesia, Act No. 25 of 1992 still contains legal gaps in definitions, membership, capital, and sanctions, leading to uncertainty and institutional stagnation. Therefore, cooperative law reform grounded in Dignified Justice is essential to align with constitutional and Pancasila values, ensuring a more inclusive and dignified national development.
