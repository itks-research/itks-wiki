## چکیده

The policies vis-à-vis a legacy of human rights abuses that have been adopted in many Latin American countries since the early eighties, once dictatorial rule was left behind and constitutional government was established or restored, have been subjected to intense international attention. After all, it was the 1983 return to a civilian regime in Argentina, as well as the corresponding measures of truth, justice and reparations adopted to address the massive violations committed by the military juntas that had governed the country for seven years, that marked the beginning of the world trend of governmental policies, social demands, comparative international analyses and normative propositions that, over time, shaped the field called transitional justice. By the early nineties, most of the political map of Latin America, previously painted with the somber colors of dictatorship, could display the hopeful hues that signaled the presence of democratically elected governments. Many Latin American countries established truth commissions in the eighties and nineties, following their transition to democratic rule. Some of these commissions (those of Argentina, Chile, El Salvador, Guatemala and Peru) are often mentioned among the most serious experiments of their kind. However, the balance of the transitional justice policies implemented in Latin America – including not only truth-telling measures but also public acknowledgment of responsibility by the institutions and sectors most responsible for the atrocities of the past, reparations, criminal justice and institutional reforms designed to prevent the recurrence of past abuses – is not so positive. Only Argentina and Chile have achieved significant results in all these interrelated and essential aspects of transitional justice.
