## چکیده

Abstract While the United States has increasingly employed economic sanctions as an imperialist practice, the question of how target states cooperate to undermine their impact has been largely understudied. This article aims at exploring economic cooperation between the sanctioned states of Iran and Venezuela and utilizing the case to further investigate the statements laid out by Bryan Early on cooperation between states sanctioned by a common sender. Accordingly, political incentives for cooperation are undermined by their commercial unfeasibility due to the absence of economic surplus and the failure of such states to act as diversion points for sanctions busting. Consequently, sanctioned states prefer to cooperate with non-sanctioned states and only seek other sanctioned states as a last resort. The article finds that cooperation between Iran and Venezuela confirms Early’s arguments regarding the insufficiency of political incentives for cooperation and seeking other sanctioned states as a last resort. However, the article challenges ‘the absence of economic surplus’ and ‘the failure to act as diversion points’ arguments. As cooperation revolves around the sanctioned oil sector, their products tend to be in abundance, whereas the circumvention techniques developed by a sanctioned state enable it to act as a diversion point for the other sanctioned state.
