## Abstract

This study provides a profound analysis of the structural flaws within South Korea’s national subsidy system for political parties, which, contrary to democratic expectations, engenders “concrete evils.” It explores fundamental reform measures to restore the financial independence and constitutional accountability of political parties. 
The current system has devolved into a venue for “Rent-Seeking,” where dominant parties secure substantial financial gains through a double-payment structure consisting of election subsidies and expense reimbursements. From an economic perspective, this manifests a “Soft Budget Constraint,” facilitating the entrenchment of “Cartel Parties” that rely on state resources rather than securing support from civil society. Furthermore, this study highlights that special subsidies for recommending women, the disabled, and youth are the result of an incoherent grafting of the “Lottery Principle” (statistical mirroring) onto the “Election Principle” of representative democracy. This contradicts the constitutional principle of “Free Mandate” and serves as a mechanism that exacerbates social conflict. 
By examining Germany’s “relative ceiling” and the United States' private funding model, this research emphasizes that Korean political parties must transition from a “state-dependent” structure to a “citizen-dependent” one. In conclusion, this paper argues that radical reforms—including strengthening the independent role of the National Election Commission, abolishing recommendation subsidies, and introducing a system where subsidies are linked to party dues—are essential prerequisites for the advancement of the Republic of Korea. 
Ultimately, the vitality of a political party should stem not from a “state-funded artificial respirator”—the taxes of the people—but from the “sovereign foundation” of political trust. Only when political parties transcend their status as “tax-dependent cartels” and transform into “associations dependent on citizens and the national interest” can the party politics of the Republic of Korea be truly normalized.
