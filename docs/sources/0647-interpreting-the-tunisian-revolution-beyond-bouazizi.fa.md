## چکیده

On 17 December 2010 a young jobless man from a remote Tunisian town, Sidi Bouzid,
set ﬁre to his body to protest against the lack of social justice and the corruption of the
government. This hopeless act of protest triggered the Tunisian Revolution that
defeated one of the most long-lasting dictatorships in the Arab region and has led to a
deep political reform in Tunisia since 14 January 2011. A genuine understanding of the
Tunisian Revolution needs to go beyond the course of events and consider the long
history of the struggle for political reforms in Tunisia.
Actually, Tunisia experienced the ﬁrst modernization attempts under the rule ofHammouda Pacha from 1782 to 1815. Some governmental institutions were established
for the ﬁrst time, illustrating, as such, the entity of the modern Tunisian state. Thus,
some ministries and state oﬃces, such as for diplomacy, were set up. Nevertheless, the
idea of reforming the governing system sprang only later from a group of people directly
related to the ruling circles. Among the elite icons one could mention Hayreddin
Pacha, Ahmad Ibn Abi Diyaf and Mohamed Bairem V (Y. Ben Achour 1980: 290-295).
The Tunisian elite of the 19th century had, mainly, shown an interest in the con-stitution. This reformist trend reﬂects the inﬂuence of reform ideas that had spread in
Turkey and Egypt during the 19th century, aiming to help those countries to rise out of
obscurantism and backwardness and face challenges imposed then by the new European
powers on the Islamic world.
Therefore, the Tunisian elite took part together with elites from Arab and Muslimcountries in reforming those nations which appeared very weak compared to the rising
European nations. This came as a result of the relations between those elites because of
their constant mobility. Hayreddin Pacha, for instance, regularly visited Istanbul, while
Ahmed Fares Al-Chadiaq lived in Tunis between 1847 and 1859 and was in direct
contact with the reformist elite, including Hayreddin Pacha (Al-Matoui 1989: 125-146).
Ahmad Ibn Abi Diyaf had also met the Egyptian reformist Rifa’a al-Tahtawi in Paris.This was a contributing factor in the spread and circulation of reformist ideas among
the Tunisian elite, which was obviously inﬂuenced by what was happening in the rest
of the Arab and Muslim world.
The Tunisian elite, however, was not solely in touch with the Arab and Islamic East.It was in close touch with Europe, too. The recurrent and often long visits helped grasp
an idea about the renaissance of European nations and the level of scientiﬁc, technological and economic progress they achieved while Tunisia was witnessing a state of
total retardation similar to all other Arab and Muslim countries. Among the famous
visits was the one carried out by Ahmad Bey (who ruled Tunisia from 1837 to 1855) to
France in response to an invitation from King Louis Philip. He was accompanied by
some of his advisers, such as Ahmad Ibn Abi Diyaf and Hayreddin Pacha. In his wellknown work Elithaf, Ahmad Ibn Abi Diyaf spoke extensively about that visit and the
Bey’s fascination with the progress France had then achieved then (Ibn Abi Diyaf,
1990).
It can be said that the fascination by the progress Western civilization had achievedwas a feature shared by all Tunisian and non-Tunisian reformists during the 19th
century. This was obvious, for instance, in Paris Proﬁle, written by Rifa’a Al-Tahtawi
(Al-Tahtawi, 1991). This fascination was accompanied by a deep awareness of the
backwardness of the Tunisian society in terms of bridging the gap to modernism and
civilization. Therefore, reformists agreed on the necessity of imitating the West in all
the necessary steps to achieve progress in Tunisian society and to get rid of the conservative ideas refusing all that came from outside the Muslim world (Ben Hammed
2010: 199-251).
The Reformist leaders considered that one of the main reasons for the progress ofthe West was the management of government in a rational way. Tunisia needed, thus,
to reach the same goal: to set up of a rule of law that could reduce abuse and help state
institutions perform better and bring about welfare to all.
Therefore, Hayreddin Pacha (also called Khair al-Din al-Tunsi), in his book Aqwamal-masalik li ma’rifat al-mammilk [The Surest Path to Knowledge the Condition of
Countries], issued in 1867, called for the establishment of a constitutional monarchy
and modern institutions that could help Tunisian society prosper economically and
socially (al-Din al-Tunsi 1998: 81-85). He strove hard to persuade religious leaders to
support the reforms given their great inﬂuence on all sectors of Tunisian society (Green
1978: 103-128).
Ahmed Bey tried to introduce some reforms aiming to modernize the Tunisian statein a way similar to his predecessor Hammuda Pacha and in order to respond to the
aspirations of the Tunisian elite. But he went too fast in implementing formal reforms
because of his fascination with what he saw in his visit to France. As a result, his
reforms did not bring the expected results (Brown 1974).
Nonetheless, reform eﬀorts did not stop after Ahmed Bey. Those who believed inreform continued. This coincided with pressures exercised by France and Britain on the
Bey to accept reforms guaranteeing the respect of the rights of the European community
residing in Tunisia. For this reason, Mohamed Bey issued the Fundamental Pact (A’hd
al-Aman) in 1857 which involved equal rights for all individuals regardless of their religion.
The Fundamental Pact can be regarded as a sort of declaration of rights similar tothe ones in Europe and America, in particular during the 17th and 18th centuries. It
contained the same principles brought by the ‘Tanzimet’ in Turkey and more precisely
the ‘Khati Hamayon’ issued in 1856.
