## چکیده

The following article comprises a preliminary version of three chapters of a manuscript on the background and development of the Iranian Constitutional Revolution of 1905-11. To give a very brief background to the events of 1900-05: in 1896 the powerful and long-lived Naser ed-Din Shah had been assassinated by a political radical, and he was succeeded by his weak and ailing son, Mozaffar ed-Din Shah (1896-1907). The new Shah, although of a more kindly disposition than his father, was incapable of ruling, and was accompanied to Tehran from his former post in Tabriz by a group of greedy courtiers, mostly of Azerbaijani Turkish origin, who came to be known as the Tabrizi or Turkish party. Gifts, pensions, and sales of crown lands to this group quickly drained the Iranian territory, while the Shah began almost immediately to clamour for a foreign loan to finance the trips abroad that were recommended by his doctors. Mozaffar ed-Din Shah at first dismissed his father's unpopular Prime Minister, the Amin os-Soltan who, since the defeat of British interests at the time of the mass movement against a British tobacco monopoly in 1891-92, had been following a pro-Russian policy. The new Shah appointed the liberal and pro-British Amin od-Dauleh to be Prime Minister in 1897, and he attempted reforms that stirred up some of the ulama and other vested interests. In 1898, weakened by the high level opposition and by the British failure to offer the loan that the Shah was seeking, the Amin od-Dauleh was forced to resign, and soon afterwards the Amin os-Soltan was recalled to power. The Shah's demands for money hastened the movement toward substantial Russian control over the Iranian government in these years.
