## Abstract

ABSTRACT This article proposes a feminist international political economy (IPE) framework to explore how economic sanctions, an underexplored economic tool of international conflict, may (re)shape gendered regimes of labor and survival in sanctioned contexts. Focusing on Iran’s intensified sanctions regime since 2012, and building on feminist IPE work on crisis and social reproduction, it argues that sanctions-induced crises in Iran have led to a feminization of survival in the country. Emphasis is placed on how feminized informality is simultaneously mobilized and invisibilized by the Iranian state as women’s productive and reproductive roles have been reshaped in the state’s narratives and policies. While patriarchal ideologies associating women with socially reproductive labor have been entrenched, women have also been constructed as “economic patriots” in the state’s neoliberal policies, which encourages home-based informal activities as part of a national poverty reduction strategy. Methodologically, the article combines this feminist policy analysis with insights earned from a six-month period of fieldwork involving 25 semi-structured interviews to show how these policy shifts, combined with the direct effects of sanctions, which have fundamentally reshaped and constrained everyday life, are turning feminized informality into a survival strategy for many middle-class urban households in Iran.
