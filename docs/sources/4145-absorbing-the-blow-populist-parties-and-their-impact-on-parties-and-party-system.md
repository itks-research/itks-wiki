## Abstract

Absorbing the blow starts from the premise that populist parties have gained sufficient electoral power that one might reasonably expect them to influence party systems. The editors and the chapter authors evaluate whether and to what extent populist parties exert such influence. Steven Wolinetz and Andrej Zaslove correctly note that ‘to date, most research on populism and party systems has focused on populist radical right parties and why they arise’ (p. 9). In other words, they study populism as a dependent variable. Treating populist parties as a cause, and evaluating their effect on the party systems in which they arise, is not totally unique (see, for example, Cas Mudde, Populist radical right parties in Europe, Cambridge University Press, 2007) but still an innovative approach, especially in so far as it draws important cross-country comparisons. The volume consists of nine case-study chapters and an introduction and conclusion by the editors. Focusing on how existing party structures respond to competition from populist parties, each chapter addresses the extent to which mainstream parties adapt strategies towards voters and policy positions to accommodate populist challengers (competition for votes), and the extent to which the strength of populist parties changes approaches to the formation of coalitions (competition for government). The case selection aims to provide a ‘regional cross-section’ of countries and ‘diverse types of party and political systems’ (p. 15). The result is a cluster of four chapters on countries with simple and extended multiparty systems (Austria, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Scandinavia and Finland), and four further chapters on bipolar and post-communist party systems (France, Italy, Poland and Hungary).
