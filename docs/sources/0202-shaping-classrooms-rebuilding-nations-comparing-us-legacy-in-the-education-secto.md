## Abstract

[Purpose] This study examines the role of U.S. military governance in shaping postwar educational reforms in South Korea and Japan, highlighting how these reforms intersected with ideological transformation and national reconstruction. 
[Methods] To this end, we analyzed contemporaneous documents produced by the U.S. Military Government as well as relevant educational institutions in both countries. 
[Results] Both countries adopted the American 6-3-3 school system and democratic education ideals, but their reforms diverged due to distinct colonial legacies, U.S. intervention, and domestic political conditions. In South Korea, reforms aimed to dismantle colonial legacies and rebuild a national identity based on language, culture, and democracy. In Japan, the focus was on eliminating imperial ideologies and establishing a pacifist democracy under U.S. supervision. While the U.S. played a more hands-on role in Japan, Korea’s reform allowed for greater input from local agency, though domestic unrest led to inconsistencies in implementation. Despite such differences, both countries viewed education as key to rebuilding and modernization. 
[Conclusion] ]This study underscores that postwar education reforms were both tools of liberation and instruments of power, with lasting impacts on political cultures and civic values.
