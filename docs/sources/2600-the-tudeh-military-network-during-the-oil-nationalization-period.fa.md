## چکیده

In analysing Iran's modem political history, it is most useful to consider the role of the popular movements in effecting change. From the abdication of Reza Shah in 1941 to the overthrow of Mussadeq's government in 1953, many political parties and groups competed with one another for the mobilization of the Iranian public. Among these groups, the Hezb-e Tudeh-e Iran (Party of the Iranian Masses) had considerable success. The Tudeh Party was established immediately after the forced abdication of Reza Shah by the Allied Forces in September 1941, gathering Marxists and the pro-Marxist intelligentsia as its central figures. Its radical platform captured the hearts of many Iranian workers who had suffered politically and economically under the longtime dictatorship of Reza Shah. In these circumstances, the party and its influence rapidly expanded. The Tudeh Party, made up mainly from the working class, had reached its zenith by May Day 1946. It had more than 40,000 party members, and had affiliated 355,000 blueand white-collar workers into its labor unions.' In May of the same year, a series of drastic strikes were called at oil refineries in Abadan by the Tudeh-dominated labor unions. This forced Parliament to approve labor legislation drafted by the central government. In the formation of a cabinet in August 1946, a Tudeh deputy, Iraj Eskandari, was appointed Minister of Labor. He was to have great influence on labor administration. However, the party suffered a severe setback in 1949 when the Tudeh experienced mass arrests and defections and the dismissal of its ministers from the government. Furthermore, the 1949 ban on the party caused a major setback. Despite such hardships and privations, however, the Tudeh Party gradually regained its power, going on to play an important role during the Oil Nationalization Period (1951-53). By mid-1953, the strength of the party had surpassed even the heights of its earlier prosperity. It was commonly said that the party would be able to take over the government without resorting to violence. The increased influence of the Tudeh Party frightened Westem countries such as Great Britain and the United States, and threatened the royalist and religious forces inside Iran. Most important, the growth of the Tudeh seriously weakened Mussadeq's government which had been tolerant of the movement, and led to the dictatorship of Mohammad Reza Shah by the coup on 19 August 1953.
