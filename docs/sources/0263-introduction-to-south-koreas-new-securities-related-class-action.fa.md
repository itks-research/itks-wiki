## چکیده

I. INTRODUCTION South Korea's National Assembly has approved a securities-related class action bill that will take effect in January 2005.1 Starting in January 2005, minority shareholders may file class actions against companies with assets of two trillion won2 or more, and the same will be effective against companies with assets of less than two trillion won beginning in January 2007.3 After three years of painstaking efforts, South Korea has finally enacted a law-probably its only effective law-aimed at protecting the rights of minority shareholders as a class.4 This initiative to strengthen the rights of minority shareholders is an effort by the Korean government to transform South Korea's corporate sector, which is substantially under the control and influence of the family-owned conglomerates (chaebols).5 Since the financial crisis of 1997, corporate governance has been a crucial issue in South Korea's financial sector. Nevertheless, South Korea's corporate governance is still deemed to be weak.6 Although the movement to protect minority shareholder rights has become active in the past few years, minority shareholders are generally reluctant to vote against majority shareholders or against management even when their rights are injured.7 This fact alone demonstrates the need for adequate legal remedies for minority stockholders. Thus, this Note will discuss the arguments for and against class action lawsuits, and provide an introduction to the relevant statutory provisions of the new securities-related class action law in South Korea. II. BACKGROUND A. Creation of the Chaebol Since the Korean War, which ended in 1953, South Korea has grown to be one of the world's largest economies.8 The Government led the industrialization of South Korea, relying heavily upon rewarding targeted entrepreneurs or industries with preferential loan treatments, and utilizing banks as the sole source of external financing.9 Governmentsupported capital and permission for select companies to enter new sectors10 led to the creation of the chaebol groups-large conglomerates run by autocratic chairmen and their families.11 The culture in these conglomerates is authoritarian.12 In other words, when it comes to management, free exchange of ideas is almost impossible and no one can possibly challenge the decision of a chaebol chairman.13 B. The Chaebol and the Economic Crisis of 1997 The chaebol structure has proven to be catastrophic as evidenced by the financial crisis of 1997. The chairmen of the chaebols constantly borrowed to fuel the chaebols' rapid growth, relying heavily on bank financing and assuming that the government would not let the chaebols fail.14 However, during the 1990s, most of the chaebols were not profitable, and fewer than half of them earned a market rate of return on their investments.15 The chaebol chairmen single-mindedly pursued their own ambitions, completely disregarding the interests of the chaebols' shareholders.16 The 1997 Asian financial crisis evidently exposed the weakness and inefficiency of South Korea's corporate governance structure. By December 1997, the Korean won lost more than half of its value against the U.S. dollar,17 Korea's major banks became insolvent,18 and eight chaebols went bankrupt in 1997 alone.19 C. Problem of Family-Centered Corporate Governance after the Crisis Despite the economic crisis, the chaebol chairmen were not persuaded that South Korea needed major corporate governance reform.
