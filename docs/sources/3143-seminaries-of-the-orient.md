## Abstract

Media and policy narratives concerning Islamic education focus on intolerance, exclusion, and indoctrination, pivoting on the madrasa as a “school of hate.” These themes are magnified in the case of Qom as seat of the Shīʿī clerical establishment in Iran. Since the Islamic Revolution of 1978–79, the center of learning (ḥawza ʿilmīyya) in Qom has been represented in Orientalist fashion as a site of medievalism and mystery. The ‘ulama’ or “mullahs” therein have been associated with an authoritarian intransigence founded on premodern logic. While traditionalism signals the absence of reason and hence an irrationality necessitating the language of force, the outdated logic of scholasticism requires modernizing reform, if not outright violence. Yet political engagement with “moderates” has typically fallen short, as principal actors of the ḥawza ʿilmīyya have been described as notoriously difficult to read from the metropole. The impassive Ayatollah thus renews the image of “inscrutable” men of the East, interpreted by native informants and insiders who contribute policy analysis on the ḥawza systems of Qom and Najaf. This article unpacks the mythology around Qom, the ḥawza and ‘ulama, and counters with a view of contested higher education in global perspective.
