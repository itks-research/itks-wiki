## چکیده

Abstract The primary aim of this article is to examine the policies of the elite in Iran in relation to political parties and organizations that have emerged between 1979 and 2009. It also attempts to argue that the politics of factionalism has proven to be incapable of creating a viable political system. On the other hand, it has been difficult to anticipate the emergence of a system based on party politics in Iran more than three decades after the revolution. The findings of this article demonstrate how determined the elite were in shaping politics in Iran, and will also show that the move away from factionalism to a system based on party politics between 1997 and 2009 faced stiff opposition from the conservative elite. The situation polarized politics in Iran and further increased intra-elite factionalism. This article relies mostly on Iranian primary sources, accessing opinions that have not yet been published or discussed. The focus is on examining the relationship between high-ranking members of the elite and organizations representing different interests within the system that came to existence either before or immediately after the revolution in 1979. Since 1979, Iranian politics has been dominated by Shi'a clerics and religious lay persons divided into two main ideological factions, sub-divided in turn into several smaller factions. Despite the shift of power between the two main factions, the real power remained in the hand of Ayatollah Khomeini and then his successor, Ayatollah Ali Khamene'i. The concept of 'ruling elite' in this article refers to both main factions. However, divisions between the two sides have widened since 1997, and the political boundaries have become increasingly transparent around two opposing poles. From 2003 onwards, the ultra-conservatives emerged, comprised of the Supreme Leader's own office (bayt-e rahbary), his vast network of officials and advisors linked to him directly as well as Friday Emams, several nationwide foundations, and his representative in each provinces. The Leader strengthened his link with the traditional conservatives and from 2003 he forged strong ties with the revolutionary guard and the intelligence forces as an alternative to reformists.
