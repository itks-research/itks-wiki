## چکیده

Abstract The analysis of six cases of successful or failed institutionalization of civilian control in this chapter allows for a systematic tracing of the strategies of political control used by civilians; how they were linked to structural determinants; and how such strategies affected the reform of post-authoritarian civil–military relations. The three successful cases (South Korea, Chile, and Spain) show that there were important differences in the mix of strategies, particularly regarding the early-on prioritization of the role of the defense ministry and an emphasis on the democratic socialization of the military. However, control strategies that played an important role in cases of weakly institutionalized civilian control, such as the co-optation of military elites, were of little importance in the three successful countries. Second, governments in South Korea, Chile, and Spain first emphasized weak strategies but later adopted new, more robust instruments of ensuring the military’s subordination, possible because of the maturing of democratic institutions, the growth of civil societies, and good socioeconomic performance, which strengthened the legitimation of democratic institutions and political authorities. In contrast, the most striking similarity among the three cases of stagnant or weakly institutionalized civilian control (the Philippines, Russia, and Madagascar) was the attempt to personalize and centralize political control by the incumbent president. All three countries had weak civil societies and democratic institutions; a challenging economic environment; and a potential for mass discontent. In all three cases, civil–military relations were mainly dependent on the incumbent’s ability to co-opt elites in various sectors of society.
