## چکیده

This thesis argues that fanfic operates through a paradox of legitimation. Using the current cult &#13;\ntext Supernatural (CW, 2006-) as a case study, discourse theory adapted from Foucault is &#13;\nutilized to establish that discursive formations from the source text can be de- and re-constructed, &#13;\nsometimes consolidating canon’s constructions, but at other times, altering Othered &#13;\ncharacterizations and criticising statements from canon. Paradoxically, however, this process &#13;\nutilizes and functions through the capital of the already-empowered: the White male Author &#13;\n(Jenkins 1995; Hills 2002; 2010a; Wexelblat 2002; Gray 2010; Kompare 2011; Scott 2011), &#13;\nand/or the White male protagonists of the series (c.f. Dyer 1992). &#13;\nThe discursive formations studied are identified from the researcher’s situated position as fan-&#13;\ninsider and academic (c.f. Hills 2002; Hodkinson 2005). They are judged to be of significance in &#13;\nthe canon and fandom, and pertinent to the questions of power and Authority this study &#13;\naddresses. The methodology utilizes some techniques from network analysis (Park and Thelwall &#13;\n2003) to chart the impact of fan-statements in an innovative fashion, using both quantitative and &#13;\nqualitative measures, whilst retaining insights from discourse theory to account for the &#13;\nspecificity of fiction as a particular form of writing. In this way, the strength of statements, &#13;\ndiscursive boundaries, and techniques for alteration can be observed.
