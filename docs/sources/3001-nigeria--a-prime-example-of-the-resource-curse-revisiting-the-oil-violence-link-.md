## Abstract

This paper studies the oil&amp;#8208;violence link in the Niger Delta, systematically taking into consideration\ndomestic and international contextual factors. The case study, which focuses on\nexplaining the increase in violence since the second half of the 1990s, confirms the differentiated\ninterplay of resource&amp;#8208;specific and non&amp;#8208;resource&amp;#8208;specific causal factors. With regard\nto the key contextual conditions responsible for violence, the results underline the\nbasic relevance of cultural cleavages and political&amp;#8208;institutional and socioeconomic weakness\nthat existed even before the beginning of the âoil era.â Oil has indirectly boosted the\nrisk of violent conflicts through a further distortion of the national economy. Moreover, the\ntransition to democratic rule in 1999 decisively increased the opportunities for violent\nstruggle, in a twofold manner: firstly, through the easing of political repression and, secondly,\nthrough the spread of armed youth groups, which have been fostered by corrupt\npoliticians. These incidents imply that violence in the Niger Delta is increasingly driven by\nthe autonomous dynamics of an economy of violence: the involvement of security forces,\npoliticians and (international) businessmen in illegal oil theft helps to explain the perpetuation\nof the violent conflicts at a low level of intensity.
