## چکیده

Throughout the nineteenth century military modemization was a constant preoccupation of Iranian reformers and the history of the Qajar period is peppered with attempts to create a standing army on the European model with the help of missions of foreign officers. These attempts were undertaken in the hope that Iran could, in conscious imitation of the Ottoman example, develop sufficient military strength to defend itself against its external enemies and that the needs of the new army would provide an impetus to modernization in other areas of state and society. Nineteenth-century Iran saw no less than eight military missions from various European countries' while three major projects for the construction of a modern army within comprehensive frameworks of reform may be identified: under Abbas Mirza in the early part of the century and subsequently under Amir-i Kabir in 1848-51 and under Mirza Husayn Khan, Mushir al-Dawla, in the 1870s.'
