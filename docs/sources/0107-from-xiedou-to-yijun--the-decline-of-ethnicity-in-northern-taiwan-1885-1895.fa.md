Vol. 11, No. 1 Late Imperial ChinaJune 1990 FROM XIEDOU TO YIJUN, THE DECLINE OF ETHNICITY IN NORTHERN TAIWAN, 1885-1895* Stevan Harrell Righteous Armies and the Yiwei War In 1895 (Yiwei by the cyclical calendar), as a result of the disastrous defeat of the Chinese forces by Japan in engagements in Korea, the island province of Taiwan was ceded by the Qing to the Japanese Empire. In April of that year, after several vain efforts to drum up international support to change the conditions of the treaty, and after Li Hongzhang was sent by the Qing court to attempt to mollify outraged Taiwanese natives and officials posted to Taiwan, the provincial governor, Tang Jingsong, along with other officials and gentry, announced the formation of the Democratic State of Taiwan (Taiwan Minzhu Guo), and along with establishing a constitution, printing paper money, and taking over many of the administrative functions of the former Qing government, also organized military resistance to the Japanese occupying forces. There was considerable fighting at the north tip of the Island in April and May of 1985, and the Japanese did not win such places as Ruifang and Danshui without having to fight for them. But the resistance collapsed at the outskirts of Taibei, the newly rebuilt and enlarged provincial capital, and the Japanese entered that city without a fight.1 At this point, Tang Jingsong and many of his lieutenants fled the island (he was not a native of Taiwan anyway) and the Taiwan Minzhu Guo, as an administrative entity at any rate, ceased to exist. What did not cease to exist at this time, however, was popular resistance to the Japanese takeover. Of fifty-two battles between native forces and the Japanese troops shown on Ng Yuzin's map,2 six took place in Haishan, the area consisting of the present day zhen of Shulin, Sanxia, and Yingge. There were fights at Fengguidian (now Shulin), at Ganyuan, and at several sites near Sanjiaoyong (presentday Sanxia), including most famously Fude Keng (also known as Tudigong Keng), and Erjiajiu, on the Dakekan River between Sanxia and Yingge. Descriptions of these fights vary, but perhaps the flavor can be gained by looking at two portrayals of the same fight, one from an American observer attached to the Japanese occupation forces, and one from a Taiwanese historian writing in the 1950s. James Davidson describes the fight for Fude Keng as follows: The main column, under the personal command of MajorGeneral Yamane, advanced, on the morning of the 22nd [July 1985] from Tohokam [Dakekan, now Daxi] to the vicinity of a village about five miles from Sankakeng. Here they encountered and repulsed a body of some five or six hundred insurgents posted on an eminence, inflicting a loss on them of some thirtyfive killed, and suffering themselves a los of three wounded. Such houses in the vicinity as had harbored the insurgents were burnt to the ground. The enemy were found next morning (23rd) reassembled on the high hills surrounding the Japanese position.
