## Abstract

In the last few years, there has been growing talk amongst development actors and agencies about a&#13;\n“rights-based approach” to development. Yet what exactly this consists of remains unclear. For some, its&#13;\ngrounding in human rights legislation makes such an approach distinctive, lending it the promise of repoliticising&#13;\nareas of development work that have become domesticated as they have been “mainstreamed”&#13;\nby powerful institutions like the World Bank. Others complain that like other fashions it has become the&#13;\nlatest designer item to be seen to be wearing and has been used to dress up the same old development.&#13;\nThis paper seeks to unravel some of the tangled threads of contemporary rights talk. Where is today’s&#13;\nrights-based discourse coming from? Why rights and why now? What are the differences between versions&#13;\nand emphases articulated by different international development actors? What are their shortcomings, and&#13;\nwhat do these imply for the practice and politics of development? Reflecting on these questions, we&#13;\nexplore some of the implications of the range of different ways of relating human rights to development.&#13;\nWe argue that ultimately, however it is operationalised, a rights-based approach would mean little if it&#13;\nhas no potential to achieve a positive transformation of power relations among the various development&#13;\nactors. Thus, however any agency articulates its vision for a rights-based approach, it must be interrogated&#13;\nfor the extent to which it enables those whose lives are affected the most to articulate their priorities and&#13;\nclaim genuine accountability from development agencies, and also the extent to which the agencies&#13;\nbecome critically self-aware and address inherent power inequalities in their interaction with those people.&#13;\nKeywords: rights, donors, rights-based approaches.
