## چکیده

I. INTRODUCTION The People's Republic of China's (PRC or China) bid to join World Trade Organization (WTO) will fundamentally impact world economy and will critically affect China's developing stock market. Although much attention has been devoted to needed strengthening of China's intellectual property rights as a precondition to joining WTO, potential changes in China's stock markets have received scant attention. Basic improvements in efficiency, transparency, and liquidity of China's stock markets will flow from WTO membership.(1) Specifically, changes mandated by WTO's General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS)(2) will liberalize competition in China's financial and accounting services markets, increase number of financial intermediaries, and foster development of China's stock markets. This Article analyzes several issues implicated by China's WTO bid. Part II examines rationale behind China's desire to join WTO. Part III examines some of domestic, economic, and political issues implicated by China's WTO bid. Part IV discusses GATS and its impact on China's financial and accounting services. Part IV also comparatively analyzes how GATS affects financial markets in Hong Kong, Indonesia, and Malaysia and will try to discern lessons for China from analysis. Hong Kong is valuable for two reasons: (1) as a member of WTO recently reunited with PRC, Hong Kong can provide China with practical knowledge on compliance with WTO standards, and (2) with arguably most liberal securities markets in world, Hong Kong should represent a goal for liberalizing Asian economies, including PRC. Malaysia, along with Indonesia, provides insight into developing Asian securities markets. Because Malaysia has more experience liberalizing its securities markets, it possesses a more developed securities market. Indonesia also provides valuable insight into most rapidly developing securities market in Asia, along with economic liberalization that is starting to dismantle somewhat pervasive government involvement in economy. Indonesia also provides some insight into recent Asian financial crisis, with potential lessons for China. II. CHINA'S RATIONALE FOR JOINING THE WORLD TRADE ORGANIZATION When People's Republic of China emerged from political, cultural, and economic chaos of 1970s, it was confronted with an Asia whose economies were growing at an astonishingly fast rate. In response, since early 1980s, China has engaged in a series of economic reforms with goal of development through economic liberalization. China's economic liberalization and reform are fueled, in part, by profits from China's labor-intensive, export-led growth strategy.(3) Economic liberalization has been somewhat successful. Between 1978 and 1993, China's exports grew by US$82.05 billion while China's GDP, as measured in constant 1987 yuan, grew by US$1.396 trillion.(4) The pressure to continue exporting has continued into 1990s because by end of 1990s, China is expected to become a net importer of food and energy, exhausting export earnings from petroleum exports.(5) In light of exhaustion of petroleum export revenues, China must look for new export sectors to provide basic resources for its domestic markets.(6) In order to develop and exploit new export sectors, China must be given access to foreign markets and maintain that access, especially to U.S. market.(7) Protecting access to open international markets has long been goal of WTO. Therefore, the PRC must ... support [GATT] to reduce barriers to international trade.(8) In 1986, PRC began negotiating to resume its General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT)(9) membership, which had been withdrawn by Taiwan in 1950.
