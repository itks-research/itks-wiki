# Judicial independence as consolidation anchor

**Domain:** Institutional
**Framework Node:** L3.2
**Evidence Sources:** 0

## Description

Building genuine judicial independence during early transition phases—by insulating judges from political pressure, establishing merit-based appointment, and guaranteeing tenure—creates an institutional anchor that can resist backsliding pressures. Independent courts enforce the rules of democratic competition and constrain even elected majorities' ability to overturn democratic procedures.

## Context Requirements

- **Constitutional Judicial Protections:** explicit_and_strong
- **Judicial Appointment Mechanism:** merit_based_not_political
- **International Legal Precedent:** available_for_training
- **Civil Society Monitoring:** court_watching_and_transparency
- **Executive Acceptance:** de_facto_court_authority_recognized

## Expected Outcomes

Durable judicial independence; enforcement of electoral rules; constraint on majoritarian excess; democratic consolidation

## Typical Triggers

- Constitutional convention establishes judicial independence
- First independent court decision overturns executive action
- Judges refuse political pressure on controversial case
- Court protects rights of opposition parties
- Public confidence in courts rises with visible independence

**Typical Timeframe:** 48 months
