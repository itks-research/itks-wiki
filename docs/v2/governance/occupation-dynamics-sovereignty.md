# پویایی اشغال و حاکمیت



## نمای کلی

**Military occupation** — the exercise of administrative authority over territory by a foreign power following armed conflict — is a historically distinct governance form. The comparative record spans: (1) **Total occupation** (Germany/Japan 1945-1952): comprehensive administrative control, economic reconstruction, constitutional redesign; (2) **Transitional administration** (Bosnia 1995-present, Kosovo 1999-2008): international administration with local institutional development; (3) **Indirect occupation** (Iraq CPA 2003-2004): nominally temporary authority that shaped institutional foundations for years; (4) **Advise-and-assist** (Afghanistan 2001-2021): formal sovereignty preservation with pervasive international influence. The 2026 Iran situation (airstrikes but no ground occupation) does not clearly fit any model, but the Islamabad talks could impose CPA-analog conditions without physical occupation.

## چارچوب نظری

**Occupation success theory** (Edelstein 2004; Dobbins et al. 2003): Military occupation achieves stable transition when: (a) the occupied population perceives occupation as temporary and legitimate; (b) the occupying power has long-term political commitment; (c) an external security threat provides strategic incentive (Cold War for Germany/Japan); and (d) the occupied society has pre-existing democratic institutional foundations. Germany and Japan succeeded on all four dimensions. Iraq failed on (a) and (b). Afghanistan failed on all four.

**Collaboration and resistance theory** (Kalyvas 2006): Occupied populations exhibit a spectrum from collaboration to resistance, shaped primarily by perceived security provision, economic reconstruction delivery, treatment of civilians, and legitimacy of pre-occupation regime. Collaboration is rational when occupation provides security goods; resistance becomes rational when occupation fails to deliver or imposes unacceptable conditions.

**Sovereignty transfer theory** (Chesterman 2004): The formal-informal distinction in sovereignty transfer is crucial — formal sovereignty restoration (Iraq June 2004) often precedes real autonomy by years. Three transfer modalities: (a) **quick transfer** (risk: institutional void); (b) **benchmarked transfer** (linked to institutional milestones — Bosnia/Kosovo model); (c) **indefinite occupation** (risk: resistance escalation).

**Non-occupation analog**: External powers can demand governance changes as ceasefire terms without occupying territory — creating 'sovereignty with conditionality' analogous to post-Gulf War Iraq 1991 (inspections, sanctions, no-fly zones) rather than 2003 (occupation). The question is whether Iran's government has sufficient institutional capacity to implement demanded changes, or whether external monitoring mechanisms are required.

## تحلیل ایران

**Current relevance — April 2026:** The critical question is whether the Islamabad talks produce a sovereignty-with-conditionality framework (Iraq 1991 model) or escalate demands toward occupation-analog governance (Iraq 2003 model). The comparative literature strongly favors the 1991 model for Iran's specific conditions:

**Why Iran resists occupation more than Iraq:**
- Population: 87 million vs. Iraq's 25 million (2003) — occupation force requirements would be prohibitive
- Nationalism: Iranian national identity is stronger and less sectarianized than Iraq's
- Geographic depth: Iran's size and terrain make military control far more costly
- Nuclear deterrent uncertainty: Even degraded, Iran's nuclear program creates escalation risks

**Sovereignty-with-conditionality framework for Iran:** The most likely Islamabad outcome is a framework modeled on post-1991 Iraq (UNSCOM inspections) combined with JCPOA-style phased sanctions relief. Key parameters:
- Nuclear program: Civilian energy preservation, enrichment cap, IAEA verification
- IRGC: Capacity limits, economic divestment timeline, political role constraints
- Governance: Unclear — US/Israeli demands may include electoral reform, constitutional revision, or simply IRGC withdrawal from politics

**Regime legitimacy under conditionality:** Mojtaba Khamenei's government faces a structural dilemma: compliance with external conditions is necessary for reconstruction access and ceasefire maintenance, but compliance undercuts the revolutionary legitimacy narrative. The historical parallel is Gorbachev's dilemma — reform enough to survive externally, not so much as to lose the institutional coalition that keeps you in power.
