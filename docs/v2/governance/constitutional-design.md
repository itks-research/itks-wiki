# Constitutional Design & Drafting

***Sources:** 622 | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-10*



## Overview

> 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟢 Strong** (82%)

This article examines how countries emerging from authoritarian or theocratic rule have designed new constitutions — who gets to participate, when the process happens, and what features make constitutions durable. Drawing on eight case studies (Chile, Czech Republic, Indonesia, Iran, Poland, South Korea, Spain, and Tunisia), the key finding is that the most durable post-authoritarian constitutions combine elite negotiation that protects all major actors' minimum interests with broad public participation and well-designed amendment procedures that allow future revision without requiring full replacement. The article identifies five specific implications for Iran's future constitutional design, including the need to incrementally remove the Velayat-e Faqih provisions rather than seeking wholesale replacement, to establish an independent constitutional court before full democratic competition begins, and to build in asymmetric federalism provisions for Kurdish, Azeri, Arab, and Baluch communities from the start of the drafting process.

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (25 sources) | 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟢 Strong** (82%)

The most significant empirical contribution in the constitutional design literature comes from Ginsburg, Elkins, and Blount's analysis of 935 national constitutions spanning 1789 to 2005. Their central finding reframes the entire field: the median constitutional lifespan is only 19 years. Constitutional design is therefore not about creating permanent documents but about creating documents durable enough to become entrenched through practice and political investment.

Four design variables predict longevity with statistical significance. First, amendment flexibility: constitutions with more accessible amendment procedures survive longer, contradicting the common assumption that rigidity protects constitutional integrity. The mechanism is straightforward. Rigid constitutions force political actors to choose between living with outdated provisions and replacing the entire document, while flexible ones allow incremental adaptation. Second, inclusivity in drafting: constitutions produced by processes that include minority groups, opposition parties, and civil society representatives outlast those drafted by narrow elites. Public ratification adds approximately 8% to survival probability. Third, specificity: more detailed constitutions survive longer than skeletal frameworks, likely because specificity reduces interpretive disputes. Fourth, power distribution: constitutions that distribute authority across multiple institutions demonstrate greater stability than those concentrating it in a single actor.

These findings draw on the broadest dataset ever assembled for constitutional analysis. The implications extend beyond any single country: they suggest that the common instinct to protect a new constitution through rigid entrenchment is precisely backwards. This insight has particular relevance for societies emerging from authoritarian rule, where the temptation to lock in democratic gains through unamendable provisions is strongest.

South Korea's 1987 constitutional moment demonstrates how concentrated popular pressure can compress decades of institutional reform into weeks. The June Democratic Struggle (June 10-29, 1987) saw mass pro-democracy protests across major cities, driven by student movements, labor unions, and a broad middle-class coalition outraged by the military regime's plan to install Roh Tae-woo through an indirect electoral college. On June 29, Roh capitulated, issuing an eight-point declaration conceding direct presidential elections, press freedom, political prisoner releases, and constitutional amendment.

The resulting constitution embedded distinctive design choices shaped by the authoritarian experience. The five-year single-term presidency was a direct response to the manipulations of both Rhee Syngman and Park Chung-hee, who had repeatedly extended their rule. Rather than allowing re-election, the framers imposed an absolute ban. This prevents incumbents from using state resources for self-entrenchment but creates what Choi (2022) calls the "lemon presidency" problem: each president enters office as a lame duck with a fixed horizon, producing the temptation to personalize power through captured judicial and prosecutorial institutions before the clock runs out.

The constitution was drafted through the National Assembly, approved by referendum on October 28, 1987 with 93% support, and took effect in February 1988. Yeh (2010) documents how South Korea's Constitutional Court subsequently became a "facilitator of dialogue between political actors" rather than a mere arbiter of constitutionality. This dialogic function proved critical during democratic stress, when the Court could mediate between competing institutional claims without precipitating constitutional breakdown. The Court's intervention during the 2004 presidential impeachment of Roh Moo-hyun demonstrated this mediating capacity.

Spain's 1978 Constitution is the longest-surviving product of the "third wave" of democratization, and its drafting process is the most frequently cited model of consensus constitution-making. The seven-member ponencia (drafting commission) included representatives from the governing UCD, the PSOE, the Catalan CiU, the Communist PCE, and the conservative AP, spanning the political spectrum from former Francoists to former underground communists. Their deliberations across more than 100 sessions between July 1977 and March 1978 produced a text that no party fully endorsed but all could accept.

The consenso approach rested on three pillars. First, procedural equality: all major parties had drafting representation regardless of their electoral weight, preventing majoritarian imposition. Second, substantive ambiguity: on issues where consensus was impossible (the unity-autonomy tension, the role of the monarchy, church-state relations), the drafters chose deliberately vague language that could accommodate multiple interpretations. Third, external anchoring: the prospect of European Community membership provided an external reference point and institutional constraint that disciplined all parties toward democratic norms.

Article 2 of the 1978 Constitution contains the most consequential example of creative ambiguity, simultaneously guaranteeing "the indissoluble unity of the Spanish Nation" and "the right to autonomy of the nationalities and regions." This formulation enabled the initial transition by deferring resolution of Spain's fundamental territorial question, but it also planted the seed of the Catalan crisis that would emerge decades later.

The consenso model's emphasis on constitutional ambiguity as a precondition for agreement finds parallels in other post-transition constitutional moments. Boryslavska (2021) examines how judicial reform in post-communist democracies emerged from constitutional agreements that deliberately left structural arrangements ambiguous to facilitate multi-party consensus. Martin (2011) documents the Iranian Constitutional debates of 1906-1907, where drafters similarly embraced constitutional vagueness regarding state powers to accommodate conflicting ideological factions. Randjbar-Daemi (2013) shows how the 1979 Iranian Constitution preserved competing conceptions of religious and secular authority within a single framework, generating decades of interpretive conflict. These comparative cases suggest that consenso's ambiguity-as-feature strategy reflects a broader pattern in constitutional engineering where competing blocs accept incomplete settlements.

Tunisia's 2014 Constitution represents the most extensively documented case of democratic constitution-making in the ITKS corpus. The National Constituent Assembly (NCA), elected October 2011 with 217 members, spent approximately 27 months drafting the constitution through iterative revision. Four successive drafts (August 2012, December 2012, April 2013, June 2013) allowed progressive refinement and pressure-testing of contested provisions. The final ratification vote on January 26, 2014, produced an overwhelming 200-12 result with 4 abstentions.

The critical institutional innovation was the Tunisian National Dialogue Quartet's intervention during the summer 2013 crisis. When political assassinations and the Egyptian military coup against Morsi created fears of authoritarian reversion, the NCA's work halted. The Quartet comprised four organizations with institutional weight: the UGTT (trade union federation), UTICA (employers' federation), ONAT (national bar association), and LTDH (human rights league). Their marathon negotiations produced a package deal: the Ennahda-led government would dissolve in favor of a technocratic caretaker, new elections would be held by end of 2014, and the constitutional process would accelerate to completion. The Quartet's mediation earned the 2015 Nobel Peace Prize.

Equally significant was the Ennahda party's willingness to make concessions from a position of electoral strength. Despite holding over 40% of NCA seats, Ennahda dropped its goal of declaring Tunisia an Islamic state, abandoned attempts to enshrine Sharia as a source of legislation, and accepted the formulation of Tunisia as a "civil state based on citizenship, the will of the people, and the supremacy of law." Internal party dynamics shaped these concessions: on the vote regarding Sharia's constitutional role, only 22 Ennahda deputies supported inclusion, 17 opposed, and 39 abstained.

Tunisia's constitution-making process and its civic infrastructure outcomes have attracted sustained scholarly attention. Usman et al. (2025) provide comprehensive documentation of Tunisia's socio-political, legal, and educational transformation in the post-Arab Spring decade, detailing the institutional conditions supporting civil society participation. Maboudi (2019) specifically analyzes the Tunisian constitutional reform following the Arab Spring, demonstrating how participatory deliberation shaped constitutional content. He documents the mechanisms through which civil society organizations translated citizen input into constitutional language, particularly regarding fundamental rights and democratic procedures. Lavie (2021) situates Tunisia within a broader comparative study of the constitutionalization of the civil state across Egypt, Tunisia, and Yemen, analyzing how post-uprising constitution-making redefined the relationship between state, civil society, and Islamic institutions. These sources reveal that Tunisia's Quartet model emerged within specific institutional constraints and opportunities shaped by prior constitutional traditions and post-conflict dynamics.

Chile's rejected 2022 constitutional proposal provides the most important negative case in the comparative constitutional design literature. On September 4, 2022, Chilean voters rejected the proposed constitution by 62% to 38%, a decisive margin that defied expectations given the broad public consensus that Pinochet's 1980 constitution needed replacement.

The Constitutional Convention comprised 155 members elected in May 2021, including 17 reserved indigenous seats and strict gender parity. However, party-less independents won 65 seats, the left won 28, center-left 25, and center-right 37. This produced a convention dominated by actors without party discipline or legislative experience. Larrain, Negretto, and Voigt (2023) identify three structural failures. First, the convention's control by independents lacking party structures prevented the kind of disciplined negotiation and trade-off management that Spain's ponencia achieved. Second, the political right was exceptionally underrepresented relative to its electoral weight, producing a text that lacked buy-in from a significant portion of the electorate. Third, the decentralized and fully public drafting process, while maximizing transparency, prevented the private compromises that comparative evidence suggests are essential for bridging fundamental disagreements.

The resulting 388-article document was criticized as excessively long, ideologically skewed leftward, and disconnected from median voter preferences. Hutchcroft and Rocamora (2003) provide the theoretical backdrop through the Philippines, demonstrating that "strong demands and weak institutions" produce persistent democratic deficits even across a century of constitutional experience.

Chile's constitutional reform trajectory offers critical lessons about the constraints on participatory constitution-making. Smith (2023) examines religion, sexuality politics, and the transformation of Latin American electorates, documenting the deep value conflicts embedded in Chilean society that constrained the 2022 constitutional process. Brieba (2019) analyzes Chile's electoral reforms and their impact on voter behavior, showing how institutional changes triggered unexpected political mobilization that destabilized pre-existing coalitions. Lijphart (1996) provides comparative institutional design analysis showing that majoritarian democracies (including Chile's post-1990 system) face fundamental challenges in mediating ideological polarization through deliberative institutions. These sources reveal that the 2022 failure resulted not from technical design flaws in participatory mechanisms but from attempting expansive constitutional refoundation across fundamentally polarized electorates—a structural condition no institutional mechanism can overcome.

Andrew Arato's Post Sovereign Constitution Making (2016) provides the theoretical framework most directly applicable to Iran's situation. Arato distinguishes three models of constitution-making: revolutionary, where a constituent assembly claims sovereign power to create a new order ex nihilo; imposed, where existing authorities decree a constitution; and post-sovereign, where multiple actors negotiate a constitutional settlement that none fully controls.

The post-sovereign model involves two stages. In Stage 1, roundtable negotiations between the existing regime and opposition forces establish ground rules, procedural frameworks, and an interim constitutional arrangement. In Stage 2, an elected constitutional assembly drafts the permanent constitution within the constraints established by the roundtable, with a constitutional court providing oversight to ensure the assembly respects the negotiated ground rules.

Arato develops this model from the empirical experience of Spain (1977-78), Poland and Hungary (1989-90), and especially South Africa (1993-96), which he describes as the context where the model was "more or less perfected." The South African case involved the 1993 interim constitution establishing procedural frameworks and 34 constitutional principles, followed by the Constitutional Assembly drafting the 1996 final constitution under Constitutional Court review. The Court famously sent the first draft back for revision, demonstrating that the oversight mechanism had genuine teeth.

The key innovation is democratic self-restraint: even in foundational moments, the process includes checks, negotiated constraints, and multi-stage deliberation to prevent majoritarian overreach or power concentration. This directly addresses the pathology Chile demonstrated, where an unconstrained convention produced a document that the electorate rejected.

Arato's framework for post-sovereign constitution-making encounters its most severe test in the Iranian context. Kar (2003) examines constitutional constraints on rights protection in Iran, analyzing how formal constitutional commitments to individual liberty remain systematically undermined by extra-constitutional authorities and competing normative frameworks. Samii (2001) documents the Guardians Council as a structural obstacle to democratic constitutional governance, showing how designed ambiguity in the 1979 Constitution enabled clerical veto over popular sovereignty. Tarock (2002) provides historical analysis of reform efforts within Iran's constitutional order, demonstrating how successive attempts to advance democratic interpretation have failed against institutional resistance from security actors and clerical hierarchy. These sources reveal the depth of structural resistance facing any constitutionalizing process that attempts to weaken executive and security apparatus powers—the very outcome Arato's framework envisions.

Spain's 1978 constitutional framework created the "Estado de las Autonomías" (State of Autonomies) as a novel middle path between federalism and unitarism, fundamentally reshaping how post-authoritarian states organize territory and ethnicity. During the transition from Franco's centralized dictatorship, Spanish architects confronted a territorial question complicated by distinct historical nations—Catalonia, the Basque Country, and Galicia—each with separate linguistic and cultural traditions demanding recognition.

The initial constitutional design employed the principle of "café para todos" (coffee for everyone), offering all regions equal opportunities for autonomy status despite their vastly different political demands and historical weight. This symmetrical framework proved untenable; Catalonia and the Basque Country possessed mobilized nationalist movements with historical states, while smaller regions had weaker territorial consciousness. The constitution allowed for asymmetry through Article 151's supermajority pathway, enabling faster devolution to regions that demanded it—a safety valve that transformed the system into what scholars recognize as "asymmetric federalism."

Fiscal autonomy emerged as the critical cement binding the territorial compact. The Basque Country and Navarre negotiated cupo (quota) systems allowing them to collect most taxes and remit a portion to Madrid, fundamentally different from the standard revenue-sharing model. Catalonia secured similar arrangements through the 2006 Estatut d'Autonomia, gaining control over major tax bases. These fiscal compacts directly addressed how territorial elites could satisfy both their constituents' demands for self-governance and maintain economic interdependence with the central state.

The 2017 Catalan independence crisis tested this design to destruction. When Madrid revoked the 2006 Estatut, declaring key devolved powers unconstitutional, Catalan nationalism radicalized. The October 2017 referendum and declaration of independence exposed the Estado de las Autonomías' core vulnerability: asymmetric arrangements satisfied neither maximalist separatists nor those fearing privileged regions undermined equality. The crisis demonstrated that fiscal autonomy and even primary legislative power in cultural matters could not substitute for resolution of the fundamental question—whether a historical nation can be accommodated within a state structure without triggering separatism.

## Key Findings Across Case Studies

### The Process Question: Who Drafts and When

Research across eight cases demonstrates that constitution-making processes matter as much as constitutional content [316]. The central finding of Ginsburg, Elkins, and Blount's comparative study is that constitutional longevity correlates with the breadth of inclusion during drafting — constitutions written by narrow elite committees without popular ratification show significantly shorter lifespans than those subject to public deliberation and referendum [316]. This finding is corroborated by the Spanish, Indonesian, and Tunisian cases.

Spain's 1978 Constitution emerged from a carefully managed "pacted transition" (transición) in which democratic opposition forces and reformist Francoists jointly negotiated a constitutional text through the Cortes Generales, then ratified it by national referendum [391, 387]. Arato's post-sovereign constitution-making framework identifies Spain as a paradigmatic case of "learning from the authoritarian past while not fully breaking with it" — deliberate ambiguity on contested issues (regional autonomy, the Church's role, monarchical versus republican form) enabled sufficient consensus to proceed [391]. Constitutions in the global financial crisis scholarship confirms that Spain's elastic amendment provisions proved essential when financial crisis tested constitutional structures in 2010–2012 [394].

Indonesia's 1945 Constitution presented the opposite dynamic: a bare-bones text originally designed as a temporary measure that successive authoritarian regimes (Sukarno's Guided Democracy, Suharto's New Order) weaponized for their consolidation [1558]. The 1999–2002 amendment process — four sequential amendments rather than a single replacement — deliberately preserved constitutional continuity while systematically dismantling authoritarian provisions: term limits were introduced, direct presidential elections mandated, and a constitutional court established [1558, 1600]. Voigt's positive constitutional economics analysis confirms that Indonesia's incremental amendment approach reduced regime destabilization risks that a full replacement might have triggered [1600].

### Handling Authoritarian Legacy Provisions

The most consistent cross-case finding concerns the removal of "authoritarian enclaves" — constitutional provisions that privileged the outgoing regime's institutional base. Chile provides the clearest case: Pinochet's 1980 Constitution embedded high amendment thresholds (two-thirds supermajority), designated senators (unelected), and institutional veto players (military commanders as ex-officio senators) that functioned as insurance policies for the authoritarian right [5157, 5196]. Ensalaco's analysis documents how even the July 1989 plebiscite — approved by 85.7% — only partially dismantled these provisions, with the full removal of designated senators not completed until the 2005 Lagos reforms [5157, 5196].

Fuentes's comparative work on Chile's constitutional reforms demonstrates that authoritarian enclaves are removed not through confrontation but through a gradual erosion process that requires the authoritarian-era party system to accept reforms in exchange for stability guarantees [5196]. The mechanism is quid pro quo rather than victor's justice: right-wing parties accepted democratic reforms when their economic interests were constitutionally protected [5196].

The 2019 Chilean social uprising and subsequent 2022 constitutional drafting process provides a cautionary counter-case. Larraín, Negretto, and Voigt's analysis of the failed September 2022 referendum — in which Chilean voters rejected the new constitution 61.9% to 38.1% despite widespread dissatisfaction with the Pinochet document — identifies overreach in the constituent assembly process: the assembly's composition was dominated by left-wing and indigenous activists who drafted a document perceived as too ideologically maximalist by moderate voters [551]. This finding directly contradicts a simple "more participation = better outcomes" narrative; process design, ideological balance, and public trust in the drafting body all matter independently.

Poland's post-1989 constitutional trajectory demonstrates the risks of delayed constitution-making. The 1989 Roundtable Agreement between Solidarity and the Communist Party operated through a "constitutional moment" that redistributed power before a proper constitution was drafted [4052, 4057]. Poland operated under a heavily amended communist constitution until 1997 — an eight-year interregnum that, Lijphart's institutional design research suggests, created institutional path dependencies that later populist forces (Law and Justice after 2015) exploited to attack constitutional court independence [4052, 4061, 4062].

### Tunisia and Islamic Constitutionalism

Tunisia's 2014 Constitution is the most directly relevant comparator for Iran on the question of Islam's constitutional role. Ahmed and Ginsburg's cross-national study of "Islamic supremacy clauses" documents how Tunisia's National Constituent Assembly navigated internal conflict between Islamist Ennahda and secular opposition by adopting a compromise formula: Islam as the religion of the state but civil liberties constitutionally protected and no explicit reference to sharia as a legal source [539]. This "creative ambiguity" approach enabled the document's passage but created interpretive contests that contributed to President Saied's 2021 self-coup and suspension of the constitution [539].

Afary's historical analysis of Shi'i clerical participation in Iran's 1906 Constitutional Revolution provides important baseline comparison: even in Iran's first constitutional moment, the clerical class was internally divided between those supporting constitutionalism as a check on despotism and those fearing secular limitation of religious authority [635]. This internal division within religious establishments — present in both 1906 Iran and 2011 Tunisia — represents a structural feature with direct implications for future Iranian constitutional design.

### South Korea and the 1987 Moment

South Korea's 1987 constitutional transition exemplifies what scholars call the "critical juncture" model: mass popular mobilization (June Democracy Movement) forcing elite negotiation that produced a new constitutional framework [123, 97]. Yeh's comparative analysis of South Korean and Taiwanese constitutional adjudication demonstrates that the 1987 Constitution's creation of a strong Constitutional Court provided a vital institutional channel for resolving political conflicts without returning to authoritarian rule [123]. The constitutional court became a "fire break" preventing political crises from destabilizing the system — a function it demonstrated most dramatically in the 2016–2017 impeachment of President Park Geun-hye [123, 153].

The South Korean case also illustrates the "sequencing problem": constitutional design occurred under significant time pressure of military-civilian negotiation, producing a document that subsequent scholarly analysis identifies as containing internal tensions (a strong president alongside strong courts) that periodically generated constitutional crises [42, 123]. Constitutional reform debates in South Korea today center on precisely these structural ambiguities inherited from the rushed 1987 process [42].


## Cross-Country Patterns

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (13 sources) | 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟢 Strong** (78%)

Two cross-cutting patterns emerge from the longevity data. First, there is a consistent inverse relationship between constitutional rigidity and survival. Countries that designed their constitutions to be nearly unamendable (requiring unanimity or supermajority thresholds above 80%) experienced shorter constitutional lifespans than those with more moderate thresholds (60-67% legislative supermajority plus ratification). Second, the inclusivity effect is multiplicative rather than additive: constitutions that combined broad participation in drafting with public ratification survived significantly longer than those that had only one or the other.

The specificity finding challenges the minimalist school of constitutional design, which holds that constitutions should establish broad principles and leave details to legislation. The empirical evidence suggests the opposite: detailed constitutions that specify institutional roles, enumerate rights, and establish procedures survive longer, likely because they leave less room for powerful actors to reshape the framework through creative interpretation.

---

Mosler (2020) finds that the constitution's longevity (39 years without amendment) results from a paradox: the very factors that create pressure for reform also prevent consensus on what amendments should look like. Any change to presidential term structure benefits some political actors and threatens others. This gridlock-as-stability dynamic differs fundamentally from the Ginsburg/Elkins flexibility finding, suggesting that non-amendment can be a path to longevity under specific conditions: when the constitution is detailed enough to constrain actors and when the political cost of replacement exceeds the cost of living with imperfect provisions.

The "lemon presidency" pattern recurred across multiple administrations. Lee Myung-bak and Park Geun-hye both attempted to personalize their regimes through captured judicial authorities, despite the 1987 safeguards. This suggests that term limits alone are insufficient without independent judicial institutions capable of resisting executive capture.

---

The fault line emerged where ambiguity was greatest. Colino (2008) traces how bottom-up revision of subnational constitutional arrangements, particularly Catalonia's 2006 Statute of Autonomy reform, tested the limits of the 1978 framework. The Constitutional Court's 2010 ruling striking down portions of the Catalan Statute precipitated the secessionist crisis that culminated in the 2017 unilateral declaration of independence. Rico and Lineira (2014) document how secessionism moved from fringe to mainstream in the 2012 Catalan election.

The pattern reveals a fundamental tradeoff in constitutional design: creative ambiguity can enable transitions by deferring unresolvable conflicts, but the deferred conflicts do not disappear. They re-emerge when political actors push the ambiguous language beyond its elastic limits. The critical variable is whether the constitutional framework includes institutions capable of managing these deferred tensions when they surface: empowered courts, formal intergovernmental forums, and clear procedures for resolving center-periphery conflicts.

Two competing patterns emerge from this comparative analysis. First, constitutional ambiguity can facilitate consensus but generates downstream conflicts requiring authoritative resolution. Spain, Iran 1906, and Iran 1979 all illustrate how fuzzy constitutional language defers rather than resolves fundamental disputes (Martin 2011, Randjbar-Daemi 2013). Second, constitutional designers in fragmented polities face a choice: explicit conflict (impossible consensus) versus deferred conflict (ambiguous consensus). Boryslavska (2021) shows that post-1989 Eastern European democracies often chose earlier, more adversarial constitution-making processes that eliminated ambiguity through struggle rather than agreement.

Tunisia's experience reveals two structural patterns. First, civil society organizations with institutional weight (organized labor, professional associations, employer federations) can serve constitutional functions that go beyond advocacy: mediation, process guarantorship, and legitimacy anchoring. This role requires organizations with independent membership bases, organizational capacity for sustained engagement, and credibility with multiple political factions. Advocacy NGOs, however important, typically lack these characteristics.

Second, the iterative multi-draft approach produced demonstrably better outcomes than single-round processes. Each successive draft incorporated lessons from public debate, expert review, and political negotiation. The four-draft, 27-month timeline, initially criticized as excessive delay, proved essential for building the broad consensus reflected in the final 200-12 vote. Negretto and Sanchez-Talanquer (2021) provide the empirical framework, arguing that constitutions most enhance liberal democracy when they emerge through plural agreement among political elites with distinct social support bases.

Three patterns emerge from the Tunisia case. First, institutional innovation in constitution-making requires prior civic organizational capacity. Usman et al. (2025) document extensive civil society networks pre-existing the Quartet model, suggesting that institutional design alone cannot substitute for organizational infrastructure. Second, participatory constitution-making succeeds when narrowly focused on specific constitutional questions. Maboudi (2019) shows that Tunisia's deliberative success concentrated on fundamental rights and democratic procedures, where technical expertise and citizen input aligned, while remaining limited on state structure questions where political power remained centralized. Third, constitutional compromises may exclude key ideological groups. Lavie (2021) observes that Tunisia's constitutionalization of the civil state required negotiated settlements that did not fully satisfy either Islamist or secularist factions, illustrating the persistent tensions in post-uprising constitutional orders.

---

Chile's failure reveals a crucial pattern: procedural openness and substantive maximalism can be fatal in combination. The convention's fully public deliberations, while admirable in principle, prevented the behind-the-scenes compromises that enabled Spain's consenso. The dominance of independents without party structures meant that no actors had the organizational capacity to make binding commitments across issue areas, the essential mechanism of constitutional horse-trading.

The rejection also demonstrates that constitutional processes must maintain connection to median voter preferences. The convention's composition skewed heavily toward activist and left-leaning delegates, producing a document that reflected movement preferences rather than social consensus. The 62-38 rejection margin suggests the disconnect was substantial and well-understood by the electorate.

Two patterns constrain participatory maximalism in polarized settings. First, value polarization on non-negotiable issues (Smith 2023 documents religion and sexuality as exactly such issues in Latin America) cannot be overcome through deliberative procedures. When constitutional redesign explicitly addresses these values, participation mobilizes opposition rather than consensus. Second, institutional changes that attempt to expand participation may activate latent conflicts (Brieba 2019). Chile's electoral reforms designed to increase inclusivity destabilized existing party coalitions, making constitutional consensus impossible. Lijphart (1996) shows that majoritarian systems achieve consensus through power-sharing among pre-existing elites, not through expanded mass participation on fundamental issues.

---

The post-sovereign model addresses a fundamental tension in constitutional design: how to create a democratic founding document through a process that is itself democratically constrained. Revolutionary constituent assemblies claim unlimited power, which risks majoritarian overreach (as Chile demonstrated). Imposed constitutions lack democratic legitimacy. The two-stage model resolves this by separating the procedural framework (negotiated at the roundtable) from the substantive constitution (drafted by an elected assembly within that framework).

South Africa's experience demonstrates that the oversight mechanism must have genuine enforcement capacity. When the Constitutional Court returned the first draft, it established that the assembly was bound by the constitutional principles agreed in the roundtable stage. This creates a hierarchy of democratic legitimacy: the roundtable agreement constrains the assembly, and the constitutional court enforces those constraints.

Three critical constraints emerge from Iran's constitutional history. First, constitutional ambiguity about ultimate authority persists across institutional actors: Samii (2001) shows the Guardians Council operates as a shadow veto player unconstrained by constitutional text that ostensibly limits its powers. Second, constitutional reform itself becomes blocked when security actors perceive institutional changes as threats (Tarock 2002). Third, rights provisions become hollow when unconstrained executive and security powers remain institutionalized (Kar 2003). Arato's two-stage model assumes space for civil society constitution-making followed by institutional consolidation; Iran's experience suggests security actors will resist both stages if they perceive genuine constitutional constraints.

---

Post-authoritarian constitutional designers face an enduring dilemma: whether to grant equal autonomy to all regions (symmetry) or to accommodate historically mobilized nations through differentiated powers (asymmetry). Symmetry offers democratic elegance and prevents resentment among regions denied special status, but fails to address why some territories possess institutionalized national movements with centuries of distinct governance. Asymmetry recognizes political reality and historical difference, but generates zero-sum conflicts where denied regions demand equal treatment.

Spain's Estado de las Autonomías attempted to transcend this binary through recursive asymmetry—offering all regions the formal right to asymmetric arrangements while few possessed the political organization to exercise it. This created stability through asymmetric equilibrium: regions with nationalist movements gained accelerated devolution and fiscal autonomy, while regions without such pressure remained integrated but could theoretically demand parity. The mechanism worked through three decades because nationalist mobilization remained geographically contained.

Fiscal autonomy proved essential to sustaining the territorial bargain. When historically-mobile regions gained control over revenue generation and spending, they developed stake in the system beyond cultural symbolism. The Basque cupo system and Catalan tax arrangements created administratively-complex but politically-functional interdependence. However, fiscal autonomy's success depends on equilibrium: if autonomy advances seem one-directional, or if the center reclaims powers, the compact unravels. The 2017 crisis erupted when Madrid's Constitutional Court reasserted central authority precisely when Catalonia expected continued devolution, creating the perception of broken promises.

The Spanish model's broader applicability depends on institutional prerequisites: a constitutional court accepted as neutral arbiter, multiple veto points preventing unilateral revision, political elite commitment to elite-pacting rather than majoritarianism, and crucially, willingness of historically-dominant regions to accept reduced privilege. Spain's 2017 crisis reveals these are not inevitable—they depend on sustained political will.

## Cross-Case Patterns and CMO Analysis

### Pattern 1: The Participation-Legitimacy Trade-off

Across all cases, a consistent tension emerges between the speed of drafting (necessary for political stability) and the breadth of participation (necessary for legitimacy). Spain's rapid 1978 process achieved closure because elite actors controlled the process and made deliberate bargains [391, 387]. Tunisia's longer 2011–2014 process achieved broader legitimacy but at the cost of internal conflicts that weakened the resulting document's durability [539]. Chile's 2019–2022 process demonstrates that participatory design without institutional guardrails (ideological balance requirements, moderation mechanisms) can produce a document too radical to achieve ratification [551].

The meta-pattern is: constitutional processes that are participatory but include structured elite veto mechanisms (Spain's Cortes negotiations, Indonesia's sequential amendments, Poland's parliamentary roundtable) show higher durability than processes that are either purely elite-driven (like Pinochet's 1980 Constitution, produced by a commission of military and technocratic loyalists) or purely populist (like Chile's 2022 constituent assembly).

### Pattern 2: Amendment Design Predicts Survival

Elkins, Ginsburg, and Blount's empirical finding — corroborated across cases here — is that constitutional survival correlates with the existence of well-designed amendment procedures: neither too rigid (creating pressure for extra-constitutional change) nor too flexible (inviting continuous constitutional engineering) [316, 334]. Indonesia's constitution was amended four times in four years, then settled — the amendment process itself was the transition mechanism [1558]. Spain's constitution has been formally amended only twice (1992 and 2011) but its ambiguity has permitted significant interpretive evolution [387, 391].

Poland represents the pathological variant: a constitution with strong amendment procedures that a parliamentary majority was able to circumvent through ordinary legislation, attacking the Constitutional Tribunal through statute rather than constitutional amendment [4061, 4062]. This reveals a critical mechanism: formal constitutional entrenchment is only as durable as the political culture that enforces it.

### Pattern 3: Authoritarian Legacy as Constitutional Path Dependency

The Czech Republic's 1993 constitution after the Velvet Divorce illustrates how rapid, elite-managed constitutional design can produce surprisingly durable institutions when (a) the authoritarian legacy is externally located (Soviet pressure rather than indigenous military rule), (b) the transition elites share broadly liberal institutional commitments, and (c) EU accession provides external anchoring [864, 4057]. This differs structurally from cases where the authoritarian establishment is domestic and retains significant coercive capacity at the constitutional moment.

Iran's structural position differs from all comparative cases on this dimension: no case in the dataset combines an authoritarian establishment with significant coercive capacity (IRGC, Basij, VEVAK), an institutionalized theocratic framework built into the constitutional text (Velayat-e Faqih), and an absence of external anchoring institutions (EU accession, IMF conditionality). The closest analogues on the "entrenched authoritarian in the text" dimension are Chile (military enclaves) and Tunisia (Islamic supremacy clauses), but neither approaches the structural depth of Iran's constitutional entrenchment problem.

### Pattern 4: Timing and Sequencing

The cases consistently show that constitutional design works best when it follows (not precedes) minimal stabilization: a functioning transitional authority, basic security guarantees for key actors, and at least preliminary elite agreement on the rules of the transition game. Indonesia delayed its constitutional process because Suharto's fall was chaotic; this delay paradoxically allowed Indonesian civil society to organize and produce more legitimate input [1558]. Chile's 2019 uprising moved too quickly to a constituent assembly without adequate institutional filtering, resulting in collapse [551].

Constitutional design for Iran must now be analyzed through the lens of constitution-writing under conditions of foreign military intervention and negotiated ceasefire — a fundamentally different analytical category than constitution-writing under domestic political transition. The comparative evidence from Iraq, Afghanistan, Bosnia, and Kosovo provides specific and often cautionary lessons.

**1. The Intervention-Era Constitution-Writing Problem**

The central finding from Iraq, Afghanistan, and Bosnia is that constitutions written under external military pressure reflect the power dynamics of the intervention rather than the organic political preferences of the society. Iraq's 2005 constitution, drafted under US occupation with a compressed timeline mandated by the Transitional Administrative Law (TAL), produced a document that entrenched ethno-sectarian division through federalism provisions that reflected US preferences for Kurdish autonomy and Shia majority governance rather than Iraqi national consensus. The constitution's Article 140 (Kirkuk referendum), Article 111 (oil revenue distribution), and the ambiguous federalism provisions in Chapter V became sources of sustained political conflict precisely because they reflected external priorities.

Afghanistan's Bonn Agreement (December 2001) established a constitutional framework under direct US military occupation. The resulting 2004 constitution created a presidential system reflecting American preferences, concentrating power in Kabul in ways that proved incompatible with Afghanistan's decentralized political geography. The constitution's centralist design contributed to the governance failures that ultimately enabled the Taliban's return.

Bosnia's Dayton Agreement (1995) embedded the constitution as Annex 4 of a peace treaty — making the constitution itself an artifact of external mediation. The resulting two-entity structure (Federation of Bosnia and Herzegovina / Republika Srpska) froze wartime ethnic division into constitutional architecture, creating governance paralysis that persists three decades later.

**2. Iran's Constitutional Design Challenge Under Islamabad**

If the Islamabad talks produce a framework agreement that includes constitutional reform provisions — as ceasefire agreements frequently do — Iran faces the risk of a Dayton-type outcome: constitutional design driven by external negotiating dynamics rather than domestic political deliberation. Mojtaba Khamenei's legitimacy deficit (his selection by the Assembly of Experts was contested, and his authority lacks the revolutionary credentials of his father or Khomeini) creates additional pressure: he may accept externally driven constitutional concessions to secure regime survival, producing a constitution that stabilizes short-term power arrangements but lacks popular legitimacy.

The institutions requiring constitutional reconstitution remain those identified in pre-intervention analysis: replacement of the Supreme Leader's office with a distributed authority structure; replacement of the Guardian Council with an independent constitutional court; abolition of the Assembly of Experts; transformation of the Expediency Council; and integration of parallel governance structures (IRGC, bonyads) into civilian constitutional authority. However, the intervention changes the sequencing and political feasibility of each reform.

**3. What Intervention Enables vs. Blocks**

*Enabled:* External pressure can force constitutional concessions that domestic politics alone could not achieve. Iraq's Kurdistan Region gained constitutional recognition of its autonomy through the US-backed drafting process — a concession that Saddam's regime would never have granted voluntarily. For Iran, intervention pressure could force: abolition of velayat-e faqih as a governing principle; recognition of minority language and cultural rights (Kurdish, Azerbaijani, Baloch, Arab); separation of religious and state authority; and IRGC subordination to civilian authority.

*Blocked:* External pressure systematically undermines constitutional legitimacy. Constitutions perceived as imposed by foreign powers lack the democratic legitimacy that sustains them through political crises. Iraq's constitution, despite a popular referendum, was widely perceived as an American document — a perception that undermined its authority during the 2006-2008 civil war and the 2014 ISIS crisis. For Iran, any constitution visibly shaped by the Islamabad framework risks the same delegitimization, particularly given Iranian nationalism's deep sensitivity to foreign imposition (rooted in the 1953 coup, the Capitulations crisis, and the 1919 Anglo-Persian Agreement).

**4. Process Design: Lessons for Iran**

The comparative evidence suggests several process design principles: (a) Temporal separation between ceasefire and constitution-writing — the Bonn model's immediate constitutional framework produced better results than Dayton's embedded constitution, because Bonn established a transitional administration (2001-2004) before the constitutional loya jirga, allowing political relationships to develop before constitutional choices were fixed; (b) Inclusive drafting processes — Iraq's constitutional committee was dominated by Shia and Kurdish parties with Sunni underrepresentation, producing a document that excluded a major community. Iran's process must include representatives of ethnic minorities (Kurds, Azerbaijanis, Baloch, Arabs), women, secular nationalists, religious reformists, and diaspora voices; (c) International facilitation without imposition — the South African model, where international actors provided technical assistance without dictating outcomes, produced a more legitimate constitution than the Iraq or Bosnia models where external actors drove content; (d) Referendum legitimacy — any constitutional outcome must be validated through a genuinely free popular referendum, not a referendum conducted under occupation conditions (as in Iraq 2005) or a referendum boycotted by major groups.

**5. Mojtaba Khamenei's Legitimacy Deficit and Constitutional Reform**

Mojtaba Khamenei's contested succession creates a specific constitutional dynamic: he may seek constitutional reform that legitimizes his authority (perhaps transforming the Supreme Leader role into an elected presidency with enhanced powers) while resisting reforms that eliminate the velayat-e faqih framework entirely. This is analogous to negotiated transitions where regime incumbents accept partial democratization to preserve personal power — the Pinochet model in Chile (1988 plebiscite preserving military prerogatives) or the SCAF model in Egypt (2011-2013 constitutional manipulation preserving military autonomy). The risk is a "managed constitutionalism" that reforms the surface while preserving authoritarian institutional cores.

## Key Research Questions Derived from Analysis

1. **The Velayat-e Faqih substitution problem:** What constitutional mechanisms can substitute for the Guardian Council's legislative review function and the Supreme Leader's coordination role during a transition period without creating a constitutional power vacuum? The Spain case (retaining monarchy as coordination mechanism) and Tunisia case (retaining Article 1's Islam clause) suggest partial answers, but no case has managed the equivalent structural depth of IRI theocratic entrenchment.

2. **Federal-consociational design for Iran's ethnic minorities:** How should a post-transition Iranian constitution balance centralized sovereignty with meaningful autonomy for Kurdish, Azeri, Arab, and Baluch communities? Indonesia's decentralization process (post-1999 regional autonomy laws operating under the new constitution) provides comparative reference, but Iran's ethnic geography and the IRI's history of suppressing minority autonomy movements create unique design requirements. No existing case study in the dataset addresses the intersection of ethnic federalism and post-theocratic constitutional design.

3. **Amendment threshold calibration:** What amendment procedure design minimizes both the risk of authoritarian entrenchment (too rigid, as in Chile 1980–2005) and the risk of constitutional erosion (too flexible, as in Poland post-2015)? Ginsburg et al.'s general findings [316] need to be tested against Iran-specific institutional conditions, particularly the role of a future constitutional court in enforcing amendment rules against potential populist majorities.

4. **Sequencing and transitional authority:** Under what transitional governance arrangements (caretaker government, revolutionary council, internationally-supervised interim authority) can Iranian constitutional design be initiated that minimizes the risk of a single faction capturing the process? The Roundtable model (Poland 1989), the National Dialogue model (Tunisia 2013–2014), and the parliamentary committee model (Indonesia 1999–2002) represent different sequencing strategies whose applicability to Iran's specific transition scenarios requires dedicated analysis.

## Research Gaps and Knowledge Limitations

### Gap 1: Post-Theocratic Constitutional Design — Absence of Direct Comparators

The single most significant knowledge gap in this comparative study is the absence of any case that successfully dismantled a constitutionally entrenched theocratic governing authority through democratic constitutional redesign. Tunisia's 2014 experience (negotiating Ennahda's participation in a partially secular constitutional order) is the closest, but the structural depth of IRI theocratic entrenchment (Velayat-e Faqih as a complete constitutional doctrine, not merely an "Islam as state religion" clause) has no parallel in the comparison set. This represents a genuine comparative frontier: the transition literature has not yet produced a case study equivalent to what Iran would require.

### Gap 2: IRGC Economic Interests and Constitutional Bargaining

The existing literature on authoritarian enclave removal (Chile, Spain) focuses primarily on military institutional interests (career paths, budget autonomy, amnesty for past crimes). The IRGC's situation differs: its economic interests are constitutionally and extra-constitutionally embedded at a scale (estimated 20–40% of Iran's GDP through bonyad and Khatam al-Anbiya networks) with no equivalent in the comparison cases. There is no research in this dataset that systematically analyzes how constitutionally-embedded economic empires of this magnitude are addressed in constitutional bargaining. The Chile case addresses economic policy but not military-economic empires of this structural depth.

### Gap 3: Diaspora Role in Constitutional Design

Iran's diaspora (estimated 4–5 million Iranians abroad, with significant concentrations of constitutional lawyers, academics, and activists) represents a constitutional drafting resource with no parallel in Spain, Indonesia, or South Korea at equivalent transition moments. The comparative literature does not adequately address how diaspora communities can be incorporated into constitutional design processes in ways that are legitimate domestically without being captured by diaspora political factions who may not accurately represent in-country preferences. Tunisia's National Constituent Assembly explicitly excluded Tunisian diaspora from voting, while later Bolivian and Iraqi constitution-making processes attempted incorporation with mixed results.

### Gap 4: Ethnic Federalism and Post-Theocratic Transition Interaction Effects

The dataset contains significant literature on federalism and decentralization (Indonesia, Spain) and on theocratic constitutional design (Tunisia, Iran), but no literature that specifically addresses how ethnic federal demands interact with the dismantlement of theocratic constitutional structures. For Iran, this interaction is central: Kurdish, Baluch, and Arab communities have had their autonomy demands suppressed partly through IRI theocratic governance frameworks. A post-IRI constitutional moment would simultaneously require addressing both theocratic dismantlement and federal redesign, an interaction that the comparative literature does not model.

### Gap 5: Constitutional Process Design Under Sanctions Conditions

All comparative cases with successful constitutional redesign operated in contexts of either international normalization (South Korea, Spain's EU path) or at minimum, absence of active international economic warfare. Iran's constitutional transition will likely occur under some form of sanctions regime, affecting the resources available for constitutional design processes (international technical assistance, funding for participatory processes, convening support) and potentially creating international pressure dynamics that are absent from the comparison cases. The literature does not address how severe economic constraints imposed by external actors shape constitutional design choices.

### Methodological Caveat

The evidence confidence for Chile and Spain is HIGH (multiple independent sources, strong convergent findings, direct case documentation). For Indonesia, evidence confidence is MODERATE (two primary sources but high Iran utility scores). For Poland, Czech Republic, and South Korea, evidence confidence is MODERATE (solid sources but primary findings come from institutional design literature rather than direct constitutional process documentation). For Tunisia and Iran, evidence confidence is MODERATE-HIGH for historical analysis and LOW for forward-looking implications, as Tunisia's constitutional failure (2021 Saied coup) occurred after most sources were published and Iran's transition dynamics are inherently speculative.

## Theoretical Framework

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Constitutional design scholarship draws on three major theoretical traditions that structure how scholars understand why constitutions succeed or fail.

**Historical Institutionalism** treats constitutions as path-dependent frameworks: the choices made during founding moments constrain all subsequent political development. This tradition emphasizes that the politics of constitutional drafting leave lasting marks on institutional evolution. Constitutions that emerge from negotiated settlements tend to produce more inclusive political systems than those imposed by dominant actors.

**Rational Choice Institutionalism** models constitutional design as a coordination game: parties to the founding agree on rules because they are uncertain about their future political positions. This Rawlsian "veil of ignorance" insight explains why constitutional framers often agree to minority protections and rights provisions they might otherwise reject — they cannot know whether they will be in the majority or minority once the constitution takes effect.

**Empirical Constitutionalism** (associated with Ginsburg, Elkins, and colleagues) uses large-N comparative data to test which constitutional features actually predict longevity, democratic performance, and human rights compliance. This tradition has yielded the most policy-relevant findings: amendment flexibility predicts survival; inclusive drafting predicts rights compliance; power distribution predicts stability.

For Iran, all three frameworks are relevant. Historical institutionalism warns that Iran's theocratic founding in 1979 will be a contested reference point in any successor constitution. Rational choice theory suggests that uncertainty about post-transition winners creates incentives for negotiated compromise. Empirical constitutionalism provides specific design prescriptions tested across many transitions.
