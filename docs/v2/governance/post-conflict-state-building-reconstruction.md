# دولت‌سازی و بازسازی پس از جنگ



## نمای کلی

**Post-conflict state-building** involves the reconstruction of political, security, economic, and social institutions following armed conflict. The comparative record across Iraq (2003-present), Afghanistan (2001-2021), Bosnia (1995-present), Kosovo (1999-present), and post-war Germany and Japan reveals divergent outcomes shaped by: the mode of regime termination, the extent of institutional destruction during conflict, the presence of spoiler groups, reconstruction financing structures, and the governance model imposed. The most consistent finding is that state-building fails when security precedes institution-building but institutions fail to deliver services before their social contract window closes — typically 18-36 months post-conflict. The 2026 Iran ceasefire creates a potential state-building window whose durability depends on Islamabad talks outcomes.

## چارچوب نظری

**Reconstruction sequencing theory** (Paris 2004): Liberalization before institutionalization (LBI) — moving too quickly to free markets and elections before establishing security and rule of law — consistently produces fragile outcomes. Iraq (2005 elections before security consolidation) entrenched sectarian politics rather than building national coalitions.

**De-Ba'athification and lustration theory** (Loyle & Binningsbø 2018; Stan & Nedelsky 2013): Comprehensive lustration succeeded in Czech Republic and East Germany where: (a) security services were discredited; (b) replacement talent pools existed; and (c) international integration provided an anchor. Iraq's de-Ba'athification failed because it removed mid-level technocrats essential for state function, dissolved the army without reintegration provision, and created an unemployed armed class that fed insurgency. The lesson for de-IRGC-ification: targeted vs. comprehensive vetting produces better stability outcomes.

**Aid effectiveness and reconstruction conditionality** (Collier 2007; Easterly 2006): Post-conflict aid works best when conditionality is achievable and monitored, local ownership is genuine, and aid modalities match institutional capacity. The Afghan case demonstrated how aid exceeding absorptive capacity creates corruption and parallel governance rather than institution-building.

**Iran transferability**: Iran's pre-existing state institutions (bureaucracy, judiciary, central bank, universities) are stronger than Iraq's or Afghanistan's. This reduces the institutional void problem but creates the opposite challenge: which institutions to preserve vs. reform. The IRGC's economic empire penetrates institutions in ways that make selective reform politically difficult.

## تحلیل ایران

**Current relevance — April 2026:** The Islamabad talks will implicitly negotiate the post-conflict reconstruction framework for Iran. Three models are possible:

1. **Iraq 2003 model** (occupation + de-Ba'athification equivalent): Demands for comprehensive IRGC dissolution, bonyad restructuring, and constitutional redesign under external supervision. The de-Ba'athification parallel suggests this approach would destroy state capacity faster than it can be rebuilt — particularly given Iran's IRGC control of 15-25% of GDP.

2. **Iraq 1991 model** (sanctions + inspections without regime change): External verification mechanisms (nuclear inspectors, IRGC capacity monitors) combined with phased sanctions relief. Preserves state function but risks reconstruction stagnation if sanctions relief is too slow.

3. **Kosovo/Bosnia model** (benchmarked transition with international support): Conditionality linked to institutional milestones (elections, constitutional reform, IRGC economic divestment) with international reconstruction support. Most likely to produce stable outcomes but requires credible international commitment Iran has not historically granted.

**De-IRGC-ification challenge**: Unlike de-Ba'athification, which targeted a party with clear membership records, IRGC penetration of Iranian institutions is structural and economic. Dissolving the IRGC as a military force while preserving its economic assets would create a new oligarchic structure; dissolving both would create an immediate governance vacuum. The Afghanistan model of nominal integration with retained autonomy is the most likely outcome under time pressure.
