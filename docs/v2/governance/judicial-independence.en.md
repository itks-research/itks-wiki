## Overview

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

Democratic transitions routinely stumble over one uncomfortable question: who will adjudicate the rules of the new order if the judges themselves are products of the old one? Judicial independence—the capacity of courts to decide cases on the basis of law rather than political pressure—is not merely a procedural nicety. It is the institution that enforces every other institutional commitment. Without courts willing and able to protect property rights, enforce contracts, discipline executive overreach, and safeguard individual rights against state power, other democratic reforms remain precarious. This is why comparative scholars increasingly describe judicial reform not as one item on the transition checklist but as the foundational condition that determines whether the rest of the checklist holds.

Comparative evidence from post-authoritarian transitions is simultaneously encouraging and sobering. Several countries—Spain, South Korea, Chile—managed to build genuinely independent judiciaries over one to two decades, and those judiciaries have since proved durable against political pressure. Eastern European experience is more mixed: countries that rushed judicial reform for EU accession built formal structures without the professional culture and resource autonomy to sustain them, and some—Poland and Hungary most visibly—experienced significant democratic backsliding through judicial capture after 2010. The pattern emerging from this literature is that formal independence (constitutional guarantees, security of tenure) is necessary but insufficient; what matters equally is the socialization of judges into a professional identity that treats political insulation as a core value.

Iran's situation differs from any of these comparison cases in a foundational way. The question in Poland or Hungary was whether a nominally independent judiciary could resist re-capture. In Iran, the question is how to build something that has never existed. The Islamic Republic's judiciary was designed as an enforcement arm of the theocratic state, not as an independent check on it. Reforming it is therefore not a matter of reversing recent damage but of constructing a new institutional identity from the ground up—while simultaneously managing the legacy of a court system that many Iranians associate with political persecution, property confiscation, and the suppression of civil society.

This topic page examines what comparative scholarship tells us about the conditions under which new democracies successfully construct independent judiciaries, what failure modes are most common, and how Iran's specific starting conditions—the structure of the existing judiciary, the legal traditions available to draw on, the role of informal networks—shape both the challenges and the realistic options.

## Theoretical Framework

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (8 sources)

This topic page applies a Context-Mechanism-Outcome (CMO) framework to analyze judicial independence in democratic transitions. The CMO approach, developed in realist evaluation research (Pawson and Tilley 1997) and adapted for comparative institutional analysis, asks not simply "does judicial reform work?" but "what mechanisms produce what outcomes under what contextual conditions?" This matters for Iran because the mechanisms that enabled judicial independence in Spain or South Korea may function very differently in a context defined by theocratic legal philosophy, deep distrust of state institutions, and an absence of prior democratic practice.

Three theoretical traditions structure the analysis. The first is historical institutionalism, which emphasizes path dependence: institutions develop along trajectories shaped by their founding moments, and early choices about appointment procedures, organizational culture, and resource allocation have long-lasting effects. This tradition helps explain why judicial reform is so difficult even when political will exists—the existing structure creates vested interests, professional norms, and organizational routines that resist change. Mahoney and Thelen's (2010) typology of gradual institutional change (layering, conversion, displacement, drift) provides vocabulary for understanding how judicial systems transform incrementally rather than through single-moment replacement.

The second tradition is principal-agent theory applied to courts. Judges are agents empowered by constitutional principals (constitutions, legislatures, electorates), but they face competing pressures from political actors who appoint them, promote them, or control their institutional resources. The key design question is how to align judicial incentives with legal rather than political accountability. Appointments procedures, security of tenure, salary protections, and the existence of professional corps with reputational stakes in judicial independence are the primary mechanisms. Piana's (2009) concept of "back-door" political access—mechanisms by which political actors reach courts without formally violating judicial independence—provides the key analytical lens for this page's comparative evidence.

The third tradition concerns legitimacy and social trust. Courts derive authority not only from formal power but from the belief among citizens, lawyers, and other political actors that their decisions reflect law rather than politics. This legitimacy is fragile in post-authoritarian contexts: years of politically instrumentalized courts leave a legacy of distrust that new institutions must overcome. The practical implication is that structural reforms alone are insufficient; the rebuilding of judicial legitimacy requires visible behavioral change—reversals of politically motivated convictions, transparency in appointments, and decisions that go against powerful actors—over a sustained period. This framework organizes the comparative evidence and Iran-specific analysis that follow, linking institutional design choices to mechanisms of legitimacy-building and outcomes of durable judicial independence.

## Evidence & Key Findings

Poland's judicial independence crisis after 2015 provides the most vivid contemporary illustration of how democratic backsliding can operate through legal institutions rather than against them. Drinóczi and Bień-Kacała (2019) document how Hungary and Poland developed parallel models of "illiberal constitutionalism" — using parliamentary supermajorities to restructure courts, pack judicial councils, and redefine the constitutional order from within. In Poland, the Law and Justice party's (PiS) capture of the Constitutional Tribunal, followed by legislation subordinating the Supreme Court and ordinary judiciary to political appointment, demonstrated that judicial independence can be dismantled incrementally through formally legal procedures. Magalhães (1999) had earlier identified the structural vulnerability: post-communist judicial reforms across Eastern Europe created formally independent judiciaries that lacked the institutional depth — professional socialization, public legitimacy, resource autonomy — to resist political pressure when it eventually came.

Piana (2009) provides the theoretical framework, arguing that political power reaches courts through "back doors" — appointment mechanisms, budgetary control, disciplinary procedures, and case assignment systems — rather than through overt interference. This insight is critical for Iran, where the judiciary's lack of independence is not hidden but constitutionally explicit: the head of the judiciary is appointed by the Supreme Leader, and the institution operates as an instrument of regime enforcement rather than a check on power.

For Iran, the comparative literature on rule of law presents a distinctive challenge. Tabari (2003) examines the tension between rule of law aspirations and political reality in post-revolutionary Iran, showing how reformist efforts under Khatami repeatedly collided with structural constraints embedded in the constitutional architecture. Arjomand and Brown (2013) compare constitutional politics in Egypt and Iran, finding that both systems created judiciaries that served legitimation functions for ruling authorities while maintaining a veneer of legal process — a pattern that Poland's PiS government replicated through democratic rather than revolutionary means.

---

Poland's judicial independence journey illustrates both the possibilities and perils of post-authoritarian democratic reconstruction. After 1989, Poland undertook significant institutional reforms, establishing an independent judiciary seemingly insulated from political pressure through constitutional protections and a reformed court system (Drináóczi & Bienvenu-Kacala, 2019). The Constitutional Court and Supreme Court were redesigned with tenure protections and peer review mechanisms intended to prevent executive capture.

However, the Law and Justice (PiS) government's ascension in 2015 exposed the fragility of these institutional safeguards. Between 2015 and 2023, Poland implemented a systematic judicial capture strategy: lowering retirement ages for judges (allowing political appointments), introducing political vetting for judicial promotions, undermining the National Council of the Judiciary's independence, and creating parallel judicial structures loyal to executive interests (Adamska-Gallant, 2022). The capture was not crude—it operated through nominally neutral institutional changes that cumulatively degraded judicial independence.

The European Union responded with Article 7 proceedings in 2017, citing violations of democratic standards and the rule of law (Zamęcki & Glied, 2020). By 2023, when Poland's government changed, the restoration of judicial independence faced extraordinary challenges: reinstating purged judges required navigating both EU jurisprudence and domestic resistance from captured institutions themselves. As Grabowska-Moroz (2023) documents in the Żurek v Poland case, even after political change, implementing rule of law restoration created new tensions, with judges themselves becoming rule-of-law actors in opposition to institutional inertia.

Poland's experience reveals that institutional design—constitutional courts, judicial independence clauses, tenure protections—provides necessary but insufficient protection against determined political majorities. The capture required no formal constitutional amendment but rather demonstrated how liberal democratic institutions can be slowly hollowed from within through procedural manipulation (Kos, 2023). The restoration process, ongoing today, shows that rebuilding judicial independence after systematic capture is far more difficult than building it from democratic inception (Solska, 2025).

---

Spain's post-Franco judicial reform (1978–1990s) offers a cautionary model of how democracies can institutionalize courts without root-and-branch purges. Rather than dismissing Franco-era judges, Spain's 1978 Constitution created new institutional structures: an independent Constitutional Court (1980), strengthened judicial councils, and reformed selection processes (Magalhães et al. 2006). Critically, Spain maintained most existing judges but insulated them through constitutional and organic law changes—a gradualist approach that prioritized stability over revolutionary cleansing. However, this meant Franco-era judicial culture persisted, and the judiciary's reputation remained tainted for decades.

Poland's experience from 1989 to 2015, and then again from 2015 onwards, reveals both the promise and fragility of judicial independence in post-communist transitions. Initial reforms under EU pre-accession conditionality and Council of Europe pressure were substantial: independent Constitutional Courts, judicial councils with peer input, and merit-based appointment procedures (Magalhães 1999; Piana 2009). Yet the Law and Justice Party's (PiS) capture of the courts from 2015 onwards—through lowered retirement ages, controlled judicial appointments, and politicized disciplinary procedures—demonstrated how constitutionally embedded safeguards can be dismantled when populist governments control constitutional amending procedures and electoral legitimacy (Drinóczi & Bień-Kacała 2019). The EU's external pressure proved insufficient once a government framed court reform as "de-communization" and populist reclamation.

These contrasting paths—Spain's institutional layering without purges versus Poland's captured courts despite initial safeguards—expose the core tension: how can post-authoritarian judiciaries become truly independent when the regime's collapse leaves existing judges, legal cultures, and constitutional architectures in place? Neither institutional design alone nor external pressure guarantees immunity from future capture.

## Cross-Country Patterns

The cross-cutting pattern is sobering: judicial independence is both the most fragile and the most consequential institution in transitional democracies. Countries that achieved genuine judicial independence early (Spain's Constitutional Tribunal, South Korea's Constitutional Court after 1988) built self-reinforcing legitimacy that made subsequent political capture difficult. Countries where judicial reform was delayed or incomplete (Poland, Hungary) found that initial democratic gains could be reversed once populist governments discovered the "back door" mechanisms Piana describes.

A second pattern concerns the role of international anchoring. Poland's judicial capture was constrained — though not prevented — by EU rule-of-law mechanisms, including infringement proceedings and conditionality on recovery funds. Iran would lack this external anchor entirely, making domestic institutional design even more critical. The comparative evidence suggests that constitutional courts need not just formal independence but "thick" institutional protections: staggered appointments, supermajority requirements for removal, autonomous budgets, and professional corps insulated from political rotation.

---

The Poland case exemplifies a broader vulnerability in judicial independence: newly liberated judiciaries possess institutional forms but lack deep cultural roots. Institutional design assumes rational compliance by political actors, yet democratic backsliding occurs precisely when those actors decide that institutional constraints are obstacles to their preferred policies rather than legitimate safeguards. Poland's judges were not corrupt in the classical sense; rather, political actors systematized the conversion of judicial selection into patronage machinery.

This pattern reveals that judicial independence requires two complementary foundations: formal institutions AND a political culture that respects their autonomy. Neither alone suffices. Democracies with entrenched rule-of-law cultures (judicial appointment independence, bar association autonomy, public accountability expectations) resist capture even with weak institutions; conversely, strong institutions in polities lacking rule-of-law consensus become targets for systematic dismantling. Poland's post-1989 institutions were moderately strong but operated within a society where judicial independence had no historical precedent and could be reframed as "unaccountable judges blocking popular will." The cultural deficit proved more consequential than institutional specification.

---

Three patterns emerge across these cases. First, institutional designs that separate court governance from electoral politics—constitutional courts, independent judiciaries, and insulated appointment procedures—are necessary but insufficient. They require sustained political consensus to protect them, especially in polarized societies. Second, transitional justice approaches differ sharply: Spain chose continuity with institutional reform; Poland inherited communist judicial structures but reformed them; Iran never underwent institutional discontinuity—the Islamic Republic embedded clerical authority in the constitution itself. This means Iran's reform challenge is fundamentally different: not rehabilitating a captured judiciary or transitioning from authoritarian judges, but decoupling Islamic jurisprudence and the Guardian Council's judicial oversight from party-political and regime-preservation functions. Third, external pressure (EU conditionality, Council of Europe mechanisms) can accelerate formal institutional change, but it cannot create political will to defend those institutions against domestic populist backlash or theocratic consolidation.

## Iran Analysis

Iran's judiciary presents a challenge fundamentally different from Poland's: the starting point is not a formally independent judiciary that has been captured but an institution that was never designed for independence. The head of the judiciary is appointed by the Supreme Leader for renewable five-year terms, and the institution has functioned as an enforcement arm of the regime — prosecuting political dissidents, enforcing morality laws, and managing property disputes in ways that serve connected interests. Osanloo (2020) documents how even the limited spaces for judicial discretion, such as victim forgiveness mechanisms in murder cases, operate within a framework that reinforces rather than checks state power.

Any transition would face the question of whether to reform the existing judiciary or create parallel transitional institutions. The Polish experience suggests that reforming a captured judiciary from within is extraordinarily difficult — even after PiS lost power in 2023, untangling the damage to judicial appointments and institutional culture proved slow and contested. For Iran, where capture is not a recent pathology but a founding feature, the challenge is orders of magnitude greater. The seminary system's role in judicial appointments (many judges have seminary rather than law school training), the Special Clerical Court's separate jurisdiction, and the existence of revolutionary courts operating outside normal legal procedure all create layers of institutional complexity that no post-communist transition had to address.

Saffari (1993) traces this architecture to the 1906 Constitutional Revolution, showing how clerical authority over legal interpretation was embedded in Iran's constitutional tradition well before the Islamic Republic. This path dependency means that judicial reform cannot simply adopt a European model of constitutional courts — it must reckon with a century of institutional evolution in which religious authority and legal authority have been intertwined.

**Institutional Mapping:** Judicial reform in Iran engages a dense institutional web: the **Head of the Judiciary** (رئیس قوه قضائیه), appointed directly by the **Supreme Leader** for renewable five-year terms; the **Guardian Council**, whose six jurist-members are nominated by the judiciary head; the **Special Clerical Court** (دادگاه ویژه روحانیت), operating entirely outside normal legal procedure under the Supreme Leader's direct authority; **Revolutionary Courts** (دادگاه‌های انقلاب) handling political and security cases with minimal due process; and the **General Inspection Organization** (سازمان بازرسی کل کشور), nominally an oversight body but structurally subordinate to the judiciary it supervises. The **howzeh** (حوزه علمیه) shapes the judiciary through personnel — many judges hold seminary rather than law school credentials.

**Structural Parallels:** Poland's rule-of-law crisis under PiS demonstrates that judicial capture can occur even in democracies with formal constitutional protections, through appointment mechanisms, budgetary control, and disciplinary procedures — Piana's 'back doors.' Iran represents the limiting case: the judiciary was never designed for independence but as an enforcement arm of ولایت فقیه. While Poland's challenge was restoring captured independence, Iran's is creating independence that never existed within its constitutional tradition. Saffari's tracing of clerical legal authority to the 1906 Constitutional Revolution reveals a century of path dependency intertwining religious and legal authority — deeper than any post-communist institutional legacy.

**Key Actors and Sequencing:** The **Supreme Leader's office** is the ultimate blocker, since judicial appointment authority is constitutionally reserved to the رهبر. The judiciary head, currently positioned as a regime enforcer, would resist any reform diminishing his authority over appointments, budgets, and the revolutionary court system. Potential enablers include reformist jurists trained in both فقه and modern law, and civil society lawyers who have operated within the system's margins. Sequencing should proceed: (1) separation of the Special Clerical Court and revolutionary courts from the ordinary judiciary, subjecting all courts to unified procedural standards; (2) establishment of a **Supreme Judicial Council** (شورای عالی قضایی) with staggered appointments, supermajority removal requirements, and autonomous budgetary authority — drawing on Spain's Consejo General del Poder Judicial model but adapted to prevent the partisan capture that undermined Spain's version; (3) creation of a constitutional court with jurisdiction over institutional disputes, staffed through a mixed appointment process involving parliament, judiciary, and legal profession; (4) gradual professionalization requiring law school credentials for all judicial appointments.

**Risks and Prerequisites:** The seminary-trained judiciary represents both a human capital constraint and a political constituency — mass removal risks both a justice system personnel crisis and organized clerical opposition. A transitional mechanism allowing current judges to serve while requiring new appointees to hold law degrees could manage the transition over a generation. The absence of an external anchor equivalent to EU rule-of-law conditionality means domestic institutional design must be correspondingly more robust. Iran's judiciary also manages vast property and commercial dispute systems in which connected interests have accumulated advantages — judicial reform thus threatens not just political power but accumulated economic rents, multiplying resistance.

Iran's judicial system operates within a fundamentally different institutional logic than Poland's, yet reveals parallel vulnerabilities to judicial capture—though in this case, capture by clerical authority rather than electoral majorities. Iran's dual court system comprises secular courts for ordinary litigation, revolutionary courts for state security cases, and the Special Clerical Court adjudicating disputes among the clergy (Tabari, 2003). This fragmentation ensures the judiciary's ultimate subordination to theocratic authority: the Supreme Leader appoints the judiciary's head and retains authority over revolutionary courts, rendering the entire system an instrument of theocratic governance rather than an independent rule-of-law mechanism.

The revolutionary courts, technically a separate institution, function as political instruments without pretense of independence (Saffari, 1993). They process trials of political opponents with predetermined outcomes, conducting what superficially resemble judicial proceedings but operate under executive direction. This differs from Poland's institutional capture because Iran never attempted to build an independent judiciary constrained by electoral accountability; rather, Iran institutionalized clerical supremacy explicitly, making the judiciary's subordination a constitutional feature.

However, the instructive parallel to Poland emerges in attempted liberal reform efforts. During Khatami's presidency (1997-2005), reformists attempted to use educational and judicial channels to advance rule-of-law norms within Iran's theocratic framework (Mehran, 2003; Razavi, 2010). These efforts assumed that institutional changes within existing structures could gradually shift judicial practice toward impartiality. Yet the reformist judges and prosecutors faced the same constraint that Polish judges encountered: clerical authority retained ultimate power and reasserted control when reforms threatened political interests. Unlike Poland, where democratic change eventually restored institutional autonomy, Iran's theocratic structure has proven resistant to incremental rule-of-law expansion within the system itself.

The deeper parallel: both judicial systems reveal that institutional autonomy is not naturally stable. In Poland's democracy, captured institutions require external pressure (EU intervention) and political change to restore. In Iran's theocracy, captured institutions are the designed outcome, yet efforts to achieve incremental independence from within fail because structural authority remains unchanged. This suggests judicial independence requires not only institutional design and political culture, but also a governing structure that genuinely permits judicial autonomy—a condition neither Poland before 2023 nor Iran currently satisfies.

---

**Institutional Mapping:** Building judicial independence in Iran requires confronting a system designed for clerical supremacy rather than rule of law. The **head of the judiciary** (رئیس قوه قضائیه) is directly appointed by the **Supreme Leader** for five-year terms, with no confirmation by the Majles or any independent body. The **Supreme Court** (دیوان عالی کشور) exercises appellate jurisdiction but operates under the judiciary head's authority. **Revolutionary Courts** (دادگاه‌های انقلاب) handle national security, narcotics, and political cases with procedures that lack basic due process protections — closed hearings, limited defense access, and predetermined outcomes for political defendants. The **Special Clerical Court** (دادگاه ویژه روحانیت), operating outside the formal judiciary entirely, adjudicates cases involving clergy and reports directly to the Supreme Leader. The **Guardian Council** reviews legislation for Islamic compatibility, functioning as a quasi-constitutional court without adversarial proceedings. The **Administrative Justice Court** (دیوان عدالت اداری) handles citizen complaints against government agencies but lacks enforcement authority against security institutions.

**Structural Parallels:** Poland's experience with judicial capture under Law and Justice (2015-2023) provides the most precise cautionary parallel. Both cases demonstrate that judicial independence is vulnerable to systematic procedural manipulation — lowering retirement ages to enable political appointments, creating parallel judicial structures, and vetting promotions for political loyalty. The critical difference is that Poland's capture occurred *within* a democratic framework (making restoration possible through electoral change), while Iran's judicial subordination is *constitutionally designed* — the system was never intended to be independent. This means post-transition judicial reform in Iran must build independence from scratch rather than restore a previous democratic baseline. The Polish restoration process (post-2023), which required navigating entrenched resistance from judges appointed during the capture period, previews the challenge Iran would face with revolutionary court judges whose careers depend on the current system.

**Enablers and Blockers:** Reform enablers include **legal academics** in Iranian law faculties who have studied comparative judicial systems, **reformist lawyers** within the bar association (کانون وکلای دادگستری) who have advocated for due process reforms, and **international legal institutions** (ICJ, Venice Commission) that could provide technical assistance. Diaspora legal professionals constitute a significant resource. Blockers include the **revolutionary court judiciary** whose institutional existence depends on the current system, **IRGC and Ministry of Intelligence** officials who rely on revolutionary courts for political prosecutions, **hardline clerics** who view Islamic jurisprudence (فقه) as superior to secular legal frameworks, and the **Supreme Leader's office** which loses its most direct instrument of political control if judicial appointment authority is transferred.

**Iran-Specific Challenges:** Iran's judicial reform challenge is structurally more severe than Poland's for three reasons. First, Iran lacks any historical precedent for judicial independence — neither the Qajar, Pahlavi, nor Islamic Republic periods established genuinely independent courts, meaning the cultural foundation that Poland (despite its weaknesses) possessed is absent. Second, the dual legal system — secular law and Islamic jurisprudence — creates a constitutional question that Poland never faced: what is the role of شریعت (Sharia) in a post-transition legal order? Even a democratic Iran would likely maintain some Islamic legal principles in personal status law, requiring institutional design that separates religious jurisprudential authority from judicial independence in criminal, commercial, and constitutional matters. Third, the revolutionary courts have created decades of contested judgments — executions, property confiscations, political imprisonments — that any new judiciary must address through transitional justice mechanisms, creating immediate conflicts between judicial independence and political accountability demands.

---

Iran's dual legal system—combining civil code, Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh), and clerical institutional oversight—presents a singular challenge for judicial independence that neither Spain's nor Poland's models address. The velayat-e faqih (guardianship of the jurist) framework constitutionalized clerical authority over the judiciary in 1979 (Saffari 1993), embedding the Supreme Leader's ideological control within formal law. The Guardian Council functions as a dual watchdog: vetting legislation for Islamic compliance and supervising elections, while the Special Clerical Court operates beyond ordinary judicial channels to try clergy members accused of crimes against the state. This design is not a captured court system that can be reformed through institutional insulation—it is an intentional theocratic integration of law and executive authority.

Yet Iran's own legal system contains seeds of judicial independence claims that reformists have invoked since the 1997 Khatami era. Iran's Criminal Code recognizes qisas (retaliation) and diyah (blood-money) as victim-centered alternatives to state prosecution, and modern jurisprudence has interpreted forgiveness (aff) as a rights-bearing institution independent of regime preference (Osanloo 2020). Reformist jurists and politicians have argued that Islamic law itself mandates rule of law, due process, and limits on arbitrary state power—claims echoed in constitutional politics elsewhere in the Islamic world (Arjomand & Brown 2013). For Iran, the judicial independence puzzle is not how to reform captured courts but how to deconstruct the legal-ideological fusion binding judicial authority to velayat-e faqih, while defending legal pluralism and the jurisprudential traditions (particularly Twelver jurisprudence's emphasis on ijtihad and reasoned interpretation) that could support genuinely independent judicial reasoning.

**Institutional Mapping**

Iran's judicial architecture represents the most comprehensive fusion of religious and state authority in any contemporary legal system. The Supreme Leader (رهبر معظم انقلاب) directly appoints the Head of Judiciary (رئیس قوه قضائیه), who controls all judicial appointments, court administration, and prosecution policy. The Guardian Council (شورای نگهبان) — six clerical members appointed by the Supreme Leader, six jurists nominated by the Head of Judiciary and approved by parliament — reviews all legislation for Islamic compliance. The Special Clerical Court (دادگاه ویژه روحانیت) operates entirely outside the ordinary judicial system, trying clerics accused of political crimes with no right of appeal to regular courts. Revolutionary Courts (دادگاه‌های انقلاب) prosecute national security, drug, and political cases under procedures with reduced due process protections. The Supreme Court (دیوان عالی کشور) exercises appellate jurisdiction but cannot override Guardian Council interpretations. The Administrative Justice Court (دیوان عدالت اداری) reviews government administrative actions but lacks authority over security or clerical bodies. The howzeh seminary system produces the jurisprudential interpretations that legitimize judicial authority under ولایت فقیه.

**Structural Parallels**

Spain's post-Franco judicial reform — maintaining existing judges while creating new institutional structures — provides a gradualist model. But Spain transitioned from secular authoritarianism where judges, however politically compromised, operated within civil law traditions. Iran's judges operate within fiqh-based jurisprudence fused with state authority. Poland's PiS capture of courts (2015-2023) offers an inverse lesson: it demonstrates how constitutionally embedded safeguards can be dismantled by determined political actors — a risk directly relevant to Iran, where any post-transition judicial reforms would face resistance from clerical networks determined to preserve their authority. The EU external pressure that proved insufficient in Poland would be entirely absent for Iran. The parallel that holds most powerfully is Arjomand and Brown's insight that Islamic law itself contains rule-of-law claims: reformist jurists have argued that ijtihad (اجتهاد, independent reasoning) and Twelver jurisprudence's emphasis on justice support genuinely independent judicial reasoning. This internal resource — absent in secular authoritarian transitions — could anchor judicial reform in Islamic legitimacy.

**Key Actors and Sequencing**

Enablers: reformist jurists and lawyers who have invoked rule-of-law concepts within Islamic jurisprudence (the Montazeri tradition's human rights jurisprudence); women judges who, despite systemic barriers, have become voices for reform within state institutions; legal academics trained in both fiqh and comparative law; diaspora legal professionals with expertise in constitutional design; and the civil rights movement that emerged around the "right to counsel" advocacy in political cases. Blockers: the Supreme Leader's appointment power over the judiciary; the Special Clerical Court's extrajudicial authority; Revolutionary Court judges whose careers depend on current institutional arrangements; and the Guardian Council's legislative veto over judicial reform. Sequencing: (1) abolish Revolutionary Courts, transferring jurisdiction to regular courts with full due process protections — the most urgent reform; (2) abolish the Special Clerical Court, subjecting clergy to the same legal system as citizens; (3) establish an independent judicial appointment commission replacing Supreme Leader appointment of the Head of Judiciary; (4) create a genuine Constitutional Court separate from the Guardian Council, with authority to review legislation for constitutional (not merely Islamic) compliance; (5) retrain judges in constitutional law and due process standards while respecting jurisprudential pluralism; (6) reform legal education integrating fiqh-based reasoning with constitutional and international law.

**Risks**

Rapid judicial reform risks either institutional vacuum (if existing judges are purged) or persistent illiberalism (if they are retained without reorientation). Spain's experience — maintaining Franco-era judges while building new structures — demonstrates the latter risk: judicial culture persisted for decades after formal institutional change. Iran would face this at greater intensity because judges are trained in seminary-influenced jurisprudence that treats religious authority as constitutive of legal legitimacy. A second risk is post-transition judicial capture by new political actors — Poland's PiS experience shows this can happen even in EU-member democracies with external pressure. Iran, lacking such external anchoring, would be more vulnerable. The most dangerous risk is that failure to reform the judiciary enables transitional justice abuses: without independent courts, accountability processes for regime crimes could become political vendettas. Prerequisites: constitutional reform separating judicial appointment from Supreme Leader authority; transitional justice framework establishing accountability principles before court reform; sustained investment in judicial retraining (5-10 year program); and preservation of Islamic jurisprudential tradition as scholarly resource while removing its coercive state enforcement.

Iran's transition pathways intersect with this framework in critical ways. Understanding the institutional, structural, and actor dynamics is essential for analyzing viable reform trajectories.

**Institutional Mapping**
The Iranian state apparatus encompasses several competing institutional nodes that would shape implementation of reform frameworks. The Supreme Leader's office maintains direct control over the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), which manages vast economic enterprises and security apparatus. The Ministry of Science and Technology oversees research institutions but lacks autonomous budgetary authority. The Central Bank operates under constrained independence. Parliament (Majles) holds theoretical legislative authority but faces executive constraints. These institutional fragmentation patterns create both barriers to coordinated reform and potential leverage points for transition initiators.

**Structural Parallels**
Several comparative cases illuminate Iran's institutional dynamics. Indonesia's decentralized authoritarianism (1967-1998) shows how institutional pluralism can enable reform coalitions, though Indonesia's military centralization differs from Iran's ideological-security hybrid. Spain's gradual liberalization (1970s) involved elite consensus and institutional pacting but lacked Iran's sectarian dimension. Tunisia's 2011-2014 transition succeeded partly through constitutional assemblies that Iran lacks institutional precedent for. Poland's Round Table negotiations (1989) demonstrated how insider-outsider coalitions crystallize, though Poland operated within Cold War geopolitics absent from Iran. South Korea's democratic transition (1987-1992) shows industrial elites' role in pressuring authoritarian coalitions. The critical gap: Iran's revolutionary ideology is constitutionally entrenched in ways these comparators lacked.

**Key Actors and Stakeholder Mapping**
Enablers of transition frameworks include: emerging technocratic elites within state institutions; Bazaar merchant networks historically connected to reformist factions; academic and civil society clusters despite organizational restrictions; and international business interests seeking normalized relations. Blockers include: IRGC-affiliated economic holders; hardline clerical guardians of revolutionary identity; security apparatus with institutional interests in threat narratives; and ideological constituencies opposing Western-aligned institutional models. Negotiators and potential swing actors include: pragmatist clerics (Rafsanjani-aligned networks); nationalist elements within military hierarchies; student-intellectual networks; and diaspora capital holders seeking repatriation opportunities.

**Transition Scenarios and Sequencing**
A negotiated transition pathway would require IRGC accommodation through economic guarantees or parallel institutional authority structures. This parallels post-Cold War military transitions in Poland and Hungary where security apparatus retained influence. A revolutionary-rapid pathway faces coordination problems: security apparatus dominance makes external military intervention risky given 1953 historical precedent sensitivity. A gradual reform pathway proceeds through constitutional amendments, expansion of parliamentary authority, and incremental IRGC economic transparency—most institutionally feasible but extends timeline across decades.

**Risks and Critical Sequencing Issues**
Premature institutional reform without security apparatus accommodation risks coercive state response, as occurred in 2009 and 2019-2020. Conversely, sequencing security apparatus buy-in before institutional opening creates moral hazard where reform commitments dissolve post-stabilization. International engagement timing proves critical: premature sanctions relief removes leverage; delayed sanctions lifting maintains revolutionary coalition cohesion against external threats. Regional proxy commitments (Iraq, Syria, Yemen, Lebanon operations) provide IRGC institutional rationale; sequencing these down requires phased regional negotiation exceeding Iran's autonomous decision space. Constitutional amendment pathways require consensus-building within Guardian Council structures dominated by hardline clerics, representing substantial institutional rigidity. The window for managed transition closes as demographic-economic pressures mount: youth unemployment exceeds 30%, capital flight continues, and sanctions-driven isolation strengthens security-apparatus dependency.
