# Power-Sharing During Transitions

***Sources:** 34 | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-10*



## Overview

When authoritarian regimes end, the first and most dangerous problem is usually not what happens next but who decides what happens next. Transitions are inherently contests over institutional design, and the actors who sit at the negotiating table—and those who are excluded from it—shape outcomes for decades. Power-sharing arrangements are the mechanisms by which competing elites, factions, and social groups manage this contest: they define who participates in transition negotiations, what guarantees are extended to potential spoilers, and how the costs and benefits of democratic change are distributed. Getting these arrangements right—inclusive enough to prevent defection, bounded enough to allow governance—is one of the defining challenges of any democratic transition.

Comparative scholarship on transitions has moved away from earlier "elite pact" frameworks that emphasized the central role of regime moderates and opposition moderates striking deals over the heads of hardliners and radicals. More recent work emphasizes that durable transitions require broader social foundations: pacts struck between narrow elite groupings tend to produce exclusive democratic systems that eventually generate grievances and instability. The Spanish and South African cases, which involved both elite negotiation and significant civil society mobilization, have become comparative benchmarks not simply because they succeeded but because the breadth of inclusion they achieved created democratic systems with greater subsequent resilience.

Iran presents a distinctive variant of this problem. The Islamic Republic has operated for decades with a structured form of elite power-sharing—among reformist, principalist, and pragmatist factions—that provides genuine intra-regime competition while excluding any fundamental challenge to the theocratic framework or the Supreme Leader's authority. This intra-regime competition has produced real policy variation and has created a class of political actors with experience in negotiation, coalition-building, and institutional maneuvering. Whether and how this experience could translate into democratic power-sharing in a transition scenario is one of the most consequential and least-studied questions in Iranian political analysis.

This topic page examines the comparative evidence on how power-sharing arrangements are structured in successful and unsuccessful transitions, what mechanisms make them durable, and how Iran's factional landscape—including both regime insiders and the organized opposition outside—might shape the design and prospects of any transitional power-sharing framework.

## Theoretical Framework

This page applies a CMO framework to analyze power-sharing in democratic transitions, using Mahoney and Snyder's (1999) framework distinguishing transition modes (negotiated, rupture, and hybrid) as a starting point. The framework asks: under what contextual conditions do which power-sharing mechanisms produce durable democratic outcomes, versus unstable elite accommodation or renewed authoritarianism? Context includes the nature of the outgoing regime (personalist, party-based, military, theocratic), the degree of civil society development, the presence or absence of external actors, and the economic environment at the moment of transition. Mechanisms include formal pact institutions, constitutional commissions, transitional governments, and informal channels of elite coordination.

Three theoretical traditions structure the analysis. First, consociationalism and its critics: Arend Lijphart's consociational model argues that deeply divided societies require formal power-sharing institutions—grand coalitions, proportionality, mutual veto rights—to achieve democratic stability. Critics, particularly those drawing on Horowitz's (1985) work on ethnic conflict, argue that consociational arrangements entrench divisions and may delay integration. This tension is directly relevant to Iran, where the relevant cleavages include ideological commitments, regional and ethnic identities, and generational rifts, all of which would need to be accommodated in any durable framework.

Second, game-theoretic frameworks: O'Donnell and Schmitter's (1986) foundational work modeled transition as a strategic interaction in which actors with different discount rates and risk tolerances make decisions about whether to negotiate, defect, or use force. More recent formal models (Acemoglu and Robinson 2006) extend this to examine when economic elites accept democratization as a credible constraint on redistribution. The key insight for Iran is that transition is not a single negotiation but a sequence of strategic interactions in which credible commitment mechanisms—international guarantees, constitutional locks, transitional justice frameworks—can shift equilibria from mutual defection to negotiated settlement.

Third, sociological institutionalism emphasizes that formal power-sharing arrangements must be embedded in informal norms, networks, and cultural understandings to be durable. Elite pacts that lack civil society legitimacy tend to erode as excluded actors mobilize against settlements they did not endorse. This literature highlights the importance of procedural legitimacy—not only who sits at the table but how the process of negotiation is perceived by broader publics—as a condition for durable power-sharing. Taken together, these three frameworks allow the page to analyze power-sharing not as a template to be applied but as a contextually variable process whose outcomes depend on specific mechanisms of inclusion, commitment, and legitimation.

## Evidence & Key Findings

Power-sharing arrangements during democratic transitions represent a critical mechanism for managing conflict and facilitating institutional transformation in deeply divided societies. Historical analysis of such transitions reveals distinct patterns in how competing elites negotiate the boundaries of political inclusion and institutional design. The transition in Spain following Franco's death exemplifies this dynamic: the Moncloa Pacts of 1977 established a framework in which former regime actors, monarchist conservatives, Christian democrats, socialists, and communist forces negotiated simultaneously on constitutional structure, economic policy, and regional autonomy. Rather than a zero-sum competition for state control, the Pacts functioned as a consensual mechanism that locked in both substantive commitments (constitutional guarantees, labor protections) and procedural commitments (consensus-seeking in parliament). The success of this arrangement stemmed partly from elite calculation that inclusive negotiation reduced both the costs of uncertainty and the risks of defection to extraconstitutional actors (Zanotti, 2006).

South Korea's dual transition—concurrent movement toward both political democracy and economic liberalization in the late 1980s and 1990s—demonstrates how power-sharing arrangements must navigate multiple transition axes simultaneously. The negotiated transition of 1987, while contested, created sufficient space for direct presidential elections and expanded legislative voice, yet retained significant security-state authority and military prerogatives in national security and labor law. This partial power-sharing arrangement reflected a bargain: the military and business elites accepted electoral constraints in exchange for maintenance of key coercive and economic institutions (Anonymous, 2008). The sustainability of this bargain, however, required that no single faction—whether democratizing civil society or entrenched authoritarian actors—could unilaterally impose its preferred outcome. Crucially, the dual nature of the transition meant that economic power (chaebol-dominated growth) and political power (electoral competition) remained partially decoupled, reducing the zero-sum character of political competition and enabling factional coexistence.

These historical cases illuminate a core tension in pact-making during transitions: the degree to which inclusive power-sharing mechanisms enable democratic consolidation versus the degree to which they constrain democratic deepening. Where pacts succeed in establishing credible commitments to institutional restraint and protect minority rights within new democratic frameworks—as the Spanish case demonstrates—they can lay foundations for enduring democratic governance. Conversely, where pacts freeze asymmetric power distributions and prevent erosion of state capacity or elite privileges—as aspects of the South Korean transition illustrate—they may produce democratic governance that is procedurally competitive but substantively constrained, limiting citizen participation in security, economic, and cultural domains (Anonymous, 2002). The critical variable appears to be whether pacts function as temporary transitional devices that yield to more inclusive democratic practices, or whether they become institutionalized settlements that perpetuate prior inequality under a democratic veneer (Ulrich, 2018).

## Cross-Country Patterns

Power-sharing mechanisms during transitions operate through several distinct logics. First, they manage the inclusivity-efficiency tradeoff: broader representation in pact negotiations increases perceived legitimacy and reduces risks of defection, but expands the number of veto points and slows decision-making. Successful transitional pacts typically resolve this by creating nested institutions—broad-based constitutional commissions for major questions, narrower executive councils for implementation. Second, pact mechanisms specifically address the "spoiler problem": actors with capacity to disrupt transition through violence or non-cooperation receive credible guarantees of institutional voice in post-transition governance, reducing incentives to maintain extraconstitutional threats. However, this mechanism can entrench spoilers within democratic institutions, as veto power becomes a permanent feature rather than a transitional accommodation.

The relationship between power-sharing and democratic deepening remains contested. Pacts that successfully transition from negotiated settlements to competitive democratic practice—where veto powers erode through electoral competition and institutional learning—facilitate democratic deepening. Conversely, pacts that become institutionalized constraints on majority rule, protecting narrowly defined elite interests against democratic will, produce "limited" or "managed" democracy characterized by persistent informal authority, parallel institutional structures, and restricted policy space. The critical juncture appears to be the degree to which second-order actors (civil society, non-elite factions, emerging movements) can mobilize electoral or organizational power to challenge pact restrictions.

## Iran Analysis

**[CONTEXT UPDATE — April 2026: Islamabad talks represent real-world power-sharing negotiation; ceasefire creates transitional power-sharing dynamics; Mojtaba Khamenei's legitimacy deficit shapes power-sharing geometry]**

Iran's power-sharing landscape has been fundamentally altered by three simultaneous shocks: the assassination and succession of the Supreme Leader (February-March 2026), the active military conflict with US and Israel, and the ceasefire creating a negotiation context that involves external actors in Iran's governance trajectory for the first time since 1953. The standard comparative analysis of factional power-sharing within authoritarian constraints must now be supplemented by analysis of **externally-conditioned power-sharing** — a structurally different problem.

**Intervention lens — imposed vs. emergent power-sharing:**

The comparative literature distinguishes sharply between:

- **Emergent power-sharing** (Poland's Roundtable 1989, South Africa's CODESA 1991-1993): Internal factions negotiate the terms of transition, with external actors providing support/pressure but not dictating outcomes. These processes produce more durable settlements because all parties have ownership.

- **Imposed power-sharing** (Iraq CPA 2003-2004, Bosnia Dayton 1995): External actors design the power-sharing architecture, creating formal structures that often lack indigenous legitimacy. Bosnia's Dayton entities remain contested 30 years later; Iraq's ethno-sectarian apportionment system entrenched communal divisions rather than building cross-cutting coalitions.

Iran's Islamabad context sits between these models: external military pressure (coercive) but a surviving government (not collapsed), creating a negotiation rather than imposition dynamic. The most relevant comparative case may be **South Korea's 1980s democratization** — where US pressure created space for opposition but internal civil society drove the actual transition terms — or the **Chile 1990 plebiscite** — where Pinochet's government survived but was constrained to accept democratic transition through international isolation.

**Current power-sharing geometry — April 2026:**

The pre-war Iranian factional landscape (reformist / pragmatist / principalist triangle) has been substantially altered:

1. **Mojtaba Khamenei as Supreme Leader**: Elected by the Assembly of Experts March 9, 2026 with a reported 62 of 88 votes — weaker mandate than his father's original selection. His legitimacy deficit (no senior clerical rank, dynastic succession, wartime leadership) means he requires IRGC backing as a substitute for clerical legitimacy. This creates a structural IRGC-Supreme Leader dependency that strengthens IRGC political role.

2. **IRGC as dominant power broker**: Ahmad Vahidi (IRGC commander) holds more effective power than in any previous period — wartime conditions have eliminated civilian oversight and the IRGC's decision-making role in Islamabad negotiations is unclear but likely significant.

3. **Reformists and pragmatists**: Severely marginalized by wartime emergency governance. However, the Islamabad talks context creates potential for reformist factions to position as interlocutors with external actors — similar to how Iraqi exile groups positioned themselves during Gulf War diplomacy. The difference: Iranian reformists have domestic legitimacy that Iraqi exiles lacked.

4. **Exile opposition**: Currently fragmented and not formally included in Islamabad talks (as of April 10). The question of exile opposition's role is a critical Islamabad variable — analogous to the Iraqi exile groups' role in CPA governance design. Including them risks undermining the surviving government's legitimacy; excluding them loses the most democratically legitimate Iranian voices.

**Power-sharing mechanisms applicable under intervention conditions:**

*For a negotiated settlement framework:*
- **Inclusive national dialogue** (Yemen model, albeit a failed one): Formal multi-stakeholder body including IRGC-aligned government, reformists, ethnic minority representatives, exile opposition. Requires external facilitation and security guarantees.
- **Constitutional commission with proportional representation**: Delegates authority for constitutional redesign to a body with explicit representation of major factions. The Iraq 2005 model failed because de-Ba'athification excluded Sunni participation; an Iranian version would need to explicitly include reformist and minority voices.
- **Transitional technocratic government**: Appoint technocratic cabinet (not IRGC-aligned) to manage reconstruction and institutional reform while political settlement is negotiated. Analogous to Tunisia's Beji Caid Essebsi transitional arrangement.

*For a coercive compliance framework (more likely):*
- External actors accept continued IRGC-adjacent government in exchange for capacity limits and governance benchmarks
- Reformists gain political space through reconstruction conditionality (external pressure for civil society opening, free press, political prisoner release) rather than formal power-sharing
- This reproduces a liberalizing authoritarianism dynamic (Mexico 1977-2000, Taiwan 1986-1996) rather than negotiated transition

**Critical variable — reconstruction conditionality as power-sharing lever:** The Islamabad talks' reconstruction access terms may do more to shape Iran's internal power-sharing than any formal political agreement. If reconstruction conditionality includes: free press requirements, political prisoner release, civil society registration freedom, and electoral reform — these create structural space for opposition participation without requiring regime change. This is the model most consistent with what external actors can credibly deliver and Iran can survivably accept.

## Knowledge Gaps & Open Questions

1. How do power-sharing arrangements during transition affect capacity for accountability and transitional justice? Most analysis focuses on survival incentives and stability; less work examines whether pact-driven settlements create barriers to prosecution of prior regime abuses or truth commissions, and how these trade-offs vary across contexts.

2. What explains variation in pact durability? Why do some transitional pacts (Spain) erode gracefully as democratic institutions mature, while others (South Korea) persist as structured constraints? What are the micro-institutional mechanisms that shift pacts from temporary settlements to institutionalized asymmetries?

3. How do external actors—regional powers, international institutions, diaspora communities—affect the terms and viability of transitional pacts in deeply divided societies? Most scholarship treats pact-making as endogenous to elite negotiation; external pressure receives less systematic attention.
