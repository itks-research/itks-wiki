# Bonyad System Reform

***Sources:** 66 | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-10*



## Evidence & Key Findings

The Iranian bonyad system represents a distinctive institutional form of revolutionary state ownership, originating from confiscations of assets belonging to the former monarchy and its affiliated interests following the 1979 Iranian Revolution. These semi-autonomous religious foundations, most notably Bonyad-e Mostazafan va Janbazan-e Engelab-e Eslami (Foundation of the Oppressed and Disabled), consolidated control over vast economic holdings including industrial enterprises, agricultural land, and financial institutions. According to estimates cited in comparative studies, Iran's major bonyads collectively control between 10-20% of the formal economy, positioning them as significant state actors operating outside conventional fiscal oversight mechanisms (Astrov et al., 2018; Botenga, 2009). The scale and autonomy of these foundations reflect their status as institutions nominally serving social welfare objectives—providing support for war veterans, disabled persons, and orphans—while simultaneously functioning as economic conglomerates answerable primarily to Supreme Leader oversight rather than parliamentary regulation.

This institutional configuration finds comparative parallels in other post-authoritarian and post-revolutionary contexts, though each exhibits distinctive characteristics. Indonesia's military foundations (yayasan), established during the Suharto era, similarly created parallel structures linking security institutions to commercial enterprise and social provision, with comparable governance deficits and resistance to civilian oversight (Corsi, 2011). The Czech Republic's voucher privatization system, though pursuing different ideological objectives during post-communist transition, similarly created difficulties in concentrated ownership, asset transparency, and stakeholder accountability during institutional reform (Sadeghi-Boroujerdi, 2014). What distinguishes Iran's bonyad system is the theocratic foundation of its authority: bonyads derive legitimacy from religious revolutionary ideology and maintain constitutional independence from legislative branch scrutiny, a feature absent in secular military-commercial complexes or transitional privatization schemes.

The economic significance of bonyads extends beyond quantitative holdings. These institutions operate tax-exempt regimes, maintain proprietary accounting practices, and retain significant financial autonomy in capital allocation decisions, creating structural barriers to fiscal transparency and sectoral accountability. Their welfare functions—funding hospitals, schools, and veteran support—constitute essential social safety nets integrated with commercial operations, distinguishing them from corporate holdings in purely private economies and complicating potential disaggregation or divestment frameworks (Mashayekhi, 2019). The concentration of major economic sectors under bonyad management, including the Foundation of the Oppressed's dominant position in mining, construction, and food industries, generates path dependencies that any reform initiative must address.

## Cross-Country Patterns

Revolutionary and post-authoritarian states frequently encounter structural impediments when attempting to reform regime-connected economic conglomerates, reflecting both institutional path dependencies and fundamental political economy constraints. The challenge extends beyond technical asset valuation: regime-affiliated enterprises embody multiple functions simultaneously—generating state revenue, distributing patronage, maintaining constituency support, and fulfilling claimed social welfare objectives. Divestment initiatives encounter resistance from multiple stakeholder coalitions: beneficiary networks dependent upon foundations' welfare programs, management cadres whose authority derives from revolutionary legitimacy, and political factions viewing economic assets as leverage in factional struggles (Ghandehari, 2018). 

Transparency requirements represent a consistent pressure point in such transitions. External oversight mechanisms, particularly those demanded by international capital markets or multilateral institutions, conflict with foundational principles of institutional autonomy and Supreme Leader supervision. Historical precedent from other post-revolutionary contexts demonstrates that asset divestment without accompanying institutional transparency reforms typically results in informal concentration of control rather than genuine marketization (Dadgar, 2018). The sequencing question—whether transparency and structural reform precedes or follows political transition—remains unresolved in comparative literature, with different contexts producing divergent outcomes based on relative power configurations among reformist and conservative political factions.

## Iran Analysis

**[CONTEXT UPDATE — April 2026: Mojtaba Khamenei's selection as Supreme Leader (March 9) places the bonyad system under new principal-agent dynamics — his authority as constitutional trustee is legally established but institutionally contested. The air campaign has disrupted some bonyad-connected supply chains. Islamabad ceasefire talks may introduce international oversight of major quasi-state economic entities including bonyads for the first time.]**

Iran's bonyad system — dominated by Bonyad-e Mostazafan (400+ companies), Astan-e Qods-e Razavi, the Imam Khomeini Relief Foundation, and the Martyrs Foundation — now operates in a dramatically altered institutional environment. Three dimensions of the 2026 crisis reshape the reform calculus.

**1. The New Trusteeship Question Under Mojtaba Khamenei**

Bonyads are constitutionally accountable to the Supreme Leader as principal trustee. Ali Khamenei's assassination (February 28, 2026) and Mojtaba Khamenei's disputed succession create an unprecedented trusteeship gap. Mojtaba lacks his father's decades of accumulated patronage relationships with bonyad directors; many bonyad heads were personal appointees of Ali Khamenei, and their loyalty to his son is uncertain. This institutional rupture creates a window for reform that did not exist under the previous Supreme Leader's consolidated control — but also creates a risk of unauthorized asset stripping as bonyad directors exploit the authority vacuum.

The comparative parallel is the dissolution of Yugoslav state enterprises after Tito's death — where the loss of a centralizing patron figure enabled regional managers to convert institutional assets to personal holdings. In Iraq, the collapse of Saddam's patronage network in 2003 produced rapid conversion of state assets into personal wealth by former regime figures, a pattern the CPA was unable to prevent. The Iranian bonyad system faces the same risk: directors may accelerate asset conversion under the cover of wartime disruption, using the fog of conflict to obscure transactions.

**2. Bonyads as Reconstruction Financing Vehicles**

The air campaign has created massive reconstruction needs — destroyed infrastructure, disrupted supply chains, displaced populations. Bonyads, with their existing institutional capacity, asset base, and distribution networks, are positioned as potential reconstruction vehicles. This creates a tension: reformers seek transparency and eventual divestment, but the immediate practical need is for institutional capacity to deliver reconstruction services. The Iraq parallel is directly relevant. When the CPA dissolved Iraqi state enterprises (2003-2004), it eliminated the institutional infrastructure that could have managed reconstruction, forcing reliance on foreign contractors (Bechtel, Halliburton) whose performance was widely criticized. The bonyad system, whatever its governance deficiencies, possesses operational capacity — hospitals, construction companies, food distribution networks — that would take years to replicate through new institutions.

This argues for a sequenced approach: preserve bonyad operational capacity for immediate reconstruction, while imposing transparency and accountability requirements as conditions for continued operation. The Islamabad talks offer a mechanism for this — bonyad reform milestones (annual audits, welfare function separation, asset disclosure) could be embedded in a phased sanctions relief framework, creating external enforcement of reforms that domestic institutions lack the authority to impose.

**3. Bonyad Assets as Ceasefire Negotiation Leverage**

Bonyad assets — estimated collectively at hundreds of billions of dollars in real estate, industrial holdings, agricultural land, and financial investments — represent significant economic leverage in ceasefire negotiations. Several scenarios emerge: (a) bonyad asset freezes or seizure as part of a reparations framework; (b) bonyad assets placed under international trusteeship as a confidence-building measure; (c) bonyad reform commitments as conditionality for sanctions relief; (d) bonyad welfare functions absorbed into a post-conflict social safety net financed by international development institutions. Each scenario has comparative precedent. Iraq's Development Fund for Iraq (DFI), established under UNSC Resolution 1483 (2003), placed Iraqi oil revenues under international oversight — a model that could apply to bonyad commercial revenues. Bosnia's post-Dayton entity privatization, managed with international oversight, provides a template for transparent divestment under peace agreement conditionality.

**Comparative Transferability Under Intervention**

The intervention fundamentally changes which comparative models are most relevant. Pre-2026, Indonesia's post-Reformasi yayasan (foundation) reform was the closest parallel — both systems embedded economic conglomerates within revolutionary-legitimacy institutions outside civilian fiscal oversight. That parallel remains relevant for the commercial dimension of bonyad reform, but the intervention adds the dimension of external imposition that makes Iraq's CPA-era restructuring and Bosnia's Dayton-mandated privatization more directly applicable.

The critical distinction is between models of imposed restructuring (Iraq CPA, Kosovo UNMIK) and models of conditioned restructuring (Libya WMD deal, post-Dayton economic reforms). The Islamabad framework, if it produces a negotiated agreement rather than a capitulation, points toward the latter — bonyad reform as a voluntary commitment made credible by external monitoring, rather than externally imposed dissolution. The Afghan experience warns against the alternative: international donors' failure to address warlord-controlled economic assets during the Bonn process (2001) created parallel economies that undermined state-building for two decades.

**Welfare Function Transition**

Bonyad-operated hospitals, schools, and pension systems serve constituencies — war veterans, disabled persons, rural populations — with limited alternative provision. Under intervention conditions, these welfare functions become more critical, not less, as the air campaign creates new categories of casualties and displaced persons. The Afghanistan parallel is cautionary: the Taliban's return in 2021 was facilitated partly by the failure of the Karzai/Ghani-era state to build welfare delivery capacity independent of informal networks. If bonyad welfare functions are disrupted without substitution, the resulting social crisis strengthens hardline actors who position themselves as welfare providers.

**Reform Sequencing Under Intervention Conditions**

A credible sequence under current conditions would require: (1) immediate transparency measures — bonyad asset disclosure as an Islamabad confidence-building commitment; (2) operational continuity for welfare functions under international monitoring during reconstruction; (3) forensic asset mapping with international accounting support, leveraging sanctions-era intelligence on bonyad financial structures; (4) separation of welfare functions into an independent social insurance system before commercial divestiture; (5) phased marketization of commercially viable holdings under transparent ownership, with anti-asset-stripping safeguards learned from Russian and Iraqi privatization failures.

## Knowledge Gaps & Open Questions


1. What mechanisms could effectively separate bonyad welfare functions from commercial operations without creating service delivery gaps, and which international comparators demonstrate feasible institutional disaggregation models?

2. How would bonyad reform processes achieve transparency requirements demanded by international actors without violating the constitutional Supreme Leader oversight arrangement, and what hybrid governance models might satisfy both conditions?

3. Under what political configurations—factional realignment, constitutional reform, or external economic pressure—might sufficient political coalitions emerge to sustain comprehensive bonyad structural reform despite concentrated beneficiary resistance?
