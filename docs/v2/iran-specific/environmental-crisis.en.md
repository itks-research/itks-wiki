## Theoretical Framework

Framework: Narrative Synthesis — Popay et al. (2006)

This synthesis examines environmental governance and resource sustainability in Iran through the lens of institutional readiness for green transition. The central hypothesis posits:

**Theory of change:** Resource-dependent economies with weak environmental regulatory frameworks face a dual trap — environmental degradation accelerates because extractive industries dominate, while the institutional capacity needed to enforce environmental protections is subordinated to short-term revenue extraction. Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous legal-institutional reform (enforceable environmental impact assessment), resource efficiency improvements (material flow optimization), and energy system transformation (renewable energy policy). In Iran's case, the sanctions economy compounds this by discouraging the foreign investment and technology transfer that typically catalyze green transitions.

This hypothesis is tested against four sources: two Iran-specific studies on environmental law and material flows, and two comparative frameworks on green growth and renewable energy policy. The evidence base is limited in volume but spans the institutional, economic, and technological dimensions of environmental sustainability.

## Evidence & Key Findings

The evidence organizes into three dimensions of environmental governance relevant to Iran's resource sustainability challenge.

**Legal-Institutional: Environmental Impact Assessment as Governance Indicator.** Salari and Mousavi (2025) provide the most directly relevant analysis, comparing Iran's environmental impact assessment (EIA) legal framework with China and the International Finance Corporation (IFC) standards. Their critical finding is that Iran recognized EIA in its legal system early, but the framework suffers from three structural weaknesses: dispersion of laws and regulations across multiple agencies, lack of operational detail in enforcement provisions, and absence of effective sanctions for non-compliance. This legal architecture produces a paradox — formal environmental protections exist on paper but lack the institutional teeth to constrain development decisions. The comparative lens is revealing: China's EIA system, despite its own limitations, has achieved greater enforcement capacity through centralized administrative authority, while IFC standards represent the international benchmark for project-level environmental assessment. Iran's gap is not in legal recognition but in implementation infrastructure.

**Resource Efficiency: Material Flow Analysis as Diagnostic Tool.** Mashayekh et al. (2018) contribute an empirical analysis of Iran's national-level material flows compared with three reference countries. Their finding that Iran's export-import material flow is of 'strategic importance' due to the economy's dependence on raw material exports (primarily hydrocarbons) and manufactured goods imports directly illustrates the resource curse mechanism. The sevenfold growth in global per-capita raw material consumption since 1900 creates a context where Iran's extractive economic model is both environmentally unsustainable and economically vulnerable — locked into commodity price cycles that preclude long-term planning. The policy recommendations emphasize the need for dematerialization strategies that decouple economic growth from resource throughput.

**Energy Transition: Policy Frameworks for Renewable Energy.** Lu et al. (2020) provide the broadest comparative analysis, reviewing sustainable energy policies across multiple countries for promoting renewable energy sources. Their study, with 478 citations, represents the highest-impact source in this synthesis. The key insight for Iran is that successful renewable energy transitions require coherent policy packages — not individual incentives but integrated strategies combining feed-in tariffs, renewable portfolio standards, tax incentives, and technology transfer mechanisms. The authors emphasize that policy recommendations must be context-specific and tailored to end-users, which in Iran's case means navigating both the sanctions-constrained investment environment and the dominance of state-owned energy enterprises.

**Green Growth as Integrating Framework.** Hallegatte et al. (2011), in a World Bank policy research paper with 134 citations, argue that green growth should not be understood as a trade-off between environmental protection and economic development but as a strategy for making growth processes 'resource-efficient, cleaner and more resilient.' This framework is particularly relevant to Iran's post-transition planning because it rejects the false dichotomy between environmental protection and economic recovery — a dichotomy that sanctions-era economic pressure has reinforced by prioritizing short-term revenue over environmental investment.

## Cross-Country Patterns

Two patterns emerge from this limited but coherent evidence base.

First, **Iran's environmental governance gap is institutional, not legal.** Salari and Mousavi (2025) demonstrate that Iran possesses formal environmental protections but lacks enforcement capacity. This mirrors a pattern documented across resource-dependent states: environmental laws exist as aspirational statements rather than binding constraints because the economic incentive structure rewards extraction over protection. Mashayekh et al.'s (2018) material flow data confirm this at the empirical level — Iran's economy remains structured around raw material export despite decades of formal environmental commitments.

Second, **the sanctions economy actively retards green transition.** Lu et al.'s (2020) comparative analysis shows that successful renewable energy transitions depend on foreign investment, technology transfer, and integrated policy packages — all of which are constrained or blocked by Iran's sanctions environment. Hallegatte et al.'s (2011) green growth framework assumes access to international capital markets and technology partnerships that Iran currently lacks. This suggests that environmental sustainability cannot be meaningfully pursued under the current sanctions regime, making it a post-transition priority rather than a concurrent reform.

**Evidence limitations:** This synthesis draws on only four sources, and the comparative studies (Lu et al. 2020; Hallegatte et al. 2011) do not address Iran specifically. The Iran-specific evidence (Salari & Mousavi 2025; Mashayekh et al. 2018) is diagnostic rather than prescriptive. Additional research directives on Iran's water crisis, desertification, Lake Urmia, air pollution in Tehran and Isfahan, and the environmental footprint of IRGC-controlled industries would substantially strengthen this category.

## Iran Analysis

Iran's environmental crisis is among the most severe in the Middle East, encompassing water scarcity (Lake Urmia's near-disappearance, aquifer depletion), desertification, urban air pollution, and oil industry environmental damage. The evidence reviewed here addresses the institutional and policy dimensions rather than the physical crisis itself, but the implications for post-transition governance are significant.

Salari and Mousavi's (2025) EIA comparison suggests that a post-transition government would not need to create environmental law from scratch — the legal foundations exist. The priority would be consolidating dispersed environmental regulations into a coherent framework with enforceable sanctions, modeled on elements of the IFC standard adapted to Iranian institutional realities. The Chinese comparison is instructive but cautionary: centralized environmental enforcement can be effective but risks subordination to political priorities, a pattern Iran must avoid by establishing an independent environmental regulatory authority.

Mashayekh et al.'s (2018) material flow analysis points to the fundamental structural challenge: Iran's economy must dematerialize — reducing dependence on raw material extraction and export — as a condition for environmental sustainability. This aligns with the broader ITKS analysis of sanctions unwinding and economic diversification: environmental sustainability and economic restructuring are not separate agendas but interdependent components of post-transition development.

The renewable energy opportunity is substantial. Iran has exceptional solar and wind resources, and Lu et al.'s (2020) policy framework provides a template for the integrated incentive structures needed to catalyze investment. However, implementation requires sanctions relief (to access technology and capital), energy sector restructuring (currently dominated by state enterprises and IRGC-linked entities), and a regulatory framework that Hallegatte et al.'s (2011) green growth model could inform.

A credible post-transition environmental strategy would need to: (1) consolidate and strengthen EIA enforcement as an immediate governance reform, (2) integrate environmental sustainability into economic restructuring plans rather than treating it as a separate domain, (3) prioritize water crisis management as the most acute environmental threat, and (4) develop a renewable energy transition plan conditioned on sanctions relief and energy sector reform.

---

**Institutional Mapping:** Environmental governance in Iran is institutionally dispersed and politically subordinated. The **Department of Environment** (سازمان حفاظت محیط زیست), headed by a vice-president, formally coordinates environmental policy but lacks enforcement authority against powerful development actors. The **Ministry of Energy** (وزارت نیرو) controls water resource management — the most acute environmental crisis — and prioritizes agricultural and industrial allocation over ecological sustainability. The **Ministry of Petroleum** and **NIOC** manage hydrocarbon extraction with minimal environmental accountability. **Khatam al-Anbiya** (قرارگاه خاتم‌الانبیا), the IRGC's engineering arm, executes major infrastructure projects (dams, roads, pipelines) with documented environmental impacts and effective immunity from environmental review. The **Ministry of Agriculture** (وزارت جهاد کشاورزی) has promoted water-intensive farming practices contributing to aquifer depletion. Local **governorates** (استانداری‌ها) manage regional environmental enforcement but are politically subordinate to central government priorities.

**Structural Parallels:** The evidence demonstrates that Iran's environmental governance gap is institutional rather than legal — formal protections exist on paper but lack enforcement teeth. This mirrors the pattern Salari and Mousavi (2025) document through comparison with China's EIA system, where centralized enforcement achieved greater compliance despite similar authoritarian constraints. The parallel is instructive but cautionary for Iran: China's centralized environmental enforcement succeeded partly because environmental protection served regime legitimacy interests; in Iran, environmental enforcement consistently loses to extractive economic interests defended by the **IRGC** and **bonyad system**. The key insight from Lu et al.'s (2020) renewable energy policy review is that successful green transitions require integrated policy packages combining incentives, regulation, and technology transfer — all of which are blocked or constrained by Iran's sanctions environment.

**Enablers and Blockers:** Environmental reform would find allies among Iran's **environmental NGOs** (despite severe operational constraints, organizations like the Persian Wildlife Heritage Foundation have mobilized public awareness), **university environmental science departments**, and **urban populations** suffering from air pollution in Tehran, Isfahan, and Ahvaz. The **Department of Environment** itself contains technocratic capacity constrained by political weakness. Blockers include the **IRGC's construction entities** whose projects (dams, industrial zones) are primary environmental degradation drivers, the **Ministry of Petroleum** which resists energy transition away from hydrocarbons, **agricultural lobbies** dependent on unsustainable water allocation, and **bonyad-controlled industries** operating with effective environmental immunity.

**Iran-Specific Challenges:** Iran's environmental crisis is among the most severe in the Middle East and differs from comparative cases in its existential urgency. **Lake Urmia's** near-disappearance, **aquifer depletion** across the central plateau, **desertification** in eastern provinces, and **catastrophic air pollution** in major cities represent immediate threats that cannot wait for post-transition institutional reform. The IRGC's environmental footprint is particularly significant: Khatam al-Anbiya's dam-building program has disrupted river systems, and IRGC-controlled industrial zones operate without meaningful environmental oversight. A post-transition environmental strategy must: (1) consolidate dispersed environmental regulations into an independent environmental protection agency with enforceable sanctions, (2) conduct environmental impact audits of IRGC and bonyad industrial operations, (3) prioritize the water crisis as a national security issue requiring immediate interstate (with Afghanistan, Turkey, Iraq) and intrastate water governance reform, (4) develop a renewable energy transition plan leveraging Iran's exceptional solar and wind resources — contingent on sanctions relief enabling technology transfer and foreign investment in clean energy infrastructure.

## Knowledge Gaps & Open Questions

- Can Iran's environmental crisis (water scarcity, desertification, urban pollution) be meaningfully addressed without first resolving the sanctions-driven economic constraints that prevent technology transfer and green investment?
- How should a post-transition government sequence environmental priorities — immediate crisis management (water, air pollution) versus structural transformation (energy transition, dematerialization) — given limited institutional capacity and competing demands?
- What institutional design for environmental regulation would balance enforcement capacity with independence from political interference, given Iran's history of subordinating environmental protection to economic and security priorities?
- Could Iran's renewable energy potential (solar, wind) serve as a catalyst for economic diversification and international reintegration, or would energy sector interests (oil, gas, IRGC-controlled enterprises) resist this transition regardless of political regime change?
