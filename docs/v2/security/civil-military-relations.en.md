This article examines why existing theories about military-civilian relations — developed for Western democracies and post-Cold War transitions — fail to explain Iran's Revolutionary Guards (IRGC). The IRGC is not simply a military force: it also runs major economic enterprises, enforces ideological conformity, and provides social services. No existing theory was designed to analyze an institution that does all three simultaneously. This article reviews the dominant theoretical frameworks and shows where each breaks down when applied to Iran's specific case.

## Summary for General Readers

Iran's military force — the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) — has survived the loss of multiple commanders in the US-Israel war of 2026 and has emerged from the crisis with *more* domestic political power, not less. This seems paradoxical: how does a military get stronger when it's being bombed?

Comparative history from Indonesia, Poland, and South Korea gives the answer: military organizations that face existential external threats tend to entrench their political power as a survival strategy, using the war emergency to justify control they couldn't assert in peacetime. Indonesia's military did exactly this after Suharto fell — it formally reduced its overt political role while quietly maintaining informal control for decades.

For Iran, the key question is whether the ongoing ceasefire negotiations can create the conditions that broke this pattern in other countries: external leverage (like NATO membership for Poland) combined with Iranian civilian reformers who can act as counterweights to IRGC power. Currently, both of those ingredients are missing. Any deal that lifts sanctions without including IRGC reform requirements will likely cement the IRGC's dominant position for a generation.

## Methodological Framework: Realist Synthesis

**Methodology:** Realist Synthesis (Pawson 2006) — Context-Mechanism-Outcome (CMO) Configuration

**Research Question:** Under what conditions does civilian control over a politically embedded military institution become durable, and what mechanisms produce that outcome?

### CMO Configuration

**Context (C):**
Both Poland (post-1989) and Indonesia (post-1998) undertook transitions from authoritarian rule in which the military held autonomous political roles embedded in law, doctrine, or informal practice. In Poland, the communist-era party-controlled military retained institutional loyalty structures and economic interests. In Indonesia, the TNI's *dwifungsi* (dual function) doctrine formally authorized military participation in both defense *and* civilian governance. Both militaries had economic interests (defense industries, business holdings) that gave them independent revenue streams insulating them from civilian budget control.

**Mechanism 1 — External Institutional Anchor (Poland):**
NATO accession conditionality required Poland to demonstrate civilian control over defense policy, democratic legislative oversight, and depoliticization of the officer corps before membership was granted (1999). This created a binding external accountability mechanism: reform was a prerequisite for integration into a high-value security community, not merely a domestic political preference. NATO socialization then transferred professional norms to the officer corps, replacing party-based identity with transnational military professionalism.

**Mechanism 2 — Autonomous Reform with Epistemic Coalition (Indonesia):**
Without an equivalent external anchor, Indonesia's reform relied on an internal coalition: reform-minded civilian defense academics, a subset of progressive military officers, and international donor pressure (U.S., EU, Australia). This epistemic community generated reform proposals and sustained political pressure through the democratic transition. However, the mechanism lacked binding leverage — it depended on sustained political will among civilian leaders and the continued marginalization of conservative military factions.

**Predicted Outcomes:**
- Poland (M1): Durable civilian supremacy through institutionalized legal subordination + identity transformation
- Indonesia (M2): Partial civilian control with persistent informal military power + risk of reversal when political conditions shift

**This article tests whether the evidence from each case confirms or disconfirms these predicted outcomes, and draws implications for Iran's transition challenge with the IRGC.**

## Evidence & Key Findings

> 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟢 Strong** (78%)

The comparative record on military economic empires reveals a consistent pattern: armed forces that build autonomous economic bases resist divestment in every documented case. Indonesia's TNI operated over 200 directly-owned businesses plus approximately 1,000 cooperatives through 23 foundations. Law No. 34/2004 mandated complete divestment within five years, yet the government was still only preparing to divest by 2009. Egypt's military economy, estimated at 1.5-2% of GDP by conventional measures, expanded after the 2011 revolution rather than contracting. Pakistan's Fauji Foundation accumulated net assets of approximately $6 billion, including control of roughly 80% of the fertilizer market. These cases underscore that Iran faces a challenge without successful precedent.

Iran's IRGC represents a qualitative escalation of the military-economic problem. The Khatam al-Anbiya Construction Headquarters alone employs approximately 25,000 engineers and staff, with project values exceeding $7 billion as early as 2006. The IRGC Cooperative Foundation extends into telecommunications, petrochemicals, steel, agriculture, and banking. International sanctions have not dismantled these holdings but driven them into less transparent ownership structures. Unlike Indonesia or Egypt, the IRGC's economic empire is entangled with sanctions evasion and ideological legitimacy, adding layers of complexity absent from other cases.

The institutional predictability that Rodrik (2000) identifies as essential for prosperity is fundamentally undermined when a military-economic conglomerate operates outside market discipline and civilian oversight. Bardhan (2005) reinforces that institutional quality — not just institutional existence — determines economic outcomes. For Iran, this means security sector reform and economic restructuring are inseparable challenges.

---

South Korea's trajectory from military authoritarianism to civilian democracy is the most extensively documented success case in the civil-military literature — yet recent scholarship reveals how incomplete that success remains. Chung (2009) documents how "politics was the continuation of war by other means" under the security-dominated post-Korean War state. The election of civilian president Kim Young-sam in 1992 marked a critical inflection. His 1993 purge of the Hanahoe — a secret society of Korean Military Academy graduates centered on coup leaders Chun Doo-hwan and Roh Tae-woo — removed the informal power structure sustaining military political dominance. Over 1,000 officers were retired; the December 1995 arrest of Chun and 16 co-conspirators on insurrection charges signaled that military impunity was over. This decisive action carries direct implications for Iran, where informal networks within the IRGC command structure may prove as consequential as formal institutions.

However, Kim and Kuehn (2022) present a significant corrective to the triumphalist narrative. Their analysis of 1,060 employees across 21 Ministry of National Defense departments reveals a persistent "clear cut between the military domain and the civilian domain within the MND." Professional soldiers retained effective decision-making power over critical policy areas despite 30 years of civilian efforts. Kim K-J (2014) similarly finds that parliamentary control through the National Defense Committee was formally established but limited in practice. Croissant (2011) places South Korea in comparative East Asian context, noting that both structural and agential factors — not just formal institutional design — determine outcomes.

Kuehn and Croissant's (2023) systematic six-case comparison confirms that defense ministry civilianization and parliamentary oversight are the two pillars of successful reform. Spain's early prioritization of defense ministry restructuring and emphasis on "democratic socialization" of the officer corps proved decisive. The pattern suggests that formal institutional redesign without cultural transformation produces hollow reforms — a warning directly applicable to any post-transition restructuring of Iran's defense establishment.

---

Makara (2013) develops a theory of coup-proofing based on three strategies tested against the Arab Spring: building parallel security institutions, distributing material incentives, and exploiting communal ties. His key finding is that parallel security institutions make military defection less likely because no single security body has sufficient autonomous capacity to side with protesters. This finding has been empirically confirmed across multiple cases: in Egypt, the relationship between the military and the parallel security services shaped the trajectory of the 2011 revolution; in Syria, overlapping security bodies remained loyal precisely because their fragmented structure prevented coordinated defection. The transition implications are profound for Iran, where the parallel architecture is among the most elaborate in the world.

Tamburini (2018) examines anti-terrorism legislation across Algeria, Mauritania, Morocco, and Tunisia, finding a common pattern of using counter-terrorism frameworks to control dissidence. Counter-terrorism laws, ostensibly designed for security, became instruments for curtailing personal freedoms and suppressing opposition. This pattern directly illuminates how Iran's security apparatus justifies its political role through the language of national security and counter-terrorism.

Ouedraogo (2014) documents how weak military professionalism in Africa — manifested as coups, mutinies, and corruption — persists decades after colonialism despite billions in security sector assistance. Cunningham (2015) adds that external military aid relationships can paradoxically weaken sovereignty by giving foreign powers leverage over domestic civil-military dynamics. These findings together suggest that security sector reform requires addressing both internal institutional cultures and external dependency relationships.

---

On February 23, 1981, Lieutenant-Colonel Antonio Tejero led 200 armed Civil Guard officers into the Spanish Congress, holding parliamentarians hostage during a prime ministerial investiture vote. King Juan Carlos I's televised address denouncing the coup and affirming constitutional order proved decisive. The 23-F coup attempt paradoxically accelerated rather than retarded civilian control. The manifest failure of military intervention — and the monarchy's unambiguous alignment with democracy — shifted the political equilibrium decisively. This dynamic carries critical lessons for Iran, where crisis moments have consistently been resolved in favor of the security establishment rather than reform.

Huntington (1995) places Spain within a broader typology, distinguishing between military governments, one-party regimes, personal dictatorships, and racial oligarchies. Spain, classified as a personal dictatorship under Franco, followed what Huntington calls the "tortoise strategy" — gradual, persistent expansion of civilian authority over military prerogatives. The 23-F crisis converted contested terrain into settled consensus: whether democracy was compatible with Spanish military tradition was no longer debatable. Spain's subsequent NATO entry (1982) and EU integration provided external institutional frameworks that further constrained military autonomy.

Kuehn and Croissant (2023) confirm that Spain's early prioritization of defense ministry reform and "democratic socialization" of the officer corps were key differentiators. The comparison with Chile — where Pinochet retained a Senate seat and military prerogatives persisted for decades — illustrates how decisive early action during a crisis window shapes long-term trajectories. The tortoise strategy succeeded in Spain precisely because the 23-F moment created political permission for accelerated reform that would otherwise have required decades more of gradual pressure.

The challenge of military institutionalization extends beyond Spain itself. Lubis (2026) examines the civil-military boundary in emerging democracies through a socio-legal lens, identifying how military law structures can either facilitate or obstruct the professionalization process. Bruneau (2018) develops a conceptual framework for analyzing civil-military relations that highlights the structural conditions enabling military institutions to accept constitutional subordination. These analytical frameworks illuminate the conditions that allowed the Spanish armed forces to transition from the guardrail role demonstrated in 1981 toward genuine institutional accountability. Safi (2024) documents military depolitization strategies adopted in comparable democratic transitions, providing comparative evidence for understanding Spain's civil-military evolution post-1981.

---

Huntington's The Soldier and the State (1957) and his 1995 essay provide the dominant theoretical vocabulary in civil-military relations. His concept of "objective civilian control" — professional military autonomy in operational matters combined with civilian authority over policy — and the tortoise strategy of gradual reform have been widely applied. Kuehn and Croissant's (2023) six-case comparison broadly validates this approach: in South Korea, Chile, and Spain, incremental reform achieved more durable results than dramatic confrontation. Kuehn (2008) reinforces this through the Taiwan case, showing how elected civilians gradually curbed military political power and widened influence over former exclusively military prerogatives. These frameworks, while powerful, emerged from contexts fundamentally different from Iran's institutional landscape.

Makara's (2013) coup-proofing framework identifies three mechanisms — parallel institutions, material incentives, communal ties — and tests them against the Arab Spring. Croissant (2011) adds the structural-agential distinction: formal institutional design alone does not determine outcomes; the agency of specific political actors within those structures matters equally. Together these frameworks provide analytical tools for understanding transition dynamics, but each assumes a relatively clean separation between military and civilian domains that does not exist in Iran.

The MIT Security Studies Program's reassessment of Huntington identifies a "paradox of professionalism" — greater military autonomy paradoxically decreases civilian oversight. For conventional militaries, this means professionalization can undermine civilian control. But for Iran, the paradox is inverted: the IRGC's lack of professional military autonomy (it is a political body) means that introducing Western-style professionalism would actually increase civilian control — but only if the ideological infrastructure is simultaneously reformed.

## Evidence

### Poland: The External Anchor Model

Poland's post-1989 civil-military transition unfolded across five reform dimensions documented by Yaniszewski (2002) [1295]: dismantling Communist-era control mechanisms (political commissars, party cells within the military), establishing constitutionally-guaranteed civilian authority lines, enacting democratic oversight legislation, restructuring the defense ministry with civilian leadership, and integrating into NATO organizational standards. All five were substantially completed by Poland's NATO accession in 1999 and fully consolidated by the mid-2000s.

The mechanism through which NATO produced reform is documented in Epstein (2005) [1303]. NATO conditionality created what Epstein terms "institutional leverage": reform was not merely normatively desirable but instrumentally necessary for membership in a security community Poland's civilian leadership prioritized. This conditionality was reinforced by socialization — as Polish officers participated in NATO exercises, planning structures, and professional education, they adopted transnational professional military identities that replaced party-based loyalties. Sarvaš (1999) [1313] documents the corresponding decline in corporate group identity among Central and Eastern European officer corps, including Poland's, as military resources and independent economic interests were progressively brought under civilian oversight.

Tomaszycki (2006) [1297] offers a practitioner's assessment: by the mid-2000s, Poland's civilian defense ministry exercised full control over strategic decision-making, military appointments, and budget allocation. The officer corps had been depoliticized — military leadership neither sought nor held independent political roles. The Polish transition was not conflict-free (the 1981 martial law episode cast a long shadow), but the combination of historical anti-communist democratic aspiration, a reforming civilian political class, and NATO's institutional conditionality produced durable outcomes.

### Indonesia: The Autonomous Reform Model — Progress and Limits

Indonesia's 1998 transition triggered formal dismantling of *dwifungsi* doctrine, the withdrawal of military representatives from the legislature (completed 2004), and creation of a civilian defense oversight framework. This represented genuine progress. However, Khairurrizqo (2024) [1538] documents that formal reform and informal power diverged: after the legal architecture of civilian supremacy was established, the TNI retained influence through business networks, placement of retired officers in civilian bureaucracies, and informal advisory roles to executives. The military "returned to the barracks" in institutional form but maintained leverage in practice.

Kosandi and Wahono (2020) [1528] assess the record after two decades of reform: the TNI "has not executed all necessary reform agendas toward a professional military." They describe a "new subtle role" — not direct military intervention in politics but sustained informal influence on policy outcomes, facilitated by the weakness of civilian institutions in defense governance. Bradford (2024) [5504] argues the TNI is a "fiercely independent institution" that "defies many of the most basic precepts of conventional civil-military relations theory," with the TNI-civilian government relationship better described as co-dependent than hierarchical.

The international dimension of Indonesia's reform is instructive. Lachica (2003) [1534] documents that donor-assisted security sector reform programs — from the U.S., EU, and Australia — had "only limited success" by the early 2000s, with army reform appearing to "have ground to a halt." External pressure without internal political will and without binding conditionality could not sustain reform momentum. Wardoyo (2018) [1537] identifies the epistemic community (defense academics, reform-minded officials, civil society organizations) as the critical reform driver, but also notes that this community's influence was contingent on continued access to civilian political leadership — access that weakened as reformist coalitions within Indonesia's party system fragmented.

Greenlees (2011) [1559] documents the reversal risk clearly: thirteen years after reform began, conservative military figures were publicly calling for restoration of direct military governance roles, citing what they characterized as the "fragility" of Indonesian democracy. Civil society pushback was active but insufficient to prevent gradual erosion. This dynamic culminated in the 2025 TNI Law controversy: Jamaludin and Fadhilah (2025) [5491] analyze a draft law that would substantially expand the military's role in civilian governance structures, which Putri et al. (2025) [5508] document generated significant public opposition through digital platforms — evidence of a contested field rather than settled civilian supremacy.

### Direct Comparative Evidence

Safi (2024) [5525] provides the most direct comparative analysis, explicitly studying Poland's depoliticization and professionalization model as a potential strategy for Indonesia. The study identifies the core asymmetry: Poland's reform was externally anchored in NATO accession with binding consequences for non-compliance, while Indonesia's reform was internally negotiated with no equivalent external constraint. The Polish model's transferability to Indonesia is assessed as partial — the normative content (civilian oversight, legislative accountability, officer corps professionalization) is applicable, but the mechanism (conditionality) cannot be replicated without a comparable institutional framework.

## Comparative Evidence

### Indonesia: Dwifungsi, Reformasi, and the Limits of Civilian Control

Indonesia's TNI (military) under Suharto exercised what it called *dwifungsi* — the "dual function" doctrine justifying military roles in both security and political-civilian governance. At its peak, the military held 100 reserved legislative seats, ran civilian businesses, managed regional administration, and operated through a network of territorial commands parallel to civilian government.

Suharto's fall in 1998 initiated a reform process. Key findings from the comparative literature:

**What worked:** TNI's formal withdrawal from the legislature was completed by 2004. The territorial command structure was partially reduced. Military businesses were formally prohibited (though informally continued). Civilian defense ministers gradually gained authority. Civil society pressure and international donor conditionality accelerated reform.

**What didn't:** TNI retained informal political influence through "former officer" networks in parties and bureaucracies. Business interests were never fully divested. Military justice remained separate from civilian courts. Intelligence services retained operational autonomy. Post-Reformasi presidents — including Megawati, Yudhoyono, Jokowi — depended on military support for political stability.

**Critical variable:** The reform process succeeded in reducing TNI's *overt* political role because Suharto's fall created a legitimacy vacuum that civilian reformers could fill. The military had no external existential threat to rally around during the transition period. It faced pressure without the option of reversion to military rule given international and domestic constraints.

**Key source finding:** Assessing military reform in Indonesia (Heiduk 2011) documents that "substantial formal reform and continued informal military influence" coexisted — a dual-track pattern where institutional compliance masked persistent informal power. This "reform in form, persistence in practice" pattern is well-documented across post-authoritarian transitions.

### Poland: NATO Integration as External Discipline

Poland's post-1989 civil-military transition was driven largely by the external discipline of NATO accession requirements. Key dynamics:

**Structural leverage:** NATO's Partnership for Peace program (1994) and accession process (1999) imposed specific civilian oversight requirements — democratic control legislation, civilian defense ministry authority, legislative oversight, apolitical officer corps norms. Poland's military had strong incentives (security guarantee, prestige, resources) to comply.

**What this required:** Elimination of the political officer/commissar system (direct PZPR/communist party structures); subordination to civilian defense minister; parliamentary oversight of deployments; transparent budgeting. These were achieved within approximately 5 years of transition.

**Limits:** Reform occurred fastest where it had direct institutional champions (civilian defense ministries, parliamentary committees) and external auditors (NATO). Areas without institutional champions (military intelligence, special operations) lagged. Post-accession backsliding was possible (as seen in 2015-2023 under PiS).

### South Korea: Gradual Civilian Control Through Electoral Accountability

South Korea's trajectory shows that military influence can be reduced through sustained electoral pressure without military defeat or external imposition. The key mechanism was that each democratically elected government from Roh Tae-woo (1988) onward incrementally reduced the formal political role of the military, prosecuted previous coup leaders (Chun Doo-hwan), and established civilian investigative authority over military corruption.

The IRGC-Korea parallel is limited by the fact that the Korean military was a conventional force whose political role was a historical legacy, not a constitutional doctrine — unlike the IRGC, whose revolutionary mandate is explicitly encoded in the Islamic Republic's founding documents.

## Cross-Country Patterns

> 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟢 Strong** (75%)

Two cross-cutting patterns define the military-economic divestment challenge. First, voluntary divestment does not occur. In every documented case — Indonesia, Egypt, Pakistan — military establishments either resisted divestment entirely, exploited legal delays, or expanded their economic footprint during transition periods. Egypt's case is the starkest: the Armed Forces Engineering Authority undertook 276 projects worth $3.96 billion, and a Ministry of Defense affiliate holds a 51% stake in the New Administrative Capital. Second, sanctions create perverse incentives. Rather than weakening military-economic actors, international sanctions can drive economic activity underground, making it harder to track and harder to dismantle during any future transition.

---

The central pattern across cases is that civilianizing defense ministry leadership is necessary but insufficient. South Korea removed political generals and prosecuted coup leaders, yet internal bureaucratic structures preserved military decision-making dominance for decades. The information asymmetry between civilian appointees and career military officers creates a persistent advantage for uniformed personnel in policy debates. This finding suggests that "civilianization" must be understood as a multi-layered process: removing political control at the top, restructuring career pathways in the middle, and building civilian expertise at the working level. Each layer operates on a different timeline — years for the first, a decade for the second, a generation for the third.

---

The decisive cross-cutting pattern is that coup-proofing structures are double-edged for transitions. They stabilize authoritarian regimes by preventing military defection during protests — as confirmed by Iran's experience in 2009, 2019, and 2022, when the security apparatus remained intact. But they simultaneously destabilize any reform process because dismantling parallel structures without adequate institutional alternatives produces security vacuums. The collapse of parallel security structures in Iraq (2003), Syria (post-2011), and Libya (2011) provides cautionary evidence: fragmentation of coercive capacity produced competing armed factions, not civilian supremacy. The pattern suggests a sequencing dilemma: parallel structures must be reformed gradually to prevent fragmentation, but their existence blocks the very civilian supremacy needed to direct reform.

---

The cross-cutting pattern is that crises can function as reform catalysts, but only under specific conditions. First, civilian leadership must have institutional blueprints prepared before the crisis. Spain's reformers had detailed plans for defense ministry restructuring. Second, a legitimacy anchor must exist to rally cross-factional support during the crisis window. King Juan Carlos served this role in Spain. Third, the crisis must be resolved decisively and publicly — ambiguous outcomes allow military establishments to regroup. The comparison between Spain (where the 23-F was resolved within 18 hours by a clear royal intervention) and Egypt (where the 2011 revolution produced an ambiguous civil-military outcome) illustrates this difference.

Cross-cutting institutional patterns emerge from comparative military law scholarship. The depoliticization framework central to Spain's post-1981 trajectory appears across multiple democratic transitions, though success rates vary significantly with institutional design. Lubis (2026) and Safi (2024) both emphasize that military professionalism requires sustained legal constraints that prove difficult to sustain during security crises. This pattern suggests Iran's security sector reform faces structural obstacles that Spain's constitutionalists overcame through sustained consensus-building.

---

The overarching pattern is theoretical insufficiency. All existing frameworks assume one or more conditions that Iran violates. Huntington assumes a clear military-civilian distinction; the IRGC operates across military, economic, intelligence, political, and ideological domains simultaneously. Makara assumes that coup-proofing is primarily a regime survival strategy; for Iran, the parallel structure is also an economic and ideological infrastructure. Professionalization theory assumes apolitical military norms; the IRGC's professionalism is defined by ideological loyalty rooted in velayat-e faqih doctrine and seminary-linked training. The convergence of these theoretical gaps suggests that Iran may require an entirely new analytical vocabulary — one that integrates security sector reform, economic restructuring, and ideological de-institutionalization as interdependent components of a single challenge.

## Patterns and Comparative Analysis

### Pattern 1: The Conditionality Gap

The single most explanatory variable distinguishing Poland's durable outcome from Indonesia's partial one is the presence or absence of binding external conditionality attached to reform. Poland's NATO accession created what social scientists call a "commitment device": civilian leaders could use external requirements to overcome domestic resistance from conservative military factions, and military leadership understood that institutional interests (budget, status, international standing) were served by compliance rather than resistance. In Indonesia, international donors offered incentives but not binding conditions — donor programs could be managed or delayed without catastrophic institutional consequences.

This pattern is consistent with broader comparative evidence on institutional reform: externally anchored reform processes tend to produce more durable outcomes than domestically negotiated ones in contexts where reformed institutions have strong interests in resisting change (see Epstein 2005 [1303]; Cottey et al. 2002 [1301]).

### Pattern 2: Formal vs. Informal Power Separation

Both cases reveal a distinction between formal institutional subordination (legal framework, organizational charts, legislative oversight mechanisms) and the informal exercise of military influence. Poland achieved both: the legal-formal architecture of civilian supremacy was created, and over time, informal military influence in politics also diminished as officer corps identity transformed. Indonesia achieved the former but not the latter: formal *dwifungsi* was abolished, but informal influence persisted and in some domains expanded.

This pattern suggests that civilian supremacy requires not only legal reform but identity transformation — a change in how military officers understand their professional role. NATO's socialization mechanism produced this transformation in Poland (Sarvaš 1999 [1313]); Indonesia's epistemic community approach produced some movement in this direction but reached a ceiling limited by the absence of a transformative institutional environment.

### Pattern 3: Economic Independence as Reform Obstacle

Both TNI and the Polish communist military held economic interests (business holdings, defense industries, pension systems) that gave them revenue independent of civilian budget control. In Poland, NATO integration requirements included restructuring these arrangements; civilian oversight of the defense budget was a NATO conditionality requirement, which reduced the military's economic autonomy. In Indonesia, reform of TNI business holdings was proposed but inconsistently implemented; the military retained substantial economic independence that insulated it from civilian leverage. Sebastian (2006) [5511] documents how the TNI's economic holdings were integral to its "realpolitik ideology" — a self-conception of institutional autonomy grounded in economic and political interests, not just professional doctrine.

### Pattern 4: Reversal Risk Correlates with Reform Mechanism

Poland's civilian supremacy has proven durable across multiple changes of government, including periods when parties skeptical of Western integration held power. Indonesia's civilian supremacy has proven reversible: the 2025 TNI Law controversy shows that institutional gains made in 1998–2004 are being contested nearly three decades later. This suggests that the mechanism of reform — external anchor vs. internal coalition — is predictive of durability: binding conditionality produces more stable outcomes than contingent coalitional bargaining.

### Refined CMO Configuration (Post-Evidence):
- **C:** Post-authoritarian military with embedded political/economic roles + weak civilian institutions + absent or present external institutional anchor
- **M1 (confirmed):** External anchor (NATO) → conditionality + socialization → identity transformation + legal reform → durable civilian supremacy
- **M2 (confirmed with revision):** Internal coalition → legal reform without identity transformation → formal civilian control + persistent informal power + contingent durability (reversal-vulnerable)

## Cross-Case Patterns and Mechanisms

### Pattern 1: Military Decapitation Does Not Reliably Produce Civilian Control

The historical record shows no consistent relationship between the loss of military leaders (through death, prosecution, or forced retirement) and progress toward civilian control. Three pathways emerge:

**Consolidation pathway (most common under external threat):** When military organizations face external existential threats while losing leaders, they tend to centralize authority in the surviving institutional core and resist civilian oversight as a matter of organizational survival. The IRGC's current behavior — formalizing political control over civilian decisions under Vahidi, decentralizing tactical command while centralizing political authority — fits this pattern precisely.

**Vacuum pathway (common in rapid regime collapse):** When decapitation is part of broader regime collapse (as in Romania 1989, Libya 2011), the military may fragment rather than consolidate, creating space for civilian actors but also for armed factions. Iran is not currently in this pathway: the IRGC has sufficient institutional depth to avoid fragmentation.

**Reform-opportunity pathway (rarest, requires specific conditions):** Some cases show decapitation creating reform opportunities when: (a) civilian reformers have a credible alternative power base; (b) the military sees reform as its best survival strategy; (c) international actors provide both pressure and support. Indonesia's reformasi partially fit this model — but required Suharto's political system to collapse, not just his military commanders to die.

### Pattern 2: Organizational Depth Determines Survival Under Pressure

The IRGC's deliberate resilience strategy — three-rank successor chains, decentralized operations — mirrors findings from comparative military studies of forces designed to survive decapitation. This is a different organizational model from conventional militaries. The literature on "hybrid security forces" (incorporating political, economic, and coercive functions) finds that such forces are more durable under leadership losses than conventional militaries.

This matches Iran's observed behavior: despite losing two IRGC commanders in succession, the organization did not fragment. The decentralization strategy reduced central coordination but preserved operational capacity and political coherence.

### Pattern 3: Economic Embeddedness Increases Resistance to Reform

The comparative literature consistently finds that militaries with deep economic interests (business holdings, construction monopolies, import licenses) resist reform more tenaciously than those without. Indonesia's TNI business divestiture was the most contested part of reformasi; South Korea's military-industrial complex required decades of progressive reduction; Pakistan's Fauji Foundation remains unreformed despite decades of civilian pressure.

The IRGC's estimated 15-25% GDP share through Khatam al-Anbiya, Setad, and affiliated entities means that security sector reform in Iran requires economic restructuring, not merely institutional reform. This dramatically raises the reform cost for any transition government and gives IRGC commanders material incentives to block reform independent of ideological commitments.

## Causal Mechanisms

### Mechanism A: Institutional Survival Instinct Under External Threat

**Logic:** Military organizations that face existential external threats develop a "garrison state" logic in which institutional survival overrides all other considerations, including accountability to civilian oversight. This mechanism explains why the IRGC has *increased* its domestic political control during a period of military defeat and leadership decapitation.

**Evidence:** This mechanism is well-documented in the security sector reform literature. Studies of Lebanon's armed factions, Iraq's Shia militias, and Pakistan's ISI all show that organizations facing external threats use the crisis to entrench rather than reform. The specific mechanism involves three steps: (1) framing any civilian oversight demand as a "fifth column" threat during wartime; (2) using crisis conditions to place loyalists in civilian positions; (3) establishing precedents of IRGC control that become difficult to reverse after the crisis ends.

**Wartime consolidation leaves institutional residue:** Even after wars end, organizations that consolidated power during conflict retain structural advantages (control over intelligence, embedded networks in ministries) that persist. Poland's pre-reform military retained influence in intelligence for years after formal restructuring; Indonesia's TNI territorial commanders maintained informal governance roles long after their formal abolition.

### Mechanism B: External Leverage as Reform Catalyst

**Logic:** The most successful civil-military transitions involve external actors who provide both positive incentives (security guarantees, economic integration) and negative pressure (sanctions, isolation) simultaneously. Poland's NATO track worked because reforming governments could point to tangible benefits (NATO membership) that required civilian control as a precondition.

**Application to Iran:** If a US-Iran deal is reached that lifts sanctions, the question is whether Washington will condition sanctions relief on IRGC reform — specifically, the divestiture of IRGC business holdings and withdrawal from domestic politics. The JCPOA (2015) did not include these conditions; any successor deal will face pressure from US Congressmen to include them. Iran will resist. This negotiation will determine whether external leverage becomes a reform catalyst or a deal-killer.

### Mechanism C: Legitimacy Substitution During Succession

**Logic:** When political authority transitions to a figure with weak independent legitimacy (Mojtaba Khamenei), military organizations often "loan" their coercive legitimacy to the new leader in exchange for political protections and operational autonomy. This creates a dependency relationship in which the Supreme Leader needs the IRGC more than in his predecessor's time.

**Evidence:** This pattern is documented in Pakistan (ISI backing of civilian government transitions), Egypt (SCAF's relationship to both Morsi and Sisi), and to some extent Russia (FSB/military relationship to Putin after his accession). The short-term outcome is a civilian leader who appears more powerful but is actually more constrained by military preferences.

## Iran Analysis

> 📎 **Source Alignment: 🟡 Moderate** (70%)

**[CONTEXT UPDATE — April 2026: Active ceasefire; IRGC decentralizing under air campaign]**

The IRGC's institutional architecture presents both parallels and asymmetries with the militaries examined in this article. Like the militaries in South Korea and Indonesia, the IRGC controls extensive economic assets through affiliated holdings and import licensing, creating deep integration with political authority. However, the 2026 context — active US-Israel military campaign, two-week ceasefire, Islamabad talks — has introduced a new dimension: the IRGC is now undergoing **forced fragmentation** rather than the gradual reform scenarios the comparative literature typically addresses.

**Intervention lens — April 2026:** The US-Israel air campaign (February-April 2026) targeted IRGC command structures and facilities, producing observable decentralization: regional commanders are operating with increased autonomy, the Quds Force's external network has been severely degraded, and central command and control capacity is diminished. This mirrors the fragmentation pattern of the Iraqi Republican Guard under Coalition air campaigns in 2003 — with one critical structural difference: IRGC regional commanders retain lateral coordination capacity and economic independence that the Iraqi military lacked.

**Three intervention-conditioned reform scenarios:**

1. **Coercive degradation without dissolution (most likely — parallels Iraq 1991):** The Islamabad ceasefire framework may demand IRGC capacity limits (similar to UN imposed on Iraqi military post-1991) without formal dissolution. This preserves state institutional function but creates a rump IRGC that retains economic assets and domestic coercive capacity while losing extraterritorial mandate. The Polish and South Korean models (civilian control through institutional reform) remain theoretically applicable here — but only if the Islamabad framework includes governance conditionality.

2. **Coercive dissolution with CPA-analog replacement (lowest probability — parallels Iraq 2003):** External pressure for comprehensive IRGC dissolution would face the same failure modes as Iraq's CPA Order 2 (dissolution of Iraqi army): eliminating state security capacity faster than alternatives can be built, creating an armed unemployed class with organizational networks intact. The de-Ba'athification parallel is instructive — targeting the organization rather than specific individuals produced worse outcomes than selective vetting.

3. **IRGC survival through wartime consolidation (possible — parallels Russian siloviki post-1991):** If the ceasefire holds without imposed institutional conditions, IRGC consolidation of domestic political power (replacing civilian governance functions) is possible. The pattern: military organizations facing existential external threats entrench rather than reform, using emergency conditions to justify expanded domestic authority. Indonesia's military used anti-communist emergency framing similarly during the 1965-1966 period.

**Institutional mapping for Islamabad talks:** Reform would need to engage: IRGC Supreme Commander (subordinate to Supreme Leader, now Mojtaba Khamenei), the Artesh (conventional forces, less politically compromised), IRGC economic enterprises (critical — dissolving military before economic divestment creates the Iraqi failure mode), Basij (internal security, politically most sensitive), and Quds Force remnants (now operating under Ahmad Vahidi as IRGC commander).

**Key constraint the comparative literature identifies:** Security sector reform under wartime conditions almost always fails when attempted rapidly. South Korea's reform succeeded over 15+ years of gradual civilian control development. Indonesia's required the 1998 financial collapse as a triggering condition. Poland's worked because NATO integration provided an external anchor. None of these conditions apply to Iran's current situation — suggesting that the most realistic outcomes are either fragmented/diminished IRGC persistence or rapid dissolution followed by institutional vacuum.

**Iran transferability of case study lessons:** The Indonesia *dwifungsi* model remains the most relevant long-run template (military political withdrawal in exchange for preserved economic role) but requires civilian political capacity to absorb the power transfer. That capacity is currently suppressed under wartime emergency conditions. The more immediate Iraq/Libya parallel suggests that the sequencing of any reform matters more than its content — security stabilization must precede institutional redesign.

Existing civil-military relations theory focuses on the transition moment. Almost no theoretical attention has been paid to the multi-decade process of transforming military-political-economic conglomerates into conventional professional militaries in years after democratic transition.
