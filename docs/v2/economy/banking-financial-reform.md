# Banking & Financial System Reform

***Sources:** 332 | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-05*

## Overview

Financial system reform may be the most technically demanding dimension of democratic transition. Economies that were organized around state control of credit allocation—directing lending to politically favored sectors, maintaining exchange rate regimes that served regime interests, and managing inflation through administrative rather than market mechanisms—face a deep restructuring challenge when political systems change. The difficulty is not simply technical: financial reform intersects with virtually every other dimension of transition. Banking sector liberalization affects employment, investment, and access to credit; central bank independence requires enforceable constitutional or statutory commitments; and the resolution of state-owned enterprises' debts touches on questions of economic justice, property rights, and the distribution of transition costs.

Comparative evidence from post-communist and post-authoritarian transitions underscores a consistent pattern: the sequence and pace of financial reform matters enormously. Countries that liberalized capital accounts before establishing prudential regulation and central bank independence—South Korea in the mid-1990s, Czech Republic in the late 1990s—experienced severe financial crises that set back both economic reform and democratic consolidation. Countries that sequenced reforms more carefully—Poland, Estonia, Chile—achieved more durable financial stabilization even when initial liberalization was slower. The "Washington Consensus" approach that dominated international financial institution advice in the 1990s, which emphasized rapid comprehensive liberalization, has been substantially revised in light of this evidence; the current scholarly and policy consensus emphasizes sequencing, institutional capacity-building, and careful management of the transition from state-directed to market-based credit allocation.

Iran's banking sector adds layers of complexity absent from most comparison cases. Four decades of international financial sanctions have severed Iranian banks from the global financial system—no SWIFT connections, no correspondent banking relationships with major international banks, limited access to international capital markets. The banking sector is simultaneously dominated by state ownership, burdened with non-performing loans, fragmented by the parallel operation of Islamic finance principles alongside informal lending, and penetrated by Revolutionary Guard-affiliated entities operating outside normal supervisory frameworks. Unwinding this architecture in a transition scenario while maintaining macroeconomic stability and preventing capital flight would require sequencing decisions of exceptional delicacy.

This topic page examines comparative evidence on financial sector reform in transitional contexts, identifies the key mechanisms linking financial reform to broader democratic consolidation, and maps the Iran-specific structural challenges that any reform program would need to address.

## Theoretical Framework

This page employs a CMO framework to analyze banking and financial system reform in democratic transitions, focusing on the contextual conditions under which specific reform mechanisms produce outcomes of financial stability and economic democratization rather than crisis and elite capture. The framework distinguishes between contexts defined by the degree of prior financial development, the character of state-bank relationships, the presence of external conditionality, and the institutional capacity available for financial supervision. Outcomes range from successful financial deepening and democratization of credit access to financial crisis, oligarchic capture of privatized assets, or preservation of state-directed credit under new political leadership.

The primary theoretical framework is new institutional economics applied to financial systems. North's (1990) framework distinguishes between formal rules (banking laws, central bank statutes, capital adequacy regulations) and informal constraints (practices around related-party lending, political pressure on credit decisions, tolerance of regulatory forbearance). Successful financial reform requires changing both: new laws without changed practices replicate the dysfunctions of the old system under new legal cover, a pattern extensively documented in post-Soviet banking reform (Perotti, 1994). This distinction between formal and informal institutional change is particularly salient for Iran, where the relevant informal constraints—relationships between state banks, IRGC-affiliated enterprises, and political patrons—are deeply embedded and would survive purely formal regulatory change.

The second theoretical tradition is the political economy of financial reform, which examines how distributional conflicts shape the pace and character of liberalization. Alesina and Drazen's (1991) "war of attrition" model shows how competing social groups delay necessary but costly reforms when each hopes to shift adjustment costs to others. Applied to banking reform, this explains why non-performing loan resolution, privatization of state banks, and central bank independence all tend to be delayed in democratic transitions: each involves concentrated costs on politically organized constituencies. Rajan and Zingales's (2003) work on "saving capitalism from the capitalists" adds a further insight: incumbent financial actors often resist financial market development precisely because deeper, more competitive markets would erode their privileged access to credit.

The third tradition addresses Islamic finance specifically—its institutional logic, its regulatory implications, and its relationship to conventional banking. Kuran's (2004) analysis of Islamic economic institutions emphasizes that Islamic finance as practiced in contemporary Muslim-majority countries reflects political and ideological pressures as much as theological requirements, and that the prohibition of riba (interest) has been implemented through legal fictions (hilyal) that preserve economic substance while changing contractual form. This has regulatory implications: dual banking systems create regulatory arbitrage opportunities and complicate macroprudential oversight. The framework thus organizes the comparative evidence by asking which mechanisms—sequencing of liberalization, institutional capacity-building, non-performing loan resolution, supervisory architecture—interact with Iran's specific context to produce viable reform pathways.

## Evidence & Key Findings

South Korea's financial liberalization trajectory in the 1980s-1990s provides crucial comparative lessons for understanding the preconditions and hazards of premature financial market opening. Beginning with the Park Chung-hee era's state-directed industrialization model, South Korean financial markets operated under tight government control, with capital flows heavily regulated and directed toward strategic sectors. The transition toward liberalization, accelerated under democratization in the late 1980s, proceeded with insufficient attention to the sequencing of reforms or the maturity of regulatory institutions (International Monetary Fund, 1998).

The 1997 financial crisis—triggered by unsustainable short-term foreign borrowing by conglomerates (chaebol) and inadequate bank supervision—revealed the dangers of liberalizing capital accounts before establishing robust prudential regulations and transparent corporate governance standards. The crisis resulted in a contraction of approximately 5.8% in 1998, massive unemployment, and a bailout from the IMF requiring comprehensive institutional restructuring (World Bank, 1999). Critically, financial liberalization without corresponding institutional development in banking supervision, corporate transparency, and regulatory capacity created moral hazard: firms had access to international capital markets while maintaining opaque financing structures and implicit state guarantees.

The Czech Republic's banking transition provides a parallel case. Rapid privatization of state-owned banks following 1989 occurred without adequate regulatory frameworks or market discipline mechanisms. This created an endemic problem of non-performing loans, connected lending, and minimal capital adequacy standards, ultimately requiring government recapitalization in 1996-1997 (Fries & Taci, 2005). These comparative cases demonstrate that financial liberalization sequencing matters profoundly: institutional readiness must precede capital account liberalization, and banking regulation must develop in tandem with market opening, not sequentially thereafter.

## Cross-Country Patterns

Financial liberalization interacts critically with democratic institution-building, creating a paradoxical window of vulnerability. During democratic transitions, newly pluralistic governments face pressure to demonstrate economic openness and attract investment, creating political incentives to accelerate capital market liberalization before institutional safeguards are operational. This temporal mismatch—between political demands for opening and technical capacity for regulation—reproduces the South Korean pattern globally.

The specific danger lies in the interaction between three factors: (1) weak banking supervision institutions, often inherited from authoritarian regimes lacking modern central banking independence; (2) fragmented regulatory authority during transitions, where multiple agencies compete without clear hierarchies; and (3) political pressure from both foreign investors and domestic private capital seeking unrestricted market access. When these coincide, premature liberalization creates systematically procyclical credit growth, excessive risk-taking by financial institutions competing for market share, and contagion through undercapitalized banking systems. The sequencing problem thus becomes a democratic problem: how to sustain political support for institutional development that constrains financial actors, during a period when capital mobility creates exit options for those same actors.

## Iran Analysis

Iran's banking system presents a structurally distinct but institutionally comparable case to 1990s South Korea and post-1989 Czech Republic. The Islamic Republic's financial architecture reflects decades of theocratic control, foreign sanctions, and revolutionary institutional experimentation rather than market socialism. State-owned banks (including Bank Melli Iran, Bank Tejarat, and Bank Sepah) continue to dominate credit allocation with limited transparency or commercial discipline. Approximately 80% of banking sector assets remain under direct or indirect state control, compared to roughly 20-30% in comparable middle-income countries (Central Bank of Iran data, 2024).

A central structural constraint differentiates Iran from East Asian liberalizers: the Islamic banking system itself encodes regulatory fragmentation. Islamic banking principles (prohibition of riba [interest], asset-backing requirements, profit-sharing mechanisms) create a dual regulatory framework operating alongside conventional banking, with separate supervisory mechanisms and inconsistent application across institutions. This dualism complicates rather than clarifies regulatory authority, creating multiple paths for regulatory arbitrage and shifting control between Sharia compliance committees and conventional banking supervisors.

The secondary but critical problem involves IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps)-connected financial institutions. An estimated 20-30% of banking sector assets and credit allocations flow through Revolutionary Guard Corps-affiliated institutions (Asan Bank, Refah Bank, and specialized military credit institutions), operating with reduced transparency and distinct governance structures accountable primarily to security rather than commercial logic (Human Rights Watch, 2019). Sanctions removal post-JCPOA created ostensible opportunities for international integration, but without concurrent restructuring of state bank governance, IRGC financial dominance, or Islamic banking regulation, capital account liberalization would likely reproduce the same procyclical dynamics and systemic fragility visible in 1990s South Korea—but within a more politically fragmented institutional environment where security services compete with central banking authorities.

---

**Institutional Mapping:** Financial liberalization in Iran engages the **Central Bank of Iran** (بانک مرکزی ایران), which lacks genuine independence — its governor is appointed by the president with parliamentary approval but operates under de facto political direction; **state-owned banks** (Bank Melli, Bank Tejarat, Bank Sepah, and others) controlling approximately 80% of banking sector assets; **IRGC-connected financial institutions** (including Ansar Bank, formerly Ghavamin Bank, and other Revolutionary Guard-affiliated credit institutions) managing an estimated 20-30% of banking assets with reduced transparency and security-oriented governance; the **Guardian Council** and **Expediency Council**, which have blocked FATF compliance legislation; and **Sharia compliance committees** (کمیته‌های فقهی) operating within banks alongside conventional supervisory mechanisms, creating dual regulatory frameworks and arbitrage opportunities.

**Structural Parallels:** South Korea's 1997 financial crisis provides the most instructive parallel — financial liberalization without adequate prudential regulation, transparent corporate governance, and supervisory capacity created moral hazard where firms accessed international capital markets while maintaining opaque financing structures and implicit state guarantees. Iran's banking system exhibits analogous pathologies in more extreme form: state-directed lending to connected entities, non-performing loan ratios estimated at 15-40% (depending on classification methodology), and implicit government guarantees that create systemic moral hazard. Czech Republic's post-1989 banking transition — rapid privatization without adequate regulatory frameworks producing endemic non-performing loans and connected lending — mirrors what would likely occur if Iran liberalized its banking sector before establishing supervisory capacity. Both cases demonstrate the critical sequencing insight: institutional readiness must precede capital account liberalization.

**Key Actors and Sequencing:** The **IRGC-connected financial institutions** are the primary structural obstacle — they operate with security-oriented governance accountable to commanders rather than commercial logic or regulatory authority, creating a banking subsystem that undermines system-wide prudential standards. The **Central Bank** technocrats represent potential enablers but have historically lacked institutional power relative to political and security actors. The **Guardian Council** has been a specific blocker on FATF compliance, which is a prerequisite for international financial integration. Sequencing: (1) Central Bank independence legislation, establishing governor appointment, removal, and mandate protections insulated from political cycles — drawing on post-crisis South Korean reforms but adapted to Iran's institutional landscape; (2) comprehensive audit and recapitalization of state-owned banks, including recognition and resolution of non-performing loans estimated at hundreds of billions of dollars; (3) subordination of IRGC-affiliated financial institutions to Central Bank regulatory authority, requiring either their transformation into commercially-governed entities or their orderly unwinding; (4) harmonization of Islamic banking regulatory frameworks with international prudential standards, resolving the dual regulatory structure that enables arbitrage; (5) gradual capital account liberalization only after supervisory capacity, bank governance, and FATF compliance are demonstrably operational.

**Risks:** Premature financial liberalization during a political transition — when newly pluralistic governments face pressure to demonstrate economic openness — would reproduce the South Korean 1997 pattern in a more fragmented institutional environment. The temporal mismatch between political demands for economic opening and technical capacity for financial regulation is the critical vulnerability. Iran's Islamic banking system adds a distinctive complication: the prohibition of ربا (riba/interest), asset-backing requirements, and profit-sharing mechanisms create legitimate alternative financial structures but also regulatory opacity that sophisticated actors exploit. The interaction between IRGC financial networks, sanctions-era informal channels (hawala, cryptocurrency), and a formally liberalizing banking system could produce financial instability far exceeding the South Korean or Czech crises if sequencing is not rigorously maintained.

## Knowledge Gaps & Open Questions

1. **Institutional Prerequisites Question**: How can Iran develop autonomous central banking capacity and independent banking supervision while maintaining theocratic political control and parallel IRGC financial authority? South Korea's resolution occurred through democratic transition; Czech banking reform required EU institutional alignment. Neither path is available to Iran.

2. **Sequencing Dilemma for Dual Financial Systems**: Should Islamic banking regulation be harmonized with conventional banking supervision before capital account liberalization, or does the current dualism constitute an insurmountable obstacle? Existing cross-country evidence provides minimal guidance on sequencing reforms within dual financial systems.

3. **Sanctions Legacy and Information Asymmetry**: Given decades of sanctions-driven financial isolation, what mechanisms could establish baseline transparency and risk assessment in Iranian banking before international capital exposure? Most South Korean and Czech liberalization occurred from positions of relative information transparency.
