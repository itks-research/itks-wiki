# Velayat-e Faqih & Clerical Authority

***Last Updated:** 2026-04-05*

## Current State — Velayat-e Faqih & Clerical Authority (as of April 16, 2026)

The Velayat-e Faqih (Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist) system faces its most severe legitimacy crisis since its founding. Ali Khamenei's assassination on February 28, 2026 removed the person who embodied the system's political-theological authority for 35 years.

**Mojtaba Khamenei's legitimacy deficit:** Unlike Khomeini (Grand Ayatollah, revolutionary leader) or Ali Khamenei (who gradually built clerical credentials over 35 years), Mojtaba is a mid-ranking cleric without independent clerical accomplishment, scholarly output, or marja-level following. His election by the Assembly of Experts reflects IRGC and hardline political preferences more than theological consensus. The constitutional requirement (Article 5) that the Supreme Leader be a qualified faqih is met only in the most formalistic reading of Mojtaba's credentials.

**Hawza and Qom response:** The major marjas in Qom have notably not uniformly endorsed Mojtaba's election. The separation between political authority (Velayat-e Faqih) and theological authority (marjas followed for religious guidance and emulation) — already wide under Ali Khamenei — has widened further. The Hawza's response has been characterized by cautious silence from major figures rather than open opposition; but silence in this context is itself a form of non-endorsement. Grand Ayatollahs such as Sistani (Najaf), Makarem Shirazi, and Safi Golpaygani have not issued public endorsements of Mojtaba's succession.

**Legitimacy mechanism shift:** Mojtaba's authority rests primarily on coercive legitimacy (IRGC backing) rather than the original dual legitimacy of the Velayat-e Faqih system (theocratic-democratic). This is a structural change in how the Supreme Leader position functions, not merely a transitional gap.

**Wartime context:** The war and ceasefire have temporarily suppressed public debate about Mojtaba's legitimacy. Any successful diplomatic outcome at Islamabad would give Mojtaba a political win that could shore up his position. A failed diplomacy leading to resumed war would accelerate the legitimacy crisis.

## Key Actors — Velayat-e Faqih System (as of April 16, 2026)

**Mojtaba Khamenei** — Third Supreme Leader; son of Ali Khamenei; elected March 9, 2026. Legitimacy rests on IRGC backing and dynastic continuity rather than clerical consensus. Has not yet established an independent power base beyond his father's institutional inheritance. His ceasefire diplomacy via Islamabad talks is his first opportunity to build political legitimacy.

**Assembly of Experts** — Elected Mojtaba; played constitutionally correct role but the election reflected political realities (IRGC pressure, hardliner dominance) more than theological deliberation. Has not subsequently asserted oversight authority.

**Qom marjas (Grand Ayatollahs)** — Sistani (Najaf), Makarem Shirazi, Safi Golpaygani and other senior marjas have not publicly endorsed Mojtaba's succession. Their silence is notable. The Hawza's relationship with the new Supreme Leader is the most consequential long-term legitimacy variable.

**Ahmad Jannati (Guardian Council)** — Elder hardliner; important legitimizing figure within regime structures; has not publicly challenged Mojtaba.

**IRGC (Ahmad Vahidi)** — The real power behind Mojtaba's succession and governance. IRGC loyalty is the irreducible foundation of his authority. The theological legitimacy of Velayat-e Faqih has been replaced by coercive legitimacy — a transformation with long-term systemic implications.

**Montazeri legacy (institutional)** — Grand Ayatollah Montazeri's model of quietist Shia authority (marjas should not govern) has gained retroactive credibility as Velayat-e Faqih faces crisis. Some Qom clerics are revisiting his critiques of the system. Not an active political actor, but an intellectual reference point for reform-minded clerics.

**Independent clergy (grassroots):** Non-regime clerics in communities, some of whom maintained moral authority during the protest movement; a potentially significant actor if regime stability deteriorates further.

## Trend Analysis — Velayat-e Faqih

**Trajectory:** The theological legitimacy of Velayat-e Faqih is declining sharply. The combination of:
- Khamenei's assassination (undermining the Supreme Leader's inviolability)
- Dynastic succession (contradicts the meritocratic clerical principle)
- IRGC dominance (reveals who actually holds power)
- Declining public religiosity (reduces the religious appeal of clerical governance)
...represents a cumulative delegitimization that may be irreversible.

**Key question for ITKS:** Does the Velayat-e Faqih system collapse from within (clerical rejection), from without (military coup or popular revolution), or does it adapt (become a rubber stamp for IRGC rule)?

**Near-term:** Mojtaba will likely try to consolidate through successful ceasefire deal — a peace deal would give him a political victory his father never achieved. Failure would accelerate his delegitimization.

**Historical parallel:** The Islamic Republic's trajectory resembles post-revolutionary states where ideological founding principles erode under the pressure of institutional survival — the question is what replaces them.

## Explicit Assumptions — Velayat-e Faqih (April 16, 2026)

1. **Mojtaba Khamenei is Supreme Leader** — Legally elected by Assembly of Experts March 9, 2026; constitutionally valid but theologically contested. No open internal challenge as of April 16.
2. **IRGC is the power behind the throne** — Mojtaba's authority depends on IRGC loyalty; no independent clerical or popular base. This is the structural foundation of his governance.
3. **Qom is not uniformly behind Mojtaba** — Notable silence or distancing from major marjas; Hawza's non-endorsement is a legitimacy gap that will widen over time if unaddressed.
4. **Velayat-e Faqih system intact structurally** — Guardian Council, Expediency Council, Assembly of Experts all operational; constitutional mechanisms functioning.
5. **Legitimacy mechanism has shifted** — From theocratic-democratic dual legitimacy to coercive legitimacy (IRGC-backed); this is a systemic change, not a transitional state.
6. **Wartime conditions suppress open legitimacy debate** — Active diplomacy and ceasefire frame limits public theological debate; Islamabad success could give Mojtaba a political win to shore up position.
7. **Public religiosity declining** — Long-term secularization trend accelerated by repression and economic failure; war damage and regime failures accelerate this.
8. **Key uncertainty:** Whether Mojtaba can establish independent legitimacy through successful diplomacy at Islamabad, or whether the theocratic legitimacy deficit becomes permanent and structurally destabilizing.
