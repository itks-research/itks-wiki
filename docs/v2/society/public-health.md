# Public Health & Healthcare Access

***Sources:** 95 | **Last Updated:** 2026-04-05*

## Theoretical Framework

Framework: Narrative Synthesis — Popay et al. (2006)

This synthesis examines Iran's public health system through the lens of institutional reform capacity under external constraint. The central hypothesis posits:

**Theory of change:** Health system reforms in authoritarian states with external sanctions face a compound challenge — structural reforms (coverage expansion, payment reform, service quality) require sustained financing and institutional continuity, while sanctions systematically undermine both. The result is a cycle where reform ambitions outpace implementation capacity, producing partial gains that erode under renewed external pressure.

This hypothesis is tested against seven Iran-focused studies spanning 2019-2025, covering the Health System Reform Plan (HSRP), universal health coverage (UHC) pathways, and the documented health impacts of US economic sanctions across hospital systems, cancer care, pharmaceutical access, and population health outcomes. The evidence base is unusually cohesive: all studies address Iran directly, and five employ qualitative or mixed methods grounded in Iranian institutional realities.

## Evidence & Key Findings

The evidence organizes into three interconnected channels through which Iran's health system navigates reform under constraint.

**Health System Reform Plan: Ambitious Design, Fragile Implementation.** Kalroozi et al. (2020) provide the most systematic critical analysis of Iran's HSRP, initiated in June 2015 across public hospitals affiliated with Medical Sciences Universities. Their review of 45 studies identifies genuine gains: relative decreases in patient cost-sharing, improved emergency wards, and reduced cesarean section rates. However, the analysis reveals structural fragilities — the HSRP's success depended on stable and adequate financing, proper resource management, payment mechanism reform, and private sector participation. Each of these dependencies was subsequently disrupted by the reimposition of US sanctions following the 2018 JCPOA withdrawal. The authors document how the plan relied on 'a few instructions' rather than comprehensive institutional infrastructure, making it vulnerable to external shocks.

**Universal Health Coverage: Identifying the Control Knobs.** Derakhshani et al. (2021) contribute the most methodologically rigorous study — a multi-method design combining systematic review, focus group discussion, and expert panels — to identify 105 factors affecting Iran's path to UHC. These factors are organized within the WHO's 'control knobs' framework: financing (19 factors), payment systems (7), organization (23), regulation and supervision (33), and behavior (11). The study's most significant finding is that 'political commitment during political turmoil' emerged as a leading factor, alongside excessive focus on treatment over prevention, referral system weaknesses, out-of-pocket payment burdens, and — critically — economic sanctions and health system fragmentation. This confirms that Iran's UHC barriers are not primarily technical but political-institutional.

**Sanctions and Health: The Empirical Record.** Five studies document sanctions' impact through complementary lenses. Aloosh et al. (2019) provide the broadest epidemiological perspective, documenting an 11.8% GDP contraction in 2012, 40% inflation, and 200% currency depreciation, with downstream effects on mental health and medication access. Their finding that GDP growth rebounded 14.1% in 2016 — one year after sanctions relief — provides a natural experiment confirming the causal link between sanctions and health system deterioration.

Karami Matin et al. (2022) narrow the focus to hospital performance in Kermanshah Province, identifying five impact themes through 20 interviews: resource management challenges, financial restrictions, planning interruptions, service quality reductions, and organizational relationship changes. The qualitative depth reveals mechanisms invisible in aggregate data — individual hospitals unable to procure replacement parts for diagnostic equipment, pharmaceutical supply chains disrupted by banking restrictions.

Nazari et al. (2025) provide the most granular patient-level evidence, documenting sanctions' effects on cancer care through 21 interviews with patients, families, and NGO representatives. Their four-theme framework — medicine (price, scarcity, dispensing complexity, profiteering), medical equipment, human resources (specialist access, distorted doctor-patient relationships), and financial resources — reveals how sanctions create cascading failures that compound patient vulnerability.

Pourmohammadi et al. (2020) contribute a strategic analysis of Iranian public hospitals using PESTLE environmental scanning, identifying sanctions as a macro-level factor alongside government corruption, healthcare centralization, and disease pattern changes. Al Mustanyir (2024) offers a comparative perspective, finding that government healthcare spending in Iran — unlike Venezuela — did not respond significantly to sanctions, suggesting that countries with long sanctions experience develop coping mechanisms, though at the cost of quality rather than quantity of spending.

## Cross-Country Patterns

Two cross-cutting patterns emerge from the evidence.

First, **Iran's health reforms follow a 'two steps forward, one step back' trajectory driven by the sanctions cycle.** The HSRP (2015) was launched during the brief JCPOA relief period and achieved measurable gains; the reimposition of sanctions (2018) undermined those gains by disrupting financing, supply chains, and institutional capacity. Aloosh et al.'s (2019) GDP data confirm this cyclicality quantitatively, while Kalroozi et al. (2020) and Karami Matin et al. (2022) document it qualitatively at the institutional level. This pattern suggests that any post-transition health reform strategy must be sanctions-resilient by design — decoupled from the assumption of stable external economic conditions.

Second, **the sanctions impact on health operates through institutional degradation, not direct deprivation.** The evidence consistently shows that sanctions do not cause direct medical shortages (medications are technically exempt under humanitarian carve-outs). Instead, they degrade the institutional infrastructure that delivers care: banking channels for pharmaceutical procurement, hospital budgeting processes, specialist retention, and patient purchasing power. Nazari et al.'s (2025) cancer care findings illustrate this most vividly — the problem is not that cancer drugs are sanctioned but that the financial, logistical, and human systems required to deliver them are systematically weakened. This institutional degradation mechanism means that simply 'lifting sanctions' is insufficient; the damaged institutional capacity must be actively rebuilt.

## Iran Analysis

Iran's health system challenge in a transition context is distinctive because it combines three features: (1) a partially modernized health infrastructure with genuine technical capacity (Iran's HSRP demonstrates the ability to design sophisticated reform), (2) chronic institutional erosion from decades of sanctions that has weakened implementation capacity, and (3) a centralized governance structure where health policy is entangled with political control rather than technocratic independence.

The transferability assessment reveals that Derakhshani et al.'s (2021) 105-factor UHC framework provides the most actionable roadmap for post-transition planning. Their finding that regulation and supervision factors (33 of 105) dominate the landscape suggests that Iran's primary UHC barrier is governance architecture, not resource scarcity. A post-transition government would inherit substantial physical health infrastructure but would need to rebuild the regulatory and supervisory systems that currently serve regime control rather than public health optimization.

The sanctions-health literature (Aloosh et al. 2019; Karami Matin et al. 2022; Nazari et al. 2025) collectively suggests that Iran has developed institutional coping mechanisms — informal supply networks, alternative procurement channels, NGO-mediated patient support — that function as a parallel health system. Al Mustanyir's (2024) finding that Iranian healthcare spending proved resilient to sanctions (unlike Venezuela) supports this interpretation. These coping mechanisms represent both a vulnerability (they are opaque, inefficient, and inequitable) and an asset (they demonstrate adaptive institutional capacity that could be formalized post-transition).

A credible post-transition health strategy would need to: (1) preserve existing institutional knowledge while dismantling the politicized control structures, (2) rapidly restore formal pharmaceutical and equipment supply chains through sanctions relief and banking normalization, (3) formalize the informal coping networks as transitional mechanisms rather than eliminating them prematurely, and (4) implement Derakhshani et al.'s control knob framework with depoliticized regulatory capacity as the foundation.

**Institutional Mapping:** Iran's health system reform engages a layered institutional architecture. The **Ministry of Health and Medical Education** (وزارت بهداشت، درمان و آموزش پزشکی) administers the Health System Reform Plan (HSRP) and oversees public hospitals affiliated with Medical Sciences Universities. The **Supreme Leader's office** exercises indirect influence through appointment of university heads and control over parallel health institutions operated by **bonyads** — particularly the **Imam Khomeini Relief Foundation** (کمیته امداد) and the **Martyrs Foundation** (بنیاد شهید), which operate hospitals and clinics serving regime-loyal constituencies. The **IRGC** maintains its own military-medical infrastructure through **Baqiyatallah Hospital** and affiliated facilities. The **Islamic Health Insurance Organization** and **Social Security Organization** (سازمان تامین اجتماعی) manage coverage but face chronic underfunding exacerbated by sanctions. The **Majles Health Committee** legislates health policy but cannot compel funding commitments that compete with security expenditures prioritized by the Supreme Leader and IRGC.

**Structural Parallels:** The evidence reveals that Iran's HSRP, launched during the brief JCPOA sanctions-relief window (2015-2018), achieved measurable gains before being undermined by reimposed sanctions — paralleling reform trajectories in other externally constrained health systems. The "two steps forward, one step back" pattern driven by sanctions cycles finds no precise parallel in comparative health policy literature because few countries face Iran's combination of genuine technical capacity, chronic external disruption, and politically fragmented health governance. The closest analogy is Cuba's health system, which maintained impressive outcomes under sanctions through centralized commitment but at the cost of innovation and equipment modernization.

**Enablers and Blockers:** Health reform enablers include the **medical professional community** — Iran produces highly trained physicians and has substantial hospital infrastructure — and **technocratic health planners** who designed the HSRP's sophisticated multi-component reform. The **pharmaceutical industry** (both domestic producers and importers) would benefit from sanctions relief and supply chain normalization. Blockers include **IRGC-affiliated pharmaceutical importers** who profit from sanctions-era scarcity rents and parallel supply chains, **bonyad health facilities** that resist integration into unified national health governance, and **security establishment actors** who prioritize military-intelligence budgets over health spending.

**Iran-Specific Challenges:** Iran's health reform challenge is uniquely compounded by the sanctions-institutional degradation nexus documented in the evidence. Sanctions do not directly deprive Iran of medicines (humanitarian carve-outs technically exist) but systematically degrade the institutional infrastructure for delivering care — banking channels for pharmaceutical procurement, hospital budgeting processes, specialist retention, and patient purchasing power. This means that sanctions relief alone is insufficient; the institutional damage accumulated over decades of disruption must be actively rebuilt. A post-transition health strategy must: (1) preserve the existing medical knowledge base and institutional coping mechanisms (informal supply networks, NGO-mediated patient support) while formalizing them, (2) rapidly restore formal pharmaceutical supply chains through banking normalization, (3) depoliticize health governance by separating bonyad and IRGC health facilities from patronage functions and integrating them into a unified national system, and (4) implement the 105-factor UHC framework identified by Derakhshani et al. with regulatory independence as the foundation rather than political loyalty.

## Knowledge Gaps & Open Questions

- How can a post-transition government preserve the institutional learning and coping mechanisms developed under sanctions while replacing the informal, opaque structures with transparent, accountable ones — without creating a dangerous gap in healthcare delivery during the transition?
- What is the realistic timeline for restoring formal pharmaceutical supply chains after sanctions relief, given that banking relationships, logistics networks, and supplier trust must all be rebuilt simultaneously?
- Can Iran's demonstrated capacity for health reform design (the HSRP) translate into sustained implementation if the sanctions constraint is removed, or are there deeper institutional pathologies (centralization, corruption, politicization) that would persist regardless?
- How should a transition health strategy sequence priorities — immediate humanitarian relief (medication access, equipment procurement) versus structural reform (UHC architecture, payment systems, regulatory independence)?
