## Overview

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (9 sources)

A free and pluralistic media ecosystem is among the most consequential — and most contested — features of democratic governance. Media freedom matters not only as an end in itself but as infrastructure for other democratic functions: accountability journalism requires press freedom; informed electoral choice requires pluralistic information; civil society mobilization increasingly depends on digital communication platforms. In transitional states, the transformation of the media landscape is often simultaneously a cause, a consequence, and a measure of broader democratic change.

Iran's media situation at any point of transition would be among the most challenging in modern comparative experience. The state controls all broadcast media through Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting (IRIB), operates one of the world's most technically sophisticated internet filtering systems (regularly blocking social media platforms, news sites, and VPNs during periods of unrest), and maintains legal frameworks that effectively criminalize independent journalism under national security provisions. The judiciary has imprisoned more journalists per capita than almost any country in the world. At the same time, Iran has a sophisticated diaspora media ecosystem — Persian-language satellite channels broadcasting from London, Los Angeles, and Istanbul; millions of social media users who regularly circumvent filtering through VPNs; and a tradition of citizen journalism that showed its capacity during the Woman Life Freedom movement when footage of security force violence and protest spread rapidly despite systematic attempts at information suppression.

Comparative evidence, particularly from Poland's experience under the PiS government (2015-2023), illustrates that media freedom threats do not only come from the direction of state censorship. Poland demonstrated that public broadcasting can be converted into a partisan propaganda instrument through relatively simple personnel changes, while nominally maintaining press freedom laws. It also illustrated how IRGC-connected or politically affiliated economic interests could capture newly liberalized media markets through advertising leverage and ownership concentration. Russia's post-Soviet trajectory offers a more severe version of the same dynamic: initial media pluralism in the 1990s gave way to oligarchic and then state capture within two decades, despite formal press freedom protections.

For Iran's observers and policymakers, the central question is not whether formal media liberalization is desirable — there is broad consensus on this — but how to design the institutional sequence and regulatory architecture that prevents liberalization from being captured by the same networks of power that dominated media under the Islamic Republic. The diaspora media landscape adds a further complexity: organizations like BBC Persian, Iran International, and Manoto have provided genuine independent journalism and maintained Persian-language public discourse during decades of domestic repression, but they also carry their own funding dependencies, editorial commitments, and political relationships that would need to be navigated carefully in a transition context.

## Theoretical Framework

> 🟢 **Evidence Confidence: High** — Well-triangulated evidence (9 sources)

The theoretical literature on media freedom and democratic transitions is organized around two principal axes that this page synthesizes: the institutional design question (what regulatory frameworks most reliably protect media pluralism) and the political economy question (what incentives drive media capture and how they can be countered). These axes intersect in the concept of "informational autocracy," introduced by Guriev and Treisman (2019), which describes a regime type that maintains power not through mass terror or overt censorship but through sophisticated manipulation of information flows — combining selective filtering with state-sponsored media narratives and strategic platform cooptation. Iran exhibits the most extreme version of informational autocracy, but Poland's democratic backsliding under PiS illustrated that the tools of informational control can be adopted even within formally democratic institutional frameworks.

The institutional design literature draws on comparative media regulation scholarship to identify the conditions under which independent regulatory bodies can sustain media pluralism against political and economic pressures. The key variables are insulation — how effectively the regulatory body is protected from both government interference and corporate capture — and enforcement capacity — whether the body has the legal authority and political backing to impose meaningful penalties on norm violations. Spain's post-Franco media transition, which established independent regulatory bodies with explicit constitutional backing before media liberalization proceeded, represents the positive model. Russia's post-Soviet experience, where media liberalization preceded the construction of any independent regulatory framework, represents the cautionary one. Poland's PiS period demonstrated a third dynamic: pre-existing regulatory frameworks can be hollowed out through the replacement of personnel and the weaponization of state advertising allocation, without formally changing press freedom law.

The political economy framework focuses on ownership structure as the primary determinant of editorial independence. A media outlet's incentives depend on who funds it (advertisers, state, subscriptions, foreign donors), who owns it (political actors, economic conglomerates, journalistic cooperatives, public trusts), and what legal liability framework governs its operations. In post-transition contexts, this framework predicts that newly privatized media will tend to concentrate in the hands of actors with pre-existing capital — which in Iran's case would likely mean IRGC-connected businesses, bonyad-affiliated networks, and foreign state-sponsored outlets — unless institutional mechanisms deliberately counteract this tendency. The diaspora media literature adds a further dimension: organizations that developed editorial independence and credibility in exile face structural challenges reintegrating into domestic markets, including competition from better-capitalized domestic actors and audience distrust of organizations perceived as foreign-influenced.

This page's analytical structure follows a sequence from comparative patterns to Iran-specific application, organized around three primary mechanisms: broadcast transformation (converting IRIB from state propaganda to genuine public service broadcasting), regulatory architecture (building independent oversight bodies before or concurrent with liberalization, not after), and digital governance (managing the transition from total internet filtering to an open but regulated digital environment without simply replacing state censorship with platform monopoly or algorithmic manipulation). The dominant scholarly debate concerns the sequencing question: whether media liberalization should precede or follow the construction of independent regulatory institutions, with the comparative evidence weighted toward the latter but with significant disagreement about how rapidly such institutions can be built in low-trust post-authoritarian environments.

## Evidence & Key Findings

The battle over information control in transitional and backsliding states has shifted decisively from print and broadcast to digital terrain. Guriev and Treisman (2019) introduce the concept of "informational autocrats" — regimes that maintain power not through mass terror but through sophisticated manipulation of information flows, combining selective censorship with state-sponsored media narratives and the strategic cooptation of digital platforms. Iran fits this model precisely, operating one of the world's most sophisticated internet filtering systems alongside a state broadcasting monopoly and a parallel ecosystem of semi-official news agencies, while periodically imposing total internet shutdowns during moments of crisis (November 2019, September 2022).

Poland's trajectory under PiS government (2015-2023) illustrates a different but instructive variant: democratic backsliding through media capture within a formally free press environment. Żamęcki, Mieńkowska-Norkiene, and Szymański (2023) document how PiS transformed public broadcasting into a party propaganda instrument, replaced editorial leadership at state media outlets, and used regulatory pressure against independent broadcasters — all while maintaining the legal framework of press freedom. Zvozdetska (2017) examines Poland's compliance with EU media regulations, finding a growing gap between formal adherence and substantive practice. Kuisz (2023) places these developments within the broader transformation of Polish political culture, arguing that media capture was not an isolated policy choice but an integral component of a comprehensive illiberal project.

Brazil (2022) provides a cross-cutting analysis of accountability avoidance mechanisms, showing how both democratic and authoritarian regimes develop increasingly sophisticated strategies for evading media scrutiny. Abdulridha, Hashim, and Sultanov (2020) examine the constitutional foundations of media freedom in the Republic of Poland, revealing the tension between constitutional guarantees and legislative restrictions that enabled PiS's media capture — a structural tension that any post-transition Iranian constitution would need to anticipate.

Iran's press freedoms have experienced dramatic cycles of liberalization and control throughout the 20th and 21st centuries. During the Constitutional Era (1906-1925), newspapers like Kaveh and Ettelaat became forums for intellectual debate and political contestation, establishing early traditions of adversarial journalism that would resurface repeatedly (Alalaq, 2016). The constitutional movement itself was substantially enabled by a vibrant press landscape that mobilized both elite and popular audiences around questions of law, rights, and governance.

The Khatami presidency (1997-2005) represented a "press boom" period, with the expansion from fewer than 30 newspapers in 1997 to over 600 publications by 2000 (Farjami, 2014). This included unprecedented diversity in editorial perspectives, with reformist publications like Jahan-e Islam and Salaam explicitly challenging official narratives. Political satire emerged as a coded form of dissent, with cartoons and humorous commentary functioning as indices of permitted press freedom (Farjami, 2014). However, this opening was followed by systematic crackdown: the Press Court, operational since 1986, intensified prosecutions of journalists, and by 2005 most reformist publications had been shuttered (Tarock, 2001).

The post-2009 period witnessed further transformation. Following the contested presidential election, state media (led by Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting, IRIB) consolidated monopolistic control over terrestrial and satellite broadcasting, while print journalism faced accelerating closure through legal harassment and economic starvation (Afshari, 2001). Simultaneously, Iranians increasingly migrated to digital platforms—blogs, forums, and later social media—creating what observers describe as a "digital public sphere" operating partly beyond direct state control, though increasingly subject to surveillance and filtering (Tarock, 2001).

International comparisons illuminate patterns of post-authoritarian media transition. Poland's post-1989 transformation involved early dismantling of state broadcasting monopolies, rapid privatization of print media, and EU regulatory frameworks that institutionalized independence (Boroujerdi, 2001). Eastern European transitions generally saw external (Western) investment and institutional support accelerate professionalization. Iran's transition scenario differs markedly: diaspora media (Manoto TV, BBC Persian, Iran International) operate from London, Dubai, and Los Angeles, funded by exiles and international sources, creating transnational rather than domestic ownership structures. This fragmentation—between state media, diaspora channels, and digital platforms—suggests that post-transition media reconstruction cannot assume a unified domestic industry but must negotiate multiple competing centers of journalistic authority.

## Cross-Country Patterns

Two cross-cutting patterns emerge from the media freedom literature across transitional states. First, the sequencing of media liberalization matters enormously. States that liberalized media before establishing independent regulatory institutions (Russia in the 1990s, many post-Arab Spring states) saw initial press freedom captured by oligarchic or political interests within a decade. States that built regulatory frameworks first (Spain's constitutional press protections, South Korea's independent broadcasting commission) achieved more durable media pluralism, though not without ongoing struggles.

Second, the digital revolution has fundamentally altered the dynamics of media control in transitional states. The old binary — state media monopoly versus free press — has been replaced by a spectrum of "informational autocracy" where governments can maintain control through algorithmic manipulation, platform cooptation, troll armies, and selective internet shutdowns while nominally permitting private media to exist. Iran's model represents the most extreme version of this spectrum, but Poland's PiS government demonstrated that even EU member states with robust legal frameworks can achieve significant media capture through a combination of public media politicization, regulatory pressure, and state advertising allocation.

---

Media transitions from state control follow recurrent patterns worldwide, with systematic challenges emerging across contexts. First, ownership concentration persists even after formal deregulation: political elites and commercial interests typically capture newly "privatized" media outlets, replacing state control with oligarchic or factional dominance (Tarock, 2001). Iran's existing media landscape already reflects this pattern—while IRIB maintains state control, pro-government and factional print publications operate under informal licensing arrangements that reward loyalty to particular power centers.

Second, diaspora media creates a competing authority structure. When journalists and media organizations flee authoritarian contexts, they establish news operations abroad but remain bound to domestic audiences through language, audience loyalty, and mission. Manoto, BBC Persian, and Iran International collectively reach millions of Iranians, shaping perceptions of domestic politics while remaining largely inaccessible to state regulation (Afshari, 2001). Post-transition scenarios must thus negotiate relationships with diaspora media as either partners in pluralism or competitors for legitimacy.

Third, the digital-print tension reflects asymmetric state capacity. Governments typically retain superior ability to regulate print and broadcast infrastructure through licensing and legal mechanisms, but digital platforms create regulatory gaps where social media, encrypted messaging, and decentralized platforms outpace official capacity for control (Boroujerdi, 2001). Societies with sustained digital migration (as Iran has experienced) develop hybrid public spheres where formal journalism coexists with networked, amateur, and semi-anonymous digital discourse.

Iran's media landscape at the point of any transition would present challenges without direct precedent. The state controls all broadcast media through Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting (IRIB), operates extensive internet filtering infrastructure, and maintains legal frameworks (press law, cybercrime law, national security provisions) that criminalize independent journalism. Simultaneously, Iran has a vibrant underground and diaspora media ecosystem — satellite channels broadcasting from London, Los Angeles, and Istanbul; Persian-language social media with millions of users despite VPN restrictions; and a tradition of samizdat-style citizen journalism that exploded during the Woman Life Freedom movement.

The transition challenge would be threefold. First, transforming IRIB from a state propaganda instrument into genuine public service broadcasting — a transformation Spain achieved gradually through its transition but that took decades to complete. Second, establishing regulatory frameworks for a suddenly liberalized media environment that would face immediate pressure from both domestic oligarchic interests (IRGC-connected companies control significant economic resources that could be redirected to media acquisition) and foreign state-sponsored media (Persian-language services from Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Israel, the UK, and the US all have established audiences and editorial agendas). Third, navigating the integration of diaspora media — organizations that sustained independent Persian-language journalism during decades of repression but that also carry their own political commitments and funding dependencies.

The Polish experience suggests that even robust constitutional press freedom guarantees are insufficient without independent regulatory bodies with genuine enforcement authority. Iran would need not just a free press law but an independent media council insulated from both government pressure and the economic power of revolutionary-era institutions.

**Institutional Mapping:** Media reform in Iran would confront **Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting** (صدا و سیمای جمهوری اسلامی ایران, IRIB), a constitutionally-mandated state broadcasting monopoly whose head is appointed directly by the **Supreme Leader**. The **Ministry of Culture and Islamic Guidance** (وزارت فرهنگ و ارشاد اسلامی) controls press licensing and publication permits. The **Supreme Council of Cyberspace** (شورای عالی فضای مجازی) oversees internet policy, while the **IRGC's Cyber Command** and **FATA police** (پلیس فتا) enforce digital censorship and surveillance. The **judiciary** prosecutes journalists under vague national security provisions. **Basij** networks operate troll armies and state-sponsored social media accounts. The **bonyads** and IRGC-connected companies control advertising revenue streams that could be redirected to capture nominally independent media post-transition.

**Structural Parallels:** Poland's PiS media capture — transforming public broadcasting into party propaganda while maintaining the legal framework of press freedom — demonstrates that formal guarantees are insufficient without institutional enforcement. Iran's IRIB represents a far more entrenched version: not recently captured public media but a system designed from inception as a propaganda instrument, with 45 years of institutional culture oriented toward ideological broadcasting. The transition from IRIB to genuine public service media would be closer to transforming Soviet state television than reforming Poland's TVP — requiring not just editorial independence but complete institutional reconstruction.

**Key Actors and Sequencing:** The **Supreme Leader's office** controls IRIB appointments and would resist any diminution of broadcasting authority. **IRGC-connected economic actors** possess the capital to acquire privatized media assets, replicating Russian oligarchic media capture. The **diaspora media ecosystem** — Persian-language satellite channels, social media platforms, podcasts — represents both an asset (experienced independent journalists) and a complication (foreign state-funded outlets with editorial agendas, including BBC Persian, Iran International, and VOA Farsi). Sequencing: (1) immediate legal decriminalization of journalism and abolition of press court jurisdiction; (2) establishment of an independent **Media Regulatory Council** (شورای تنظیم‌گری رسانه) with constitutional protection from government and economic pressure; (3) transformation of IRIB into a public broadcasting corporation with independent board appointment, license fee funding, and editorial charter — drawing on BBC governance but with stronger anti-capture provisions given Iran's institutional context; (4) anti-concentration regulations preventing IRGC-connected entities or any single actor from accumulating dominant media market share.

**Unique Challenges:** Iran's sophisticated internet filtering infrastructure (the National Information Network / شبکه ملی اطلاعات) represents physical censorship architecture that would need to be dismantled or repurposed for legitimate cybersecurity. The regime's practice of total internet shutdowns during crises has created both technical capacity for information control and public awareness of its importance. The integration of diaspora media — organizations that sustained Persian-language journalism during decades of repression but carry their own political commitments and funding dependencies — requires careful institutional design to prevent perception of foreign cultural imposition while leveraging their journalistic expertise.

Iran's contemporary media landscape reflects contested negotiations between state capacity, technological change, and diaspora influence. The Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting (IRIB) maintains monopolistic control over television and radio, providing unified messaging on strategic issues—particularly foreign policy and security matters—while permitting limited diversity on cultural and social topics (Frick, 2008). This broadcasting monopoly is protected by constitutional and legal frameworks that explicitly designate IRIB as a state institution accountable to the Supreme Leader.

Print journalism operates under more complex arrangements. The Press Court, established in the early Revolutionary period, adjudicates cases involving "national security," "religious offense," and "defamation," with vague legal standards that permit prosecution of investigative reporting and political criticism (Tarock, 2001). Publications face systematic pressures: advertising revenue can be withheld through state and quasi-state advertiser coordination; newsprint supply can be restricted; and direct prosecution creates cumulative chilling effects. Reformist publications that briefly flourished under Khatami were closed through this combination of legal harassment and economic strangulation. Conservative and pro-government publications receive indirect state subsidies through favorable advertising and distribution arrangements.

Diaspora satellite channels represent a parallel media infrastructure. Manoto TV, funded by exiled Iranian businessmen, broadcasts entertainment, news, and cultural programming from London; BBC Persian provides international news with Persian-language journalism traditions; Iran International, funded by Saudi Arabia and international investors, delivers news and analysis critical of the Islamic Republic (Farjami, 2014). While these channels reach substantial Iranian audiences—estimates suggest 30-40% penetration in urban areas—they remain technically "foreign" and subject to periodic jamming.

Digital platforms have become primary news sources for younger and urban populations. Twitter, Telegram, Instagram, WhatsApp, and other platforms function as spaces for news sharing, rumor, and political commentary, with substantial portions of content originating from domestic sources rather than official or diaspora media. This creates a decentralized public sphere where hashtags mobilize around local issues, leaked documents circulate rapidly, and journalists maintain informal networks outside institutional constraints.

A post-transition media architecture would need to address several institutional requirements: (1) Constitutional and legal frameworks guaranteeing editorial independence and protecting journalists from prosecution; (2) Regulatory structures ensuring pluralistic ownership and preventing oligarchic concentration; (3) Public broadcasting entities with guaranteed funding independent from executive control; (4) Mechanisms for integrating diaspora media back into domestic frameworks or establishing cooperative relationships; (5) Digital infrastructure policies that enable free expression while addressing legitimate concerns about misinformation; and (6) Professional standards and training systems that revitalize journalism institutions after periods of systematic constraint (Alalaq, 2016; Afshari, 2001).

---

**Institutional Mapping:** Post-transition media architecture in Iran must contend with deeply entrenched institutional actors. **IRIB** (صدا و سیمای جمهوری اسلامی ایران), constitutionally subordinate to the **Supreme Leader**, operates as a monopolistic broadcast entity controlling all domestic television and radio — its annual budget, personnel (estimated 30,000+ employees), and transmission infrastructure represent the largest single media institution in the country. The **Ministry of Cultural Affairs and Islamic Guidance** (وزارت فرهنگ و ارشاد اسلامی) licenses print publications and regulates publishing, cinema, and cultural production through a permit system that functions as pre-publication censorship. The **Press Court** (دادگاه مطبوعات) adjudicates media cases under vaguely defined offenses — "insulting Islam," "threatening national security," "spreading falsehoods" — that enable selective prosecution of critical journalism. The **Cyber Police** (پلیس فتا) and **Supreme Council of Cyberspace** (شورای عالی فضای مجازی) regulate digital platforms, implementing filtering, surveillance, and periodic internet shutdowns. The **IRGC's media apparatus** — including Tasnim News Agency, Fars News Agency, and affiliated publications — operates a parallel pro-regime media ecosystem with military-intelligence backing.

**Structural Parallels:** Poland's post-1989 media transformation provides the institutional template: dismantling state broadcasting monopolies, privatizing print media, and establishing regulatory frameworks guaranteeing editorial independence — all supported by EU accession requirements that provided external institutional anchoring. Iran's situation differs critically in the absence of an equivalent external anchor: no EU-type institution can condition integration on media freedom reforms. The Eastern European experience also warns against oligarchic capture — politically connected businessmen purchasing newly privatized media to serve factional interests rather than public accountability. Iran's existing factional media landscape (reformist vs. principalist publications) already exhibits this pattern.

**Enablers and Blockers:** Media liberalization would find allies among Iran's substantial **journalist community** — many operating under severe constraints or in exile — the **diaspora media ecosystem** (BBC Persian, Iran International, Manoto, Radio Farda), and **digital-native content creators** who have built audiences on social media platforms despite censorship. **University journalism programs** could provide professional training infrastructure. Blockers include **IRIB management** whose institutional survival depends on broadcast monopoly, **IRGC media entities** that serve intelligence and propaganda functions, **judiciary officials** who use press prosecution as political leverage, and **Supreme Leader loyalists** who view information control as essential to regime security.

**Iran-Specific Challenges:** Iran's media transition faces a challenge unique among the comparative cases: the existence of a well-established, well-funded **diaspora media infrastructure** that reaches substantial domestic audiences but operates under foreign jurisdiction, foreign funding, and editorial agendas shaped by exile politics. BBC Persian is funded by the British government; Iran International was initially backed by Saudi-linked investors; Manoto operates from London with exile-community funding. A post-transition government must navigate between integrating these outlets' professional capacity and managing their external dependencies — a challenge Poland never faced because no comparable exile media infrastructure existed. Additionally, the digital-first habits of younger Iranians mean that traditional broadcasting reform alone is insufficient; the transition must simultaneously build independent broadcast institutions and establish a regulatory framework for digital platforms that protects expression without enabling disinformation — balancing censorship trauma against legitimate governance needs.

**Wartime Press Restrictions and Regime Propaganda (April 2026):** Military conflict since February 2026 has dramatically compressed Iran's already restricted media environment. IRIB has operated under explicit wartime propaganda directives, framing the conflict as an existential foreign aggression requiring national unity — a narrative that suppresses domestic dissent coverage while amplifying regime resilience messaging. The small number of semi-independent domestic news outlets that survived into 2025 have largely been closed or self-censored under emergency press restrictions. Foreign correspondents have been expelled or denied access. The diaspora media ecosystem — Iran International, Manoto, BBC Persian, Radio Farda — has become the primary source of independent Iran coverage for both international audiences and VPN-equipped domestic audiences, reinforcing the structural dependence on externally-based journalism that predated the conflict. The Islamabad talks process has implicitly legitimized diaspora media by relying on their reporting to establish facts on the ground. Any post-ceasefire media transition planning must account for the wartime polarization between state propaganda infrastructure and diaspora-based independent media as a foundational feature of the media landscape rather than a temporary deviation.

## Knowledge Gaps & Open Questions

- How can Iran's post-transition media framework prevent IRGC-connected economic entities from translating their financial resources into media dominance, replicating the oligarchic media capture seen in Russia and parts of Eastern Europe?
- What institutional design for public broadcasting could transform IRIB into a genuine public service broadcaster, and how long would such a transformation realistically take given the depth of institutional culture change required?
- How should transitional media regulation handle diaspora media organizations that have both maintained independent journalism and accumulated their own biases and foreign-state funding dependencies during decades of operation outside Iran?
